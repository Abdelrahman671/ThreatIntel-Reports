class="c35 doc-content">
 <p class="UmcNyNBsWB-c7"><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c2 UmcNyNBsWB-c38">Posted by the </span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c20 UmcNyNBsWB-c2"><a href="#bigsleepteam">Big Sleep team</a></span></p>
 <p class="UmcNyNBsWB-c7"><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c21 UmcNyNBsWB-c27 UmcNyNBsWB-c28 UmcNyNBsWB-c33">Introduction</span></p>
 <p class="UmcNyNBsWB-c7"><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c2">In our previous post, </span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c20 UmcNyNBsWB-c2"><a href="https://googleprojectzero.blogspot.com/2024/06/project-naptime.html">Project Naptime: Evaluating Offensive Security Capabilities of Large Language Models</a></span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c13 UmcNyNBsWB-c2">, we introduced our framework for large-language-model-assisted vulnerability research and demonstrated its potential by improving the state-of-the-art performance on Meta&#39;s CyberSecEval2 benchmarks. Since then, Naptime has evolved into Big Sleep, a collaboration between Google Project Zero and Google DeepMind.</span></p>
 <p class="UmcNyNBsWB-c7"><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c27">Today, we&#39;re excited to share the first real-world vulnerability discovered by the Big Sleep agent</span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c2">: an exploitable stack buffer underflow in </span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c20 UmcNyNBsWB-c2"><a href="https://sqlite.org/">SQLite</a></span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c2">, a widely used open source database engine. We discovered the </span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c20 UmcNyNBsWB-c2"><a href="https://project-zero.issues.chromium.org/issues/372435124">vulnerability</a></span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c2">&nbsp;and reported it to the developers in early October, who </span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c20 UmcNyNBsWB-c2"><a href="https://sqlite.org/src/info/41d58a014ce89356">fixed it</a></span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c2">&nbsp;on the same day. Fortunately, we found this issue </span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c27">before it appeared in an official release, so SQLite users were not impacted</span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c13 UmcNyNBsWB-c2">.</span></p>
 <p class="UmcNyNBsWB-c7"><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c2">We believe this is the first public example of an AI agent finding a previously unknown exploitable memory-safety issue in widely used real-world software. Earlier this year at the DARPA AIxCC event, Team Atlanta </span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c20 UmcNyNBsWB-c2"><a href="https://team-atlanta.github.io/blog/post-asc-sqlite/">discovered a null-pointer dereference</a></span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c13 UmcNyNBsWB-c2">&nbsp;in SQLite, which inspired us to use it for our testing to see if we could find a more serious vulnerability.</span></p>
 <p class="UmcNyNBsWB-c7"><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c13 UmcNyNBsWB-c2">We think that this work has tremendous defensive potential. Finding vulnerabilities in software before it&#39;s even released, means that there&#39;s no scope for attackers to compete: the vulnerabilities are fixed before attackers even have a chance to use them. Fuzzing has helped significantly, but we need an approach that can help defenders to find the bugs that are difficult (or impossible) to find by fuzzing, and we&#39;re hopeful that AI can narrow this gap. We think that this is a promising path towards finally turning the tables and achieving an asymmetric advantage for defenders.</span></p>
 <p class="UmcNyNBsWB-c7"><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c13 UmcNyNBsWB-c2">The vulnerability itself is quite interesting, along with the fact that the existing testing infrastructure for SQLite (both through OSS-Fuzz, and the project&#39;s own infrastructure) did not find the issue, so we did some further investigation.</span></p>
 <p class="UmcNyNBsWB-c7"><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c21 UmcNyNBsWB-c27 UmcNyNBsWB-c28 UmcNyNBsWB-c33">Methodology</span></p>
 <p class="UmcNyNBsWB-c7"><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c2">A key motivating factor for Naptime and now for Big Sleep has been the </span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c20 UmcNyNBsWB-c2"><a href="https://blog.google/threat-analysis-group/0-days-exploited-wild-2022/%23:~:text%3DOver%252040%2525%2520of%2520the%25200%252Ddays%2520discovered%2520were%2520variants%2520of%2520previously%2520reported%2520vulnerabilities">continued in-the-wild discovery</a></span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c13 UmcNyNBsWB-c2">&nbsp;of exploits for variants of previously found and patched vulnerabilities. As this trend continues, it&#39;s clear that fuzzing is not succeeding at catching such variants, and that for attackers, manual variant analysis is a cost-effective approach.</span></p>
 <p class="UmcNyNBsWB-c7"><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c13 UmcNyNBsWB-c2">We also feel that this variant-analysis task is a better fit for current LLMs than the more general open-ended vulnerability research problem. By providing a starting point &ndash; such as the details of a previously fixed vulnerability &ndash; we remove a lot of ambiguity from vulnerability research, and start from a concrete, well-founded theory: &quot;This was a previous bug; there is probably another similar one somewhere&quot;.</span></p>
 <p class="UmcNyNBsWB-c7"><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c2">Our project is still in the research stage, and we are currently using small programs with known vulnerabilities to evaluate progress. Recently, we decided to put our models and tooling to the test by running our first extensive, real-world variant analysis experiment on SQLite. We collected a number of recent commits to the SQLite repository, manually removing trivial and documentation-only changes. We then adjusted the prompt to provide the agent with both the commit message and a diff for the change, and asked the agent to review the current repository (at </span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c2 UmcNyNBsWB-c20"><a href="https://sqlite.org/src/info/2f7eab381e167609">HEAD</a></span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c13 UmcNyNBsWB-c2">) for related issues that might not have been fixed.</span></p>
 <p class="UmcNyNBsWB-c7"><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c21 UmcNyNBsWB-c27 UmcNyNBsWB-c28 UmcNyNBsWB-c33">Discovered Vulnerability</span></p>
 <p class="UmcNyNBsWB-c7"><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c2">The vulnerability is an interesting one where a special sentinel value </span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c9">-1</span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c2">&nbsp;is used in an (otherwise) index-typed field </span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c9">iColumn</span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c13 UmcNyNBsWB-c2">:</span></p>
 <p class="UmcNyNBsWB-c15"><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c2"></span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c1">7476:</span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c6">&nbsp; &nbsp;</span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c1">struct</span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c6">&nbsp;</span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c1">sqlite3_index_constraint</span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c6">&nbsp;</span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c1">{</span></p>
 <p class="UmcNyNBsWB-c15"><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c1">7477:</span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c6">&nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; </span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c1">int</span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c6">&nbsp;</span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c1">iColumn;</span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c6">&nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; </span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c1">/*</span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c6">&nbsp;</span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c1">Column</span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c6">&nbsp;</span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c1">constrained.</span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c6">&nbsp; </span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c11">-1</span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c17">&nbsp;</span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c11">for</span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c17">&nbsp;</span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c11">ROWID</span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c6">&nbsp;</span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c1">*/</span></p>

 <p class="UmcNyNBsWB-c7"><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c24 UmcNyNBsWB-c2 UmcNyNBsWB-c22 UmcNyNBsWB-c19">The agent produces a reasonable assessment of the changes in the diff, and uses this to form a starting point for research:</span></p>

 <p class="UmcNyNBsWB-c0"><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c6"><br></span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c2">The CL improves the handling of WHERE terms shared between OR branches in virtual table queries by introducing the </span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c9">termFromWhereClause</span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c2">&nbsp;function.  <p class="UmcNyNBsWB-c7"><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c2 UmcNyNBsWB-c19">However, it&#39;s particularly interesting to see that once the program has crashed the model does a much better job of explaining things - while producing a root-cause analysis and a full crash report we can see the agent clearly crafting a testcase to trigger the specific, identified edge-case:</span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c13 UmcNyNBsWB-c2"><br></span></p>

 <p class="UmcNyNBsWB-c0"><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c2"><br>The program crashes with the input </span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c9">SELECT * FROM generate_series(1,10,1) WHERE ROWID = 1;</span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c2">&nbsp;due to an assertion failure in function </span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c9">seriesBestIndex</span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c2">. <br><br>The assertion </span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c9">assert(iCol&gt;=0 &amp;&amp; iCol&lt;=2)</span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c2">&nbsp;fails because the </span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c9">iColumn</span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c2">&nbsp;field in the </span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c9">sqlite3_index_info</span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c2">&nbsp;struct can contain values outside of the valid range for column indexes, such as </span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c9">-1</span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c13 UmcNyNBsWB-c2">&nbsp;when a constraint involves the ROWID.</span></p>
 <p class="UmcNyNBsWB-c0 UmcNyNBsWB-c4"><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c13 UmcNyNBsWB-c2"></span></p>

 <p class="UmcNyNBsWB-c15 UmcNyNBsWB-c4"><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c13 UmcNyNBsWB-c2"></span></p><h2 class="UmcNyNBsWB-c34" id="h.n8khgp4u0snd"><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c21 UmcNyNBsWB-c27 UmcNyNBsWB-c28 UmcNyNBsWB-c36">What about Fuzzing?</span></h2>
 <p class="UmcNyNBsWB-c7"><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c2">Given the apparent simplicity of the reproduction case, an obvious question arises: why wasn&rsquo;t this bug discovered earlier by traditional fuzzing? The &ldquo;simple&rdquo; answer lies in the configuration of the fuzzing harnesses. The harness used by OSS-Fuzz isn&#39;t built with the </span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c9">generate_series</span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c2">&nbsp;extension enabled, and the alternative </span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c9">fuzzingshell.c</span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c2">&nbsp;harness contained an older version of the </span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c9">seriesBestIndex</span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c2">&nbsp;function, unaffected by the bug. Although the </span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c20 UmcNyNBsWB-c2"><a href="https://www.sqlite.org/afl/dir?ci=tip">SQLite AFL repo</a></span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c13 UmcNyNBsWB-c2">&nbsp;contains a configuration for fuzzing the same CLI binary that we provided to the Big Sleep agent, it appears not to be widely used.</span></p>
 <p class="UmcNyNBsWB-c7"><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c2">To understand whether the bug is truly &ldquo;shallow&quot;, we attempted to rediscover it through fuzzing. We followed the fuzzing instructions from the </span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c20 UmcNyNBsWB-c2"><a href="https://www.sqlite.org/afl/doc/trunk/README.md">SQLite documentation</a></span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c2">&nbsp;and used the CLI target. We also verified that the fuzzing corpus contained the required </span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c9">generate_series</span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c2">&nbsp;and </span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c9">rowid</span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c13 UmcNyNBsWB-c2">&nbsp;keywords before launching an AFL run. However, the issue remained undiscovered after 150 CPU-hours of fuzzing.</span></p>
 <p class="UmcNyNBsWB-c7"><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c13 UmcNyNBsWB-c2">We then tried to simplify the task for the fuzzer by, for example, adding the necessary keywords to AFL&#39;s SQL dictionary. However, it seems the bug can only be quickly found if the corpus contains an example very close to the crashing input, as code coverage doesn&#39;t appear to be a reliable guide for this particular issue.</span></p>
 <p class="UmcNyNBsWB-c7"><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c2">Admittedly, AFL isn&#39;t the most suitable tool for a text-based format like SQL, where most inputs are syntactically invalid and will be rejected by the parser. Nevertheless, it&#39;s interesting to compare this result with </span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c20 UmcNyNBsWB-c2"><a href="https://lcamtuf.blogspot.com/2015/04/finding-bugs-in-sqlite-easy-way.html">Michal Zalewski&rsquo;s blog post on fuzzing SQLite</a></span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c2">&nbsp;from 2015. Back then, AFL was quite effective at uncovering bugs in SQLite; after years of fuzzing, it seems the tool has reached a natural saturation point. While our results so far seem minor in comparison to the dramatic step-change in effectiveness that came with the release of AFL, it&#39;s interesting to see that it has its own strengths and might be able to effectively uncover a </span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c27">distinct</span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c13 UmcNyNBsWB-c2">&nbsp;set of vulnerabilities.</span></p><h1 class="UmcNyNBsWB-c16" id="h.85i6df6n8fiq"><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c21 UmcNyNBsWB-c27 UmcNyNBsWB-c28 UmcNyNBsWB-c33">Conclusion</span></h1>
 <p class="UmcNyNBsWB-c7"><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c30 UmcNyNBsWB-c2">For the team this is a moment of validation and success - finding a vulnerability in a widely-used and well fuzzed open source project is </span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c2 UmcNyNBsWB-c41">an exciting result! </span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c2">When provided with the right tools, current LLMs can perform vulnerability research.</span></p>
 <p class="UmcNyNBsWB-c7"><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c2 UmcNyNBsWB-c30">However, we want to reiterate that these are highly experimental results</span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c21 UmcNyNBsWB-c2 UmcNyNBsWB-c29">. The position of the Big Sleep team is that at present, it&#39;s likely that a target-specific fuzzer would be at least as effective (at finding vulnerabilities). </span></p>
 <p class="UmcNyNBsWB-c7"><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c30 UmcNyNBsWB-c2">We hope that in the future this effort will lead to a significant advantage to defenders - with the potential not only to find crashing testcases, but also to provide high-quality root-cause analysis, triaging and fixing issues could be much cheaper and more effective in the future. We aim to continue sharing our research in this space, keeping the gap between the public state-of-the-art and private state-of-the-art as small as possible. <br><br></span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c13 UmcNyNBsWB-c2">The Big Sleep team will continue to work in this space, advancing Project Zero&#39;s mission of making 0-day hard.</span></p><h3 class="UmcNyNBsWB-c5" id="h.1tivp1wdbnt9"><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c14 UmcNyNBsWB-c21 UmcNyNBsWB-c28"><a id='bigsleepteam'>The Big Sleep Team</a></span></h3>
 <p class="UmcNyNBsWB-c7"><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c2">This isn&#39;t just a Project Zero effort any more, and everyone who has contributed to this effort is listed below</span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c2 UmcNyNBsWB-c19">&nbsp;(names in alphabetical order)</span><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c13 UmcNyNBsWB-c2">:</span></p>
 <p class="UmcNyNBsWB-c8 UmcNyNBsWB-c10"><span class="UmcNyNBsWB-c40">Miltos Allamanis, Martin Arjovsky, Charles Blundell, Lars Buesing, Mark Brand, Sergei Glazunov, Dominik Maier, Petros Maniatis, Guilherme Marinho, Henryk Michalewski, Koushik Sen, Charles Sutton, Vaibhav Tulsyan, Marco Vanotti, Theophane Weber, Dan Zheng</span></p>
