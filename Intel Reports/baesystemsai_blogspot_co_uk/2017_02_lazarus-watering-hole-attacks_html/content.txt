<h3 class='post-title entry-title' itemprop='name'>
Lazarus & Watering-hole attacks
</h3>
<div class='post-header'>
<div class='post-header-line-1'>
</div>
</div>
<div class='post-body entry-content' id='post-body-1154826587609206396' itemprop='articleBody'>
On 3rd February 2017, researchers at badcyber.com released an <a href="https://badcyber.com/several-polish-banks-hacked-information-stolen-by-unknown-attackers/" target="_blank">article</a> that detailed a series of attacks directed at Polish financial institutions. The article is brief, but states that <em>"This is &#8211; by far &#8211; the most serious information security incident we have seen in Poland"</em> followed by a claim that over 20 commercial banks had been confirmed as victims.<br />
<a name='more'></a><br />
This report provides an outline of the attacks based on what was shared in the article, and our own additional findings.
<br />
<h5>
ANALYSIS</h5>
<h5>
As stated in the blog, the attacks are suspected of originating from the website of the Polish Financial Supervision Authority (knf.gov[.]pl), shown below:</h5>
<br />
<div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
<a href="https://blogger.googleusercontent.com/img/b/R29vZ2xl/AVvXsEgMzqY9AP7r9TMBFOe-a4OisTN9oIrJk-EpP7MHQSKmSY1CJocMvBWe3XcyocODheWR8bX3_AMIDC3jreQ16j-rvMQI0WFfCB0Ps6UjrZ56p7vYbUoShsHWUqnrYlWqbivMU0wGBTFF0M8/s640/Lazarus_and_watering_hole_attacks_one.png" imageanchor="1" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;"><img border="0" height="321" src="https://blogger.googleusercontent.com/img/b/R29vZ2xl/AVvXsEgMzqY9AP7r9TMBFOe-a4OisTN9oIrJk-EpP7MHQSKmSY1CJocMvBWe3XcyocODheWR8bX3_AMIDC3jreQ16j-rvMQI0WFfCB0Ps6UjrZ56p7vYbUoShsHWUqnrYlWqbivMU0wGBTFF0M8/s640/Lazarus_and_watering_hole_attacks_one.png" width="640" /></a></div>
<br />
<br />
From at least 2016-10-07 to late January the website code had been modified to cause visitors to download malicious JavaScript files from the following locations:
<br />
<br />
<div style="background: rgb(242, 242, 242); border-image: none; border: 1pt solid rgb(166, 166, 166); padding: 2pt;">
<div style="background: rgb(242, 242, 242); margin-left: 5px;">
<code>hxxp://sap.misapor[.]ch/vishop/view.jsp?pagenum=1<br />
hxxps://www.eye-watch[.]in/design/fancybox/Pnf.action
</code>
</div>
</div>
<br />
Both of these appear to be compromised domains given they are also hosting legitimate content and have done for some time. The malicious JavaScript leads to the download of malware to the victim&#8217;s device.
<br />
<br />
Some hashes of the backdoor have been provided in BadCyber's technical analysis:
<br />
<br />
<div style="background: rgb(242, 242, 242); border-image: none; border: 1pt solid rgb(166, 166, 166); padding: 2pt;">
<div style="background: rgb(242, 242, 242); margin-left: 5px;">
<code>85d316590edfb4212049c4490db08c4b<br />
c1364bbf63b3617b25b58209e4529d8c<br />
1bfbc0c9e0d9ceb5c3f4f6ced6bcfeae
</code>
</div>
</div>
<br />
The C&amp;Cs given in the BadCyber analysis were the following IP addresses:
<br />
<br />
<div style="background: rgb(242, 242, 242); border-image: none; border: 1pt solid rgb(166, 166, 166); padding: 2pt;">
<div style="background: rgb(242, 242, 242); margin-left: 5px;">
<code>125.214.195.17<br />
196.29.166.218
</code>
</div>
</div>
<br />
<h5>
LAZARUS MALWARE</h5>
<br />
Only one of the samples referenced by BadCyber is available in public malware repositories. At the moment we cannot verify that it originated from the watering-hole on the KNF website &#8211; but we have no reason to doubt this either.
<br />
<br />

<br />
The file is packed with a commercial packer known as <em><a href="http://enigmaprotector.com/en/about.html" target="_blank">'Enigma Protector'</a></em>. Once unpacked it drops a known malware variant, which has been seen as part of the Lazarus group&#8217;s toolkit in other cases over the past year.
<br />
<br />
The unpacked executable takes several command line arguments:
<br />
<br />
<div style="background: rgb(242, 242, 242); border-image: none; border: 1pt solid rgb(166, 166, 166); padding: 2pt;">
<div style="background: rgb(242, 242, 242); margin-left: 5px;">
<code>-l</code>: list service names, available for its own registration<br />
<code>-o</code>: open specified event<br />
<code>-t</code>: set specified event<br />
<code>-x [PASSWORD] -e [SERVICE_NAME]</code>: drop/install DLL under specified <code>[SERVICE_NAME]</code><br />
<code>-x [PASSWORD] -f [SERVICE_NAME]</code>: recreate the keys that keep the password for the next stage DLL, under the specified <code>[SERVICE_NAME]</code>
</div>
</div>
<br />
The provided password's MD5 hash is used as an RC4 password. On top of that, there is one more RC4-round, using a hard coded 32-byte RC4 password:
<br />
<br />
<div style="background: rgb(242, 242, 242); border-image: none; border: 1pt solid rgb(166, 166, 166); padding: 2pt;">
<div style="background: rgb(242, 242, 242); margin-left: 5px;">
<code>53 87 F2 11 30 3D B5 52 AD C8 28 09 E0 52 60 D0 6C C5 68 E2 70 77 3C 8F 12 C0 7B 13 D7 B3 9F 15</code>
</div>
</div>
<br />
Once the data is decrypted with two RC4 rounds, the dropper checks the decrypted data contains a valid 4-byte signature: <code>0xBC0F1DAD</code>.
<br />
<br />
<h5>
WATERING HOLE ANALYSIS</h5>
<br />
The attacker content on the compromised <code>sap.misapor[.]ch site</code> was not accessible at the time of writing. However, archived versions of some pages can be found:
<br />
<br />
<div style="background: rgb(242, 242, 242); border-image: none; border: 1pt solid rgb(166, 166, 166); padding: 2pt;">
<div style="background: rgb(242, 242, 242); margin-left: 5px;">
<code>
http://web.archive[.]org/web/20170203175640/https://sap.misapor.ch/Default.html<br />
http://web.archive[.]org/web/20170203175641/https://sap.misapor.ch/Silverlight.js</code>
</div>
</div>
<br />
The <code>Default.html</code> contains code to load <code>MisaporPortalUI.xap</code> &#8211; a Silverlight application which likely would contain the malicious first-stage implant. This is unfortunately not available for analysis currently.
<br />
<br />

</code>
</div>

<br />
<h5>
ADDITIONAL WATERING HOLES</h5>
<br />
The <code>eye-watch[.]in</code> domain appears to have been used in watering-hole attacks on other financial sector websites. On 2016-11-08 we observed connections to the site referred from:
<br />
<br />
<div style="background: rgb(242, 242, 242); border-image: none; border: 1pt solid rgb(166, 166, 166); padding: 2pt;">
<div style="background: rgb(242, 242, 242); margin-left: 5px;">
<code>hxxp://www.cnbv.gob[.]mx/Prensa/Paginas/Sanciones.aspx
</code>
</div>
</div>
<br />
This is the page for the Comisin Nacional Bancaria y de Valores (National Banking and Stock Commission of Mexico), specifically the portion of their site that details sanctions made by the Mexican National Banking Commission. This organisation is the Mexican banking supervisor and the equivalent of Poland's KNF.
<br />
<br />
<div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
<a href="https://blogger.googleusercontent.com/img/b/R29vZ2xl/AVvXsEjk7DPC4HoKNS-zEG9QtbBlLzk7XILaISOYi1RchgSwUtGailb2COoqnGXGo1GH5m0UYNjKztg6yrgRrS1df_XxSKhxeQed3rFMnMC2aWFxB3W6OMxI0BsLCDRuHVxHm4-AgUWXukrx-Bc/s640/Lazarus_and_watering_hole_attacks_two.png" imageanchor="1" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;"><img border="0" height="458" src="https://blogger.googleusercontent.com/img/b/R29vZ2xl/AVvXsEjk7DPC4HoKNS-zEG9QtbBlLzk7XILaISOYi1RchgSwUtGailb2COoqnGXGo1GH5m0UYNjKztg6yrgRrS1df_XxSKhxeQed3rFMnMC2aWFxB3W6OMxI0BsLCDRuHVxHm4-AgUWXukrx-Bc/s640/Lazarus_and_watering_hole_attacks_two.png" width="640" /></a></div>
<br />
<br />
In this instance the site redirected to the following URL:
<br />
<br />
<div style="background: rgb(242, 242, 242); border-image: none; border: 1pt solid rgb(166, 166, 166); padding: 2pt;">
<div style="background: rgb(242, 242, 242); margin-left: 5px;">
<code>hxxp://www.eye-watch[.]in/jscroll/images/images.jsp?pagenum=1
</code>
</div>
</div>
<br />
<br />
<br />
At the time of writing the compromise is no longer present and no archived versions of the page exist to show where the compromise was located.
<br />
<br />
A further instance of the malicious code appears to have been present on a bank website in Uruguay around 2016-10-26 when a PCAP of browsing to the website was <a href="https://virustotal.com/en/file/a9f2b6a2fa1f0da4c35237638cfb0c6f988e74ee5a912ce727981a48435eddfa/analysis/" target="_blank">uploaded</a> to VirusTotal.com.
<br />
<br />
This shows a GET request made to:
<br />
<br />
<div style="background: rgb(242, 242, 242); border-image: none; border: 1pt solid rgb(166, 166, 166); padding: 2pt;">
<div style="background: rgb(242, 242, 242); margin-left: 5px;">
<code>hxxp://brou.com[.]uy
</code>
</div>
</div>
<br />
Followed shortly after by connections to:
<br />
<br />
<div style="background: rgb(242, 242, 242); border-image: none; border: 1pt solid rgb(166, 166, 166); padding: 2pt;">
<div style="background: rgb(242, 242, 242); margin-left: 5px;">
<code>www.eye-watch[.]in:443
</code>
</div>
</div>
<br />
Unfortunately, the response was empty and it is not possible to assess what may have been delivered.
<br />
<br />

<h5>
SILVERLIGHT XAP FILE</h5>
<br />
The cambio.xap archive sample (4cc10ab3f4ee6769e520694a10f611d5) does not use <code>eye-watch[.]in</code> as a C&amp;C channel but instead was downloaded from the URL:
<br />
<br />
<div style="background: rgb(242, 242, 242); border-image: none; border: 1pt solid rgb(166, 166, 166); padding: 2pt;">
<div style="background: rgb(242, 242, 242); margin-left: 5px;">

</code>
</div>

<em>'cambio'</em> is Spanish for <em>'change'</em>. The URL is similar to that noted in the BadCyber blog, and the use of an XAP file matches what can be found in the Archive.org cache for the <code>sap.misapor[.]ch</code> site.
<br />
<br />
XAP is a software package <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/XAP_(file_format)" target="_blank">format</a> used for Microsoft Silverlight applications.

Once the payload stub is decoded, it represents itself as a PE-image, which is another .NET 4.0 assembly with the internal name <code>binaryreader.dll</code>.
<br />
<br />
This second-stage DLL assembly, <code>binaryreader.dll</code>, is heavily obfuscated. The DLL (MD5 hash: <code>7b4a8be258ecb191c4c519d7c486ed8a</code>) is identical to the one reported in a malware traffic analysis blog post from March 2016 where it was used to <a href="http://www.malware-traffic-analysis.net/2016/03/31/index.html" target="_blank">deliver</a> Qbot. Thus it is likely the code comes from a criminal exploit kit which is being leveraged for delivery in this campaign.
<br />
<br />
A similarly named <code>cambio.swf</code> (MD5 hash: <code>6dffcfa68433f886b2e88fd984b4995a</code>) was <a href="https://virustotal.com/en/file/c1b29afcfddb79cfd57545b8600922150843ae2b170fff9aeacdeaa17adbf792/analysis/" target="_blank">uploaded</a> to VirusTotal from a US IP address in December 2016.

<br />
The prominence of Polish and Mexican banks matches the observation of watering-hole code on sites in both countries.
<br />
<br />
<h5>
CONCLUSIONS</h5>
<br />
The evidence available is currently incomplete and at the moment we can only conclude the following:
<br />
<br />
<ul>
<li>&nbsp;&nbsp;&#8226;&nbsp;&nbsp;There has been a series of watering hole attacks on bank supervisor websites in Poland &amp; Mexico, and a state owned bank in Uruguay in recent months. These leverage Silverlight and Flash exploits to deliver malware.</li>
<br />
<li>&nbsp;&nbsp;&#8226;&nbsp;&nbsp;
Investigators in Poland have identified known Lazarus group implants on bank networks and associated this with the recent compromise of the Polish Financial Supervision Authority's website.</li>
</ul>
<br />
The technical/forensic evidence to link the Lazarus group actors (who we believe are behind the Bangladesh Bank attack and many others in 2016) to the watering-hole activity is unclear. However, the choice of bank supervisor / state-bank websites would be apt, given their previous targeting of Central Banks for Heists &#8211; even when it serves little operational benefit for infiltrating the wider banking sector.
<br />
<br />
Nonetheless, further evidence to connect together the pieces of this attack is needed, as well as insights into the end-goal of the culprits. We are continuing our analysis of new artefacts as they emerge and may issue further updates in due course.
<br />
<br />
<h5>
RECOMMENDATIONS</h5>
<br />
We recommend organisations use the indicators provided in Appendix A to update their defensive systems to identify attacks. For compromised legitimate websites we would suggest a minimum 1 month block be placed on the domain. Patches against CVE-2016-0034 should be applied as soon as possible.
<br />
