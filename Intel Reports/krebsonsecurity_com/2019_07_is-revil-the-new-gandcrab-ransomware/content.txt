					<h1 class="entry-title">Is &#8216;REvil&#8217; the New GandCrab Ransomware?</h1>
				</div>
							
			
			</header><!-- .entry-header -->

	<div id="primary" class="site-content">
		<div id="content" role="main">

			
					<article id="post-48177" class="post-48177 post type-post status-publish format-standard hentry category-neer-do-well-news category-ransomware category-comingstorm tag-cisco-talos tag-gandcrab tag-intel471 tag-kaspersky-lab tag-revil tag-sodin tag-sodinokibi tag-tesorion">
				<header class="entry-header">
					<div class="clear"></div>
	<div class="btm-wrap">
	<div class="below-title-meta">
		<div class="adt">
			
			
				
				<span class="date updated">July 15, 2019</span>
					
        </div>
		<div class="adt-comment">
		<span><a class="link-comments" href="https://krebsonsecurity.com/2019/07/is-revil-the-new-gandcrab-ransomware/#comments">52 Comments</a></span> 
        </div>    <div class="clear"></div>	   
    </div><!-- below title meta end -->
	</div>
		
						</header><!-- .entry-header -->
		
		
							<div class="entry-content">
												<p>The cybercriminals behind the <strong>GandCrab</strong> ransomware-as-a-service (RaaS) offering recently announced they were closing up shop and retiring after having <a href="https://krebsonsecurity.com/2019/07/whos-behind-the-gandcrab-ransomware/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">allegedly earned more than $2 billion in extortion payments from victims</a>. But a growing body of evidence suggests the GandCrab team have instead quietly regrouped behind a more exclusive and advanced ransomware program known variously as &#8220;<strong>REvil</strong>,&#8221; &#8220;<strong>Sodin</strong>,&#8221; and &#8220;<strong>Sodinokibi</strong>.&#8221;</p>
<p>&#8220;We are getting a well-deserved retirement,&#8221; the GandCrab administrator(s) wrote in their farewell message on May 31. &#8220;We are a living proof that you can do evil and get off scot-free.&#8221;</p>
<p>However, it now appears the GandCrab team had already begun preparations to re-brand under a far more private ransomware-as-a-service offering months before their official &#8220;retirement.&#8221;</p>
<p>In late April, researchers at <strong>Cisco Talos</strong> <a href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/2019/04/sodinokibi-ransomware-exploits-weblogic.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener">spotted a new ransomware strain dubbed Sodinokibi</a> that was used to deploy GandCrab, which encrypts files on infected systems unless and until the victim pays the demanded sum. A month later, GandCrab would announce its closure.</p>
<div id="attachment_48195" style="width: 604px" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><img aria-describedby="caption-attachment-48195" decoding="async" class="wp-image-48195" src="https://krebsonsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2019/07/decryptor-top.jpg" alt="" width="594" height="609" /><p id="caption-attachment-48195" class="wp-caption-text">A payment page for a victim of REvil, a.k.a. Sodin and Sodinokibi.</p></div>
<p>Meanwhile, in the first half of May an individual using the nickname &#8220;<strong>Unknown</strong>&#8221; began making deposits totaling more than USD $130,000 worth of virtual currencies on two top cybercrime forums. The down payments were meant to demonstrate the actor meant business in his offer to hire just a handful of affiliates to drive a new, as-yet unnamed ransomware-as-a-service offering.</p>
<p>&#8220;We are not going to hire as many people as possible,&#8221; Unknown told forum members in announcing the new RaaS program. &#8220;Five affiliates more can join the program and then we&#8217;ll go under the radar. Each affiliate is guaranteed USD 10,000. Your cut is 60 percent at the beginning and 70 percent after the first three payments are made. Five affiliates are guaranteed [USD] 50,000 in total. We have been working for several years, specifically five years in this field. We are interested in professionals.&#8221;</p>
<p>Asked by forum members to name the ransomware service, Unknown said it had been mentioned in media reports but that he wouldn&#8217;t be disclosing technical details of the program or its name for the time being.<span id="more-48177"></span></p>
<p>Unknown said it was forbidden to install the new ransomware strain on any computers in the Commonwealth of Independent States (CIS), which includes Armenia, Belarus, Kazakhstan, Kyrgyzstan, Moldova, Russia, Tajikistan, Turkmenistan, Ukraine and Uzbekistan.</p>
<p>The prohibition against spreading malware in CIS countries has long been a staple of various pay-per-install affiliate programs that are operated by crooks residing in those nations. The idea here is not to attract attention from local law enforcement responding to victim complaints (and/or perhaps to stay off the radar of tax authorities and extortionists in their hometowns).</p>
<p>But <strong>Kaspersky Lab</strong> discovered that Sodinokobi/REvil also includes one other nation on its list of countries that affiliates should avoid infecting: Syria. Interestingly, latter versions of GandCrab took the same unusual step.</p>
<p>What&#8217;s the significance of the Syria connection? In October 2018, a Syrian man <a href="https://twitter.com/kvbNDtxL0kmIqRU/status/1052075557112942592?ref_src=twsrc%5Etfw" target="_blank" rel="noopener">tweeted</a> that he had lost access to all pictures of his deceased children after his computer got infected with GandCrab.</p>
<p>&#8220;They want 600 dollars to give me back my children, that&#8217;s what they&#8217;ve done, they&#8217;ve taken my boys away from me for a some filthy money,&#8221; the victim wrote. &#8220;How can I pay them 600 dollars if I barely have enough money to put food on the table for me and my wife?&#8221;</p>
<p><img decoding="async" loading="lazy" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-48197" src="https://krebsonsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2019/07/gctweet-syria.jpg" alt="" width="573" height="416" /></p>
<p>That heartfelt appeal apparently struck a chord with the developer(s) of GandCrab, who soon after released a decryption key that let all GandCrab victims in Syria unlock their files for free.</p>
<p>But this rare display of mercy probably cost the GandCrab administrators and its affiliates a pretty penny. That&#8217;s because a week after GandCrab released decryption keys for all victims in Syria, the <a href="https://www.nomoreransom.org/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">No More Ransom project</a> released a free GandCrab decryption tool developed by Romanian police in collaboration with law enforcement offices from a number of countries and security firm <strong>Bitdefender</strong>.</p>
<p>The GandCrab operators later told affiliates that the release of the decryption keys for Syrian victims allowed the entropy used by the random number generator for the ransomware&#8217;s master key to be calculated. Approximately 24 hours after NoMoreRansom released its free tool, the GandCrab team shipped an update that rendered it unable to decrypt files.</p>
<p>There are also similarities between the ways that both GandCrab and REvil generate URLs that are used as part of the infection process, according <a href="https://www.tesorion.nl/aconnection-between-the-sodinokibi-and-gandcrab-ransomware-families/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">a recent report</a> from Dutch security firm <strong>Tesorion</strong>.</p>
<p>&#8220;Even though the code bases differ significantly, the lists of strings that are used to generate the URLs are very similar (although not identical), and there are some striking similarities in how this specific part of the code works, e.g., in the somewhat far-fetched way that the random length of the filename is repeatedly recalculated,&#8221; Tesorion observed.</p>
<p>My guess is the GandCrab team has not retired, and has simply regrouped and re-branded due to the significant amount of attention from security researchers and law enforcement investigators. It seems highly unlikely that such a successful group of cybercriminals would just walk away from such an insanely profitable enterprise.</p>

			<section class="comment-content comment">
				<p>Not if they can gain access to the underlying volume that virtual drive is stored on, at which point they encrypt that volume. It&#8217;s a little more complicated than that because in-use files typically can&#8217;t be altered if they&#8217;re locked by a process (as is typically the case with a virtual drive), although it can encrypt all the surrounding files on the volume.</p>
<p>Backups are how you combat ransomware but the key part I think nobody has mentioned is that you have to have offline backups. So many backup systems today are geared towards always having access to the sole backup method available, so they just mount the volume it uses, encrypt it, and voila, all your backups are gone. Cloud backup doesn&#8217;t really help you unless you&#8217;ve paid extra for the cloud provider to be able to roll your storage back to an earlier state &#8211; this typically isn&#8217;t free. DropBox, Google Drive, etc. aren&#8217;t backup systems because if I encrypt your computer I can also encrypt Google Drive and at that point both your computer and your backup are encrypted. If you had an older backup on a drive that hadn&#8217;t been connected in a week, two weeks, two months, etc. then you&#8217;re mostly safe from ransomware (important to restore to a time before the first penetration), at least for whatever systems that backup is protecting.</p>
<p>Personally I have a fairly hardened network that doesn&#8217;t allow any executables to run from within the user&#8217;s folder and none of the users are administrators. Downside to this method is spotify, etc. don&#8217;t work because they stupidly install to the user&#8217;s appdata folder and won&#8217;t install anywhere else. So expect great cries of woe to come up from users who&#8217;ve grown accustomed to abusing their internet connection. BTW, while you can whitelist spotify, their update process runs from %temp% and of course that&#8217;s the one folder you absolutely don&#8217;t want to give executable privileges to, so it&#8217;ll work for a little while after you whitelist then never update until it stops working.</p>

They aren&#8217;t doing damage to the local population, and the external damage is just not significant enough to harm international relations in any meaningful way. This is especially true because nobody really knows where the creators of this malware are located.</p>
<p>There is nobody in Kazakhstan calling Kazakhstani police saying &#8220;I&#8217;ve been hacked!&#8221;, and then when the police look into it low and behold, the hacker is a fellow Kazakhstani who can be arrested and tried immediately.</p>
<p>Instead, you&#8217;ve got a Spaniard calling Spanish police saying &#8220;I&#8217;ve been hacked!&#8221;, and when the police look into it, it turns out the hacker is in Armenia. The Spanish police can&#8217;t just go arrest the hacker. They&#8217;ve got to negotiate with the Armenians for extradition, and when the crime is for something like $3,000 or less it&#8217;s just not worth it for the Armenians OR the Spanish to put any effort into pursuing it.</p>
<p>It&#8217;s not until there are hundreds or thousands of these cases, adding up to tens of millions of dollars in damages, that the damaged nation (Spain in this example) will find it worthwhile to put strong pressure on the attacker&#8217;s home country (Armenia, in this example).</p>
<p>That&#8217;s also the beauty of the affiliate program. Because they sell affiliate rights, the attacks no longer come from one country, but dozens of countries. It spreads the source of the damage out, diffusing it and reducing the rate at which the pressure builds for any given country to &#8220;do something about it&#8221;. That magnifies the amount of pressure required to build up before they are in any real danger. </p>
<p>As long as the original creators are smart and don&#8217;t commit any attacks themselves, leaving it all up to the affiliates, and as long as they convince their affiliate partners to not attack the CIS countries they are selling this malware to, the target countries will almost be required to focus on everyone except the creators of the ransomware, even if they would like to target the creators.</p>
<p>That&#8217;s also likely why GandCrab was retired &#8211; the pressure was building to the point where their host country might actually be convinced to do something about them. By dropping the malware, the host country can say &#8220;See? It&#8217;s not a problem any more, we don&#8217;t need to deal with this.&#8221; Because to the host country, the only thing that would make it worth prosecuting is their relationship with other countries. Something like this, cut off at the right time, is just not on the level to cause them problems internationally.</p>
<p>So it probably is on law enforcement&#8217;s radar in CIS countries, but it&#8217;s going to be real low priority to catch them. There just isn&#8217;t a reason to pursue it.</p>

				<p>Proper implementation of the concept of least privilege would go a long way towards protecting against ransomware. Backups performed by a dedicated account that can only read live data and is the only account able to write to backup media help prevent backup from being completely encrypted. </p>
<p>Unfortunately, least privilege is rarely implemented even in some of the more &#8220;secure&#8221; implementations. If you can read and write data almost anywhere, and particularly for ransomware, in any data area; then you are not in a least privilege state. If you can install or run uninstalled/unregistered apps, then you are not in a least privileged state. Hint: malware is not installed/registered.</p>
<p>For the enterprise, the resources are there to implement least privilege. Even adminstrators should not have admin rights on their day to day accounts they use for email and internet access. It&#8217;s just more convenient to have admin rights all the time.</p>
<p>For the small business and home user, the administrator is usually somebody who needs help in the least privilege area, that falls on the OS developers to provide. Windows only began doing so minimally in Vista, it is better but still focuses on convenience. Linux started much earlier, but convenience is creeping in. I do not know Mac. Things can be improved in this area &#8220;out of the box&#8221;</p>
