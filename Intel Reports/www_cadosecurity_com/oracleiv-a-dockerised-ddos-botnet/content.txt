<p class="has-text-align-center"><em>Midjourney’s imagination of Oracle IV</em></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p><strong>By Nate Bill and Matt Muir</strong></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:heading --></p> 
<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Introduction</h2> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:heading --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>Cado Security Labs researchers recently discovered a novel campaign targeting publicly-exposed instances of the <a href="https://docs.docker.com/engine/api/">Docker Engine API</a>. Attackers are exploiting this misconfiguration to deliver a malicious Docker container, built from an image named "oracleiv_latest" and containing Python malware compiled as an ELF executable. The malware itself acts as a Distributed Denial of Service (DDoS) bot agent, capable of conducting DoS attacks via a number of methods.</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>It’s not the first time the Docker Engine API has been <a href="https://www.cadosecurity.com/tales-from-the-honeypot-watchdog-evolves-with-a-new-multi-stage-cryptojacking-attack/">targeted</a> by attackers. This method of initial access has become increasingly <a href="https://blog.aquasec.com/threat-alert-anatomy-of-silentbobs-cloud-attack">popular</a> in recent years and is often used to deliver cryptojacking malware. Inadvertent exposure of the Docker Engine API occurs frequently enough that several unrelated campaigns have been observed scanning for it.&nbsp;</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>This should come as no surprise, given the move to microservice-driven architectures by many software teams. Once a valid endpoint is discovered, it’s trivial to pull a malicious image and launch a container from it to carry out any conceivable objective. Hosting the malicious container in Dockerhub, Docker’s container image library, streamlines this process even further.</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:heading {"level":3} --></p> 
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Initial Access</h3> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:heading --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>In keeping with other attacks of this kind, initial access typically begins with a HTTP POST request to the<code> /images/create </code>endpoint of Docker’s API. This effectively runs a <code>docker pull</code> command on the host to retrieve the specified image from Dockerhub. A follow-up container start command is then used to spawn a container from the pulled image.&nbsp;</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>An example of the image create command used in the OracleIV command can be seen below:</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p><code>POST /v1.43/images/create?tag=latest&amp;fromImage=robbertignacio328832/oracleiv_latest</code></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:heading {"level":3} --></p> 
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Malicious Dockerhub Image</h3> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:heading --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>As can be seen in the Docker API command above, the attacker retrieves an image named <code>oracleiv_latest</code> which was uploaded to Dockerhub by the user <code>robbertignacio328832</code>. This image was still live at the time of writing and had over 3,000 pulls. Furthermore, the image itself appeared to be undergoing regular iteration, with the most recent changes pushed only 3 days prior to the writing of this blog.</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:image {"align":"center","id":8367,"width":"661px","height":"auto","aspectRatio":"1.873477038425492","sizeSlug":"full","linkDestination":"none"} --></p> 
<figure class="wp-block-image aligncenter size-full is-resized"> 
 <img src="https://14518100.fs1.hubspotusercontent-na1.net/hubfs/14518100/Imported_Blog_Media/image7-16.png" alt="" class="wp-image-8367" style="aspect-ratio:1.873477038425492;width:661px;height:auto"> 
</figure> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:image --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph {"align":"center"} --></p> 
<p class="has-text-align-center"><em>Dockerhub page for oracleiv_latest</em></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>The <code>robbertignacio328832</code> user also added the description <code>Mysql image for docker</code> to the image’s Dockerhub page, presumably to make it seem more innocuous.</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>Examining the image layers reveals commands used by the attacker to retrieve their malicious payload - named <code>oracle.sh</code>, despite being an ELF executable - and bake it into the resulting image.</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:image {"align":"center","id":8368,"sizeSlug":"full","linkDestination":"none"} --></p> 
<figure class="wp-block-image aligncenter size-full"> 
 <img src="https://14518100.fs1.hubspotusercontent-na1.net/hubfs/14518100/Imported_Blog_Media/image2-44.png" alt="" class="wp-image-8368"> 
</figure> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:image --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph {"align":"center"} --></p> 
<p class="has-text-align-center"><em>Image layer RUN command to retrieve malicious payload</em></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>The image also includes additional <code>wget </code>commands to retrieve a copy of <code>XMRig</code> and an associated miner configuration file.</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:image {"align":"center","id":8369,"sizeSlug":"full","linkDestination":"none"} --></p>

</figure> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:image --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph {"align":"center"} --></p> 
<p class="has-text-align-center"><em>Image layer RUN command to retrieve xmrig miner</em></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:image {"align":"center","id":8370,"sizeSlug":"full","linkDestination":"none"} --></p> 
<figure class="wp-block-image aligncenter size-full">

</figure> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:image --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph {"align":"center"} --></p> 
<p class="has-text-align-center"><em>Image layer RUN command to retrieve miner configuration file</em></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>It’s worth noting that Cado researchers did not observe any mining performed by this malicious container, but with these files baked into the image it would certainly be possible.</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:heading {"level":3} --></p> 
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Static Analysis</h3> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:heading --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>Since the bundled version of <code>XMRig</code> is both unused and a vanilla release of the miner, this section will focus on analysis of the <code>oracle.sh</code> executable embedded in the malicious container.</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>Static analysis of this executable revealed a 64-bit, statically-linked ELF, with debug information intact. Further investigation led to the discovery of a number of functions with <code>CyFunction</code> in the name, confirming that the malware is Python code compiled with Cython.</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:image {"align":"center","id":8371,"width":"596px","height":"auto","aspectRatio":"0.725417439703154","sizeSlug":"full","linkDestination":"none"} --></p> 
<figure class="wp-block-image aligncenter size-full is-resized"> 
 <img src="https://14518100.fs1.hubspotusercontent-na1.net/hubfs/14518100/Imported_Blog_Media/image6-26.png" alt="" class="wp-image-8371" style="aspect-ratio:0.725417439703154;width:596px;height:auto"> 
</figure> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:image --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph {"align":"center"} --></p> 
<p class="has-text-align-center"><em>Embedded Cython functions</em></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>The attacker code is relatively concise, the majority of it is dedicated to the different DoS methods present. The following functions were identified:</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:list --></p> 
<ul> 
 <!-- wp:list-item --> 
 <li><code>bot.main</code></li> 
 <p> 
  <!-- /wp:list-item --></p> 
 <p> 
  <!-- wp:list-item --></p> 
 <li><code>bot.init_socket</code></li> 
 <p> 
  <!-- /wp:list-item --></p> 
 <p> 
  <!-- wp:list-item --></p> 
 <li><code>bot.checksum</code></li> 
 <p> 
  <!-- /wp:list-item --></p> 
 <p> 
  <!-- wp:list-item --></p> 
 <li><code>bot.register_ssl</code></li> 
 <p> 
  <!-- /wp:list-item --></p> 
 <p> 
  <!-- wp:list-item --></p> 
 <li><code>bot.register_httpget</code></li> 
 <p> 
  <!-- /wp:list-item --></p> 
 <p> 
  <!-- wp:list-item --></p> 
 <li><code>bot.register_slow</code></li> 
 <p> 
  <!-- /wp:list-item --></p> 
 <p> 
  <!-- wp:list-item --></p> 
 <li><code>bot.register_five</code></li> 
 <p> 
  <!-- /wp:list-item --></p> 
 <p> 
  <!-- wp:list-item --></p> 
 <li><code>bot.register_vse</code></li> 
 <p> 
  <!-- /wp:list-item --></p> 
 <p> 
  <!-- wp:list-item --></p> 
 <li><code>bot.register_udp</code></li> 
 <p> 
  <!-- /wp:list-item --></p> 
 <p> 
  <!-- wp:list-item --></p> 
 <li><code>bot.register_udp_pps</code></li> 
 <p> 
  <!-- /wp:list-item --></p> 
 <p> 
  <!-- wp:list-item --></p> 
 <li><code>bot.register_ovh</code></li> 
 <p> 
  <!-- /wp:list-item --></p> 
</ul> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:list --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>Functions with the<code> register_ </code>prefix correspond to DoS attack methods, the details of which will be discussed in the following section.</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:heading {"level":3} --></p> 
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Dynamic Analysis</h3> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:heading --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>The bot connects back to a Command and Control server (C2) at <code>46.166.185[.]231</code> on TCP port <code>40320</code>. It then performs primitive authentication, where the bot supplies the C2 with basic information about its environment in addition to a hardcoded password.</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p><code>&nbsp;: client hello from zombie! : X86 : key: b'bjN0ZzM0cnAwd24zZA==' : os: linux</code></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>The key decodes to “<code>n3tg34rp0wn3d</code>”. Supplying an incorrect key causes the C2 to reply with “<code>f*** off c***, you're not supposed to nut inside of me!</code>”, followed by the connection being terminated.</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>Following successful authentication, the C2 will continuously send “<code>routine ping, greetz Oracle IV</code>”. This is likely due to an implementation quirk, where many novice programmers new to socket programming will implement the blocking receive operation in a loop and require constant input to keep the loop going.</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>Cado Security Labs has performed monitoring of the botnet activity, and has observed the botnet being used to DDoS a number of targets, with the operator preferring to use a UDP based flood in addition to an SSL based flood.</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p><strong>Botnet Commands</strong></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>C2 commands used to initiate the different DoS attacks take the following form:</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p><code>&lt;attack type&gt; &lt;target IP/domain&gt; &lt;attack duration&gt; &lt;rate&gt; &lt;target port&gt;</code></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>For example, to conduct an SSL DoS attack on the website example.com for 30 seconds, a rate of 30, and on port 80, the C2 server would send the following command:</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p><code>ssl example.com 30 30 80</code></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>Cado Security Labs were able to trick a botnet agent into connecting to our mimic C2 server instead of the real one, and issued commands to observe the capabilities of the botnet. The botnet has the following DDoS capabilities:</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:list --></p> 
<ul> 
 <!-- wp:list-item --> 
 <li>UDP 
  <!-- wp:list --> 
  <ul> 
   <!-- wp:list-item --> 
   <li>Performs a UDP flood with 40,000 byte packets</li> 
   <p> 
    <!-- /wp:list-item --></p> 
   <p> 
    <!-- wp:list-item --></p> 
   <li>These far exceed the threshold and consequently get fragmented. This will create an additional computational overhead on both the target and source due to the reassembly of fragments, however it’s unclear if this is intentional or not.</li> 
   <p> 
    <!-- /wp:list-item --></p> 
  </ul> <p> 
   <!-- /wp:list --></p></li> 
 <p> 
  <!-- /wp:list-item --></p> 
 <p> 
  <!-- wp:list-item --></p> 
 <li>UDP_PPS 
  <!-- wp:list --> 
  <ul> 
   <!-- wp:list-item --> 
   <li>Seems non-functional, when the command was issued we did not observe any behaviour.</li> 
   <p> 
    <!-- /wp:list-item --></p> 
  </ul> <p> 
   <!-- /wp:list --></p></li> 
 <p> 
  <!-- /wp:list-item --></p> 
 <p> 
  <!-- wp:list-item --></p> 
 <li>SSL 
  <!-- wp:list --> 
  <ul> 
   <!-- wp:list-item --> 
   <li>Opens a TCP connection, sends a large amount of data, and then closes. This process then repeats. Our dummy target server rejected all of the fake requests with an error 400, so it would appear that the attack aims at flooding the target rather than exploiting some protocol specific function.</li> 
   <p> 
    <!-- /wp:list-item --></p> 
  </ul> <p> 
   <!-- /wp:list --></p></li> 
 <p> 
  <!-- /wp:list-item --></p> 
</ul> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:list --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:image {"align":"center","id":8372,"sizeSlug":"full","linkDestination":"none"} --></p> 
<figure class="wp-block-image aligncenter size-full"> 
 <img src="https://14518100.fs1.hubspotusercontent-na1.net/hubfs/14518100/Imported_Blog_Media/image4-33.png" alt="" class="wp-image-8372"> 
</figure> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:image --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph {"align":"center"} --></p> 
<p class="has-text-align-center"><em>Tcpdump output for SSL DoS method</em></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:list --></p> 
<ul> 
 <!-- wp:list-item --> 
 <li>SYN 
  <!-- wp:list --> 
  <ul> 
   <!-- wp:list-item --> 
   <li>We anticipated this would be a SYN flood, however the observed behaviour is identical to SSL.</li> 
   <p> 
    <!-- /wp:list-item --></p> 
  </ul> <p> 
   <!-- /wp:list --></p></li> 
 <p> 
  <!-- /wp:list-item --></p> 
 <p> 
  <!-- wp:list-item --></p> 
 <li>HTTPGET 
  <!-- wp:list --> 
  <ul> 
   <!-- wp:list-item --> 
   <li>Seems non-functional, when the command was issued we did not observe any behaviour.</li> 
   <p> 
    <!-- /wp:list-item --></p> 
  </ul> <p> 
   <!-- /wp:list --></p></li> 
 <p> 
  <!-- /wp:list-item --></p> 
 <p> 
  <!-- wp:list-item --></p> 
 <li>SLOW 
  <!-- wp:list --> 
  <ul> 
   <!-- wp:list-item --> 
   <li>This is a slowloris style attack, the agent opens up many connections to the server and continuously sends small amounts of data to keep the connection open.</li> 
   <p> 
    <!-- /wp:list-item --></p> 
  </ul> <p> 
   <!-- /wp:list --></p></li> 
 <p> 
  <!-- /wp:list-item --></p> 
 <p> 
  <!-- wp:list-item --></p> 
 <li>FIVE 
  <!-- wp:list --> 
  <ul> 
   <!-- wp:list-item --> 
   <li>This is a UDP flood with 18 byte packets. Likely the packets are a part of the FiveM server protocol, and designed to denial of service a FiveM server</li> 
   <p> 
    <!-- /wp:list-item --></p> 
  </ul> <p> 
   <!-- /wp:list --></p></li> 
 <p> 
  <!-- /wp:list-item --></p> 
 <p> 
  <!-- wp:list-item --></p> 
 <li>VSE 
  <!-- wp:list --> 
  <ul> 
   <!-- wp:list-item --> 
   <li>This is a UDP flood with 20 byte packets. Similar to FIVE, this seems protocol specific to Valve source engine.</li> 
   <p> 
    <!-- /wp:list-item --></p> 
  </ul> <p> 
   <!-- /wp:list --></p></li> 
 <p> 
  <!-- /wp:list-item --></p> 
 <p> 
  <!-- wp:list-item --></p> 
 <li>OVH 
  <!-- wp:list --> 
  <ul> 
   <!-- wp:list-item --> 
   <li>This is a UDP flood with 8 byte packets, designed to circumvent OVH’s DDoS protection.</li> 
   <p> 
    <!-- /wp:list-item --></p> 
  </ul> <p> 
   <!-- /wp:list --></p></li> 
 <p> 
  <!-- /wp:list-item --></p> 
</ul> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:list --></p>

 <!-- wp:heading --></p> 
<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Conclusion</h2> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:heading --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>OracleIV demonstrates that attackers are still intent on leveraging misconfigured Docker Engine API deployments as a means of initial access for a variety of campaigns. The portability that containerisation brings allows malicious payloads to be executed in a deterministic manner across Docker hosts, regardless of the configuration of the host itself.&nbsp;</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>Whilst OracleIV is not technically a supply chain attack, users of Dockerhub should be aware that malicious container images do indeed exist in Docker’s image library - an issue that seemingly won’t be rectified in the near future. Cado researchers have reported the malicious user behind OracleIV to Docker, in the hope that the image will be removed.&nbsp;</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>Despite this, users of Dockerhub are encouraged to perform periodic assessments of the images they are pulling from the registry, to ensure that they’ve not been polluted with malicious code.&nbsp;</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>Consistent with other attacks reliant on a misconfigured internet-facing service (e.g. Jupyter, Redis etc) Cado researchers strongly urge users of these services to periodically review their exposure, and implement network defences accordingly.</p>
