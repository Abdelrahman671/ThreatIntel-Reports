    <h4>The novel phishing campaign to steal email accounts of public figures around the world</h4>

  <div class="post-content"><div class="img">

<h2 id="introduction">Introduction</h2>
<p>Certfa Lab has identified a new series of phishing attacks from the Charming Kitten<sup id="fnref:1"><a href="#fn:1" class="footnote-ref" role="doc-noteref">1</a></sup>, the Iranian hacking group who has a close relationship with Iranâs state and Intelligence services. According to our investigation, these new attacks have targeted journalists, political and human rights activists. These phishing attacks are in line with the previous activities of the group that companies like ClearSky<sup id="fnref:2"><a href="#fn:2" class="footnote-ref" role="doc-noteref">2</a></sup> and Microsoft<sup id="fnref:3"><a href="#fn:3" class="footnote-ref" role="doc-noteref">3</a></sup> have reported in detail in September and October 2019.</p>
<p>As we previously reported the activities of the Charming Kitten in 2018<sup id="fnref:4"><a href="#fn:4" class="footnote-ref" role="doc-noteref">4</a></sup>, our research indicates the Charming Kitten is still trying to target private and government institutions, think tanks and academic institutions, organizations with ties to the Baha&rsquo;i community, and many others in European countries, the United States, United Kingdom, Saudi Arabia, to extract information from them.</p>
<p>Our findings show that these new attacks by Charming Kitten are focused on stealing email account information of the victims and finding information about their contacts/networks. Also, our research shows that the group has recently participated in designing a malware for Windows machines but the spectrum and the number of its targets is still not clear for us.</p>
<hr>
<h2 id="phishing-via-fake-interviews">Phishing via Fake Interviews</h2>
<p>Phishing is one of the main tactics that has been used by the Charming Kitten, and social engineering and fake emails are the usual methods of executing it. In this campaign, the Charming Kitten has used the identity of a former Wall Street Journal (WSJ) journalist and created a fake interview scenario to target their victims. It must be noted that in the recent months, the group has used scenarios like âInvitation to a Deutsche Welle Webinarâ and âCNN Interviewâ with the related topics of Iran and international affairs in order to trick their targets.</p>
<p><strong>Step 1 - Gaining Trust:</strong> In one of the cases, the hackers forged the New York Times journalist Farnaz Fassihiâs identity as a Wall Street Journal reporter - where she used to work - to send interview request emails to victims and guide them to their phishing websites. In the first step of the fake interview, emails were sent from farnaz.fassihi [at] gmail [dot] com to gain the victimsâ trust. The below image is a sample of the content.</p>
<div class="img">
<img class="asyncImage" src= "/img/post/fake-interview-the-new-activity-of-charming-kitten/01_tn.jpg" data-src= "/img/post/fake-interview-the-new-activity-of-charming-kitten/01.png" alt="A sample of the fake interview request via an email">
</div>
<h5 id="figure-1-a-sample-of-the-fake-interview-request-via-an-email">Figure 1. A sample of the fake interview request via an email</h5>
</br>
<blockquote>
<p><strong>Translation:</strong> </br>
Hello *** ***** ******<em></br>
My name is Farnaz Fasihi. I am a journalist at the Wall Street Journal newspaper.</br>
The Middle East team of the WSJ intends to introduce successful non-local</em> individuals in developed countries. Your activities in the fields of research and philosophy of science led me to introduce you as a successful Iranian. The director of the Middle East team asked us to set up an interview with you and share some of your important achievements with our audience. This interview could motivate the youth of our beloved country to discover their talents and move toward success.</br>
Needless to say, this interview is a great honor for me personally, and I urge you to accept my invitation for the interview.</br>
The questions are designed professionally by a group of my colleagues and the resulting interview will be published in the Weekly Interview section of the WSJ. I will send you the questions and requirements of the interview as soon as you accept.</br>
*Footnote: Non-local refers to people who were born in other countries.</br>
Thank you for your kindness and attention.</br>
Farnaz Fasihi</br></p>
</blockquote>
</br>
<p>In these emails, all the links in the footnotes (Figure 2), including social media links, WSJ and Dow Jones websites, are all in the short URL format. As a result, by clicking on them, the hackers can guide the victim to legitimate addresses while getting basic information about the victimâs device such as IP address, the type of Operating System, and the browser. This is a common method of gathering information by hackers in order to prepare for the main attacks based on the victimsâ devices.</p>
<div class="img">
<img class="asyncImage" src= "/img/post/fake-interview-the-new-activity-of-charming-kitten/2-6_tn.jpg" data-src= "/img/post/fake-interview-the-new-activity-of-charming-kitten/2-6.png" alt="Details of short URL that allows hackers to collect basic information about the target">
</div>
<h5 id="figure-2-details-of-short-url-that-allows-hackers-to-collect-basic-information-about-the-target5">Figure 2. Details of short URL that allows hackers to collect basic information about the target<sup id="fnref:5"><a href="#fn:5" class="footnote-ref" role="doc-noteref">5</a></sup></h5>
<br/>
<p><strong>Step 2, The Main Attack:</strong> After communication and relative trust are established through the initial email, hackers send their victim an exclusive link as a file that contains the interview questions. According to our samples, the Charming Kitten has been using a page that is hosted on Google Sites (Figure 3). This method is a relatively new tactic that has been widely used in phishing attacks by hackers in the past year<sup id="fnref:6"><a href="#fn:6" class="footnote-ref" role="doc-noteref">6</a></sup> in order to make the targets trust the destination domain, for example this URL: hxxps://sites.google[.]com/view/the-wall-street/xxxx. By using this tactic, the hacker can evade the spam detections.</p>
<div class="img">
<img class="asyncImage" src= "/img/post/fake-interview-the-new-activity-of-charming-kitten/07_tn.jpg" data-src= "/img/post/fake-interview-the-new-activity-of-charming-kitten/07.png" alt="A sample of fake WSJ page that is hosted on Google Site.">
</div>
<h5 id="figure-3-a-sample-of-fake-wsj-page-that-is-hosted-on-google-site">Figure 3. A sample of fake WSJ page that is hosted on Google Site.</h5>
<br/>
<p>After clicking the download button on the Google Site page (Figure 3), the target is sent to another fake page in two-step-checkup[.]site domain where login credential details of his/her email such as the password and two factor authentication (2FA) code are requested by phishing kits.</p>
<p>The structure of the phishing page is listed below:</p>
<ul>
<li>hxxps://two-step-checkup[.]site/securemail/secureLogin/challenge/url?ucode=xxxx-xxxx&amp;service=mailservice&amp;type=password</li>
<li>hxxps://two-step-checkup[.]site/securemail/secureLogin/challenge/url?ucode=xxxx-xxxx&amp;service=mailservice&amp;type=smscode</li>
<li>hxxps://two-step-checkup[.]site/ymail/secureLogin/challenge/url?ucode=xxxx-xxxx&amp;service=mailservice&amp;type=password</li>
<li>hxxps://two-step-checkup[.]site/ymail/secureLogin/challenge/url?ucode=xxxx-xxxx&amp;service=mailservice&amp;type=smscode</li>
</ul>
<p>Using phishing kits such as Modlishka<sup id="fnref:7"><a href="#fn:7" class="footnote-ref" role="doc-noteref">7</a></sup> to steal passwords and two factor authentication codes is an important step in targeted attacks, which has been widely used by hackers in the past year and many reports have been written about them<sup id="fnref:8"><a href="#fn:8" class="footnote-ref" role="doc-noteref">8</a></sup>. As mentioned, Certfa Lab published an extended report in 2018 about the Charming Kitten and their use of this method. Figure 4 is a sample of the phishing page that was used to steal the SMS authentication code.</p>

<h2 id="malware-development">Malware Development</h2>
<p>One important point that caught our attention in this campaign was using âpdfreader.exeâ, a piece of malware with a backdoor feature. Our investigation shows this file was first uploaded in VirusTotal by an anonymous user on 3 October 2019<sup id="fnref:9"><a href="#fn:9" class="footnote-ref" role="doc-noteref">9</a></sup>.</p>
<p>The technical assessment of the malwareâs function shows that the developers of malware are directly in contact with the people behind the recent phishing attacks, and it could be interpreted as all these malicious activities being done by one group, which we believe to be the Charming Kitten.</p>
<p><strong>pdfReader.exe Function:</strong> This malware, which is identified as a Win32/Backdoor by antiviruses, is a mid-level piece of malware - due to lack of design sophistication - with various harmful capabilities. Our assessment shows the malware causes changes in the Windowsâ Firewall and Registry settings in order to run automatically itself and gathers information from the victimâs device and sends it to its developer. This feature allows the hackers to run new malware and spyware remotely on the victimâs target. Figure 5 shows the process graph of this malware.</p>

<h2 id="charming-kittens-footprints">Charming Kittenâs Footprints</h2>
<p>Our research on the history of phishing websites in recent attacks, such as two-step-checkup[.]site, shows the attackers use âns11025.ztomy[.]comâ and âns21025.ztomy[.]comâ as the Name Servers (NS) on 14 October 2019. These servers were previously used for other phishing websites by the Charming Kitten.</p>
<p>The similarities between the method of managing and sending HTTP requests in âtwo-step-checkup[.]siteâ server with the latest techniques used by this group is further evidence of Charming Kittenâs connection to these attacks. In this technique, if sent requests to the host server of the phishing kit are denied, the user is directed to a legitimate website like Google, Yahoo!, or Outlook by â301 Moved Permanentlyâ and âFound redirect 302â responses. As a result, this method makes it harder for different pages and sections of phishing websites to be exposed to the public.</p>
<p>Figure 7 is a sample of public requests from âtwo-step-checkup[.]siteâ that has been redirected to outlook.live.com. In this scenario, the user does not have a valid request according the phishing kit, therefore, the real webpage - not the phishing one - is shown to the target.</p>

<h2 id="conclusion">Conclusion</h2>
<p>This new series of phishing attacks by the Charming Kitten are in line with previous activities seen from their group. For example, we identified similar settings for the servers used in this attack with their previous campaigns.</p>
<p>The main focus of this phishing campaign was stealing email account information of the victims, and finding information about their contacts/networks. One example detailed in this report is there impersonation of public figures such as a WSJ reporter.</p>
<p>The Charming Kitten used Google Sites for their phishing attack, and Certfa believes that they work on the development of a series of malware for their future phishing attack campaign.</p>
