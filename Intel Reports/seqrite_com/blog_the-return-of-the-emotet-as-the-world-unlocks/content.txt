			<span class="rt-reading-time" style="display: block;"><span class="rt-label">Estimated reading time: </span><span class="rt-time">5</span><span class="rt-label"> minutes</span></span><p>A threat actor named Emotet Trojan has been in the wild for more than 5 years, and now it is back after a 5 months break. It has spread globally, infecting new as well as old targets. It is re-launched with multiple Malspam Campaigns to distribute in all sectors.</p>
<p>We observed through our detection telemetry that Emotet campaigns have targeted a variety of sectors. It is spread through SpamMail with hot topics like Covid-19, Vaccine for Covid-19 and few other generic keywords like Health Insurance, Payment, Invoice, Job Update/Opening, Cyberattack, Shipping and many more.</p>
<h2>Infection chain</h2>
<figure id="attachment_4510" style="width: 1246px" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><img class="size-full wp-image-4510" src="https://www.seqrite.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/Fig-1-Infection-chain.jpg" alt="Fig 1: Infection chain" width="1246" height="484" srcset="https://www.seqrite.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/Fig-1-Infection-chain.jpg 1246w, https://www.seqrite.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/Fig-1-Infection-chain-300x117.jpg 300w, https://www.seqrite.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/Fig-1-Infection-chain-768x298.jpg 768w, https://www.seqrite.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/Fig-1-Infection-chain-1024x398.jpg 1024w" sizes="(max-width: 1246px) 100vw, 1246px" /><figcaption class="wp-caption-text">Fig 1: Infection chain</figcaption></figure>
<p>The infection chain starts by sending crafted emails to the target organization or person. The attacker uses the Hijacking email method for sending the crafted mails with an attachment. The attachment may contain a word document a macro file or a PDF. Sometimes the email body contains URLs too. As mailbox is hijacked, attachment is sent replying to old email threads or forwarding to an existing mail list, due to which the victim easily opens the attachment as the mail comes from a trusted mail id.</p>
<p>We encountered extensive count of spam mails, few of the examples are listed below-</p>

<p>The attacker has done a silly mistake here, we can see in the mail that the subject and the attachment name doesn’t match. In most of the cases, an attachment name contains “Medical report Covid-19&#8243;.</p>
<h2><strong>Document Analysis</strong></h2>
<p>Office Document attachment contains a macro which contains a heavily obfuscated VBA code responsible to deliver payload in the chain.</p>
<figure id="attachment_4523" style="width: 1115px" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><img class="wp-image-4523 size-full" src="https://www.seqrite.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/Fig-3.-Macro-code-in-an-attachment.png" alt="Fig 3. Macro code in an attachment." width="1115" height="645" srcset="https://www.seqrite.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/Fig-3.-Macro-code-in-an-attachment.png 1115w, https://www.seqrite.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/Fig-3.-Macro-code-in-an-attachment-300x174.png 300w, https://www.seqrite.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/Fig-3.-Macro-code-in-an-attachment-768x444.png 768w, https://www.seqrite.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/Fig-3.-Macro-code-in-an-attachment-1024x592.png 1024w" sizes="(max-width: 1115px) 100vw, 1115px" /><figcaption class="wp-caption-text">Fig 3. Macro code in an attachment.</figcaption></figure>
<p>After some de-obfuscation, the “<strong><em>Qndiwjphrk8an6x” </em></strong>function code is as below</p>
<p>{Qndiwjphrk8an6x = &#8220;winmgmt&#8221; + &#8220;:win32_&#8221; + &#8220;p&#8221; + &#8220;rocess&#8221;}</p>
<p>which translates into <strong>winmgmts:win32_process. O</strong>nce we removed the chunked data we got a readable code with functions and reference variables.</p>
<p>One interesting part in the directory in Macros\Ofbszpwp168r\o.stm is that we can see some obfuscated data again.</p>
<figure id="attachment_4524" style="width: 625px" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><img class="size-full wp-image-4524" src="https://www.seqrite.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/Fig-4.-Obfuscation-in-Doc-file.png" alt="Fig 4: Obfuscation in Doc file" width="625" height="474" srcset="https://www.seqrite.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/Fig-4.-Obfuscation-in-Doc-file.png 625w, https://www.seqrite.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/Fig-4.-Obfuscation-in-Doc-file-300x228.png 300w" sizes="(max-width: 625px) 100vw, 625px" /><figcaption class="wp-caption-text">Fig 4: Obfuscation in Doc file</figcaption></figure>
<p>After the initial level of de-obfuscation, we got base64 encoded PowerShell script as shown in below figure.</p>
<figure id="attachment_4525" style="width: 678px" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><img class="size-full wp-image-4525" src="https://www.seqrite.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/Fig-5.-base64-Encoded-PowerShell-code.png" alt="Fig 5: base64 Encoded PowerShell code" width="678" height="255" srcset="https://www.seqrite.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/Fig-5.-base64-Encoded-PowerShell-code.png 678w, https://www.seqrite.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/Fig-5.-base64-Encoded-PowerShell-code-300x113.png 300w" sizes="(max-width: 678px) 100vw, 678px" /><figcaption class="wp-caption-text">Fig 5: base64 Encoded PowerShell code</figcaption></figure>
<p>After decoding with base64 and processing data, we got the  below PowerShell script-</p>
<figure id="attachment_4526" style="width: 978px" class="wp-caption alignnone"><img class="size-full wp-image-4526" src="https://www.seqrite.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/Fig-6.-Base64-Decoded-PowerShell-script.png" alt="Fig 6: Base64 Decoded PowerShell script" width="978" height="329" srcset="https://www.seqrite.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/Fig-6.-Base64-Decoded-PowerShell-script.png 978w, https://www.seqrite.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/Fig-6.-Base64-Decoded-PowerShell-script-300x101.png 300w, https://www.seqrite.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/Fig-6.-Base64-Decoded-PowerShell-script-768x258.png 768w" sizes="(max-width: 978px) 100vw, 978px" /><figcaption class="wp-caption-text">Fig 6: Base64 Decoded PowerShell script</figcaption></figure>
<p>It contains malicious domains or URLs which serves Emotet executables. Using PowerShell commands Emotet executable is downloaded at “%temp%” directory in the victim’s machine.</p>
<h2><strong>Payload Analysis</strong></h2>
<p>The payload downloaded from the above file has a customized packer. The unpacking is done at runtime. Emotet’s packer code is polymorphic which makes it difficult for signature-based detection tools to detect it based on the packer code.</p>
<p>Its resource (.rsrc) section has significant data which seems to be an indication that the malware might be packed. In the below Fig. we can see that RCData has an encrypted code.</p>
<figure id="attachment_4527" style="width: 533px" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><img class="size-full wp-image-4527" src="https://www.seqrite.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/Fig-7.-File-having-encrypted-data-in-resource.png" alt="Fig 7: File having encrypted data in resource" width="533" height="189" srcset="https://www.seqrite.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/Fig-7.-File-having-encrypted-data-in-resource.png 533w, https://www.seqrite.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/Fig-7.-File-having-encrypted-data-in-resource-300x106.png 300w" sizes="(max-width: 533px) 100vw, 533px" /><figcaption class="wp-caption-text">Fig 7: File having encrypted data in resource</figcaption></figure>
<p>While debugging the file, we observed that the data will be decrypted using a slightly modified version of RC4. Key for RC4 is hardcoded in the file. After decryption, the control goes to the decrypted shellcode.</p>
<figure id="attachment_4528" style="width: 535px" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><img class="size-full wp-image-4528" src="https://www.seqrite.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/Fig-8.-RC4-used-for-decryption.png" alt="Fig 8: RC4 used for decryption" width="535" height="144" srcset="https://www.seqrite.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/Fig-8.-RC4-used-for-decryption.png 535w, https://www.seqrite.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/Fig-8.-RC4-used-for-decryption-300x81.png 300w" sizes="(max-width: 535px) 100vw, 535px" /><figcaption class="wp-caption-text">Fig 8: RC4 used for decryption</figcaption></figure>
<p>In some files, we have seen the use of <em>VirtualAllocExNuma </em>to allocate new memory. This is used for fast processing. The beginning of an obfuscated shellcode is copied to the new address after being decrypted using the modified RC4 algorithm. In addition to the relatively short shellcode, an additional PE can be seen in the memory.</p>
<figure id="attachment_4529" style="width: 480px" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><img class="size-full wp-image-4529" src="https://www.seqrite.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/Fig-9.-Decrypted-shellcode-and-PE-File.png" alt="Fig 9: Decrypted shellcode and PE File" width="480" height="293" srcset="https://www.seqrite.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/Fig-9.-Decrypted-shellcode-and-PE-File.png 480w, https://www.seqrite.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/Fig-9.-Decrypted-shellcode-and-PE-File-300x183.png 300w" sizes="(max-width: 480px) 100vw, 480px" /><figcaption class="wp-caption-text">Fig 9: Decrypted shellcode and PE File</figcaption></figure>
<p>The Shellcode deobfuscates several API calls at runtime, such as <em>LoadLibraryA, GetProcAddress, VirtualAlloc </em>and <em>VirtualProtect</em>, all of which will be used to resolve APIs and allocate memory to run the additional PE.</p>
<figure id="attachment_4530" style="width: 480px" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><img class="size-full wp-image-4530" src="https://www.seqrite.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/Fig-10.-API-Resolved.png" alt="Fig 10: API Resolved" width="480" height="140" srcset="https://www.seqrite.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/Fig-10.-API-Resolved.png 480w, https://www.seqrite.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/Fig-10.-API-Resolved-300x88.png 300w" sizes="(max-width: 480px) 100vw, 480px" /><figcaption class="wp-caption-text">Fig 10: API Resolved</figcaption></figure>
<p>After this, the malware allocates memory and copies the data of decrypted file and calls  <em>VirtualProtect </em>and<em> f</em>inally, the program jumps to the real entry point of the decrypted file.</p>
<p>Spreading mechanism of Emotet campaign remains almost the same that we had already discussed in our previous blog. Read it here in the link below.</p>
<p><a href="https://blogs.quickheal.com/evolution-4-year-old-threat-emotet-infamous-trojan-complex-threat-distributer/" data-wpel-link="internal" target="_self" rel="follow">https://blogs.quickheal.com/evolution-4-year-old-threat-emotet-infamous-trojan-complex-threat-distributer/</a></p>
<p>After executing the Emotet, it will exfiltrate the data to the CnC server. While sending, the data is encoded and sent with some random name of the file and random path to the server.</p>
<figure id="attachment_4531" style="width: 867px" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><img class="size-full wp-image-4531" src="https://www.seqrite.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/Fig11.-CnC-traffic.png" alt="Fig11: CnC traffic" width="867" height="327" srcset="https://www.seqrite.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/Fig11.-CnC-traffic.png 867w, https://www.seqrite.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/Fig11.-CnC-traffic-300x113.png 300w, https://www.seqrite.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/Fig11.-CnC-traffic-768x290.png 768w" sizes="(max-width: 867px) 100vw, 867px" /><figcaption class="wp-caption-text">Fig11: CnC traffic</figcaption></figure>
<h2>Detection hits stats</h2>
<p>In Quick Heal detection, we have successfully detected such Emotet trojans. We have multiple detection layers like Email protection, Online protection and Behaviour detection to protect our customers.</p>
<p>Here is the detection stats number of hits per day in the last 45 days.</p>
<figure id="attachment_4532" style="width: 480px" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><img class="size-full wp-image-4532" src="https://www.seqrite.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/graph.png" alt="Fig 12: Graph" width="480" height="288" srcset="https://www.seqrite.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/graph.png 480w, https://www.seqrite.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/graph-300x180.png 300w" sizes="(max-width: 480px) 100vw, 480px" /><figcaption class="wp-caption-text">Fig 12: Graph</figcaption></figure>
<h2>Conclusion</h2>
<p>Emotet is a persistent threat actor and highly successful in delivering email-based malware, with a major focus on email theft and sending additional malware. It has moderate obfuscated code to deliver and bypass the detection technique.</p>
<p>With the global impact of COVID-19, threat actors are likely to continue to use COVID-19-themed emails to deliver malware broadly in support of their objectives for all sectors.</p>
<p>Quick Heal customers have long been protected from Emotet and other COVID-19-themed emails. We continue to track and report such attacks to keep our customers safe.</p>
