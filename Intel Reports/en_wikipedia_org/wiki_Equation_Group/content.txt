title="National Security Agency">National Security Agency</a> (NSA).<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Kaspersky_Labs" class="mw-redirect" title="Kaspersky Labs">Kaspersky Labs</a> describes them as one of the most sophisticated cyber attack groups in the world and "the most advanced (...) we have seen", operating alongside the creators of <a href="/wiki/Stuxnet" title="Stuxnet">Stuxnet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Flame_(malware)" title="Flame (malware)">Flame</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-malware-galaxy_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-malware-galaxy-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ars_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ars-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Most of their targets have been in <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mali" title="Mali">Mali</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ars_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ars-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>The name originated from the group's extensive use of encryption. By 2015, Kaspersky documented 500 <a href="/wiki/Malware" title="Malware">malware</a> infections by the group in at least 42 countries, while acknowledging that the actual number could be in the tens of thousands due to its self-terminating protocol.<sup id="cite_ref-ars_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ars-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>In 2017, WikiLeaks <a href="/wiki/Vault_7" title="Vault 7">published a discussion</a> held within the <a href="/wiki/Central_Intelligence_Agency" title="Central Intelligence Agency">CIA</a> on how it had been possible to identify the group.<sup id="cite_ref-Goodin_CIA_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Goodin_CIA-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> One commenter wrote that "the Equation Group as labeled in the report does not relate to a specific group but rather a collection of tools" used for hacking.<sup id="cite_ref-What_did_Equation_do_wrong_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-What_did_Equation_do_wrong-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Discovery">Discovery</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Equation_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Discovery"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>At the Kaspersky Security Analysts Summit held in Mexico on February 16, 2015, Kaspersky Lab announced its discovery of the Equation Group. According to Kaspersky Lab's report, the group has been active since at least 2001, with more than 60 actors.<sup id="cite_ref-kaspersky_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kaspersky-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The malware used in their operations, dubbed EquationDrug and GrayFish, is found to be capable of reprogramming <a href="/wiki/Hard_disk_drive" title="Hard disk drive">hard disk drive</a> <a href="/wiki/Firmware" title="Firmware">firmware</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-malware-galaxy_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-malware-galaxy-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Because of the advanced techniques involved and high degree of covertness, the group is suspected of ties to the NSA, but Kaspersky Lab has not identified the actors behind the group.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Probable_links_to_Stuxnet_and_the_NSA">Probable links to Stuxnet and the NSA</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Equation_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Probable links to Stuxnet and the NSA"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>In 2015 Kaspersky's research findings on the Equation Group noted that its loader, "GrayFish", had similarities to a previously discovered loader, "Gauss",<span class="plainlinks"><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://github.com/loneicewolf/Gauss-Src"><sup>[repository]</sup></a></span> from another attack series, and separately noted that the Equation Group used two zero-day attacks later used in <a href="/wiki/Stuxnet" title="Stuxnet">Stuxnet</a>; the researchers concluded that "the similar type of usage of both exploits together in different computer worms, at around the same time, indicates that the EQUATION group and the Stuxnet developers are either the same or working closely together".<sup id="cite_ref-Kaspersky1_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kaspersky1-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 13">&#58;&#8202;13&#8202;</span></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Firmware">Firmware</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Equation_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Firmware"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>They also identified that the platform had at times been spread by <a href="/wiki/Interdiction" title="Interdiction">interdiction</a> (interception of legitimate CDs sent by a scientific conference organizer by <a href="/wiki/Mail" title="Mail">mail</a>),<sup id="cite_ref-Kaspersky1_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kaspersky1-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 15">&#58;&#8202;15&#8202;</span></sup> and that the platform had the "unprecedented" ability to infect and be transmitted through the <a href="/wiki/Hard_drive" class="mw-redirect" title="Hard drive">hard drive</a> <a href="/wiki/Firmware" title="Firmware">firmware</a> of several major hard drive manufacturers, and create and use hidden disk areas and virtual disk systems for its purposes, a feat which would require access to the manufacturer's <a href="/wiki/Source_code" title="Source code">source code</a> to achieve,<sup id="cite_ref-Kaspersky1_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kaspersky1-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 16–18">&#58;&#8202;16–18&#8202;</span></sup> and that the tool was designed for surgical precision, going so far as to exclude specific countries by IP and allow targeting of specific usernames on <a href="/wiki/Discussion_forum" class="mw-redirect" title="Discussion forum">discussion forums</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Kaspersky1_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kaspersky1-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 23–26">&#58;&#8202;23–26&#8202;</span></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Codewords_and_timestamps">Codewords and timestamps</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Equation_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Codewords and timestamps"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The NSA codewords  "STRAITACID" and "STRAITSHOOTER" have been found inside the malware. In addition, <a href="/wiki/Timestamps" class="mw-redirect" title="Timestamps">timestamps</a> in the malware seem to indicate that the programmers worked overwhelmingly Monday–Friday in what would correspond to a 08:00–17:00 (8:00 AM - 5:00 PM) workday in an Eastern United States time zone.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="The_LNK_exploit">The LNK exploit</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Equation_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The LNK exploit"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Kaspersky's global research and analysis team, otherwise known as GReAT, claimed to have found a piece of malware that contained Stuxnet's "privLib" in 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Specifically it contained the LNK exploit found in Stuxnet in 2010. Fanny is classified as a worm that affects certain <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Windows" title="Microsoft Windows">Windows operating systems</a> and attempts to spread laterally via network connection or <a href="/wiki/Universal_Serial_Bus" class="mw-redirect" title="Universal Serial Bus">USB storage</a>.<span class="plainlinks"><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://github.com/loneicewolf/fanny.bmp"><sup>[repository]</sup></a></span> Kaspersky stated that they suspect that the Equation Group has been around longer than Stuxnet, based on the recorded compile time of Fanny.<sup id="cite_ref-malware-galaxy_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-malware-galaxy-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Link_to_IRATEMONK">Link to IRATEMONK</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Equation_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Link to IRATEMONK"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:NSA_IRATEMONK.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/NSA_IRATEMONK.jpg/220px-NSA_IRATEMONK.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="285" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/NSA_IRATEMONK.jpg/330px-NSA_IRATEMONK.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/NSA_IRATEMONK.jpg/440px-NSA_IRATEMONK.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="1035" /></a><figcaption>The NSA's listing of its <a href="/wiki/Tailored_Access_Operations" title="Tailored Access Operations">Tailored Access Operations</a> program named IRATEMONK from the <a href="/wiki/NSA_ANT_catalog" class="mw-redirect" title="NSA ANT catalog">NSA ANT catalog</a>.</figcaption></figure>
<p><a href="/wiki/F-Secure" title="F-Secure">F-Secure</a> claims that the Equation Group's malicious hard drive <a href="/wiki/Firmware" title="Firmware">firmware</a> is <a href="/wiki/Tailored_Access_Operations" title="Tailored Access Operations">TAO</a> program "IRATEMONK",<sup id="cite_ref-FSecure_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FSecure-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> one of the items from the <a href="/wiki/NSA_ANT_catalog" class="mw-redirect" title="NSA ANT catalog">NSA ANT catalog</a> exposed in a 2013 <i>Der Spiegel</i> article. IRATEMONK provides the attacker with an ability to have their <a href="/wiki/Application_software" title="Application software">software application</a> persistently installed on desktop and laptop computers, despite the disk being <a href="/wiki/Disk_formatting" title="Disk formatting">formatted</a>, its <a href="/wiki/Data_erasure" title="Data erasure">data erased</a> or the operating system re-installed. It infects the hard drive firmware, which in turn adds instructions to the disk's <a href="/wiki/Master_boot_record" title="Master boot record">master boot record</a> that causes the software to install each time the computer is <a href="/wiki/Booting" title="Booting">booted up</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IRATEMONK_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IRATEMONK-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> It is capable of infecting certain hard drives from <a href="/wiki/Seagate_Technology" title="Seagate Technology">Seagate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maxtor" title="Maxtor">Maxtor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Western_Digital" title="Western Digital">Western Digital</a>, <a href="/wiki/Samsung" title="Samsung">Samsung</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-IRATEMONK_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IRATEMONK-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/IBM" title="IBM">IBM</a>, <a href="/wiki/Micron_Technology" title="Micron Technology">Micron Technology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Toshiba" title="Toshiba">Toshiba</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-malware-galaxy_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-malware-galaxy-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="2016_breach_of_the_Equation_Group">2016 breach of the Equation Group</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Equation_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 2016 breach of the Equation Group"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>In August 2016, a hacking group calling itself "<a href="/wiki/The_Shadow_Brokers" title="The Shadow Brokers">The Shadow Brokers</a>" announced that it had stolen malware code from the Equation Group.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Kaspersky Lab noticed similarities between the stolen code and earlier known code from the Equation Group malware samples it had in its possession including quirks unique to the Equation Group's way of implementing the <a href="/wiki/RC6" title="RC6">RC6</a> encryption algorithm, and therefore concluded that this announcement is legitimate.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The most recent dates of the stolen files are from June 2013, thus prompting <a href="/wiki/Edward_Snowden" title="Edward Snowden">Edward Snowden</a> to speculate that a likely lockdown resulting from his leak of the <a href="/wiki/Global_surveillance_disclosures_(2013%E2%80%93present)" class="mw-redirect" title="Global surveillance disclosures (2013–present)">NSA's global and domestic surveillance efforts</a> stopped The Shadow Brokers' breach of the Equation Group. Exploits against <a href="/wiki/Cisco_Adaptive_Security_Appliance" class="mw-redirect" title="Cisco Adaptive Security Appliance">Cisco Adaptive Security Appliances</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fortinet" title="Fortinet">Fortinet</a>'s firewalls were featured in some malware samples released by The Shadow Brokers.<sup id="cite_ref-EXTRABACON_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EXTRABACON-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> EXTRABACON, a <a href="/wiki/Simple_Network_Management_Protocol" title="Simple Network Management Protocol">Simple Network Management Protocol</a> exploit against Cisco's ASA software, was a <a href="/wiki/Zero-day_(computing)" class="mw-redirect" title="Zero-day (computing)">zero-day exploit</a> as of the time of the announcement.<sup id="cite_ref-EXTRABACON_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EXTRABACON-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Juniper also confirmed that its NetScreen firewalls were affected.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>18<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>  The <a href="/wiki/EternalBlue" title="EternalBlue">EternalBlue</a> exploit was used to conduct the damaging worldwide <a href="/wiki/WannaCry_ransomware_attack" title="WannaCry ransomware attack">WannaCry ransomware attack</a>.
</p>
