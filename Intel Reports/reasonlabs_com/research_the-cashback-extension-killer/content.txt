loading="lazy"/></noscript></span></figure><h2 id="summary" class="h3 h3_bold">Executive Summary</h2><p>Online piracy is a topic we’ve covered a lot in the past as it remains an increasing problem for companies, artists, and unsuspecting users - one study conducted recently<!-- --> <a target="_blank" href="https://torrentfreak.com/online-piracy-continues-to-rise-with-the-us-firmly-in-the-lead-221011/">found</a> <!-- -->that the number of visits to piracy sites in 2022 grew by more than 20% compared to 2021. Online piracy also continues to be utilized by cyber attackers who leverage it to distribute<!-- --> <a target="_blank" href="https://cyberpedia.reasonlabs.com/EN/malware.html">malware</a> <!-- -->to users.</p><p>ReasonLabs researchers have discovered numerous<!-- --> <a target="_blank" href="https://cyberpedia.reasonlabs.com/EN/malicious.html">malicious</a> <!-- -->web extensions that are being delivered to users through a<!-- --> <a target="_blank" href="https://cyberpedia.reasonlabs.com/EN/trojan.html">Trojan</a> <!-- -->installer. The installer is hidden in thousands of torrent files claiming to be prominent video games such as <b>Grand Theft Auto (GTA), Assassins Creed</b>, and more. Our<!-- --> <a href="/platform/products/edr">RAV EDR</a> <!-- -->product flagged suspicious activities on a large number of users, which led to an investigation whereby we uncovered the widespread malware campaigns.</p><p>The malicious installers we identified forcibly install one of at least three different malicious web extensions for Google Chrome or Edge, claiming to be<!-- --> <a target="_blank" href="https://cyberpedia.reasonlabs.com/EN/vpn.html">Virtual Private Networks (VPN)</a>. Google has removed all of the malicious extensions identified in this blog from the Chrome Web Store. The most prominent extension is called<!-- --> <a target="_blank" href="https://chrome.google.com/webstore/search/netplus">netPlus</a> <!-- -->and had over 1 million users, while the other two extensions,<!-- --> <a target="_blank" href="https://chrome.google.com/webstore/detail/netsave-%D1%82%D0%B2%D0%BE%D1%8F-%D0%B0%D0%BD%D0%BE%D0%BD%D0%B8%D0%BC%D0%BD%D0%BE%D1%81%D1%82%D1%8C/ipjbhjmcbgmdjfiichbgbmpmgonokpkb">netSave</a> <!-- -->and<a target="_blank" href="https://chrome.google.com/webstore/detail/netwin-%D0%B4%D0%BE%D1%81%D1%82%D1%83%D0%BF-%D0%BA-%D0%B7%D0%B0%D0%B1%D0%BB%D0%BE%D0%BA%D0%B8%D1%80%D0%BE/gandigjpilmchbomlpmfogigbjapofnc">netWin</a>, had nearly 500K combined installations.</p><h2 id="installer-does" class="h3 h3_bold">What The Installer Does</h2><p>The installer is delivered mainly from torrent downloads claiming to be prominent video games such as <b>Assassins Creed, GTA, The Sims 4</b>, and<!-- --> <b>Heroes 3</b>, and constantly presents itself as setup.exe, followed by tagging itself “by Igruha”. We have come across more than 1000 different torrent files that deliver the same setup file. The most common signer name of these files is “SPICE &amp; WOK LIMITED.” Most of the installer files are about 60MB in size, but some are even 100MB or more. The installer unpacks one of the three malicious extension files and forcefully installs it to the user’s browser.</p><p>The installer is installing the web extension through the registry key of<!-- --> <b>“SOFTWARE\Google\Chrome\PreferenceMACs\Default\extensions.settings\”</b>. This method is more sophisticated and less commonly used than the “--load-extension” command line argument or “ForceInstall” registry key force extension loading methods. This method changes browser files via Secure Preferences to complete the installation and register the extension without the user’s knowledge or need for approval.</p><p>The installer then installs two different extensions - one extension for Chrome (netSave) and a different one for Edge (netPlus).</p><h2 id="what-fake-vpn-do" class="h3 h3_bold">What The Fake VPN Extensions Do</h2><p>The<!-- --> <a target="_blank" href="https://cyberpedia.reasonlabs.com/EN/javascript.html">JavaScript</a> <!-- -->code of the extensions is massive and contains over 20,000 lines of code. It’s unclear and extremely difficult to understand the code’s full activity but we were able to verify that it’s not just a fake VPN but also runs a cashback activity hack.</p><p>To ensure its success, the extension will disable all other cashback extensions from the infected browsers, while also implementing a real VPN UI with minor functionalities to keep up the facade. All of the extensions are in Russian and seemingly targeted at Russian-speaking users. They share the same code and screens in the Google Web Store; however, the developer names, titles, and descriptions differ slightly.</p><p>Each extension has several user reviews stating that the extension was installed onto their browser without their knowledge. In addition, the installer comes with chromium-based files, probably for additional actions of web surfing, but it requires further research.</p><p>The netPlus extension was also present on the Chrome store with the same extension ID - <b>chiiididmecdffakklhibjpjkbfiaeni</b>; however, it was removed from the store in May of 2022. The code of both extensions has a lot of similarities, although with slight changes.
