                  <h2 class="lh-solid mb3"><b>Summary</b></h2>
<ul class="mt0">
<li style="font-weight: 400;" aria-level="1" class="mt2"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Candiru is a secretive Israel-based company that sells spyware exclusively to governments. Reportedly, their spyware can infect and monitor iPhones, Androids, Macs, PCs, and cloud accounts.</span></li>
<li style="font-weight: 400;" aria-level="1" class="mt2"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Using Internet scanning we identified more than 750 websites linked to Candiru&rsquo;s spyware infrastructure. We found many domains masquerading as advocacy organizations such as Amnesty International, the Black Lives Matter movement, as well as media companies, and other civil-society themed entities.</span></li>
<li style="font-weight: 400;" aria-level="1" class="mt2"><span style="font-weight: 400;">We identified a politically active victim in Western Europe and recovered a copy of Candiru&rsquo;s Windows spyware.&nbsp;</span></li>
<li style="font-weight: 400;" aria-level="1" class="mt2"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Working with Microsoft Threat Intelligence Center (MSTIC) we analyzed the spyware, resulting in the discovery of </span><a href="https://msrc.microsoft.com/update-guide/en-US/vulnerability/CVE-2021-31979" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><b>CVE-2021-31979</b></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> and </span><a href="https://msrc.microsoft.com/update-guide/en-US/vulnerability/CVE-2021-33771" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><b>CVE-2021-33771</b></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> by Microsoft, two privilege escalation vulnerabilities exploited by Candiru. Microsoft patched both vulnerabilities on July 13th, 2021.</span></li>
<li style="font-weight: 400;" aria-level="1" class="mt2"><span style="font-weight: 400;">As part of their investigation, Microsoft <a href="https://www.microsoft.com/security/blog/2021/07/15/protecting-customers-from-a-private-sector-offensive-actor-using-0-day-exploits-and-devilstongue-malware/" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener">observed</a> at least 100 victims in Palestine, Israel, Iran, Lebanon, Yemen, Spain, United Kingdom, Turkey, Armenia, and Singapore. Victims include human rights defenders, dissidents, journalists, activists, and politicians.</span></li>
<li style="font-weight: 400;" aria-level="1" class="mt2"><span style="font-weight: 400;">We provide a brief technical overview of the Candiru spyware&rsquo;s persistence mechanism and some details about the spyware&rsquo;s functionality.</span></li>
<li aria-level="1" class="mt2">Candiru has made efforts to obscure its ownership structure, staffing, and investment partners. Nevertheless, we have been able to shed some light on those areas in this report.</li>
</ul>
<h2 class="lh-solid mb3"><b>1. Who is Candiru?</b></h2>
<p class="mt0"><span style="font-weight: 400;">The company known as &ldquo;Candiru,&rdquo; based in Tel Aviv, Israel, is a mercenary spyware firm that markets &ldquo;untraceable&rdquo; spyware to government customers. Their product offering includes solutions for spying on computers, mobile devices, and cloud accounts.</span></p>
<figure class="center mw-100 ba b--light-gray" style="width:1280px;"><div class="tc pa2 bg-white"><a href="https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2021/07/Fig-1.jpg" class="pointer"><img fetchpriority="high" decoding="async" class="size-full wp-image-75174" src="https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2021/07/Fig-1.jpg" alt="" width="1280" height="640" title="Hooking Candiru: Another Mercenary Spyware Vendor Comes into Focus 1"></a></div><figcaption class="f5-ns f6 black-70 pa2 bg-light-gray">Figure 1: A distinctive mural of five men with empty heads wearing suits and bowler hats is displayed in this &ldquo;Happy Hour&rdquo; photo a previous Candiru office posted on Facebook by a catering company.<sup id="fnref:1"><a href="#fn:1" rel="footnote" class="pointer">1</a></sup></figcaption></figure>
<h3 class="lh-solid mb3"><b>A Deliberately Opaque Corporate structure</b></h3>
<p class="mt0"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Candiru makes efforts to keep its operations, infrastructure, and staff identities opaque to public scrutiny. Candiru Ltd. was founded in 2014 and has undergone several name changes<sup id="fnref:2"><a href="#fn:2" rel="footnote" class="pointer">2</a></sup></span><span style="font-weight: 400;"> (</span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">see: <strong>Table </strong></span></i><strong><i>1</i></strong><span style="font-weight: 400;">). Like many mercenary spyware corporations, the company </span><a href="https://www.haaretz.com/middle-east-news/.premium-top-secret-israeli-cyberattack-firm-revealed-1.6805950" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">reportedly</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> recruits from the ranks of Unit 8200, the signals intelligence unit of the Israeli Defence Forces.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">While the company&rsquo;s current name is Saito Tech Ltd, </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">we will refer to them as </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">&ldquo;Candiru&rdquo; as they are most well known by that name. The firm&rsquo;s corporate logo appears to be a silhouette of the reputedly-gruesome </span><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Candiru" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Candiru fish</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> in the shape of the letter &ldquo;C.&rdquo;&nbsp;</span></p>

<h3 class="lh-solid mb3"><b>Reported Sales and Investments</b></h3>
<p class="mt0"><span style="font-weight: 400;">According to a </span><a href="https://www.haaretz.com/israel-news/tech-news/.premium-mobile-spytech-millions-in-gulf-deals-top-secret-israeli-cyberattack-firm-reve-1.9125915" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">lawsuit</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> brought by a former employee, Candiru had sales of &ldquo;nearly $30 million,&rdquo; within two years of its founding. The firm&rsquo;s reported clients are located in &ldquo;Europe, the former Soviet Union, the Persian Gulf, Asia and Latin America.&rdquo; Additionally, </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">reports of possible deals with several countries have been published:&nbsp;</span></p>
<ul>
<li style="font-weight: 400;" aria-level="1" class="mt2"><b>Uzbekistan</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">: In a 2019 </span><a href="https://www.virusbulletin.com/conference/vb2019/abstracts/who-sandcat-unveiling-lesser-known-threat-actor" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">presentation</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> at the Virus Bulletin security conference, a Kaspersky Lab researcher </span><a href="https://www.forbes.com/sites/thomasbrewster/2019/10/03/meet-candiru-the-super-stealth-cyber-mercenaries-hacking-apple-and-microsoft-pcs-for-profit/?sh=5aec7c225a39" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">stated</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> that Candiru likely sold its spyware to Uzbekistan&rsquo;s National Security Service.</span></li>
<li style="font-weight: 400;" aria-level="1" class="mt2"><b>Saudi Arabia &amp; the UAE</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">: The same presentation also mentioned Saudi Arabia and the UAE as likely Candiru customers.</span></li>
<li style="font-weight: 400;" aria-level="1" class="mt2"><b>Singapore</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">: A 2019 </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">Intelligence Online</span></i> <a href="https://www.intelligenceonline.com/international-dealmaking/2019/04/03/singapore-turns-to-israeli-cyber-spies-again,108351899-art" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">report</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> mentions that Candiru was active in soliciting business from Singapore&rsquo;s intelligence services.</span></li>
<li style="font-weight: 400;" aria-level="1" class="mt2"><b>Qatar:</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> A 2020 </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">Intelligence Online</span></i> <a href="https://www.intelligenceonline.com/government-intelligence/2020/08/26/doha-like-abu-dhabi-also-hooked-on-israeli-technology,109602026-evg" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">report</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> notes that Candiru &ldquo;has become closer to Qatar.&rdquo; A company linked to Qatar&rsquo;s sovereign wealth fund has </span><a href="https://www.intelligenceonline.com/corporate-intelligence/2020/08/26/candiru-receives-boost-from-investors-linked-to-qatar,109602043-art" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">invested</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> in Candiru. No information on Qatar-based customers has yet emerged,</span></li>
</ul>
<h3 class="lh-solid mb3"><b>Candiru&rsquo;s Spyware Offerings</b></h3>
<p class="mt0"><span style="font-weight: 400;">A leaked Candiru project proposal </span><a href="https://www.themarker.com/embeds/pdf_upload/2020/20200902-161742.pdf" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">published by TheMarker</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> shows that Candiru&rsquo;s spyware can be installed using a number of different vectors, including malicious links, </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">man-in-the-middle</span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;"> attacks, and physical attacks.&nbsp; A vector named &ldquo;</span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">Sherlock&rdquo;</span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;"> is also offered, that they claim works on Windows, iOS, and Android. This may be a browser-based zero-click vector.</span></p>
<figure class="center mw-100 ba b--light-gray" style="width:1642px;"><div class="tc pa2 bg-white"><a href="https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2021/07/Fig-2.png" class="pointer"><img decoding="async" class="size-full wp-image-75176" src="https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2021/07/Fig-2.png" alt="" width="1642" height="464" title="Hooking Candiru: Another Mercenary Spyware Vendor Comes into Focus 2"></a></div><figcaption class="f5-ns f6 black-70 pa2 bg-light-gray">Figure 2: Infection vectors offered by Candiru.</figcaption></figure>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Like many of its peers, Candiru appears to license its spyware by </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">number of concurrent infections</span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;">, which reflects the number of targets that can be under active surveillance at any one instant in time. Like NSO Group, Candiru also appears to restrict the customer to a set of approved countries.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">The &euro;16 million project proposal allows for an unlimited number of spyware infection attempts, but the monitoring of only 10 devices simultaneously. For an additional &euro;1.5M, the customer can purchase the ability to monitor 15 additional devices simultaneously, and to infect devices in a single additional country. For an additional &euro;5.5M, the customer can monitor 25 additional devices simultaneously, and conduct espionage in five more countries.</span></p>
<p><a href="https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2021/07/Test.png" class="pointer"><img decoding="async" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-75182" src="https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2021/07/Test.png" alt="" width="1458" height="236" title="Hooking Candiru: Another Mercenary Spyware Vendor Comes into Focus 3"></a></p>
<figure class="center mw-100 ba b--light-gray" style="width:1494px;"><div class="tc pa2 bg-white"><a href="https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2021/07/Fig-3b.png" class="pointer"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="wp-image-75180 size-full" src="https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2021/07/Fig-3b.png" alt="" width="1494" height="384" title="Hooking Candiru: Another Mercenary Spyware Vendor Comes into Focus 4"></a></div><figcaption class="f5-ns f6 black-70 pa2 bg-light-gray">Figure 3: Proposal for a Candiru Customer indicating number of concurrent infections under a given contract.</figcaption></figure>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">The fine print in the proposal states that the product will operate in &ldquo;all agreed upon territories, &rdquo;then mentions a list of restricted countries including the US, Russia, China, Israel and Iran.&nbsp; This </span><a href="https://www.haaretz.com/middle-east-news/.premium-with-israel-s-encouragement-nso-sold-spyware-to-uae-and-other-gulf-states-1.9093465" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">same list of restricted countries</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> has previously been mentioned by NSO Group.&nbsp; Nevertheless, Microsoft observed Candiru victims in <a href="https://www.microsoft.com/security/blog/2021/07/15/protecting-customers-from-a-private-sector-offensive-actor-using-0-day-exploits-and-devilstongue-malware/" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Iran</a>, suggesting that in some situations, products from Candiru do operate in restricted territories. In addition, targeting infrastructure disclosed in this report includes domains masquerading as the Russian postal service.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">The proposal states that the spyware can exfiltrate private data from a number of apps and accounts including Gmail, Skype, Telegram, and Facebook. The spyware can also capture browsing history and passwords, turn on the target&rsquo;s webcam and microphone, and take pictures of the screen. Capturing data from additional apps, such as </span><a href="https://www.signal.org/" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Signal Private Messenger</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">, is sold as an add-on.</span></p>
<figure class="center mw-100 ba b--light-gray" style="width:1334px;"><div class="tc pa2 bg-white"><a href="https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2021/07/Fig-4.png" class="pointer"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="size-full wp-image-75183" src="https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2021/07/Fig-4.png" alt="" width="1334" height="288" title="Hooking Candiru: Another Mercenary Spyware Vendor Comes into Focus 5"></a></div><figcaption class="f5-ns f6 black-70 pa2 bg-light-gray">Figure 4: Customers can pay additional money to capture data from Signal.</figcaption></figure>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">For a further additional &euro;1.5M fee, customers can purchase a </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">remote shell</span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;"> capability, which allows them full access to run any command or program on the target&rsquo;s computer. This kind of capability is especially concerning, given that it could also be used to download files, such as planting incriminating materials, </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">onto</span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;"> an infected device.</span></p>
<h2 class="lh-solid mb3"><b>2. Finding Candiru&rsquo;s Malware In The Wild&nbsp;</b></h2>
<p class="mt0"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Using telemetry data from Team Cymru, along with assistance from civil society partners, the Citizen Lab was able to identify a computer that we suspected contained a persistent Candiru infection. We contacted the owner of the computer, a politically active individual in Western Europe, and arranged for the computer&rsquo;s hard drive to be imaged. We ultimately extracted a copy of Candiru&rsquo;s spyware from the disk image.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">While analysis of the extracted spyware is ongoing, this section outlines initial findings about the spyware&rsquo;s persistence&nbsp;</span></p>
<h3 class="lh-solid mb3"><b>Persistence</b></h3>
<p class="mt0"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Candiru&rsquo;s spyware was persistently installed on the computer via COM hijacking of the following registry key:</span></p>
<div class="bg-washed-yellow ba b--gray pa2"><code>HKEY_LOCAL_MACHINE\Software\Classes\CLSID\{CF4CC405-E2C5-4DDD-B3CE-5E7582D8C9FA}\InprocServer32</code></div>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Normally, this registry key&rsquo;s value points to the benign </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">Windows Management Instrumentation</span></i> <span style="font-weight: 400;">wmiutils.dll</span><span style="font-weight: 400;"> file, but the value on the infected computer had been modified to point to a malicious DLL file that had been dropped inside the Windows system folder associated with the Japanese input method (IMEJP) </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">C:\WINDOWS\system32\ime\IMEJP\IMJPUEXP.DLL</span><span style="font-weight: 400;">.</span><span style="font-weight: 400;"> This folder is benign and included in a default install of Windows 10, but </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">IMJPUEXP.DLL</span> <span style="font-weight: 400;">is not the name of a legitimate Windows component.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">When Windows boots, it automatically loads the Windows Management Instrumentation service, which involves looking up the DLL path in the registry key, and then invoking the DLL.</span></p>
<h3 class="lh-solid mb3"><b>Loading the Spyware&rsquo;s Configuration</b></h3>
<p class="mt0"><span style="font-weight: 400;">The IMJPUEXP DLL file has eight blobs in the PE resources section with identifiers </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">102, 103, 105, 106, 107, 108, 109, 110</span><span style="font-weight: 400;">. The DLL decrypts these using an AES key and IV that are hardcoded in the DLL. Decryption is via Windows CryptoAPI, using AES-256-CBC.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Of particular note is resource </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">102</span><span style="font-weight: 400;">, which contains the path to the legitimate </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">wmiutils.dll</span><span style="font-weight: 400;">, which is loaded after the spyware, ensuring that the COM hijack does not disrupt normal Windows functionality. Resource </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">103</span><span style="font-weight: 400;"> points to a file </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">AgentService.dat </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">in a folder created by the spyware, </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">C:\WINDOWS\system32\config\spp\Licenses\curv\config\tracing\</span><span style="font-weight: 400;">. Resource 105 points to a second file in the same directory, </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">KBDMAORI</span><span style="font-weight: 400;">.dat</span><span style="font-weight: 400;">.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">IMJPUEXP.DLL decrypts and loads the </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">AgentService.dat</span><span style="font-weight: 400;"> file whose path is in resource 103, using the same AES key and IV, and decompresses it via zlib. </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">AgentService.dat</span><span style="font-weight: 400;"> file then loads the file in resource 105, </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">KBDMAORI.dat</span><span style="font-weight: 400;">, using a second AES key and IV hardcoded in </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">AgentService.dat</span><span style="font-weight: 400;">, and performs the decryption using a statically linked OpenSSL. Decrypting </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">KBDMAORI.DAT</span><span style="font-weight: 400;"> yields a file with a series of nine encrypted blobs, each prefixed with an 8-byte little-endian length field. Each blob is encrypted with the same AES key and IV used to decrypt </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">KBDMAORI.DAT,</span><span style="font-weight: 400;"> and is then zlib compressed.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">The first four encrypted blobs appear to be DLLs from the Microsoft Visual C++ redistributable: </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">vcruntime140.dll, msvcp140.dll, ucrtbase.dll, concrt140.dll</span><span style="font-weight: 400;">. The subsequent blobs are part of the spyware, including components that are apparently called </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">Internals.dll </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">and</span><span style="font-weight: 400;"> Help.dll</span><span style="font-weight: 400;">.&nbsp; Both the Microsoft DLLs and the spyware DLLs in </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">KBDMAORI.DAT</span><span style="font-weight: 400;"> are lightly obfuscated.&nbsp; Reverting the following modifications makes the files valid DLLs:</span></p>
<ol>
<li style="font-weight: 400;" aria-level="1" class="mt2"><span style="font-weight: 400;">The first two bytes of the file (MZ) have been zeroed.</span></li>
<li style="font-weight: 400;" aria-level="1" class="mt2"><span style="font-weight: 400;">The first 4 bytes of NT header (</span><span style="font-weight: 400;">\x50\x45\x00\x00</span><span style="font-weight: 400;">) have been zeroed.</span></li>
<li style="font-weight: 400;" aria-level="1" class="mt2"><span style="font-weight: 400;">The first 2 bytes of the optional header (\x0b\x02) have been zeroed.</span></li>
<li style="font-weight: 400;" aria-level="1" class="mt2"><span style="font-weight: 400;">The strings in the import directory have been XOR obfuscated, using a 48-byte XOR key hardcoded in AgentService.dat:</span><span style="font-weight: 400;"><span style="font-weight: 400;"><br>
</span></span>
<div class="bg-washed-yellow ba b--gray pa2"><code>6604F922F90B65F2B10CE372555C0A0C0C5258B6842A83C7DC2EE4E58B363349F496E6B6A587A88D0164B74DAB9E6B58</code></div>
</li>
</ol>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">The final blob in</span><span style="font-weight: 400;"> KBDMAORI.DAT</span><span style="font-weight: 400;"> is the spyware&rsquo;s configuration in JSON format.&nbsp; The configuration is somewhat obfuscated, but clearly contains Base64 UTF-16 encoded URLs for command-and-control.</span></p>

<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">The C&amp;C servers in the configuration are:</span></p>
<div class="bg-washed-yellow ba b--gray pa2"><code>https://msstore[.]io<br>
<span style="font-weight: 400;">https://adtracker[.]link</span><span style="font-weight: 400;"><br>
</span><span style="font-weight: 400;">https://cdnmobile[.]io</span></code></div>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">All three domain names pointed to </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">185.181.8[.]155.</span><span style="font-weight: 400;"> This IP address was connected to three other IPs that matched our Candiru fingerprint </span><b>CF1</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> (Section 3).</span></p>
<h3 class="lh-solid mb3"><b>Spyware Functionality</b></h3>
<p class="mt0"><span style="font-weight: 400;">We are still reversing most of the spyware&rsquo;s functionality, but Candiru&rsquo;s Windows payload appears to include features for exfiltrating files, exporting all messages saved in the Windows version of the popular encrypted messaging app </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">Signal</span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;">, and stealing cookies and passwords from Chrome, Internet Explorer, Firefox, Safari, and Opera browsers. The spyware also makes use of a legitimate signed third-party driver, </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">physmem.sys</span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;">:</span></p>
<div class="bg-washed-yellow ba b--gray pa2"><code>c299063e3eae8ddc15839767e83b9808fd43418dc5a1af7e4f44b97ba53fbd3d</code></div>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Microsoft&rsquo;s <a href="https://www.microsoft.com/security/blog/2021/07/15/protecting-customers-from-a-private-sector-offensive-actor-using-0-day-exploits-and-devilstongue-malware/" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener">analysis</a> also established that the spyware could send messages from logged-in email and social media accounts directly on the victim&rsquo;s computer. This could allow malicious links or other messages to be sent </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">directly</span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;"> from a compromised user&rsquo;s computer.&nbsp; Proving that the compromised user did not send the message could be quite challenging.</span></p>
<h2 class="lh-solid mb3"><b>3. Mapping Candiru&rsquo;s Command &amp; Control Infrastructure</b></h2>
<p class="mt0"><span style="font-weight: 400;">To identify the websites used by Candiru&rsquo;s spyware, we developed four fingerprints and a new Internet scanning technique. We searched historical data from </span><a href="https://censys.io/" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Censys</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> and conducted our own scans in 2021. This led us to identify at least 764 domain names that we assess with moderate-high confidence to be used by Candiru and its customers. Examination of the domain names indicates a likely interest in targets in Asia, Europe, the Middle East, and North America.&nbsp;&nbsp;</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Additionally, based on our analysis of Internet scanning data, we believe that there are Candiru systems operated from Saudi Arabia, Israel, UAE, Hungary, and Indonesia, among other countries.</span></p>
<h3 class="lh-solid mb3"><b>OPSEC Mistake by Candiru Leads to their Infrastructure</b></h3>
<p class="mt0"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Using Censys, we found a self-signed TLS </span><a href="https://search.censys.io/certificates/6008708f3c88b0acc4a56a5d396a8261bde02ec1408c69bfc21f3b397fc76a4f" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">certificate</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> that included the email address &ldquo;</span><span style="font-weight: 400;"><a href="/cdn-cgi/l/email-protection" class="__cf_email__" data-cfemail="294844405d47694a48474d405b5c5a4c4a5c5b405d50074a4644">[email&#160;protected]</a></span><span style="font-weight: 400;">&rdquo;. We attributed the </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">candirusecurity[.]com </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">domain name to Candiru Ltd, because a second domain name (</span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">verification[.]center</span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;">) was registered in 2015 with a </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">candirusecurity[.]com</span><span style="font-weight: 400;"> email address and a phone number (+972-54-2552428) listed by Dun &amp; Bradstreet as the </span><a href="https://www.dunsguide.co.il/en/Cf02a0e29fc26363000a0275217acaa51_high_tech_computing_services/saito_tech/" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">fax number for Candiru Ltd</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">, also known as Saito Tech Ltd.</span></p>
<figure class="center mw-100 ba b--light-gray" style="width:1508px;"><div class="tc pa2 bg-white"><a href="https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2021/07/Fig-6.png" class="pointer"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="size-full wp-image-75187" src="https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2021/07/Fig-6.png" alt="" width="1508" height="791" title="Hooking Candiru: Another Mercenary Spyware Vendor Comes into Focus 7"></a></div><figcaption class="f5-ns f6 black-70 pa2 bg-light-gray">Figure 6: This Candiru certificate we found on Censys was the starting point of our analysis.</figcaption></figure>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Censys data records that a total of six IP addresses returned this certificate: </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">151.236.23[.]93, 69.28.67[.]162, 176.123.26[.]67, 52.8.109[.]170, 5.135.115[.]40, 185.56.89[.]66</span><span style="font-weight: 400;">. The latter four of these IP addresses subsequently returned </span><a href="https://search.censys.io/certificates/9d1afe71fa38a2d8c24664029a41ceb980862d4c8cecaf6c2278c779ac1307bd" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">another certificate</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">, which we fingerprinted (</span><b>Fingerprint CF1</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">) based on distinctive features.&nbsp; We searched Censys data for this fingerprint.</span></p>
<pre class="box--code b--gray pa2 ba mb0 overflow-auto">SELECT parsed.fingerprint_sha256
FROM`censys-io.certificates_public.certificates`
WHERE parsed.issuer_dn IS NULL
 AND parsed.subject_dn IS NULL
 AND parsed.validity.length = 8639913600
 AND parsed.extensions.basic_constraints.is_ca
</pre>
<div class="bg-light-gray pa2 mt0 f5-ns f6 black70">Table 2: Fingerprint CF1</div>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">We found 42 certificates on Censys matching </span><b>CF1</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">. We observed that six IPs matching </span><b>CF1</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> certificates later returned certificates that matched a second fingerprint we devised, </span><b>CF2</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">. The </span><b>CF2</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> fingerprint is based on certificates that match those generated by a &ldquo;Fake Name&rdquo; generator. We first ran an SQL query on Censys data for the fingerprint, and then filtered by a list of fake names.</span></p>
<pre class="box--code b--gray pa2 ba mb0 overflow-auto">SELECT parsed.fingerprint_sha256, parsed.subject_dn
FROM`censys-io.certificates_public.certificates`
WHERE (parsed.subject_dn = parsed.issuer_dn
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;AND REGEXP_CONTAINS (parsed.subject_dn, r"^O=[A-Z][a-z]+,,? 
   CN=[a-z]+\.(com|net|org)+$")
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;AND parsed.extensions.basic_constraints.is_ca
</pre>
<div class="bg-light-gray pa2 mt0 f5-ns f6 black70">Table 3: Fingerprint CF2 SQL Query.</div>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">The SQL query yielded 572 results. We filtered the results, requiring the TLS certificate&rsquo;s organization in the </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">parsed.subject_dn</span><span style="font-weight: 400;"> field to contain an entry from the </span><a href="https://metacpan.org/release/WSHELDAHL/Data-Faker-0.10/source/lib/Data/Faker/Name.pm" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">list of 475 last names</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> in the Perl </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">Data-Faker</span><span style="font-weight: 400;"> module. We suspect that Candiru is using either this Perl module, or another module that uses the same word list, to generate fake names for TLS certificates. Neither the Perl </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">Data-Faker </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">module, nor other similar modules (e.g., the Ruby </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">Faker</span><span style="font-weight: 400;"> Gem, or the PHP </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">Faker</span><span style="font-weight: 400;"> module) appear to have built-in functionality for generating fake TLS certificates. Thus, we suspect that the TLS certificate generation code is custom code written by Candiru. After filtering, we found 542 matching certificates.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">We then developed an HTTP fingerprint, called </span><b>BRIDGE</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">, with which we scanned the Internet and built a third TLS fingerprint, </span><b>CF3</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">. We are keeping the </span><b>BRIDGE</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> and </span><b>CF3</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> fingerprints confidential for now in order to maintain visibility into Candiru&rsquo;s infrastructure.</span></p>
<h3 class="lh-solid mb3"><b>Overlap with CHAINSHOT</b></h3>
<p class="mt0"><span style="font-weight: 400;">One of the IPs that matched our </span><b>CF1</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> fingerprint, </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">185.25.50[.]194</span><span style="font-weight: 400;">, was pointed to by </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">dl.nmcyclingexperience[.]com</span><span style="font-weight: 400;">, which is mentioned as a final URL of a spyware payload delivered by the CHAINSHOT exploit kit in a </span><a href="https://unit42.paloaltonetworks.com/unit42-slicing-dicing-cve-2018-5002-payloads-new-chainshot-malware/" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">2018 report</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">. CHAINSHOT is believed to be linked to Candiru, though no public reports have outlined the basis for this attribution, until now. Kaspersky has observed UAE hacking group </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">Stealth Falcon<sup id="fnref:4"><a href="#fn:4" ref="footnote" class="pointer">4</a></sup></span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;"> using CHAINSHOT, as well as an Uzbekistan-based customer that they call </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">SandCat</span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;">. While numerous analyses have focused on various CHAINSHOT exploitation techniques, we have not seen any public work that examines Candiru&rsquo;s final Windows payload.</span></p>
<h3 class="lh-solid mb3"><b>Overlap with Google TAG Research</b></h3>
<p class="mt0"><span style="font-weight: 400;">On 14 July 2021, Google&rsquo;s Threat Analysis Group (TAG) </span><a href="https://blog.google/threat-analysis-group/how-we-protect-users-0-day-attacks/" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">published a report</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> that mentions two Chrome zero-day exploits that TAG observed used against targets (</span><b>CVE-2021-21166</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> and </span><b>CVE-2021-30551</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">).&nbsp; The report mentions nine websites that Google determined were used to distribute the exploits.&nbsp; Eight of these websites pointed to IP addresses that matched our </span><b>CF3</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> Candiru fingerprint.&nbsp; We thus believe that the attacks that Google observed involving these Chrome exploits were linked to Candiru.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Google also linked a further Microsoft Office exploit they observed (</span><b>CVE-2021-33742</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">) to the same operator.</span></p>
<h3 class="lh-solid mb3"><b>Targeting Themes</b></h3>
<p class="mt0"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Examination of Candiru&rsquo;s targeting infrastructure permits us to make guesses about the location of potential targets, and topics and themes that Candiru operators believed that targets would find relevant and enticing.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Some of the themes strongly suggest that the targeting likely concerned civil society and political activity. This troubling indicator matches with Microsoft&rsquo;s observation of the extensive targeting of members of civil society, academics, and the media with Candiru&rsquo;s spyware. We observed evidence of targeting infrastructure masquerading as media, advocacy organizations, international organizations, and others (s</span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">ee: </span></i><b><i>Table 4</i></b><span style="font-weight: 400;">).&nbsp;</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">We found many aspects of this targeting concerning, such as the domain </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">blacklivesmatters[.]info</span><span style="font-weight: 400;">, which may be used to target individuals interested in or affiliated with this movement.&nbsp; Similarly, infrastructure masquerading as Amnesty International and Refugee International are troubling, as are lookalike domains for the United Nations, World Health Organization, and other international organizations.&nbsp; We also found the targeting theme of gender studies (e.g. </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">womanstudies[.]co</span><span style="font-weight: 400;"> &amp; </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">genderconference[.]org</span><span style="font-weight: 400;">)&nbsp; to be particularly interesting and warranting further investigation.</span></p>

<h2 class="lh-solid mb3"><b>4. A Saudi-Linked Cluster?</b></h2>
<p class="mt0"><span style="font-weight: 400;">A document was uploaded from Iran to VirusTotal that used an </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">AutoOpen</span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;"> Macro to launch a web browser, and navigated the browser to the URL https://cuturl[.]space/lty7uw, which VirusTotal recorded as redirecting to a URL, https://useproof[.]cc/1tUAE7A2Jn8WMmq/api, that mentions a domain we linked to Candiru, </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">useproof[.]cc</span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;">.&nbsp; The domain </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">useproof[.]cc</span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;"> pointed to 109.70.236.107, which matched our fingerprint </span><b>CF3</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">The document was blank, except for a graphic containing the text &ldquo;Minister of Foreign Affairs of the Islamic Republic of Iran.&rdquo;</span></p>
<figure class="center mw-100 ba b--light-gray" style="width:732px;"><div class="tc pa2 bg-white"><a href="https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2021/07/Fig-7.png" class="pointer"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="size-full wp-image-75188" src="https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2021/07/Fig-7.png" alt="" width="732" height="118" title="Hooking Candiru: Another Mercenary Spyware Vendor Comes into Focus 8"></a></div><figcaption class="f5-ns f6 black-70 pa2 bg-light-gray">Figure 7: A document that loads a Candiru URL was uploaded to VirusTotal from Iran, and includes a header image referencing the Minister of Foreign Affairs.</figcaption></figure>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">We fingerprinted the behaviour of </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">cuturl[.]space</span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;"> and traced it to five other URL shorteners: </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">llink[.]link</span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;">, </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">instagrarn[.]co</span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;">, </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">cuturl[.]app</span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;">, </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">url-tiny[.]co</span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;">, and </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">bitly[.]tel</span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;">. Interestingly, several of these domains were flagged by a researcher at ThreatConnect in </span><a href="https://twitter.com/kyleehmke/status/1324029830560710658" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">two</span></a> <a href="https://twitter.com/kyleehmke/status/1354420675105189892" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">tweets</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">, based on suspicious characteristics of their registration. We suspect that the AutoOpen format and the URL shorteners may be unique to a particular Candiru client.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">A Saudi Twitter user contacted us and reported that Saudi users active on Twitter were receiving messages with suspicious short URLs, including links to the domain name </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">bitly[.]tel</span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;">.&nbsp; Given this, we suspect that the URL shorteners may be linked to Saudi Arabia.</span></p>
<h2 class="lh-solid mb3"><b>5. Additional Corporate Details for Candiru</b></h2>
<p class="mt0"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Ya&rsquo;acov Weitzman (&#1493;&#1497;&#1510;&#1502;&#1503; &#1497;&#1506;&#1511;&#1489;) and Eran Shorer (&#1513;&#1493;&#1512;&#1512; &#1506;&#1512;&#1503;) founded Candiru in 2014. </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">Isaac Zack </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">(&#1494;&#1511; &#1497;&#1506;&#1511;&#1489;)</span><span style="font-weight: 400;">, also </span><a href="https://www.forbes.com/sites/thomasbrewster/2019/10/03/meet-candiru-the-super-stealth-cyber-mercenaries-hacking-apple-and-microsoft-pcs-for-profit/" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">reportedly an early investor in NSO Group</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">, became the largest shareholder of Candiru less than two months after its founding and took a seat on its board of directors. </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">In January 2019, Tomer Israeli (&#1497;&#1513;&#1512;&#1488;&#1500;&#1497; &#1514;&#1493;&#1502;&#1512;) first appeared in corporate records as Candiru&rsquo;s &ldquo;director of finance,&rdquo; and Eitan Achlow (&#1488;&#1495;&#1500;&#1488;&#1493; &#1488;&#1497;&#1514;&#1503;) was named CEO.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">A number of independent investors appear to have funded Candiru&rsquo;s operations over the years. As of Candiru&rsquo;s notice of allotment of shares filed in February 2021 with the Israeli Corporations Authority, Zack, Shorer, and Weitzman are still the largest shareholders. Three organizations are the next largest shareholders: Universal Motors Israel LTD (corporate registration 511809071), ESOP management and trust services (&#1488;&#1497;&#1505;&#1493;&#1508; &#1513;&#1497;&#1512;&#1493;&#1514;&#1497; &#1504;&#1497;&#1492;&#1493;&#1500;) corporate registration 513699538, and Optas Industry Ltd. ESOP (corporate registration no. 513699538) is an Israeli company that provides employee stock program administrative services to corporate clients. We do not know whether ESOP holds its stock in trust for certain Candiru employees. Optas Industry Ltd. is a Malta-based private equity firm (registration number C91267, shareholder Leonard Joseph O&rsquo;Brien, directors are O&rsquo;Brien and Michael Ellul, incorporated 28 March 2019). It has been </span><a href="https://www.intelligenceonline.com/corporate-intelligence/2020/08/26/candiru-receives-boost-from-investors-linked-to-qatar,109602043-art" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">reported</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> that for a decade </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">O&rsquo;Brien has served as head of investment and a board member of the Gulf Investment Fund, and that the sovereign Qatar Investment Authority has a 12% stake in the Gulf Investment Fund (through a subsidiary, Qatar Ho</span><span style="font-weight: 400;">lding). Universal Motors Israel </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">(company registration no. 511809071)</span><span style="font-weight: 400;"> as an investor (including a seat on Candiru&rsquo;s board) is curious considering their primary business is the distribution of new and used automobiles.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Besides Amit Ron</span><span style="font-weight: 400;"> (&#1512;&#1493;&#1503; &#1506;&#1502;&#1497;&#1514;)</span><span style="font-weight: 400;">, the Universal Motors Israel representative, Candiru&rsquo;s board as of December 2020 includes Isaac Zack, </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">Ya&rsquo;acov Weitzman, and Eran Shorer.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">In addition to the involvement of Zack, Candiru shares other points of commonality with NSO Group, including representation by the </span><a href="https://www.legal500.com/firms/10627-erdinast-ben-nathan-toledano-co/11092-tel-aviv-israel/" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">same law firm</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> and utilization of the </span><a href="https://www.somo.nl/wp-content/uploads/2021/05/Operating-from-the-Shadows.pdf" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">same employee equity and trust administration services company</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">. </span></p>
<h2 class="lh-solid mb3"><b>6. Conclusion</b></h2>
<p class="mt0"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Candiru&rsquo;s apparent widespread presence, and the use of its surveillance technology against global civil society, is a potent reminder that the mercenary spyware industry contains many players and is prone to widespread abuse. This case demonstrates, yet again, that in the absence of any international safeguards or strong government export controls, spyware vendors will sell to government clients who will routinely abuse their services. Many governments that are eager to acquire sophisticated surveillance technologies lack robust safeguards over their domestic and foreign security agencies. Many are characterized by poor human rights track records. It is not surprising that, in the absence of strong legal restraints, these types of government clients will misuse spyware services to track journalists, political opposition, human rights defenders, and other members of global civil society.&nbsp;</span></p>
<h3 class="lh-solid mb3"><b>Civil Society in the Crosshairs&hellip;</b><b><i>Again</i></b></h3>
<p class="mt0"><span style="font-weight: 400;">The apparent targeting of an individual because of their political beliefs and activities that are neither terrorist or criminal in nature is a troubling example of this dangerous situation. Microsoft&rsquo;s independent <a href="https://www.microsoft.com/security/blog/2021/07/15/protecting-customers-from-a-private-sector-offensive-actor-using-0-day-exploits-and-devilstongue-malware/" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener">analysis</a> is also disconcerting, discovering at least 100 victims of Candiru&rsquo;s malware operations that include &ldquo;politicians, human rights activists, journalists, academics, embassy workers and political dissidents.&rdquo;&nbsp;</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Equally disturbing in this regard is Candiru&rsquo;s registration of domains impersonating human rights NGOs (Amnesty International), legitimate social movements (Black Lives Matter), international health organizations (WHO), women&rsquo;s rights themes, and news organizations. Although we lack context around the specific use cases connected to these domains, their mere presence as part of Candiru&rsquo;s infrastructure&mdash;in light of widespread harms against civil society associated with the global spyware industry&mdash;is highly concerning and an area that merits further investigation.&nbsp;</span></p>
<h3 class="lh-solid mb3"><b>Rectifying Harms around the Commercial Spyware Market</b></h3>
<p class="mt0"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Ultimately, tackling the malpractices of the spyware industry will require a robust, </span><a href="https://www.lawfareblog.com/proposed-response-commercial-surveillance-emergency" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">comprehensive approach</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> that goes beyond efforts focused on a single high-profile company or country. Unfortunately, Israel&rsquo;s Ministry of Defense&mdash;from whom Israeli-based companies like Candiru must receive an export license before selling abroad&mdash;has so far proven itself unwilling to subject surveillance companies to the type of rigorous scrutiny that would be required to prevent abuses of the sort we and other organizations have identified. The export licensing process in that country is almost </span><a href="https://cpj.org/2021/06/israeli-lawyer-eitay-mack-transparency-surveillance-press-freedom/" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">entirely opaque</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">, lacking even the most basic measures of public accountability or transparency. It is our hope that reports such as this one will help spur policymakers and legislators in Israel and elsewhere to do more to prevent the mounting harms associated with an unregulated spyware marketplace.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">It is worth noting the growing risks that spyware vendors and their ownership groups themselves face as a result of their own reckless sales. Mercenary spyware vendors like Candiru market their services to their government clients as &ldquo;untraceable&rdquo; tools that evade detection and thus prevent their clients&rsquo; operations from being exposed. However, our research shows once again how specious these claims are. Although sometimes challenging, it is possible for researchers to detect and uncover targeted espionage using a variety of networking monitoring and other investigative techniques, as we have demonstrated in this report (and </span><a href="https://citizenlab.ca/2018/09/hide-and-seek-tracking-nso-groups-pegasus-spyware-to-operations-in-45-countries/" class="pointer"><span style="font-weight: 400;">others</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> like it). Even the most well-resourced surveillance companies make operational mistakes and leave digital traces, making their marketing claims about being stealthy and undetectable highly questionable. To the extent that their products are implicated in significant harms or cases of unlawful targeting, the negative exposure that comes from public interest research may create significant </span><a href="https://citizenlab.ca/2018/12/litigation-and-other-formal-complaints-concerning-targeted-digital-surveillance-and-the-digital-surveillance-industry/" class="pointer"><span style="font-weight: 400;">liabilities</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> for ownership, shareholders, and others associated with these spyware companies.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Finally, this case shows the value of a community-wide approach to investigations into targeted espionage. In order to remedy the harms generated by this industry for innocent members of global civil society, cooperation among academic researchers, network defenders, threat intelligence teams, and technology platforms is critical. Our research drew upon multiple data sources curated by other groups and entities with whom we cooperated, and ultimately helped identify software vulnerabilities in a widely used product that were reported to and then patched by its <a href="https://blogs.microsoft.com/on-the-issues/2021/07/15/cyberweapons-cybersecurity-sourgum-malware/" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener">vendor</a>.</span></p>
