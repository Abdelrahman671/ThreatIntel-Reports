<p>Mars Stealer is an information stealer sold on underground forums by <em>MarsTeam</em> since June 22, 2021, with the malware-as-a-service model. The malware capabilities are those of a classic stealer with a focus on cryptocurrency theft. As a quick summary, Mars Stealer is able to:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>collect data from several browsers (passwords, cookies, credit cards, <em>etc</em>.);</li>



<li>steal credentials from crypto plugins, crypto wallets and 2FA plugins;</li>



<li>grab files;</li>



<li>fingerprint the infected host.</li>
</ul>



<p>It shares code with other <a href="https://blog.sekoia.io/raccoon-stealer-v2-part-1-the-return-of-the-dead/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">information stealers</a> including Arkei, Oski and Vidar.&nbsp;</p>



<span id="more-212"></span>



<p>Given its interesting functionalities, its ease of use and reasonable price, the Mars Stealer malware has become popular on several underground forums. Moreover, the presumed developers regularly release new versions of the malware to fix some bugs and especially to improve the Mars Stealer capabilities in terms of data collection and defense evasion.</p>



<p>Mars Stealer has been recently brought to light by 3xp0rt&#8217;s in-depth analysis<sup><a href="https://3xp0rt.com/posts/mars-stealer" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">1</a></sup> and the release of a cracked version. In the blog post, 3xp0rt wrote an analysis of a Mars Stealer sample of an early version by exposing the different obfuscation methods and the data targeted by the malware on the infected hosts. A few days later, some members of the infosec community shared their findings on the builder and the administration panel of the information stealer. In recent days, some campaigns distributing Mars Stealer have been publicly described<sup><a href="https://blog.morphisec.com/threat-research-mars-stealer" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">2</a><a href="#link-3"> </a><a href="https://cert.gov.ua/article/38606" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">3</a></sup>.<br><br>SEKOIA.IO analysts have been monitoring the threat on underground forums to be up-to-date on the latest developments. We have also recently carried out an in-depth analysis of samples of different versions of Mars Stealer, and noticed many changes in the obfuscation techniques.</p>



<h1 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-why-investigate-the-mars-stealer-malware">Why investigate the Mars Stealer malware?</h1>



<p><a href="https://blog.sekoia.io/raccoon-stealer-v2-part-2-in-depth-analysis/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">Information stealers</a> are a threat to be considered, as many <a href="https://blog.sekoia.io/eternityteam-a-new-prominent-threat-group-on-underground-forums/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">threat actors</a> are using them to harvest credentials and other personal information. The stolen data can then be sold on underground forums and then possibly leveraged in &#8220;Big Game Hunting&#8221; operations, as the Lapsus$ threat group does<sup><a href="https://www.sekoia.io/en/lapsus-when-kiddies-play-in-the-big-league/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">4</a></sup>.</p>



<p>Among these information stealers, Mars Stealer has become an emerging threat in recent months.</p>



<p>First of all, the malware is widely advertised on numerous underground forums and the publications reach a large audience. Furthermore, users of Mars Stealer usually give good feedback and do not hesitate to recommend it when forum members are looking for an information stealer. The Mars Stealer malware therefore appeared in our Dark Web monitoring during the second half of 2021.&nbsp;</p>

<h1 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-a-journey-with-the-malware-developers">A journey with the malware developers</h1>



<p>In this part, we analyze the publications and the activities of the presumed Mars Stealer developers (<em>MarsTeam</em>) on underground forums. According to our Dark Web monitoring, <em>MarsTeam</em> is active on numerous underground forums including XSS.is, lolz.guru and bhf.io. However, we focus on the XSS forum as <em>MarsTeam</em> publishes first, and more frequently on this one.</p>



<p>On May 21, 2021, the Mars Stealer team joined the XSS underground forum under the name <em>MarsTeam</em> with a deposit of 0.009 Bitcoin ($336 at May 2021 exchange rate). One month later, <em>MarsTeam</em> opened a new discussion whose title translated from Russian is &#8220;Mars Stealer &#8211; a native, non-resident stealer with loader and stealer functionality&#8221;.</p>


<div class="wp-block-image">

<p>The publication introduces Mars Stealer as “<em>a new software developed for people working with crypto</em>” &#8211; to be interpreted: for people who want to steal cryptocurrencies. The malware is sold with the malware-as-a-service model for $140 per month.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-mars-stealer-capabilities">Mars Stealer capabilities</h2>



<p>As mentioned in the introduction, the Mars Stealer capabilities advertised by <em>MarsTeam</em> are those of a classic information stealer with a specific focus on cryptocurrency theft. Our technical analysis of Mars Stealer confirmed that the applications targeted by the malware samples are those described in the <em>MarsTeam</em> publications.</p>



<p>The stealer collects personal information from numerous browsers: passwords, cookies, credit cards, autofill data, history of websites visited and files downloaded. The list of supported browsers is quite wide, from the most popular (Google Chrome, Internet Explorer, Microsoft Edge, Firefox, <em>etc</em>.) to the less common. Mars Stealer collects this data in the default path of the different browser user data, or browser profiles.</p>



<p>The theft of cryptocurrencies is one of its distinguishing features, <em>MarsTeam </em>specifies that: “<em>Important feature that makes us stand out from competitors is the collection of browser plugins with an emphasis on cryptocurrency and 2FA plugins</em>” (translated from Russian). Indeed, the list of targeted crypto plugins is very long with more than 40 references, including the most used (Coinbase, MetaMask, Binance). The same goes for the list of crypto wallets and 2FA plugins targeted by the information stealer.</p>



<p>The malware also fingerprints the infected host to collect information about hardware, installed software and other personal information. Mars Stealer collects these data using WinAPI calls such as <em>GetSystemInfo</em>, <em>GetCurrentProcess</em> or by requesting the Windows Registry keys such as <em>HARDWARE\DESCRIPTION\System\CentralProcessor\0</em>, <em>SOFTWARE\Microsoft\Windows\CurrentVersion\Uninstall</em>.</p>



<p>Last but not least, Mars Stealer acts as a file grabber which is easily customizable by the operator. <em>MarsTeam</em> describes it as “<em>a powerful feature</em>” as the start path, file extension, file size and the recursive search can be set up. It also captures a screenshot of the victim&#8217;s desktop, loads files on the infected machine and executes them with arguments.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-high-quality-service-for-malware-operators">High-quality service for malware operators</h2>



<p>In addition to the stealing agent, Mars Stealer is sold with the administration panel and customer support. It is worth noting that the malware operator must host the Command &amp; Control (C2) server and associated panel on its own server to access the stolen data. <em>MarsTeam</em> insists on the fact that all the traffic is available only for the customer and does not pass through a server of the Mars Stealer developers. This functionality is often requested by information stealer users who want to control and own all the data.</p>



<p>In the first <em>MarsTeam</em>’s publication, the Mars Stealer administration panel is described as “<em>a powerful data search functionality</em>” and the user experience seems to be a priority in the development of Mars Stealer. <em>MarsTeam</em> describes multiple capabilities of the panel on which the operator can easily manage, sort, filter, and remove the logs.</p>



<p>Moreover, the purchase of Mars Stealer includes a high-quality service including the support of all issues, the access to the customer chat and the new releases of the malware.</p>



<p>All the malware capabilities accompanied by a user-friendly interface and quality support make Mars Stealer very attractive and popular to attackers on underground markets.</p>

<p>Let us consider the releases of new Mars Stealer versions on the XSS forum. <h1 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-how-to-collect-mars-stealer-iocs">How to collect Mars Stealer IoCs?</h1>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-collecting-mars-stealer-samples-nbsp">Collecting Mars Stealer samples&nbsp;</h2>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-early-versions">Early versions</h3>



<p>Before analyzing the different versions of the Mars Stealer malware, a first step consists of collecting the malware samples. We have therefore written a <a href="https://blog.sekoia.io/playbooks-yara-rules-iocs-explanation-about-the-news/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">YARA rule</a> based on the sample shared in the 3xp0rt analysis to identify other Mars Stealer samples.</p>



<p>A reliable method to detect samples of a malware family consists in searching for operation code (opcode) patterns used in the deobfuscation routine. In the early versions, the malware implemented a deobfuscation function which first decodes base64-encoded strings and then decrypts RC4-encrypted strings. This algorithm is applied on numerous obfuscated strings as shown in the following figure, which corresponds to the function loading obfuscated strings.</p>


<div class="wp-block-image">
<figure class="aligncenter"><img decoding="async" src="data:image/svg+xml,%3Csvg%20xmlns='http://www.w3.org/2000/svg'%20viewBox='0%200%200%200'%3E%3C/svg%3E" alt="Function loading obfuscated strings in a Mars Stealer early version sample" data-lazy-src="https://lh5.googleusercontent.com/Af10mx8r5I-f4WywZt9hfCmHQECYX5PCNPHU5xWbVK8wSozjo2qNFBTgxQsdsiobqTqBCUmBz9whlqWE24C8EEbGijxLutTNg4kmafMe4umLEeA4EF9q4T2dnqz58mUoVgtWZitb"/><noscript><img decoding="async" src="https://lh5.googleusercontent.com/Af10mx8r5I-f4WywZt9hfCmHQECYX5PCNPHU5xWbVK8wSozjo2qNFBTgxQsdsiobqTqBCUmBz9whlqWE24C8EEbGijxLutTNg4kmafMe4umLEeA4EF9q4T2dnqz58mUoVgtWZitb" alt="Function loading obfuscated strings in a Mars Stealer early version sample"/></noscript></figure></div>


<p class="has-text-align-center"><em>Figure 8. Function loading obfuscated strings in a Mars Stealer early version sample</em></p>



<p>Four instructions are repeated for each obfuscated data: <em>push</em>, <em>call, add</em> and <em>mov</em>. A YARA rule identifying this version of Mars Stealer can be written based on the repetition of these opcodes. Adding to the detection pattern some specific strings can fine-tune the rule in order to identify only Mars Stealer binaries. Here is a possible YARA rule to find Mars Stealer samples:</p>



<pre class="wp-block-code"><code>rule infostealer_win_mars_stealer_early_version {
    meta:
        description = "Identifies samples of Mars Stealer early version based on opcodes of the function loading obfuscated strings."
        source = "SEKOIA.IO"
        reference = "https://blog.sekoia.io/mars-a-red-hot-information-stealer/"
        classification = "TLP:WHITE"
        hash = "7da3029263bfbb0699119a715ce22a3941cf8100428fd43c9e1e46bf436ca687"

    strings:
        $dec = {a3 ?? ?? ?? ?? 68 ?? ?? ?? ?? e8 ?? ?? 00 00 83 c4 ??}

        $api00 = "LoadLibrary" ascii
        $api01 = "GetProcAddress" ascii
        $api02 = "ExitProcess" ascii
        $api03 = "advapi32.dll" ascii
        $api04 = "crypt32.dll" ascii
        $api05 = "GetTickCount" ascii
        $api06 = "Sleep" ascii
        $api07 = "GetUserDefaultLangID" ascii
        $api08 = "CreateMutex" ascii
        $api09 = "GetLastError" ascii
        $api10 = "HeapAlloc" ascii
        $api11 = "GetProcessHeap" ascii
        $api12 = "GetComputerName" ascii
        $api13 = "VirtualProtect" ascii
        $api14 = "GetUserName" ascii
        $api15 = "CryptStringToBinary" ascii

        $str0 = "JohnDoe" ascii

    condition:
        uint16(0)==0x5A4D and
        #dec &gt; 400 and 12 of ($api*) and $str0
}</code></pre>



<p class="has-text-align-center"><em>Figure 9. YARA rule identifying Mars Stealer early version samples</em></p>



<p>We shared the rule on sample sharing platforms and collected several results from this YARA rule. As expected from our Dark Web monitoring, we observed different Mars Stealer versions based on their deobfuscation routine among our collected samples. We also noticed several samples for which a new PE section name appeared: <em>LLCPPC</em>. More details on the different versions can be found in the following technical analysis.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-llcppc-versions">LLCPPC versions</h3>



<p>The PE section name <em>LLCPPC</em> is a highly discriminating factor of the Mars Stealer malware. We can therefore easily identify Mars Stealer samples using this characteristic in a YARA rule using the PE module:</p>



<pre class="wp-block-code"><code>import "pe"

rule infostealer_win_mars_stealer_llcppc {
    meta:
        description = "Identifies samples of Mars Stealer based on the PE section name LLCPPC."
        source = "SEKOIA.IO"
        reference = "https://blog.sekoia.io/mars-a-red-hot-information-stealer/"

<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-latest-version-version-8">Latest version (version 8)</h3>



<p>The LLCPPC section disappeared in the latest version of Mars Stealer, which corresponds to version 8 according to the <em>MarsTeam </em>releases on XSS. Indeed, samples of the latest Mars Stealer version only send one request to retrieve the DLLs (<em>freebl3.dll</em>, <em>mozglue.dll</em>, <em>msvcp140.dll</em>, <em>nss3.dll</em>, <em>softokn3.dll</em>, <em>sqlite3.dll</em> and <em>vcruntime140.dll</em>). These files are linked to legitimate third-party DLLs allowing Mars Stealer to collect data from the infected host. As written by <em>MarsTeam</em> in the version 8 release notes “<em>now the software makes one request after the request file, which contains all necessary libraries for correct work of the software</em>” (translated from Russian), it therefore corresponds to version 8 samples.</p>

<p>Our resulting YARA rule is:</p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-tracking-mars-stealer-c2-servers">Tracking Mars Stealer C2 servers</h2>



<p>Tracking servers used to host malware C2 servers or more widely adversary infrastructures is a proactive hunting approach we have intensively developed at SEKOIA.IO<sup><a href="https://blog.sekoia.io/centralization-of-edr-alerts-new-detections-and-trackers-the-novelties-of-november-2021/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">5</a></sup>. Concerning the Mars Stealer malware, C2 servers are hosted by the attackers and not by the malware developer. This makes it more difficult or even impossible to identify a heuristic based on the HTTP response to find the malware C2 servers, as each attacker should configure its own HTTP server hosting the Mars Stealer administration panel.</p>



<p>However, a good and simple method to track the widely sold malware consists in finding servers hosting one of the characteristic web pages of the administration panel. By searching the hash of these specific web pages (JavaScript, PHP or HTML pages) on URL scanning platforms, we can identify the servers used by the malware to download payloads and exfiltrate the stolen information.</p>



<p>Concerning the Mars Stealer administration panel, we can track several pages which are specific to the malware panel. For example:</p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-string-loading">String loading</h2>



<p>One of the first functions of the malware aims to load a small set of strings, which are used a bit later with the two known functions of <em><a href="https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/windows/win32/system-services?redirectedfrom=MSDN" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">Kernel32.dll</a></em>: <em><a href="https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/windows/win32/api/libloaderapi/nf-libloaderapi-loadlibrarya" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">LoadLibrary</a></em> and <em><a href="https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/windows/win32/api/libloaderapi/nf-libloaderapi-getprocaddress" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">GetProcAddress</a></em>. These two functions are used for further functions and library loading. In this same function (<em>load_string</em>), the first string loaded is twenty bytes long, which appears to be the key used to decrypt (RC4) strings of the malware.</p>



<p>Those obfuscated strings are stored in the <em>.data</em> section in this given format: encrypted string with RC4 (Rivest Cipher 4)<sup><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RC4" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">7</a></sup> algorithm stored in base64. Figure below shows how the RC4 key is loaded.</p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-a-new-section-entered-the-ring">A new section entered the ring</h2>



<p>As introduced in the section &#8220;Collecting Mars Stealer samples&#8221;, a new section with the singular name LLCPPC has been introduced in the Mars Stealer PE structure.</p>



<p>The structure of this section has evolved during the different releases. First of all, this section was composed of one segment that contains code used by the malware, and then authors add data, sometimes in clear text or obfuscated.</p>



<p>The new section of malware has the given structure:</p>

<p>In the previous LLCPPC section structure, the C2 URL is in clear text, that means it is not obfuscated and the XOR key is not used. However, in most samples, the C2 URL is obfuscated with the XOR key.</p>



<p>Three different versions of this section have been observed during our investigation. Data can be obfuscated with XOR operation or can be stored in clear text.</p>

            unxored += chr(c1 ^ c2)

    return unxored</code></pre>

<h5 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-llcppc-left-the-game-mars-stealer-v8">LLCPPC left the game: Mars Stealer V8</h5>

<li>Downloaded DLLs are now grouped in a single ZIP file named and fetched from the “/request” URL.</li>



<li>A new anti-analysis check is introduced.</li>
</ol>



<p>The C2 is obfuscated using the same techniques as described in the section &#8220;LLCPPC with embedded data&#8221;, but for this version, obfuscated data are located in the <em>.rdata</em> section. <h4 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-sekoia-io-interaction">SEKOIA.IO interaction</h4>



<p>Malware C2 loading and deobfuscation have been analyzed in their different versions and the behavior of the multiple versions are now identified. A FAME module<sup><a href="https://certsocietegenerale.github.io/fame/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">10</a></sup> to automatically extract Command and Control has been developed. This module uses the Python library r2pipe<sup><a href="https://www.radare.org/n/r2pipe.html" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">11</a></sup> to interact with the PE file.</p>



<p>Twice a week, one of our workers of the SEKOIA Malware Watcher project pulls samples from VirusTotal that match our YARA rule mentioned in the &#8220;Collecting Mars Stealer samples&#8221; section and submits these samples to our FAME instance. C2 are extracted via FAME and finally pushed  contextualized to SEKOIA.IO with the relationship indicating the Mars Stealer malware.</p>
