<h2>The veteran 80 Payload</h2>
<p>Best known as XOR.DDoS, this payload <a href="https://blog.malwaremustdie.org/2014/09/mmd-0028-2014-fuzzy-reversing-new-china.html" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noreferrer">has been around since 2014</a>. The core part of the malware is comparatively simple. It connects to one of the hard coded control servers, sends server metadata – CPU, memory and network information – and receives commands.</p>
<p>It can receive the following commands</p>
<ul>
<li>Stop DDoS campaign</li>
<li>Start DDoS campaign</li>
<li>In this case, the server also sends a target IP, port and attack type</li>
<li>Send system data – A list</li>
<li>Download and execute file</li>
<li>Download file and execute in place – used for upgrades</li>
<li>Update configuration</li>
</ul>
<p>The XOR.DDoS payload can perform three types of DDoS attacks:</p>
<ul>
<li>Send a flood of DNS packets as part of a DNS amplification attack</li>
<li>Send a flood of TCP SYN packets</li>
<li>Send a flood of TCP ACK packets</li>
</ul>
<h2>Detection and prevention</h2>
<p>The butter attacks are entirely driven by brute forcing SSH credentials, meaning that any device with weak credentials is vulnerable. This can include unmaintained servers and appliances with hard-coded credentials. Defenders should focus on locking down systems and pay attention to machine credentials policies to block weak passwords.</p>
<p>Since breaches are often inevitable, <a href="/our-thinking/microsegmentation" target="_self">consider segmenting</a> and monitoring your network to minimize harm and avoid breaches from weak machines. Routinely review who and what can access the servers. Monitoring outbound connections could easily uncover compromised devices communicating with cryptocurrency mining pools.</p>
