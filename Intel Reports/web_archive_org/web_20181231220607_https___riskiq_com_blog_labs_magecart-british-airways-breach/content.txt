		<header>
			<h1>Inside the Magecart Breach of British Airways: How 22 Lines of Code Claimed 380,000 Victims</h1>
            <hr/>
			<p class="Subtitle">September 11, 2018, Yonathan Klijnsma</p>
		</header>
	</div>

</section>

<div class="MainContent Blog">
	<div class="Blog-Author">
		<img src="https://web.archive.org/web/20181231220607im_/https://secure.gravatar.com/avatar/8962066e13d7841f7c8b61403d874cf8?s=96&amp;d=https%3A%2F%2Fwww.riskiq.com%2Fwp-content%2Fuploads%2F2018%2F07%2Fblog-default-user-picture.png&amp;r=g" width="96" height="96" alt="" class="avatar avatar-96 wp-user-avatar wp-user-avatar-96 photo avatar-default"/>	</div>
	<section class="MainContent-section-- Section--">

		<div class="Blog-Content">
			<div class="wrap--narrow">
				<div class="at-above-post addthis_tool" data-url="https://web.archive.org/web/20181231220607oe_/https://www.riskiq.com/blog/labs/magecart-british-airways-breach/"></div><p><span style="font-weight: 400;">On September 6th, </span><a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20181231220607/https://www.britishairways.com/en-gb/information/incident/data-theft/latest-information"><span style="font-weight: 400;">British Airways announced it had suffered a breach</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> resulting in the theft of customer data. </span><a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20181231220607/https://www.bbc.co.uk/news/uk-england-london-45440850"><span style="font-weight: 400;">In interviews with the BBC</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">, the company noted that around 380,000 customers could have been affected and that the stolen information included personal and payment information but not passport information. </span><span style="font-weight: 400;"><br/>
</span><span style="font-weight: 400;"><br/>
</span><span style="font-weight: 400;">On its website, British Airways placed an article explaining details of the incident that answered as many questions as possible for customers. The technical details were sparse but included the following pieces of information:</span></p>
<ul>
<li style="font-weight: 400;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Payments through its main website were affected</span></li>
<li style="font-weight: 400;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Payments through its mobile app were affected</span></li>
<li style="font-weight: 400;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Payments were affected from 22:58 BST August 21, 2018, until 21:45 BST September 5, 2018</span></li>
</ul>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">The report also stated very clearly that information was stolen from the British Airways website and mobile app but did not mention breaches of anything else, namely databases or servers—anything indicating the breach affected more than the payment information entered into the website. Because these reports only cover customer data stolen directly from payment forms, we immediately suspected one group: Magecart. </span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">The same type of attack happened recently when Ticketmaster UK reported a breach, after which RiskIQ found the entire trail of the incident. Because we crawl the internet and capture the details of each page, our team was able to expand the timeline and discover more affected websites beyond what was publicly reported. In this blog, we&#8217;ll investigate what happened during the breach of British Airways customer data made public on September 6, which spanned a total of 15 days according to public reporting.</span></p>
<h4><span style="font-weight: 400;">Magecart: A Familiar Adversary </span></h4>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Since 2016, RiskIQ has reported on the use of web-based card skimmers operated by the threat group Magecart. Traditionally, criminals use devices known as card skimmers—devices hidden within credit card readers on ATMs, fuel pumps, and other machines people pay for with credit cards every day—to steal credit card data for the criminal to later collect and either use themselves or sell to other parties. Magecart uses a digital variety of these devices.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Magecart injects scripts designed to steal sensitive data that consumers enter into online payment forms on e-commerce websites directly or through compromised third-party suppliers used by these sites. Recently, Magecart operatives placed one of these digital skimmers on Ticketmaster websites through the compromise of a third-party functionality resulting in a high-profile breach of Ticketmaster customer data. Based on recent evidence, Magecart has now set their sights on British Airways, the largest airline in the UK. </span></p>
<h4><span style="font-weight: 400;">Finding the Breach of British Airways</span></h4>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Our first step in linking Magecart to the attack on British Airways was simply going through our Magecart detection hits. Seeing instances of Magecart is so common for us that we get at least hourly alerts for websites getting compromised with their skimmer-code. Customer notifications through our products are automated, but our research team searches for any instances outside of these workspaces manually and adds them to our global blacklists. In the case of the British Airways breach, we had no hits in our blacklist incidents or suspects because the Magecart actors customized their skimmer in this case.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">One challenge of digging through crawl data manually is the scale of the data RiskIQ collects. We crawl more than two billion pages a day, which accumulates over time. Another is that modern websites tend to run with a lot of functionality built out in JavaScript. Just loading the main British Airways website spins up around 20 different scripts and loading the booking subpage bumps that to 30. While 30 scripts might not sound like much, many of these are minified scripts spanning thousands of lines of script.</span><span style="font-weight: 400;"><br/>
</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">For this research, we decided to focus our efforts by identifying individual scripts on the British Airways website and examining their appearance over time—we would verify all the unique scripts on the website and only look at them again if their appearance changed in our crawling. Eventually, we recorded a change in one of the scripts. Opening up the crawl, we saw this script was a modified version of the Modernizr JavaScript library, version 2.6.2 to be precise. The script was loaded from the baggage claim information page on the British Airways website:</span></p>
<div id="attachment_17057" style="width: 810px" class="wp-caption alignnone"><img class="wp-image-17057 size-full" src="https://web.archive.org/web/20181231220607im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/09/Webp.net-resizeimage-20.png" alt="RiskIQ data shows Magecart was behind the British Airways breach by compromising javascript on the airline's website with an extremely targeted attack." width="800" height="200" srcset="https://web.archive.org/web/20181231220607im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/09/Webp.net-resizeimage-20.png 800w, https://web.archive.org/web/20181231220607im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/09/Webp.net-resizeimage-20-300x75.png 300w, https://web.archive.org/web/20181231220607im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/09/Webp.net-resizeimage-20-768x192.png 768w, https://web.archive.org/web/20181231220607im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/09/Webp.net-resizeimage-20-500x125.png 500w" sizes="(max-width: 800px) 100vw, 800px"/><p class="wp-caption-text">Fig-1 Modified script</p></div>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">The noted change was at the bottom of the script, a technique we often see when attackers modify JavaScript files to not break functionality. The small script tag at the bottom immediately raised our suspicions:</span></p>
<div id="attachment_17058" style="width: 810px" class="wp-caption alignnone"><img class="wp-image-17058 size-full" src="https://web.archive.org/web/20181231220607im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/09/Webp.net-resizeimage-22.png" alt="RiskIQ data shows Magecart was behind the British Airways breach by compromising javascript on the airline's website with an extremely targeted attack." width="800" height="488" srcset="https://web.archive.org/web/20181231220607im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/09/Webp.net-resizeimage-22.png 800w, https://web.archive.org/web/20181231220607im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/09/Webp.net-resizeimage-22-300x183.png 300w, https://web.archive.org/web/20181231220607im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/09/Webp.net-resizeimage-22-768x468.png 768w, https://web.archive.org/web/20181231220607im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/09/Webp.net-resizeimage-22-500x305.png 500w" sizes="(max-width: 800px) 100vw, 800px"/><p class="wp-caption-text">Fig-2 The suspicious script tag added by Magecart</p></div>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">We found more evidence in the server headers sent by the British Airways server. The servers send a ‘</span><span style="font-weight: 400;">Last-Modified</span><span style="font-weight: 400;">’ header, which indicates the last time a piece of static content was modified. The clean version of the Modernizr script had a timestamp from December 2012:</span></p>
<div id="attachment_17060" style="width: 810px" class="wp-caption alignnone"><img class="wp-image-17060 size-full" src="https://web.archive.org/web/20181231220607im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/09/Webp.net-resizeimage-24.png" alt="RiskIQ data shows Magecart was behind the British Airways breach by compromising javascript on the airline's website with an extremely targeted attack." width="800" height="215" srcset="https://web.archive.org/web/20181231220607im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/09/Webp.net-resizeimage-24.png 800w, https://web.archive.org/web/20181231220607im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/09/Webp.net-resizeimage-24-300x81.png 300w, https://web.archive.org/web/20181231220607im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/09/Webp.net-resizeimage-24-768x206.png 768w, https://web.archive.org/web/20181231220607im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/09/Webp.net-resizeimage-24-500x134.png 500w" sizes="(max-width: 800px) 100vw, 800px"/><p class="wp-caption-text">Fig-3 Clean version of the compromised script</p></div>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">We can see on the modified, malicious version of Modernizr the timestamp matches closely to the timestamp given by British Airways as the beginning of people getting victimized:</span></p>
<div id="attachment_17059" style="width: 810px" class="wp-caption alignnone"><img class="wp-image-17059 size-full" src="https://web.archive.org/web/20181231220607im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/09/Webp.net-resizeimage-21.png" alt="" width="800" height="216" srcset="https://web.archive.org/web/20181231220607im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/09/Webp.net-resizeimage-21.png 800w, https://web.archive.org/web/20181231220607im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/09/Webp.net-resizeimage-21-300x81.png 300w, https://web.archive.org/web/20181231220607im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/09/Webp.net-resizeimage-21-768x207.png 768w, https://web.archive.org/web/20181231220607im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/09/Webp.net-resizeimage-21-500x135.png 500w" sizes="(max-width: 800px) 100vw, 800px"/><p class="wp-caption-text">Fig-4 Timestamp of when the skimming began</p></div>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Here is a cleaned up version of the script, only 22 lines of JavaScript:</span></p>
<div id="attachment_17061" style="width: 810px" class="wp-caption alignnone"><img class="wp-image-17061 size-full" src="https://web.archive.org/web/20181231220607im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/09/Webp.net-resizeimage-26.png" alt="RiskIQ data shows Magecart was behind the British Airways breach by compromising javascript on the airline's website with an extremely targeted attack." width="800" height="442" srcset="https://web.archive.org/web/20181231220607im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/09/Webp.net-resizeimage-26.png 800w, https://web.archive.org/web/20181231220607im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/09/Webp.net-resizeimage-26-300x166.png 300w, https://web.archive.org/web/20181231220607im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/09/Webp.net-resizeimage-26-768x424.png 768w, https://web.archive.org/web/20181231220607im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/09/Webp.net-resizeimage-26-500x276.png 500w" sizes="(max-width: 800px) 100vw, 800px"/><p class="wp-caption-text">Fig-5 Only 22 lines of script victimized 380,000 people</p></div>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">In essence, the script is very simple and very effective. Here is a breakdown of what it does:</span></p>
<ul>
<li style="font-weight: 400;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Once every element on the page finishes loading it will:</span>
<ul>
<li style="font-weight: 400;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Bind the </span><em><span style="font-weight: 400;">mouseup</span></em><span style="font-weight: 400;"> and </span><em><span style="font-weight: 400;">touchend</span></em><span style="font-weight: 400;"> events on a button known as </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">submitButton</span><span style="font-weight: 400;"> with the following callback-code:</span>
<ul>
<li style="font-weight: 400;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Serialize the data in a form with id </span><em><span style="font-weight: 400;">paymentForm</span></em><span style="font-weight: 400;"> into a dictionary</span></li>
<li style="font-weight: 400;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Serialize an item on the page with id </span><em><span style="font-weight: 400;">personPaying</span></em><span style="font-weight: 400;"> into the same dictionary as the </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">paymentForm</span><span style="font-weight: 400;"> information</span></li>
<li style="font-weight: 400;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Make a text-string out of this serialized data</span></li>
<li style="font-weight: 400;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Send the data in the form of JSON to a server hosted on </span><em><span style="font-weight: 400;">baways.com</span></em></li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">On websites, </span><em><span style="font-weight: 400;">mouseup</span></em><span style="font-weight: 400;"> and </span><em><span style="font-weight: 400;">touchend</span></em><span style="font-weight: 400;">, are events for when someone lets go of the mouse after clicking on a button or when someone on a touchscreen (mobile) device lets go of the screen after pushing a button. This means that once a user hits the button to submit their payment on the compromised British Airways site, the information from the payment form is extracted along with their name and sent to the attacker’s server.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">This attack is a simple but highly targeted approach compared to what we’ve seen in the past with the Magecart skimmer which grabbed forms indiscriminately. This particular skimmer is very much attuned to how British Airway’s payment page is set up, which tells us that the attackers carefully considered how to target this site instead of blindly injecting the regular Magecart skimmer.</span><span style="font-weight: 400;"><br/>
</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">The infrastructure used in this attack was set up only with British Airways in mind and purposely targeted scripts that would blend in with normal payment processing to avoid detection. We saw proof of this on the domain name </span><a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20181231220607/https://community.riskiq.com/search/baways.com"><span style="font-weight: 400;">baways.com</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> as well as the drop server path. The domain was hosted on </span><a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20181231220607/https://community.riskiq.com/search/89.47.162.248"><span style="font-weight: 400;">89.47.162.248</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> which is located in Romania and is, in fact, part of a VPS provider named Time4VPS based in Lithuania. The actors also loaded the server with an SSL certificate. Interestingly, they decided to go with a paid certificate from Comodo instead of a free LetsEncrypt certificate, likely to make it appear like a legitimate server:</span></p>
<div id="attachment_17062" style="width: 810px" class="wp-caption alignnone"><img class="wp-image-17062 size-full" src="https://web.archive.org/web/20181231220607im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/09/Webp.net-resizeimage-19.png" alt="RiskIQ data shows Magecart was behind the British Airways breach by compromising javascript on the airline's website with an extremely targeted attack." width="800" height="633" srcset="https://web.archive.org/web/20181231220607im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/09/Webp.net-resizeimage-19.png 800w, https://web.archive.org/web/20181231220607im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/09/Webp.net-resizeimage-19-300x237.png 300w, https://web.archive.org/web/20181231220607im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/09/Webp.net-resizeimage-19-768x608.png 768w, https://web.archive.org/web/20181231220607im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/09/Webp.net-resizeimage-19-500x396.png 500w" sizes="(max-width: 800px) 100vw, 800px"/><p class="wp-caption-text">Fig-6 Cert leveraged by the attackers</p></div>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Source: </span><a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20181231220607/https://community.riskiq.com/search/certificate/sha1/e1a181db8f8366660840e0b490ad2da43c78205a"><span style="font-weight: 400;">https://community.riskiq.com/search/certificate/sha1/e1a181db8f8366660840e0b490ad2da43c78205a</span></a></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">What is interesting to note from the certificate the Magecart actors used is that it was issued on August 15th, which indicates they likely had access to the British Airways site before the reported start date of the attack on August 21st—possibly long before. Without visibility into its Internet-facing web assets, British Airways were not able to detect this compromise before it was too late.</span></p>
<h4><span style="font-weight: 400;">Mobile Skimming</span></h4>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">In the security advisory from British Airways, the company made note that both the web app and the mobile app users were affected. We found the skimmer on the webpage for British Airways, but how does that translate to mobile? To figure this out we’ll look at the British Airways Android application:</span></p>
<div id="attachment_17063" style="width: 810px" class="wp-caption alignnone"><img class="wp-image-17063 size-full" src="https://web.archive.org/web/20181231220607im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/09/Webp.net-resizeimage-23.png" alt="RiskIQ data shows Magecart was behind the British Airways breach by compromising javascript on the airline's website with an extremely targeted attack." width="800" height="650" srcset="https://web.archive.org/web/20181231220607im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/09/Webp.net-resizeimage-23.png 800w, https://web.archive.org/web/20181231220607im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/09/Webp.net-resizeimage-23-300x244.png 300w, https://web.archive.org/web/20181231220607im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/09/Webp.net-resizeimage-23-768x624.png 768w, https://web.archive.org/web/20181231220607im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/09/Webp.net-resizeimage-23-500x406.png 500w" sizes="(max-width: 800px) 100vw, 800px"/><p class="wp-caption-text">Fig-7 British Airways mobile application</p></div>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Often, when developers build a mobile app, they make an empty shell and load content from elsewhere. In the case of British Airways, a portion of the app is native but the majority of its functionality loads from web pages from the official British Airways website.</span><span style="font-weight: 400;"><br/>
</span><span style="font-weight: 400;"><br/>
</span><span style="font-weight: 400;">The mobile app uses a set of different hosts to communicate back to the British Airways servers:</span><span style="font-weight: 400;"><br/>
</span></p>
<ul>
<li style="font-weight: 400;"><em><span style="font-weight: 400;">www.britishairways.com</span></em><span style="font-weight: 400;"> (The main website)</span></li>
<li style="font-weight: 400;"><em><span style="font-weight: 400;">api4-prl.baplc.com</span></em><span style="font-weight: 400;"> (An API endpoint from British Airways)</span></li>
<li style="font-weight: 400;"><em><span style="font-weight: 400;">api4.baplc.com</span></em><span style="font-weight: 400;"> (Another API endpoint from British Airways)</span></li>
</ul>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">The idea is that for quick data updates on its UI the app uses the API endpoints, but for searching, booking, and managing flights the app loads a mobile version of the main website. One of these called-up paths is:</span></p>
<p><em><span style="font-weight: 400;">www.britishairways.com/travel/ba_vsg17.jsp/seccharge/public/en_gb</span></em></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">This page is loaded when the customer requests information about fees for different countries and airports. It looks like this:</span></p>
<div id="attachment_17064" style="width: 810px" class="wp-caption alignnone"><img class="wp-image-17064 size-full" src="https://web.archive.org/web/20181231220607im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/09/Webp.net-resizeimage-25.png" alt="RiskIQ data shows Magecart was behind the British Airways breach by compromising javascript on the airline's website with an extremely targeted attack." width="800" height="849" srcset="https://web.archive.org/web/20181231220607im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/09/Webp.net-resizeimage-25.png 800w, https://web.archive.org/web/20181231220607im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/09/Webp.net-resizeimage-25-283x300.png 283w, https://web.archive.org/web/20181231220607im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/09/Webp.net-resizeimage-25-768x815.png 768w, https://web.archive.org/web/20181231220607im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/09/Webp.net-resizeimage-25-471x500.png 471w" sizes="(max-width: 800px) 100vw, 800px"/><p class="wp-caption-text">Fig-8 Magecart-compromised mobile web page</p></div>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Now, if we look at the source of this webpage we find something interesting—the page is built with the same CSS and JavaScript components as the real website, meaning design and functionality-wise, it’s a total match. From what we examined above, we know that this means we’ll also find our offending script—the one that steals name and payment information from the web app—on the mobile app:</span></p>
<div id="attachment_17065" style="width: 810px" class="wp-caption alignnone"><img class="wp-image-17065 size-full" src="https://web.archive.org/web/20181231220607im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/09/Webp.net-resizeimage-18.png" alt="RiskIQ data shows Magecart was behind the British Airways breach by compromising javascript on the airline's website with an extremely targeted attack." width="800" height="177" srcset="https://web.archive.org/web/20181231220607im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/09/Webp.net-resizeimage-18.png 800w, https://web.archive.org/web/20181231220607im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/09/Webp.net-resizeimage-18-300x66.png 300w, https://web.archive.org/web/20181231220607im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/09/Webp.net-resizeimage-18-768x170.png 768w, https://web.archive.org/web/20181231220607im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/09/Webp.net-resizeimage-18-500x111.png 500w" sizes="(max-width: 800px) 100vw, 800px"/><p class="wp-caption-text">Fig-9 Source of the mobile web page</p></div>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Our crawler captured the subresource being loaded by the page used in the mobile app; it loads the same (at the time) compromised Modernizr JavaScript library!</span><span style="font-weight: 400;"><br/>
</span><span style="font-weight: 400;"><br/>
</span><span style="font-weight: 400;">One thing to note is that the magecart actor(s) put in the </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">touchend </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">callback in the skimmer to make it work for mobile visitors as well, which again shows us the high level of planning and attention to detail displayed in this simple yet extremely effective attack. </span></p>
<h4><span style="font-weight: 400;">Conclusions</span></h4>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">As we’ve seen in this attack, Magecart set up custom, targeted infrastructure to blend in with the British Airways website specifically and avoid detection for as long as possible. While we can never know how much reach the attackers had on the British Airways servers, the fact that they were able to modify a resource for the site tells us the access was substantial, and the fact they likely had access long before the attack even started is a stark reminder about the vulnerability of web-facing assets</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">RiskIQ has been warning the market about Magecart attacks like this since 2015 and will continue to follow and report on the group as it evolves. While the Magecart attack against British Airways wasn’t a compromise of a third-party supplier like the attack on Ticketmaster, it does raise the question of payment form security. Companies, especially those that collect sensitive financial data, must realize that they should consider the security of their forms—but also the controls that influence what happens to payment information once a customer submits it. </span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">We suggest British Airways customers get a new card from their bank. Some banks have already been proactively issuing new cards for their customers, Monzo is an example of these:</span></p>
<div id="attachment_17066" style="width: 810px" class="wp-caption alignnone"><img class="wp-image-17066 size-full" src="https://web.archive.org/web/20181231220607im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/09/Webp.net-resizeimage-17.png" alt="RiskIQ data shows Magecart was behind the British Airways breach by compromising javascript on the airline's website with an extremely targeted attack." width="800" height="821" srcset="https://web.archive.org/web/20181231220607im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/09/Webp.net-resizeimage-17.png 800w, https://web.archive.org/web/20181231220607im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/09/Webp.net-resizeimage-17-292x300.png 292w, https://web.archive.org/web/20181231220607im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/09/Webp.net-resizeimage-17-768x788.png 768w, https://web.archive.org/web/20181231220607im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/09/Webp.net-resizeimage-17-487x500.png 487w" sizes="(max-width: 800px) 100vw, 800px"/><p class="wp-caption-text">Fig-10 How some banks are responding</p></div>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Source: </span><a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20181231220607/https://twitter.com/monzo/status/1038042015286607872"><span style="font-weight: 400;">https://twitter.com/monzo/status/1038042015286607872</span></a></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Magecart is an active threat that operates at a scale and breadth that rivals—or possibly surpasses—the recent compromises of point-of-sale systems of retail giants such as Home Depot and Target. The Magecart actors have been active since 2015 and have never retreated from their chosen criminal activity. Instead, they have continually refined their tactics and targets to maximize the return on their efforts.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Over time, they&#8217;ve optimized their tactics culminating in successful breaches of third-party providers such as Inbenta resulting in the theft of Ticketmaster customer data. We&#8217;re now seeing them target specific brands, crafting their attacks to match the functionality of specific sites, which we saw in the breach of British Airways. There will be more Magecart attacks, and RiskIQ will be tracking them and keeping the cybersecurity industry aware of our research.</span></p>
<p>For a deep dive on Magecart, from the group&#8217;s inception to its hack of Ticketmaster to its latest hack of British Airways, <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20181231220607/https://www.riskiq.com/webinar/inside-and-beyond-ticketmaster-webinar/?utm_campaign=magecart_webinar&amp;utm_source=blog&amp;utm_medium=blog&amp;utm_content=magecart_webinar_ba_blog">be sure to register for the webinar</a> hosted by RiskIQ Head Researcher and report author Yonathan Klijnsma.</p>
