					<div class="text border-bottom">
						<p>In the past few months, a new wave of cyberattacks has been flooding Iran. These attacks are far from minor website defacements – the recent wave is hitting national infrastructure and causing major disruptions to public services.</p>
<p>This article provides an in-depth technical analysis of one of the attacks against the Iranian national media corporation, Islamic Republic of Iran Broadcasting (IRIB) which occurred in late January 2022.</p>
<h1><strong>Key findings</strong></h1>
<ul>
<li>On January 27, Iranian state broadcaster IRIB became the subject of a targeted cyberattack that resulted in several state-run TV channels broadcasting footage of opposition leaders and calling for the assassination of the supreme leader. Check Point Research team investigated this attack and was able to retrieve the files and forensics evidence related to the incident from publicly available resources.</li>
<li>We found malicious executables whose purpose was to air the protest message, in addition, we discovered evidence that a wiper malware was used. This indicates that the attackers’ aim was also to disrupt the state’s broadcasting networks, with the damage to the TV and radio networks possibly more serious than officially reported.</li>
<li>Among the tools used in the attack, we identified malware that takes screenshots of the victims’ screens, several custom-made backdoors, and related batch scripts and configuration files used to install and configure the malicious executables. We could not find any evidence that these tools were used previously, or attribute them to a specific threat actor.</li>
<li>In this article, we provide a technical analysis of the tools related to the attack, as well as the attackers’ tactics.</li>
</ul>
<h1><strong>Background</strong></h1>
<h2>Cyberattacks Hit Iran</h2>
<p>In July 2021, an attack <a href="https://www.reuters.com/world/middle-east/hackers-breach-iran-rail-network-disrupt-service-2021-07-09/">hit</a> the Iranian national railway and cargo services, and caused “unprecedented disruptions” to the country’s trains. Just a day later, media outlets <a href="https://www.reuters.com/world/middle-east/iran-transport-ministry-hit-by-second-apparent-cyberattack-days-2021-07-10/">reported</a> that the website of Iran&#8217;s Ministry of Roads and Urban Development, in charge of transportation, was taken down in a ‘cyber disruption’, preventing access to their official portal and sub-services. As if forcing railway employees to update the train schedule manually – across all train stations – wasn’t enough, the message displayed on the train schedule boards referred perplexed passengers to the Supreme Leader’s office phone number. The previously unknown group called ‘Predatory Sparrow’ quickly <a href="https://kayhan.london/fa/1400/04/18/%D8%AD%D9%85%D9%84%D9%87-%D8%B3%D8%A7%DB%8C%D8%A8%D8%B1%DB%8C-%D8%A8%D9%87-%D8%B3%DB%8C%D8%B3%D8%AA%D9%85%E2%80%8C%D9%87%D8%A7%DB%8C-%DA%A9%D8%A7%D9%85%D9%BE%DB%8C%D9%88%D8%AA%D8%B1%DB%8C-%D8%B4%D8%B1">claimed</a> responsibility for the attacks. Besides that, Check Point Research <a href="https://research.checkpoint.com/2021/indra-hackers-behind-recent-attacks-on-iran/">investigated</a> these attacks and the tools they deployed, and found similar tactics and techniques were used in previous operations against private companies in Syria, linking all of those attacks to anti-regime group called Indra.</p>
<p>Since then, cyber-attacks continue to hit national Iranian entities. Inspecting the targets, it appears that each one was carefully selected to send a tailored message. In August 2021, the hacktivist group Tapandegan, previously known for <a href="https://www.seattletimes.com/nation-world/hackers-briefly-shut-off-airport-monitors-in-irans-tabriz/">hacking</a> and <a href="https://en.radiofarda.com/a/iran-hackers-post-protest-messages-mashad-airport/29250247.html">displaying</a> protest messages on the electronic flight arrival and departure boards in the Mashad and Tabriz international airports in 2018, <a href="https://old.iranintl.com/en/iran/hackers-release-security-footage-iran%E2%80%99s-evin-prison">released</a> security camera footage from the Evin prison, a Tehran facility in which many political prisoners are held. The videos, which show prisoner abuse, were acquired by a group called Edalat-e Ali (‘Ali’s justice’) in protest against human rights violations. In October 2021, every gas station in Iran was <a href="https://www.npr.org/2021/10/27/1049566231/irans-president-says-cyberattack-was-meant-to-create-disorder-at-gas-pumps">paralyzed</a> by an attack that disrupted the electronic payment process. The incident led to extremely long queues at gas stations for two days and prevented customers from paying with the government-issued electronic cards used to purchase subsidized fuel. When the card was swiped for payment, the Supreme Leader office phone number appeared on the screen, taunting the highest ranking office in the regime yet again. Iranian officials <a href="https://www.reuters.com/business/energy/iran-says-israel-us-likely-behind-cyberattack-gas-stations-2021-10-30/">claimed</a> that foreign actors, such as Israel and the US, were behind the attack. However, Predatory Sparrow <a href="https://www.bbc.com/news/world-middle-east-59062907">claimed</a> responsibility for this attack as well.</p>
<p>In November 2021, Iranian airline Mahan Air <a href="https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2021-11-21/iran-s-mahan-air-says-it-foiled-cyber-attack-on-systems">announced</a> that it foiled an attempted attack against its internal systems, with no harm done. Curiously, this time a group called ‘Hooshyaran-e Vatan’ (Vigilant of the Nation) <a href="https://www.jpost.com/breaking-news/irans-mahan-air-hit-by-cyberattack-685575">claimed</a> responsibility, and over the next two months published documents allegedly stolen in the hack that link the airline to the IRGC (Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps).</p>
<p>Recently, on February 7, 2022, the Edalat-e Ali group <a href="https://www.iranintl.com/en/202202075594">released</a> footage from closed-circuit cameras in another Iranian prison, Ghezel Hesar.</p>
<p><img fetchpriority="high" decoding="async" class="wp-image-25820 size-full aligncenter" src="//research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/02/1-timeline-e1645129945172_v2.png" alt="" width="1005" height="601" srcset="https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/02/1-timeline-e1645129945172_v2.png 1005w, https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/02/1-timeline-e1645129945172_v2-300x179.png 300w, https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/02/1-timeline-e1645129945172_v2-768x459.png 768w" sizes="(max-width: 1005px) 100vw, 1005px" /></p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><strong>Figure 1 &#8211; Timeline of latest cyberattacks in Iran.</strong></p>
<h2>The Voice and Vision of the Islamic Republic of Iran</h2>
<p>On January 27, only two weeks before the anniversary of the 1979 Islamic Revolution, reports were <a href="https://www.reuters.com/world/middle-east/irans-state-broadcaster-says-it-was-hacked-10-seconds-2022-01-27/">published</a> that the IRIB, Iran’s national broadcaster, was hacked. The Islamic Republic of Iran Broadcasting, also called ‘The Voice and Vision of the Islamic Republic of Iran’, is a state-operated monopoly in charge of all radio and television services in Iran. The cyberattack resulted in state-run TV channels broadcasting what was described by IRIB officials as “the faces and voices of hypocrites.”</p>
<p>‘Hypocrites’ is a term used by the Iranian regime to refer to the Mujahedin-e-Khalq (MEK, also called the People&#8217;s Mujahedin of Iran), an exiled militant organization and the biggest political opposition group, which <a href="https://www.theguardian.com/news/2018/nov/09/mek-iran-revolution-regime-trump-rajavi">advocates</a> overthrowing the current regime and installing its own government, relying on an alternative interpretation of Islam. In the hijacked video, the faces of MEK leaders Maryam and Masoud Rajavi appeared, followed by the image of Ayatollah Khamenei crossed out with red lines and  the declaration “<em>Salute to Rajavi, death to (Supreme Leader) Khamenei!.</em>” The deputy head of technical affairs for IRIB, Reza Alidadi, stated that “only the owners of the technology in use by the corporation would have been able to carry out an attack relying on the system features installed on the systems and the exploited backdoor.” He further stated that similar attacks have hit other state-operated radio channels.</p>
<p><a href="https://research.checkpoint.com/2022/evilplayout-attack-against-irans-state-broadcaster/2-video_frame/" rel="attachment wp-att-25803"><img decoding="async" width="608" height="480" class="size-full wp-image-25803 aligncenter" src="//research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/02/2-video_frame.png" alt="" srcset="https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/02/2-video_frame.png 608w, https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/02/2-video_frame-300x237.png 300w" sizes="(max-width: 608px) 100vw, 608px" /></a></p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><strong>Figure 2 &#8211; Frame from the video with the opposition leaders’ faces broadcast by state-run Iranian TV channels as a result of the cyber attack.</strong></p>
<p>Although not the part of this investigation, it is worth mentioning that several days later, on February 1, the web-based streaming platform of IRIB, Telewebion, was <a href="https://www.iranintl.com/en/202202012324">hijacked</a> yet again to broadcast protest messages <a href="https://www.rferl.org/a/iran-hackers-dissident-message-tv/31683161.html">urging</a> citizens to rise up against the Supreme Leader and stating that “the regime’s foundations are rattling”. Cleverly, the incident took place in the middle of a live broadcast of the Iran-UAE soccer match. This time, politically motivated group Edalat-e Ali, responsible for the attacks targeting prison facilities’ security cameras, <a href="https://twitter.com/EdaalateAli1400/status/1488561598583218178">claimed</a> responsibility. This claim is plausible, as the video broadcast during the hack <a href="https://twitter.com/EdaalateAli1400/status/1488548696119824389">features</a> the group’s logo on the top left corner.</p>
<h1><strong>IRIB attack artifacts</strong></h1>
<p>According to Iranian state-run news network <a href="https://t.me/akharinkhabar/301980">Akharin Khabar</a> (Latest News), “the technical and broadcasting systems are completely isolated, they are equipped with acceptable security protocols and are not accessible via the Internet.” In the same post, it was reported that security forces associated with the regime’s state broadcasting network considered sabotage as the most likely scenario, with the Iranian officials <a href="https://english.alarabiya.net/News/middle-east/2022/01/27/Iran-s-state-broadcaster-says-it-was-hacked-for-10-seconds">calling</a> the attack “extremely complex.”</p>
<p>It is still not clear how the attackers gained initial access to these networks. We were able to retrieve only the files related to the later stages of these attacks, responsible for:</p>
<ul>
<li>Establishing backdoors and their persistence.</li>
<li>Launching the “malicious” video or audio track.</li>
<li>Installing the wiper malware in an attempt to disrupt operations in the hacked networks.</li>
</ul>
<p>All of these samples were uploaded to VirusTotal (VT) from multiple sources, mostly with Iranian IPs, and included short batch scripts that install or launch payloads, several forensics artifacts like Windows Event Log files or memory dumps, and the payloads themselves. The latter are mostly .NET executables, with no obfuscation but a timestomped compilation date in the future. In addition to having the same language and the same VT submitters, these files also share other similarities, such as PDB paths, common commands, names, code reuse, and general coding style.</p>
<h1><strong>Hijacking broadcast signals</strong></h1>
<p>From the MP4 video file that was used to interrupt the TV stream, and was uploaded to VT as <code>TSE_90E11.mp4</code>, we were able to pivot to other artifacts related to the broadcast hijacking, supposedly run on servers that broadcast TV programs (playouts). To play the video file, the attackers used a program called <code>SimplePlayout.exe</code>, a .NET-based executable compiled in debug mode with the PDB path <code>c:\work\SimplePlayout\obj\Debug\SimplePlayout.pdb</code>. This executable has a single functionality: to play a video file in a loop using the .NET MPlatform SDK by Medialooks.</p>
<p><a href="https://research.checkpoint.com/2022/evilplayout-attack-against-irans-state-broadcaster/3-simpleplayout/" rel="attachment wp-att-25804"><img decoding="async" class="wp-image-25804 aligncenter" src="//research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/02/3-simpleplayout.png" alt="" width="1028" height="521" srcset="https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/02/3-simpleplayout.png 1117w, https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/02/3-simpleplayout-300x152.png 300w, https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/02/3-simpleplayout-1024x519.png 1024w, https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/02/3-simpleplayout-768x389.png 768w" sizes="(max-width: 1028px) 100vw, 1028px" /></a></p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><strong>Figure 3 &#8211; Part of the SimplePlayout code using MPlatform SDK to play the video file.</strong></p>
<p>First, the SimplePlayout program looks for a configuration file called <code>SimplePlayout.ini</code> which contains two lines: the video file path, and a number representing the video format. The respective <code>SimplePlayout.ini</code> file uploaded together with SimplePlayout specifies the values that correspond to the MP4 file located at <code>c:\windows\temp\TSE_90E11.mp4</code> and a video format of HD 1080i with a refresh rate of 50 Hz.</p>
<p>To kill the video stream already playing, the attackers used a batch script called <code>playjfalcfgcdq.bat</code>. It kills the running process and deletes the executable of <strong>TFI Arista Playout Server</strong>, a software which the IRIB is <a href="http://rd.irib.ir/documents/25760057/f39f659c-8a0b-42f3-a1e9-d716cd5b8afe">known</a> to use for broadcasting, and subsequently uninstalls the <strong>Matrox DSX</strong> driver, a part of the software for media processing in virtualized broadcast infrastructures,</p>
<p>To combine all the malicious components, another script <code>layoutabcpxtveni.bat</code> does several things:</p>
<ul>
<li>Renames the MP4 video file located at <code>c:\windows\temp\TSE_90E11.003</code> to <code>TSE_90E11.mp4</code>. This file was probably dropped there by one of the backdoors, which we discuss later.</li>
<li>Kills the running process of <code>QTV.CG.Server.exe</code>, possibly a part of Autocue QTV broadcasting software, and overwrites the original server located at <code>D:\CG 1400\QTV.CG.Server.exe</code> with SimplePlayout, the tool used by the attackers to play their video.</li>
<li>Copies <code>c:\windows\SimplePlayout.exe</code> to <code>SimplePlayout.ini</code> in the same directory where <code>QTV.CG.Server.exe</code> resides. At least this sample of the batch script contains a typo, as the actors probably meant to copy <code>SimplePlayout.ini</code> next to the malicious executable.</li>
<li>Runs <code>SimplePlayout.exe</code> from both the initial and replaced locations.</li>
</ul>
<p>In another set of related artifacts that we discovered, the attackers utilize the WAV file containing the 25 seconds audio track titled <code>TSE_90E11.001</code>, similar to the file name of the MP4 file used in the hijacked TV stream. An executable called <code>Avar.exe</code> is based on NAudio, an <a href="https://github.com/naudio/NAudio">open-source</a> .NET audio library, and  is responsible for playing the WAV file. Unlike the <code>SimplePlayout.exe</code>, <code>Avar.exe</code> does not rely on the configuration file. Instead, it contains the path to the WAV file hardcoded as <code>C:\windows\temp\TSE_90E11.001</code>. After it executes, <code>Avar.exe</code> attempts to enumerate through all active audio devices and play the WAV file on each one.</p>
<p>Finally, a batch script named <code>avapweiguyyyw.bat</code> puts the pieces together. It kills a process called <code>ava.exe</code> and replaces the executable at <code>C:\Program Files\MIT\AVA\ava.exe</code> with <code>Avar.exe</code>. The use of the name <strong>Ava</strong> in the files and folders might suggest that these files were intended for IRIB’s AVA radio, although the fact it was also <a href="https://english.mojahedin.org/news/iran-news/iran-state-tv-radio-massoud-maryam-rajavi-khamenei-irib-20220127/">impacted</a> by this attack has not been confirmed officially.</p>
<h1><strong>The Wiper</strong></h1>
<p>We found two identical .NET samples named <code>msdskint.exe</code> whose main purpose is to wipe the computer’s files, drives, and MBR. This can also be deduced from the PDB path: <code>C:\work\wiper\Wiper\obj\Release\Wiper.pdb</code>. In addition, the malware has the capability to clear Windows Event Logs, delete backups, kill processes, change users’ passwords, and more. Both samples were uploaded to VT by the same submitters and in the same timeframe as the previously discussed artifacts.</p>

<h1><strong>Backdoors</strong></h1>
<h2>WinScreeny</h2>
<p>The name of this tool comes from the PDB path: <code>C:\work\winscreeny\winscreeny\obj\Debug\winscreeny.pdb</code>. The main purpose of the backdoor is to make screenshots of the victim’s computer. We found two samples of this backdoor: the first one is the release version uploaded to VT with the name <code>mslicval.exe</code>, and the second one is the debug version named <code>precg2.exe</code>. Needless to say, these files were submitted to VT together with the other artifacts that we discovered.</p>
<p>The backdoor can be run in different ways, based on the command-line argument:</p>
<ul>
<li>None &#8211; Runs a SimpleTCPServer that listens on port 18000.</li>
<li><code>service</code> &#8211; Runs as a service named Service1 . At start, the service creates a scheduled task with the command: <code>schtasks /create /TN \"Microsoft\\Windows\\.NET Framework\\.NETASM\"/TR \” &lt;file_path&gt; \" /ST &lt;current_time + 1:10&gt; /SC ONCE /F</code>.</li>
<li><code>setup</code> &#8211; Tries to gain privileges using the <code>LsaAddAccountRights</code> API function and then run itself as a service.</li>
</ul>
<p>The malware listens for packets on port 18000, and for each packet, it checks if the message contains the <code>scr=</code> command sent with the POST method. If these conditions are met, the malware saves a screenshot to a file named <code>screeny-&lt;timestamp&gt;.png</code> and a “done” message is returned to the attacker if it succeeded.</p>
<p><a href="https://research.checkpoint.com/2022/evilplayout-attack-against-irans-state-broadcaster/6-winscreeny/" rel="attachment wp-att-25807"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" width="1227" height="526" class="size-full wp-image-25807 aligncenter" src="//research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/02/6-winscreeny.png" alt="" srcset="https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/02/6-winscreeny.png 1227w, https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/02/6-winscreeny-300x129.png 300w, https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/02/6-winscreeny-1024x439.png 1024w, https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/02/6-winscreeny-768x329.png 768w" sizes="(max-width: 1227px) 100vw, 1227px" /></a></p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><strong>Figure 6 &#8211; Winscreeny screenshot capture code.</strong></p>
<p>Interestingly, the release version of this malware is also capable of command execution: it supports the <code>s=</code> command which gets a base64-encoded string XORed with 1-byte key 0x24. The decoded string is run by cmd and the execution result is returned to the server. The code that handles this feature is also reused in the HttpService backdoor that we discuss later.</p>
<h2>HttpCallbackService</h2>
<p>HttpCallbackService is a Remote Administration Tool (RAT) with a familiar PDB path: <code>C:\work\simpleserver\HttpCallbackService\obj\Release\HttpCallbackService.pdb</code>. Its C&amp;C URL can be specified in two different ways: a command-line argument or the configuration file <code>callservice.ini</code>. Next, the received value is appended with a short string: <code>?m=</code> if the URL ends with &#8220;.aspx&#8221; or “.php”; <code>m=</code>, if the URL ends with “/”, or <code>/m=</code> in any other case.</p>
<p>Unfortunately, we didn’t find any configuration or other artifacts related to HttpCallbackService, so the C&amp;C server in this attack remains unknown.</p>
<p>Every 5 seconds, HttpCallbackService sends a request to the C&amp;C URL using the <code>webClient.DownloadString</code> method to receive the list of commands split by ‘<code>\r\n</code>’. If the malware doesn’t receive any commands in the last 5 minutes and the <code>isStayAliveMode</code> flag is disabled, this time frame is increased to 1 minute.<br />

</ul>
<h2>HttpService</h2>
<p>HttpService is another backdoor that listens on a specified port: it can be a command-line argument, the pre-defined port depending on the sample, or the value from the configuration file: <code>&lt;exe_name&gt;.ini</code>. We found several samples with the default ports 19336, 19334, 19333, as well as two different configuration files uploaded to VT, with 19336 and 19335 values.</p>
<p>Each sample has a hardcoded version. The files that we discovered belong to three different versions: 0.0.5, 0.0.11v4H and 0.0.15v4H. The version 0.0.5 listens to the specified port with a Simple TCP server, whereas 0.0.11v4H and 0.0.15v4H are based on the Simple HTTP Server. All of them use the HTML Agility Pack for HTML parsing and IonicZip library for compression actions.</p>
<p>The highest version (0.0.15v4H) of the backdoor has multiple capabilities, including command execution and manipulation with the files.</p>
<p><strong>Command execution:</strong> The command “<code>cmd</code>” makes the backdoor run the specified command with cmd.exe and return the result in this format: <code>&lt;div style='color: red'&gt;&lt;result_string&gt;&lt;/div&gt;</code>. In addition, the backdoor can launch an interactive cmd shell when it receives the “<code>i=</code>” command, whose arguments can be:</p>
<ul>
<li>“<code>1</code>” &#8211; Get the output from the shell and send it back to the C&amp;C.</li>
<li>“<code>2</code>” &#8211; End the interactive shell and clean up.</li>
<li>default &#8211; Decode and decrypt the XORed string and then run the command in the shell and save the output.</li>
</ul>
<p>Similar to WinScreeny, the malware also has the “<code>s=</code>” command with the string XORed with 1-byte key <code>0x24</code> as an argument. The decoded string is run by cmd.exe and the result is returned to the server.</p>
<p><strong>Proxy connections</strong>: After the “<code>p=</code>” or “<code>b=</code>” command is received, the backdoor uses the victim’s computer as a proxy to the URL it gets as an argument. The backdoor communicates with this URL, redirects the request of the C&amp;C server, and waits for a response to send it back to the C&amp;C.</p>
<p><strong>Download and upload files</strong>: The “<code>f=</code>” or “<code>1=</code>” command allows the backdoor to download a file from the path given as an argument or write a file given as an argument with the content of the message body. After it receives the “<code>m=</code>” command, the malware writes the body of the message to the path <code>&lt;base_directory&gt;&lt;client_address&gt;.out</code>, reads data from <code>&lt;base_directory&gt;&lt;client_address&gt;.in</code>, and sends it to the C&amp;C. If the file does not exist, the malware creates the file and writes to it the current date and time.</p>
<p><strong>Run SQL commands</strong>: The “<code>con=</code>” / “<code>c=</code>” command receives the SQL DB connection string and SQL query, and returns the result to the server.</p>
<p><strong>Manipulate the local files:</strong> The “<code>&lt;path&gt;</code>” command checks if the file/directory exists and then does one of these three things, based on the query value:</p>
<ul>
<li>“<code>zip</code>” &#8211; Creates a zip file from the directory contents and returns it to the C&amp;C.</li>
<li>“<code>unzip</code>” &#8211; Unzips the file using the path provided by the C&amp;C.</li>
<li>“<code>del</code>” &#8211; Deletes the file.</li>
</ul>
<p>Interestingly, in all three cases, the malware sends back the entire directory contents (including sub-directories) as an HTML page that contains the <code>Zip</code>, <code>Unzip</code> and <code>Delete</code> buttons, depending on the type of the file. This is how the interface looks on the attackers’ side:</p>

<h2>ServerLaunch dropper</h2>
<p>The sample of HttpServer version 0.0.5 was submitted together with its dropper, called <code>dwDrvInst.exe</code>, which mimics the remote access software executable by DameWare. The tool’s PDB path has the same pattern, <code>C:\work\ServerLaunch\Release\ServerLaunch.pdb</code>. However, the tool is written in C++, not .NET like all the others, and was compiled on December 2, 2021, almost 2 months prior to the attack.</p>
<p>ServerLaunch contains three executables in resources, which it drops to <code>ionic.zip.dll</code>, <code>httpservice2</code> and <code>httpservice4</code>, all in <code>C:\Users\Public\</code>. The malware then starts both <code>httpservice2</code> and <code>httpservice4</code> with no arguments. Each of them has a different pre-defined port to listen on, which likely allows the attackers to ensure some sort of redundancy of the C&amp;C communication.</p>
<h1><strong>Connecting the files to the attack</strong></h1>
<p>We’ve discussed several different tools and some of artifacts related to their execution. It is clear that all these tools were created by the same actor and are connected. For example, the screenshot tool Winscreeny doesn’t contain the functionality to upload the created screenshots back to the attackers, which likely means that it relies on other backdoors to perform this operation. The recurring <code>Service1</code> name for all the tools indicates that different backdoors, if running on the same machine were mostly executed with command-line arguments or provided configuration files.</p>
<p>Taking into account that the samples are related to each other, we can substantiate the connection between these files and the IRIB cyberattack:</p>
<ul>
<li>The whole cluster of activity is interconnected and was submitted to VT mostly from Iranian IPs all at the same timeframe, likely by incident responders.</li>
<li>The audio and video files utilized by the tools are the same as those broadcast live on hacked Iranian TV. The Twitter account <code>@GhyamSarnegouni</code> (“Uprising to overthrow”) featured in this video contains a few recordings of different TV channel streams that feature both the video and the audio tracks we’ve discussed.</li>
<li>Multiple artifacts such as Matrox DSX, Autocue QTV, TFI Arista Playout Server, etc. that were referenced in the samples indicate that these files were intended for a broadcast environment.</li>
<li>Among the forensics artifacts submitted together with video and executables, we discovered Windows Event Viewer files that contain evidence that the samples were attempted to be executed in the Iranian TV network environment, a domain not resolved publicly. The timestamp of these specific logs is after the time of the actual incident.</li>
</ul>

<li>Numerous other forensics evidence from this VT file cluster contains other artifacts directly related to IRIB. For example, an internal tool called <code>MIT_FreeSizeService</code> (md5:307e7440a15c8eed720566f067a2e96b) bears the IRIB logo, and the memory dump of the <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MetaSAN">MetaSAN</a> software called <code>executable.4504.exe</code> (md5:1fc57ccec4668bbcbebaa9c734a437ba) features memory strings that indicate the software was run on the machine from the <code>MIT-TV</code> domain.</li>
</ul>
<p><a href="https://research.checkpoint.com/2022/evilplayout-attack-against-irans-state-broadcaster/9-vt/" rel="attachment wp-att-25810"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" width="1426" height="232" class="size-full wp-image-25810 aligncenter" src="//research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/02/9-VT.png" alt="" srcset="https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/02/9-VT.png 1426w, https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/02/9-VT-300x49.png 300w, https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/02/9-VT-1024x167.png 1024w, https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/02/9-VT-768x125.png 768w" sizes="(max-width: 1426px) 100vw, 1426px" /></a></p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><strong>Figure 9 &#8211; VT submission of the unknown tool featuring the MIT (same as the domain name) string and containing the IRIB logo</strong></p>
<h1><strong>Attribution</strong></h1>
<p>Iranian officials appear to be confident that MEK is behind this attack, with the deputy head of technical affairs for Islamic Republic of Iran Broadcasting <a href="https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2022-01-27/iran-state-tv-says-exiled-dissidents-briefly-hacked-broadcasts">claiming</a> the same. However, the opposition group itself <a href="https://www.iranintl.com/en/202201270195">denies</a> any involvement, stating that “the group had become aware of the incident only when it happened but that the hacking might have been the work of supporters in Iran.”</p>
<p>The hacktivist group Predatory Sparrow, which claimed responsibility for the attacks against the national railway services, the transportation ministry, and the Iranian gas stations, affiliated itself with the IRIB attack via its Telegram channel. On the morning before the attack, they <a href="https://t.me/GonjeshkeDarande/143">wrote</a> “Wait for the good news from our team. Do not switch the channel.” Later the same evening, they <a href="https://t.me/GonjeshkeDarande/146">posted</a> a video from one of the disrupted TV channels, introducing it as a “cyber-attack on the country&#8217;s radio and television organization by the Predatory Sparrow team.” Currently, no technical proof of the group’s attribution to the attack has been discovered. The video displayed on the channel is available online and refers to a different Telegram account <code>@GhyamSarnegouni</code>, so these claims &#8211; as well as the relation between the two entities &#8211; should be treated with caution.</p>
<p><a href="https://research.checkpoint.com/2022/evilplayout-attack-against-irans-state-broadcaster/10-telegram/" rel="attachment wp-att-25811"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" width="502" height="468" class="size-full wp-image-25811 aligncenter" src="//research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/02/10-telegram.png" alt="" srcset="https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/02/10-telegram.png 502w, https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/02/10-telegram-300x280.png 300w" sizes="(max-width: 502px) 100vw, 502px" /></a></p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><strong>Figure 10 &#8211; Posts from ‘Predatory Sparrow’s Telegram channel, in which the group claims &#8220;responsibility&#8221; for the attack.</strong></p>
<h1><strong>Conclusion</strong></h1>
<p>In this article, we analyzed a set of tools that likely was used in a cyberattack against the IRIB, which disrupted several state-run TV and radio channels. The use of wiper malware in the attack against a state entity in Iran begs us to compare the tools with those belonging to <a href="https://research.checkpoint.com/2021/indra-hackers-behind-recent-attacks-on-iran/">Indra</a><u>,</u> who, among other attacks, is responsible for unleashing a wiper in the Iranian Railways and Ministry of Roads systems. Although these wipers are coded and behave very differently, some implementation details such as execution based on batch files, or the password changing patterns (<code>[random sequence]aA1!</code> for this attack and <code>Aa153![random sequence]</code> in Indra’s case), suggests that the attackers behind the IRIB hack may have been inspired by previous attacks happened in Iran.</p>
<p>As in the case with Indra, it appears that the actor may have many capabilities that have yet to be explored. On the one hand, the attackers managed to pull off a complicated operation to bypass security systems and network segmentation, penetrate the broadcaster’s networks, produce and run the malicious tools that heavily rely on internal knowledge of the broadcasting software used by victims, all while staying under the radar during the reconnaissance and initial intrusion stages.</p>
<p>On the other hand, the attackers’ tools are of relatively low quality and sophistication, and are launched by clumsy and sometimes buggy 3-line batch scripts. This might support the theory that the attackers might have had help from inside the IRIB, or indicate a yet unknown collaboration between different groups with different skills.</p>
<p>Meanwhile, almost two weeks after the attack happened, MEK-affiliated news <a href="https://english.mojahedin.org/news/iran-despite-utilizing-all-resources-after-12-days-regimes-radio-and-tv-networks-have-not-returned-to-a-normal-status/">published</a> a status report of the attack claiming that the “regime’s radio and TV networks have not returned to a normal status” and provided an elaborate list of affected devices with the statement “more than 600 servers, advanced digital production, archiving, and broadcasting of radio and television equipment have been destroyed, and their software has been damaged.” There is no way for us to verify these claims, but if at least some of them are true, the extent of destruction caused by the wiper and other malicious tools that we’ve discovered (and those that are yet unknown), exceeded expectations.</p>

function loadImg(element){
    var bg = '';
	switch (screenW) {
		case  'mobile':
			bg = element.getAttribute('data-bg-mobile');
			if(bg)
				break; 
		case 'tablet':
			bg = element.getAttribute('data-bg-tablet');
			if(bg)
				break; 
		case 'laptop':
			bg = element.getAttribute('data-bg-laptop');
			if(bg)
				break; 
		default: 
			bg = element.getAttribute('data-bg');
	}
	if(bg)
		element.style.backgroundImage = 'url(' + bg + ')';
