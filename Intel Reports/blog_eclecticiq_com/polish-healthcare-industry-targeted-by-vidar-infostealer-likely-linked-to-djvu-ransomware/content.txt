<h2 style="text-align: left;">Executive Summary</h2>
<p style="text-align: left;">On 21st March 2023, EclecticIQ researchers detected a spearphishing email targeting the healthcare industry in Poland. The spoofed email was designed to appear as legitimately sent from a Polish government entity called the National Health Fund (Narodowy Fundusz Zdrowia - NFZ).&nbsp;<br><br>The email contained a malicious Microsoft Excel XLL attachment that can download and execute Vidar Infostealer malware upon user execution. Vidar can collect sensitive information from infected devices and potentially lead to ransomware, which puts the Polish healthcare industry at risk of losing critical data and disruption of systems.&nbsp;<br><br>The Vidar command and control (C2) infrastructure has multiple connections to recently observed ransomware attacks from LockBit 2.0 and Djvu. Due to these overlaps, EclecticIQ researchers assess with high confidence that the observed spearphishing attack on a hospital in Poznań, Poland, was carried out by a Ransomware affiliated member or group of individuals.&nbsp;<br><br>Vidar is capable of stealing sensitive data from the infected system such as digital crypto wallets, messaging apps, credentials from web browsers and Two-Factor Authentication codes (2FA) from browser extensions like Authy, EOS and GAuth Authenticator. Annex 1 lists browsers and applications targeted by Vidar.&nbsp;</p>
<!--more-->
<h2 style="text-align: left;">Delivering Malicious XLL Files via Spear Phishing Email&nbsp;</h2>
<p style="text-align: left;">The infection chain starts with an email containing a Microsoft Excel XLL attachment. The email is addressed to szpital@lutycka.pl - the Provincial hospital in Poznań. If the attachment is opened, it will start downloading Vidar infostealer malware along with a decoy Excel sheet from app4j[.]org.&nbsp;<br>&nbsp;</p>
<ul>
<li style="text-align: left;">hxxps[://]app4j[.]org/Date2023[.]zip (Vidar Infostealer)</li>
<li style="text-align: left;">hxxps[://]app4j[.]org/Wyciek-NFZ-16-03-2023[.]xlsx (Decoy Excel sheet)&nbsp;<br><br></li>
</ul>
<p style="text-align: left;">A .XLL file is an Excel add-in file. Excel add-in files are similar to DLL files, except they are built specifically for Excel. They allow users to extend the functionality of Microsoft Excel by adding custom functions and features. &nbsp;<br><br>Malware authors used an open-source tool called “Excel-DNA” as one of the common tools for creating malicious XLL files. A XLL file written in .NET language is compiled within a standalone file containing functions that map native exports to the CLR functions, contained in a user-defined assembly DLL embedded in the resource section of the file generated by Excel-DNA. <h2 style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-size: 1.125rem;">Capabilities of Vidar Infostealer</span></h2>
<p style="text-align: left;">Vidar is a malware designed to steal sensitive information from infected systems. It is known for its ability to evade detection and analysis using various techniques. &nbsp;<br><br>One of the primary evasion techniques used by Vidar is packing and XOR encryption routines. These conceal its malicious code and prevent detection by security solutions. <h2 style="text-align: left;">Outlook<br><br></h2>
<ul>
<li style="text-align: left;">EclecticIQ analysts assess with high confidence that the Polish healthcare industry will continue to face ransomware attacks in the near future. The healthcare sector has been a prime target for cybercriminals due to the sensitive nature of patient data and the potential for financial gain through ransom payments. &nbsp;</li>
<li style="text-align: left;">Threat actors very likely used Vidar infostealer before launching a ransomware attack. By stealing sensitive information, this can increase the potential pay-out from a ransomware attack by threatening to leak the stolen data if the victim does not pay the ransom. Additionally, Vidar infostealer may be used to gather information about a victim's system and network, which can be used to increase the effectiveness of the ransomware attack.</li>
<li style="text-align: left;">Various Ransomware groups like GrandCrab, LockBit and Djvu used Vidar stealer in the process for distributing the ransomware as a second stage payload. It is important to note that the usage of Vidar infostealer before a ransomware attack is a significant threat to organizations and individuals alike. By stealing sensitive information, cybercriminals can cause significant damage to victims' reputations, finances, and privacy. Furthermore, the potential loss of critical data can disrupt operations and lead to financial and legal repercussions. EclecticIQ researchers assess with high confidence that double extortion tactics very likely will increase amongst Ransomware groups. <h2 style="text-align: left;"><br>Recommendations<br>&nbsp;</h2>
<p><strong>1. Implement Email Authentication:</strong> Spear phishing attacks rely on fraudulent emails that appear to be from a trusted source. Implementing email authentication mechanisms such as Sender Policy Framework (SPF), DomainKeys Identified Mail (DKIM), and Domain-based Message Authentication, Reporting, and Conformance (DMARC) can help prevent email spoofing and ensure that emails are originating from a trusted source.&nbsp;<br><br><strong>2. Use Anti-Phishing Filters: </strong>Anti-phishing filters can identify and block suspicious emails, including those containing phishing links or malicious attachments. These filters can be integrated into email clients or gateway appliances to detect and block such emails.&nbsp;<br><br><strong>3. Provide Security Awareness Training:</strong> Train your employees to be vigilant and recognize the signs of a phishing email. This can include training on how to identify phishing links, spoofed email addresses, and suspicious attachments.&nbsp;<br><br><strong>4. Implement Least Privilege Access:</strong> Limit the access privileges of employees to only those resources that are necessary for them to perform their job functions. This can help prevent attackers from gaining access to sensitive data or systems through compromised credentials.&nbsp;<br><br><strong>5. Regularly Backup Data:</strong> Regularly backing up critical data can help mitigate the impact of a successful ransomware attack. This way, even if an attacker manages to encrypt your data, you can restore it from a backup rather than paying the ransom. Ensure that backups are stored securely and not directly accessible from your network.&nbsp;<br><br><strong>6. Use strong passwords and two-factor authentication:</strong> Strong passwords and two-factor authentication can prevent unauthorized access to your accounts, even if an infostealer manages to steal your login credentials.&nbsp;</p>
