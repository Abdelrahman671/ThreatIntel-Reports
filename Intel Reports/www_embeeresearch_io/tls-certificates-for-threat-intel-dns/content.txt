        <section class="gh-content gh-canvas is-body">
            <p>In this blog we will identify 6 malicious domains that are likely hosting MatanBuchus malware. We will identify these domains through the usage of hardcoded subdomains in the TLS Certificate of the initial shared domain. </p><p>After leveraging the hardcoded subdomains, we will leverage registration dates and certificate providers to hone in on our final results. Ultimately this will produce 6 domains sharing the same financial theme that was shared in the initial intel. </p><h2 id="initial-intelligence">Initial Intelligence</h2><p>This post is based on initial intelligence shared by @<a href="https://twitter.com/Unit42_Intel?ref=embeeresearch.io">unit42_intel</a> in an original post <a href="https://twitter.com/Unit42_Intel/status/1772988284571877807?ref=embeeresearch.io">here</a>. </p><p>In this post, Unit42 shared details of a <code>treasurybanks[.]org</code> domain used in malicious ads targeting users looking for funds recovery services. </p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card"><img src="https://pbs.twimg.com/media/GJrraXhWMAAf6sj?format=jpg&amp;name=large" class="kg-image" alt="Screenshot of TreasuryBanks dot org. Arrow points to browser download notification. " loading="lazy" width="1600" height="900"></figure><h3 id="review-of-first-historical-address">Review of First Historical Address</h3><p>Since the initial indicator was a domain and not an IP address, we can use a passive dns tool like Validin to review indicators and history about the domain. </p><p>Our first step here is to look for historical IP resolutions which may point to similar domains hosted on the same servers. </p><p>Leveraging Validin for the <a href="https://app.validin.com/detail?type=dom&find=treasurybanks.org&ref=embeeresearch.io">initial search</a>, we can see that the domain has recently resolved to <code>47.90.170[.]226</code> and <code>91.195.240[.]123</code></p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card"><img src="https://www.embeeresearch.io/content/images/2024/04/image-17.png" class="kg-image" alt="" loading="lazy" width="2000" height="506" srcset="https://www.embeeresearch.io/content/images/size/w600/2024/04/image-17.png 600w, https://www.embeeresearch.io/content/images/size/w1000/2024/04/image-17.png 1000w, https://www.embeeresearch.io/content/images/size/w1600/2024/04/image-17.png 1600w, https://www.embeeresearch.io/content/images/2024/04/image-17.png 2027w" sizes="(min-width: 720px) 720px"></figure><p>Our next step was to review the historical records for these IP addresses and determine if they resolved to any similar-looking domains in a similar time period. </p><p>Reviewing the most recent resolved IP address of <code>47.90.170[.]226</code>, there are 19 domains that have been associated with this address. The most recent 5 domains are the original <code>treasurybanks[.]org</code> and the subdomains <code>www,get,download,file</code>. </p><p>The other domains here are from a much older time period and do not show any indications of being related to <code>treasurybanks[.]org</code>, so we decided to ignore these and come back later if other searches did not yield results. </p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card"><img src="https://www.embeeresearch.io/content/images/2024/04/image-18.png" class="kg-image" alt="" loading="lazy" width="2000" height="1195" srcset="https://www.embeeresearch.io/content/images/size/w600/2024/04/image-18.png 600w, https://www.embeeresearch.io/content/images/size/w1000/2024/04/image-18.png 1000w, https://www.embeeresearch.io/content/images/size/w1600/2024/04/image-18.png 1600w, https://www.embeeresearch.io/content/images/2024/04/image-18.png 2114w" sizes="(min-width: 720px) 720px"></figure><h3 id="review-of-second-historical-address">Review of Second Historical Address</h3><p>The second most recent resolved IP address for <code>treasurybanks[.]org</code> is <code>91.195.240[.]123</code>. </p><p>We can again pivot and investigate this IP address for similar domain records that may point us to similar sites. </p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card"><img src="https://www.embeeresearch.io/content/images/2024/04/image-17.png" class="kg-image" alt="" loading="lazy" width="2000" height="506" srcset="https://www.embeeresearch.io/content/images/size/w600/2024/04/image-17.png 600w, https://www.embeeresearch.io/content/images/size/w1000/2024/04/image-17.png 1000w, https://www.embeeresearch.io/content/images/size/w1600/2024/04/image-17.png 1600w, https://www.embeeresearch.io/content/images/2024/04/image-17.png 2027w" sizes="(min-width: 720px) 720px"></figure><p>The address <code>91.195.240[.]123</code> has a huge number of past records, indicating that is likely a proxy or some kind of "middle" infrastructure shared amongst thousands of other sites. </p><p>Reviewing the historical records did not yield any useful results. </p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card"><img src="https://www.embeeresearch.io/content/images/2024/04/image-19.png" class="kg-image" alt="" loading="lazy" width="2000" height="1053" srcset="https://www.embeeresearch.io/content/images/size/w600/2024/04/image-19.png 600w, https://www.embeeresearch.io/content/images/size/w1000/2024/04/image-19.png 1000w, https://www.embeeresearch.io/content/images/size/w1600/2024/04/image-19.png 1600w, https://www.embeeresearch.io/content/images/2024/04/image-19.png 2125w" sizes="(min-width: 720px) 720px"></figure><p>Reviewing the historical DNS records also did not yield any results for similar sites. </p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card"><img src="https://www.embeeresearch.io/content/images/2024/04/image-20.png" class="kg-image" alt="" loading="lazy" width="2000" height="868" srcset="https://www.embeeresearch.io/content/images/size/w600/2024/04/image-20.png 600w, https://www.embeeresearch.io/content/images/size/w1000/2024/04/image-20.png 1000w, https://www.embeeresearch.io/content/images/size/w1600/2024/04/image-20.png 1600w, https://www.embeeresearch.io/content/images/2024/04/image-20.png 2117w" sizes="(min-width: 720px) 720px"></figure><h3 id="finding-a-first-pivot-point">Finding a First Pivot Point</h3><p>Attempts to pivot on the historical IP records did not yield any meaningful results, so we decided to return to the interesting subdomains that we observed on the initial <code>treasurybanks[.]org</code>. </p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card"><img src="https://www.embeeresearch.io/content/images/2024/04/image-21.png" class="kg-image" alt="" loading="lazy" width="2000" height="830" srcset="https://www.embeeresearch.io/content/images/size/w600/2024/04/image-21.png 600w, https://www.embeeresearch.io/content/images/size/w1000/2024/04/image-21.png 1000w, https://www.embeeresearch.io/content/images/size/w1600/2024/04/image-21.png 1600w, https://www.embeeresearch.io/content/images/2024/04/image-21.png 2082w" sizes="(min-width: 720px) 720px"></figure><p>If we inspect the Certificate history for the <code>treasurybanks[.]org</code> domain, we can see that these subdomains are hardcoded into the Certificate (this is in contrast to a typical wildcard certificate with something like <code>*.treasurybanks[.]org</code></p><p>Since the subdomains are hardcoded, we decided to use them as a pivot point. </p><p>Of additional note here, is that the certificate leverages GeoTrust and was registered on <code>2024-03-06</code>. This information we can leverage as additional pivot points later.</p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card"><img src="https://pbs.twimg.com/media/GKJpilbaEAA3gsz?format=png&amp;name=900x900" class="kg-image" alt="Image" loading="lazy" width="842" height="876"></figure><h3 id="pivoting-on-subdomains-in-tls-certificates">Pivoting on Subdomains In TLS Certificates</h3><p>We can now attempt to leverage the following indicators to identify new domains. </p><ul><li>Hardcoded subdomains (www, file, get, download)</li><li>Use of GeoTrust for Certificate signing</li><li>Registration dates around March 2024</li></ul><p>To perform the initial search for hardcoded subdomains, we leveraged Censys to search certificates with hardcoded values beginning with the previously identified subdomains. </p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card"><img src="https://www.embeeresearch.io/content/images/2024/04/image-22.png" class="kg-image" alt="" loading="lazy" width="1448" height="247" srcset="https://www.embeeresearch.io/content/images/size/w600/2024/04/image-22.png 600w, https://www.embeeresearch.io/content/images/size/w1000/2024/04/image-22.png 1000w, https://www.embeeresearch.io/content/images/2024/04/image-22.png 1448w" sizes="(min-width: 720px) 720px"></figure><p>The initial results for this search returned 581 results. Many of which did not look anything like the original certificate for <code>treasurybanks[.]org</code>. </p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card"><img src="https://www.embeeresearch.io/content/images/2024/04/image-24.png" class="kg-image" alt="" loading="lazy" width="1763" height="1025" srcset="https://www.embeeresearch.io/content/images/size/w600/2024/04/image-24.png 600w, https://www.embeeresearch.io/content/images/size/w1000/2024/04/image-24.png 1000w, https://www.embeeresearch.io/content/images/size/w1600/2024/04/image-24.png 1600w, https://www.embeeresearch.io/content/images/2024/04/image-24.png 1763w" sizes="(min-width: 720px) 720px"></figure><p>However after scrolling slightly through the first page of results,  we can see two certificates (<code>maxrecovery[.]org</code> and <code>myfundsrecovery[.]org</code>) that look extremely similar to our initial <code>treasurybanks[.]org</code>. </p><p>We can also note that both of these certificates are registered with GeoTrust and were registered in March 2024. </p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card"><img src="https://www.embeeresearch.io/content/images/2024/04/image-23.png" class="kg-image" alt="" loading="lazy" width="1745" height="906" srcset="https://www.embeeresearch.io/content/images/size/w600/2024/04/image-23.png 600w, https://www.embeeresearch.io/content/images/size/w1000/2024/04/image-23.png 1000w, https://www.embeeresearch.io/content/images/size/w1600/2024/04/image-23.png 1600w, https://www.embeeresearch.io/content/images/2024/04/image-23.png 1745w" sizes="(min-width: 720px) 720px"></figure><h3 id="applying-filters-on-geotrustdigicert">Applying Filters On GeoTrust/Digicert</h3><p>To hone in on the results, we can add a filter for Digicert (owner of GeoTrust) certificates. https://www.embeeresearch.io/content/images/2024/04/image-26.png 1640w" sizes="(min-width: 720px) 720px"></figure><p>Some of these look exactly like our original domain, indicating that we are getting closer to desirable search results. </p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card"><img src="https://www.embeeresearch.io/content/images/2024/04/image-27.png" class="kg-image" alt="" loading="lazy" 720px"></figure><p>However if we scroll down to the end of our 15 results, there are still multiple hits for certificates that don't look anything like our <code>treasurybanks[.]org</code></p><p>We can also see that these certificates were registered years ago and are now expired, so we can leverage this to filter them out. </p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card"><img We can see this applied to the <code>parsed.validity_period.not_before</code> field, specifying a date range of 2024-01-01 to 2024-03-30. </p><p><a applying these filters</a> we are left with 7 results. </p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card"><img src="https://www.embeeresearch.io/content/images/2024/04/image-29.png" class="kg-image" alt=""
