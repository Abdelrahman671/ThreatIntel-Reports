<p>
	Network infrastructure analysis plays an important role in the study of malware distribution campaigns. Data on which IP addresses corresponded to a given domain name over time facilitate the identification of new malicious servers. In turn, retrospectively determining which domains were resolved to a given IP address provides new domains, for which the search procedure can be repeated, leading the process further. This information can be immensely helpful in establishing the geography of nodes, identifying &quot;favorite&quot; hosts and registrars, and determining which values an attacker characteristically enters into fields when registering domains.
</p>
<p>
	Metainformation that appears useless at first glance may very well prove its worth after a period of a days, weeks, or months. In the course of malware analysis, sooner or later the question of attribution inevitably arises, and indirect identifiers such as network indicators can go a long way in determining which criminal group a certain tool belongs to. 
</p>
<p>
	This article examines the most characteristic network infrastructure indicators of the TA505 group, as well as intersections between TA505 and another hacker group, Buhtrap.
</p>
<h2>Domain name registrars</h2>
<p>
	In total, we analyzed 372 domains belonging to TA505 and identified 22 organizations that facilitated the acquisition of these domains. The resources most frequently used were the following:
</p>
<ul>
	<li>WhoisGuard, Inc. — 28 domain names</li>
	<li>Eranet International Limited — 26 domain names</li>
</ul>
<p>
	<a href="http://www.whoisguard.com/">WhoisGuard</a>, an organization based in Panama, offers the service of concealing domain owners&#x27; registration data from public access. This is <a href="http://blog.ptsecurity.com/2019/07/ironpython-darkly-how-we-uncovered.html">not the first</a> time we have seen their services used by hackers to establish anonymity and hinder investigations.
</p>
<figure> <img alt="TA505 domains registered through WhoisGuard, sorted by malware family" loading="lazy" width="0" height="0" decoding="async" data-nimg="1" class="HtmlContent_image-next__g1zfK" style="color:transparent" src="https://www.ptsecurity.com/upload/corporate/ww-en/images/esc/operation-ta505-part3/01.jpg"/> <figcaption>TA505 domains registered through WhoisGuard, sorted by malware family</figcaption> </figure>
<p>
	TA505 has utilized other, similar services, though to a lesser extent. These include PROTECTSERVICE LTD, Whois Privacy Protection Foundation, and Domains by Proxy LLC.
</p>
<p>
	<a href="http://www.now.top/">Eranet International Limited</a> is one of the largest registrars in Hong Kong. It should be noted that members of TA505 tended to use <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dynamic_DNS">dynamic DNS</a> when registering domains with this provider. As a result, the IP addresses that their domain names were resolved to changed frequently, making them difficult to track.
</p>
<figure> <img alt="TA505 domains registered through Eranet International Limited, sorted by malware family" loading="lazy" width="0" height="0" decoding="async" data-nimg="1" class="HtmlContent_image-next__g1zfK" style="color:transparent" src="https://www.ptsecurity.com/upload/corporate/ww-en/images/esc/operation-ta505-part3/02.jpg"/> <figcaption>TA505 domains registered through Eranet International Limited, sorted by malware family</figcaption> </figure>

<h2>TA505 and Buhtrap</h2>
<p>
	On July 11, 2019, specialists from ESET released an <a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/2019/07/11/buhtrap-zero-day-espionage-campaigns/">article</a> about a recent attack carried out by the Buhtrap group using a zero-day vulnerability in the Win32k component of Windows. The article described a so-called &#x27;grabber&#x27; module used to harvest user passwords from email clients, browsers, and other sources. Later, we unearthed another similar module (MD5: c6e9d7280f77977a6968722e8124f51c) with the same C&amp;C server in its body (redmond.corp-microsoft[.]com).
</p>
<figure> <img alt="C&amp;C server in the Buhtrap grabber module" loading="lazy" width="0" height="0" decoding="async" data-nimg="1" class="HtmlContent_image-next__g1zfK" style="color:transparent" src="https://www.ptsecurity.com/upload/corporate/ru-ru/images/pt-esc/operation-ta505-part3/04.jpg"/> <figcaption>C&amp;C server in the Buhtrap grabber module</figcaption> </figure>
<p>
	Running a query through the <a href="https://community.riskiq.com/search/redmond.corp-microsoft.com">PaSiveTotal</a> resource reveals that this host has been rendered to the IP address 95[.]179.159.170 since June 6, 2019.
</p>
<p>
	Several days earlier, on July 2, 2019, specialists from Proofpoint <a href="https://www.proofpoint.com/us/threat-insight/post/ta505-begins-summer-campaigns-new-pet-malware-downloader-andromut-uae-south">released</a> a report regarding new tools used by the TA505 group, one of which is called Andromut (also <a href="https://blog.trendmicro.com/trendlabs-security-intelligence/latest-spam-campaigns-from-ta505-now-using-new-malware-tools-gelup-and-flowerpippi/">known</a> as Gelup).  Andromut is a downloader for the FlawedAmmyy RAT. One of the variations of the downloader that we encountered (MD5: 0cbeb424d96e5c268ec2525d603f64eb) uses the domain compatexchange-cloudapp[.]net as its C&amp;C server.
</p>
<figure> <img alt="C&amp;C server in Gelup from TA505, after decryption" loading="lazy" width="0" height="0" decoding="async" data-nimg="1" class="HtmlContent_image-next__g1zfK" style="color:transparent" src="https://www.ptsecurity.com/upload/corporate/ru-ru/images/pt-esc/operation-ta505-part3/05.jpg"/> <figcaption>C&amp;C server in Gelup from TA505, after decryption</figcaption> </figure>
<p>
	The <a href="https://community.riskiq.com/search/compatexchange-cloudapp.net">PaSiveTotal</a> resource shows us that this host has been resolved to the IP address 95[.]179.159.170 since June 8, 2019.
</p>
<p>
	These two domains were registered with the same registrar (Tucows Domains Inc.) within two days of one another, and are resolved to the same IP address. Considering that both groups carried out attacks throughout June, it is reasonable to conclude that Buhtrap and TA505 used the same host as a C&amp;C server.
</p>
<p>
	It is also worth noting that the domain compatexchange-cloudapp[.]net was used not only in the downloader earlier discussed, but also in older versions of Buhtrap components.
</p>
<figure> <img alt="First intersection of TA505 and Buhtrap found in network infrastructure" loading="lazy" width="0" height="0" decoding="async" data-nimg="1" class="HtmlContent_image-next__g1zfK" style="color:transparent" src="https://www.ptsecurity.com/upload/corporate/ru-ru/images/pt-esc/operation-ta505-part3/06.jpg"/> <figcaption>First intersection of TA505 and Buhtrap found in network infrastructure</figcaption> </figure>
<p>
	We later discovered another intersection between the two hacker groups. The domains of TA505&#x27;s Smoke Loader and a second grabber from Buhtrap displayed a similar congruence: the domain test-service012505[.]com from Smoke Loader (MD5: 5fc6f24d43bc7ca45a81d159291955d1) and the domain secure-telemetry[.] net from the gabber (MD5: 79d1a836423c7ee57b6127cf2930a9d9) have been resolved to the IP address 194[.]4.56.252 since June 17th and 16th, 2019, respectively.
</p>
<figure> <img alt="Second intersection of TA505 and Buhtrap found in network infrastructure" loading="lazy" width="0" height="0" decoding="async" data-nimg="1" class="HtmlContent_image-next__g1zfK" style="color:transparent" src="https://www.ptsecurity.com/upload/corporate/ru-ru/images/pt-esc/operation-ta505-part3/07.jpg"/> <figcaption>Second intersection of TA505 and Buhtrap found in network infrastructure</figcaption> </figure>
<h2>Conclusions</h2>
<p>
	This article has examined the network infrastructure of the hacker group TA505. Starting with a look at their preferred domain name registrars and the hosts of their C&amp;C servers, we unearthed interesting details in the client information provided by the group during domain registration. This could serve as a starting point for further investigations. We then discussed intersections that were discovered between the infrastructure of the TA505 and Buhtrap hacker groups. The incidence of shared servers between the two groups could have several explanations: the groups could have a bilateral agreement to share the servers, they could be managed and coordinated by a single entity, or they could both rent the servers from a third party (thereby economizing on expenditures). Our work investigating these groups will not end here. We will continue to monitor their activity and search for new information on their possible connections and collaboration.
</p>

	<b>Authors</b>: Alexey Vishnyakov and Maxim Anfinogenov, Positive Technologies
