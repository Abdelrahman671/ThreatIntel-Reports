<h1>Shadowserver Special Reports &#x2013; Cyclops Blink</h1>
<div class="the-date">February 23, 2022</div>
<div class="content" data-shadowserver-name="ss:content" id="content-4qA7keSlHMVu"><p><strong>UPDATE 2022-04-21: </strong>Fifth special report sent overnight containing <span data-offset-key="7upt9-0-0">511 IPs likely still infected with Cyclops Blink, corresponding to 270 ASNs in 60 countries. Remediation continuing.<br>
</span></p>
<p><strong>UPDATE 2022-04-13: </strong>Overnight 2022-04-12/13 we sent out a fourth special report with <span data-offset-key="7upt9-0-0">537 IPs likely infected with Cyclops Blink, corresponding to 281 ASNs in 61 countries. The top countries were still United States (154), Canada (58), Sweden (38), Russia (26), Germany (25). A mix of Watchguard and ASUS devices. Remediation continuing, but more patching required.<br>
</span></p>
<p><strong>UPDATE 2022-04-08: </strong>Overnight 2022-04-07/08 we sent out a third special report with an additional <span data-offset-key="7upt9-0-0">553 IPs likely infected with Cyclops Blink, corresponding to 285 ASNs in 61 countries. The top countries were United States (157), Canada (58), Sweden (38), Russia (27), Germany (26).<br>
</span></p>
<p><strong>UPDATE 2022-04-06:</strong> US <a href="https://www.justice.gov/opa/pr/justice-department-announces-court-authorized-disruption-botnet-controlled-russian-federation">DoJ announcement</a> about disruption action against Cyclops Blink infected devices.</p>
<p><strong>UPDATE 2022-04-01: </strong><a href="https://www.asus.com/content/ASUS-Product-Security-Advisory/">ASUS released updated firmware</a> for devices impacted by Cyclops Blink.</p>
<p><strong>UPDATE 2022-03-25: </strong><a href="https://www.asus.com/content/ASUS-Product-Security-Advisory/">ASUS released a security advisory</a> about Cyclops Blink impacting ASUS devices.</p>
<p><strong>UPDATE 2022-03-17:</strong> <a href="https://www.trendmicro.com/en_us/research/22/c/cyclops-blink-sets-sights-on-asus-routers--.html">Trend Micro published research</a> detailing ASUS devices also being impacted by Cyclops Blink (some ASUS device IPs were included in our second special report, but not explicitly called out at that time, since details were not public).</p>
<p><strong>UPDATE 2022-03-03:</strong> On 2022-03-03 we sent out a second special report with an additional 673 IPs likely infected with Cyclops Blink, observed on 2022-02-24. These IPs are different to those sent out in the first report. Countries with top infections: USA (188), France (92), Italy (65), Canada (55), Germany (39).</p>
<h2><strong>Original Article</strong></h2>
<p>On May 23rd 2018, the US Department of Justice (DoJ), Federal Bureau of Investigation (FBI) and Cisco Talos publicly <a href="https://www.justice.gov/opa/pr/justice-department-announces-actions-disrupt-advanced-persistent-threat-28-botnet-infected">announced</a> the disruption of a novel multi-stage modular malware platform called <b>VPNFilter</b>. This was designed to infect small office and home office (SOHO) routers and other network devices. At the time, VPNFilter was believed to be operated by the threat actor known as APT28 (also known as Fancy Bear, Pawn Storm, Sandworm, Sofacy Group, Sednit X-Agent, STRONTIUM and Tsar Team), which was allegedly associated with the Russian military intelligence agency (GRU). You can read more about that sinkholing effort in our original <a href="https://www.shadowserver.org/news/vpnfilter-fbi-sinkholing/">2018 blog post</a>, which includes links to supporting technical information.</p>
<p>Since then, we have continued notifying VPNFilter victims about infected devices worldwide via <a href="https://www.shadowserver.org/what-we-do/network-reporting/">Shadowserver&#x2019;s free daily network reports</a>.</p>
<p>In January 2021, we <a href="https://twitter.com/Shadowserver/status/1351563113716453376">collaborated</a> with partner Trend Micro on a <a href="https://www.trendmicro.com/en_us/research/21/a/vpnfilter-two-years-later-routers-still-compromised-.html">joint analysis of the remaining global VPNFilter victim population</a>. This added some further scan-based insights about third stage victim prevalence into our daily datasets.</p>
<p>From our vantage point as sinkhole operators, the peak day for VPNFilter infections globally was 2018-07-24, which saw 14,966 unique IP addresses being observed hitting the sinkhole. After the initial fairly rapid (by global Internet response standards) remediation of infected devices, a typical long tail of not yet remediated victims still remains today:</p>
<p><a href="/wp-content/uploads/2022/02/20220223-VPNFilter-Sinkhole-Graph.png"><img fetchpriority="high" decoding="async" class="alignnone wp-image-3711 " src="/wp-content/uploads/2022/02/20220223-VPNFilter-Sinkhole-Graph-1024x150.png" alt="" width="1231" height="180"></a></p>
<p><em>Figure 1 &#x2013; Unique sinkholed VPNFIlter IP address per day</em></p>
<p>Note the lack of variation in the daily/weekly number of detected IP addresses. As expected, this suggests that the infected devices are always-on routers (which is unlike the typical overnight or weekend patterns of change we usually see in sinkholed home or office PCs).</p>
<p>The IP-geolocated distribution of sinkholed VPNFilter unique IP addresses on that peak day was:</p>
<p><a href="/wp-content/uploads/2022/02/20180724-VPNFilter-Sinkhole-Treemap.png"><img decoding="async" class="alignnone wp-image-3715 size-large" src="/wp-content/uploads/2022/02/20180724-VPNFilter-Sinkhole-Treemap-1024x483.png" alt="" width="1024" height="483"></a></p>
<p><em>Figure 2 &#x2013; Unique sinkholed VPNFIlter geolocated IP addresses (2018-07-24)</em></p>
<p>With Ukraine having by far and away the most infected victim devices.</p>
<p>This compares to the current sinkholed VPNFilter IP address distribution observed yesterday:</p>
<p><a href="/wp-content/uploads/2022/02/20220222-WatchGuard-Sinkhole-Treemap.png"><img decoding="async" class="alignnone wp-image-3712 size-large" src="/wp-content/uploads/2022/02/20220222-WatchGuard-Sinkhole-Treemap-1024x483.png" alt="" width="1024" height="483"></a></p>

<h2>Cyclops Blink replaces VPNFilter</h2>
<p>On February 23rd 2022, the <a href="https://www.ncsc.gov.uk/news/joint-advisory-shows-new-sandworm-malware-cyclops-blink-replaces-vpnfilter">UK National Cyber Security Centre</a> (NCSC), the <a href="http://cisa.gov/uscert/ncas/alerts/aa22-054a">Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency</a> (CISA), the <a href="https://media.defense.gov/2022/Feb/23/2002943421/-1/-1/0/CSA_NEW_SANDWORM_MALWARE_CYCLOPS_BLINK_REPLACES_VPNFILTER_20220223.PDF">National Security Agency</a> (NSA) and the Federal Bureau of Investigation (FBI) jointly announced that they had identified that the threat actor known as Sandworm or Voodoo Bear has deployed a new, large-scale modular malware framework which is affecting network devices. They have named the malware <b>Cyclops Blink</b>. The NCSC, CISA, FBI and NSA have previously attributed the Sandworm threat actor to the Russian GRU&#x2019;s Main Centre for Special Technologies GTsST.</p>
<p>The Cyclops Blink malware is believed to be a more advanced replacement for VPNFilter. It is installed on exploited network devices as part of a legitimate firmware upgrade, allowing persistence between reboots. The UK NCSC&#x2019;s analysis explains that it is possible to recalculate the Hash-based Message Authentication Code (HMAC) value for the modified firmware image because the WatchGuard FireBox devices use a hard-coded key to initialize the hash calculation. This allows their malware to pass checks and appear to be legitimate, vendor-supplied firmware updates.</p>
<p>Infected victims&#x2019; devices are grouped into clusters, each with a list of Command and Control (C2) servers. They communicate with their operators using Transport Layer Security (TLS) running over The Onion Router (<a href="https://www.torproject.org">Tor</a>) network. To date, Cyclops Blink malware is believed to have been primarily deployed onto <a href="https://www.watchguard.com">WatchGuard</a> firewall devices (which are Linux ELF 32-bit PowerPC big-endian platforms), and all C2 servers identified to date have been for WatchGuard firewalls. However, the assessment published today indicates that it is likely that the Cyclops Blink malware could also be compiled and deployed onto other architectures and firmware. This botnet appears to have been active since at least June 2019.</p>
<p>A detailed technical analysis of Cyclops Blink by the UK NCSC can be found <a href="https://www.ncsc.gov.uk/files/Cyclops-Blink-Malware-Analysis-Report.pdf">here</a>, which includes Indicators of Compromise (IoCs) and YARA signatures to assist in detection.</p>
<h2>Cyclops Blink Malware Remediation</h2>
<p>WatchGuard have provided remediation information for system owners infected with the Cyclops Blink malware:</p>
<p><a href="https://detection.watchguard.com/">https://detection.watchguard.com</a></p>
<p>WatchGuard <a href="https://www.watchguard.com/uk/wgrd-news/blog/important-detection-and-remediation-actions-cyclops-blink-state-sponsored-botnet-0">estimate</a> that Cyclops Blink malware may have infected approximately 1% of WatchGuard firewall devices. They advise that the default configuration is to prevent access to their management interface from the Internet, so this configuration must be manually enabled by the system administrator.</p>
<p>All WatchGuard device owners should follow each step in these instructions to ensure that devices are patched to the latest version and that any infection is removed.</p>
<ul>
<li aria-level="1">If your device is identified as infected with Cyclops Blink, you should assume that any passwords present on the device have been compromised and replace them immediately.</li>
<li aria-level="1">You should ensure that the management interfaces of your network devices are not exposed to the Internet.</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>UPDATES 2022-03-25 and 2022-04-01</strong>: Asus have also provided <a href="https://www.asus.com/content/ASUS-Product-Security-Advisory/">remediation information</a> and <a href="https://www.asus.com/content/ASUS-Product-Security-Advisory/">updated firmaware</a> for impacted customers.</p>
<h2>Shadowserver Cyclops Blink Special Report</h2>
<p>We send out Special Reports whenever we are able to share one-time, high value datasets that we feel should be reported responsibly for maximum public benefit. Although the events included in these Special Reports sometimes fall outside of our <a href="https://www.shadowserver.org/what-we-do/">usual 24-hour daily reporting window</a>, we believe that there would still be significant benefit to our constituents in receiving and hopefully acting on the data.</p>
<p>On February 23rd 2022, we sent out a new Special Report covering network devices that are believed to be likely infected with the Cyclops Blink malware. This one off Cyclops Blink Special Report contained:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong> 1,573</strong> unique victim IP addresses in <strong>495</strong> different Autonomous System Numbers (ASNs) across <strong>70</strong> different countries</li>
<li><strong>25</strong> Command and Control (C2) server IP addresses in <strong>19</strong> different Autonomous System Numbers (ASNs) in <strong>7</strong> different countries</li>
</ul>
<p>A direct link to the Cyclops Blink Special Report format is <a href="/what-we-do/network-reporting/cyclops-blink-special-report/">here</a>.</p>
