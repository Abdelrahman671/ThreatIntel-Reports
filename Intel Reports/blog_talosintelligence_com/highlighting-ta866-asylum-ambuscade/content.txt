    <h1 class="text-center">Highlighting TA866/Asylum Ambuscade Activity Since 2021</h1>

    <div class="text-center m-1">
        <div class="post-author">
            <span>By </span>
                <a href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/author/edmund-brumaghin/">Edmund Brumaghin</a>, 
                <a href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/author/jordyn/">Jordyn Dunk</a>, 
                <a href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/author/nicole/">Nicole Hoffman</a>, 
                <a href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/author/holger-unterbrink/">Holger Unterbrink</a>
        </div>

        <br/>
        <time class="post-datetime" datetime="October 23, 2024 06:02">
            Wednesday, October 23, 2024 06:02
        </time>

        <div class="m-3">
                            <a href="/category/securex-3/" class="category primary-category">

    <section class="post-content-wrapper mt-5">
        <div class="post-content">
            <ul><li><a href="https://malpedia.caad.fkie.fraunhofer.de/actor/ta866" rel="noreferrer noopener"><u>TA866</u></a> (also known as Asylum Ambuscade) is a threat actor that has been conducting intrusion operations since at least 2020.&nbsp;</li><li>TA866 has frequently relied on commodity and custom tooling to facilitate post-compromise activities. These tools often perform specific functions and are deployed and used as needed in the context of specific intrusions.&nbsp;</li><li>Cisco Talos assesses with high <a href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/on-conveying-doubt/" rel="noreferrer noopener"><u>confidence</u></a> that TA866 frequently leverages business relationships with other threat actors across various stages of their attacks to help them achieve their mission objective(s).&nbsp;</li><li>We assess with high confidence that <a href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/warmcookie-analysis/" rel="noreferrer noopener"><u>recent</u></a> post-compromise intrusion activity associated with <a href="https://malpedia.caad.fkie.fraunhofer.de/details/win.warmcookie" rel="noreferrer noopener"><u>WarmCookie/BadSpace</u></a> is related to previous post-compromise activity that we attribute to TA866.&nbsp;</li><li>We assess that WarmCookie was likely developed by the same threat actor that developed the <a href="https://www.esentire.com/blog/esentire-threat-intelligence-malware-analysis-resident-campaign" rel="noreferrer noopener"><u>Resident backdoor</u></a> that was delivered in previous intrusions that we attribute to TA866.&nbsp;</li></ul><h1 id="who-is-ta866">Who is TA866?&nbsp;</h1><p><a href="https://www.proofpoint.com/us/blog/threat-insight/screentime-sometimes-it-feels-like-somebodys-watching-me" rel="noreferrer noopener"><u>TA866</u></a>, also called <a href="https://www.proofpoint.com/us/blog/threat-insight/asylum-ambuscade-state-actor-uses-compromised-private-ukrainian-military-emails" rel="noreferrer noopener"><u>Asylum Ambuscade</u></a>, is a threat actor that has been observed conducting intrusion operations since at least <a href="https://www.trendmicro.com/en_us/research/20/l/stealth-credential-stealer-targets-us-canadian-bank-customers.html" rel="noreferrer noopener"><u>2020</u></a>. TA866 has historically been associated with financially motivated malware campaigns. However, <a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/2023/06/08/asylum-ambuscade-crimeware-or-cyberespionage/" rel="noreferrer noopener"><u>prior reporting</u></a> indicates that they may also conduct espionage-related activities. Cisco Talos has been monitoring and analyzing the malware distribution campaigns, and post-compromise intrusion activity associated with TA866 and has observed continued evolution in the tooling and tactics, techniques and procedures (TTPs) employed by this threat actor since early 2023. &nbsp;</p><p>Throughout 2023, these malware campaigns typically relied on malspam or malvertising to facilitate the delivery of malicious content to potential victims. In many cases, this content is used to redirect victims to traffic distribution systems (TDS), such as 404 TDS, operated by threat actors offering malware installation services. &nbsp;</p><p>This is followed by the deployment of a variety of malicious components. Since at least early 2023, this has typically included WasabiSeed, ScreenShotter and AHK Bot. Based on analysis of post-compromise activity associated with this tooling, we assess with high confidence that TA866 also sometimes deploys a persistent backdoor called <a href="https://www.esentire.com/blog/esentire-threat-intelligence-malware-analysis-resident-campaign" rel="noreferrer noopener"><u>Resident,</u> </a><a href="https://malpedia.caad.fkie.fraunhofer.de/details/win.csharpstreamer" rel="noreferrer noopener"><u>CSharp-Streamer-RAT,</u></a> <a href="https://www.cobaltstrike.com/" rel="noreferrer noopener"><u>Cobalt Strike</u></a> and <a href="https://malpedia.caad.fkie.fraunhofer.de/details/win.rhadamanthys" rel="noreferrer noopener"><u>Rhadamanthys</u></a> on compromised systems. To enable the performance of various post-compromise enumeration and reconnaissance activities, we have also observed the use of utilities such as <a href="https://www.joeware.net/freetools/tools/adfind/" rel="noreferrer noopener"><u>AdFind</u></a> and network scanners. TA866 also commonly deploys remote access solutions on infected systems such as AnyDesk and Remote Utilities.&nbsp;</p><p>We have observed continued ongoing evolution in the implementation of the malware tooling leveraged by TA866 that enables them to operate more effectively once they obtain initial access. This demonstrates an adversary that is constantly evolving as they attempt to gain access to corporate networks and pursue their mission objective(s). &nbsp;</p><p>While analyzing recent <a href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/warmcookie-analysis/" rel="noreferrer noopener"><u>WarmCookie/BadSpace</u></a> activity, we observed a case in early 2024 where Cobalt Strike and CSharp-Streamer-RAT were deployed as follow-on payloads following the initial WarmCookie infection. The SSL certificate used on the CSharp-Streamer-RAT C2 server (<code>185[.]73[.]124[.]164</code>) appeared to have been generated using information programmatically populated using an algorithm defined by the threat actor. This same algorithm appears to have been used on three additional CSharp-Streamer-RAT C2 servers, one of which (<code>109[.]236[.]80[.]191</code>), was the C2 server for a CSharp-Streamer-RAT sample observed in a prior intrusion in 2023 that we attribute to TA866.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p><h1 id="typical-distribution-campaigns">Typical distribution campaigns&nbsp;</h1><p>As previously mentioned, initial access to target environments is typically obtained by TA866 through successfully infecting systems via either malspam or malvertising. Throughout 2022 and 2023, we frequently observed TA866 relying on both methods for initiating the infection process.&nbsp;</p><p>In the case of malspam, we have observed TA866 relying on various lure themes and techniques, including email thread hijacking, a technique where threat actors leverage replies to legitimate email threads that the recipient was previously a part of to increase the legitimacy of the malicious email. Prior <a href="https://www.proofpoint.com/us/blog/threat-insight/security-brief-ta866-returns-large-email-campaign" rel="noreferrer noopener"><u>reporting</u></a> suggests that, in previous campaigns, the malspam may have been associated with a spam botnet operated by <a href="https://malpedia.caad.fkie.fraunhofer.de/actor/ta571" rel="noreferrer noopener"><u>TA571</u></a>.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p><p>In most cases, the threat actor embedded malicious hyperlinks, either directly into the body of the email message, or within an attached document, typically PDFs or Microsoft Publisher files. Below is an example of an email from an earlier TA866 campaign.&nbsp;</p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card"><img src="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/2024/10/data-src-image-3eb42a1f-15fa-4e88-8e5e-21f77c9473cd.png" class="kg-image" alt="" loading="lazy" width="613" height="387" srcset="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/size/w600/2024/10/data-src-image-3eb42a1f-15fa-4e88-8e5e-21f77c9473cd.png 600w, https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/2024/10/data-src-image-3eb42a1f-15fa-4e88-8e5e-21f77c9473cd.png 613w"></figure><p>In the case of malvertising, we have observed instances of users being infected while browsing for legitimate software downloads for applications such as TeamViewer when the infection process began. Prior reporting indicates that TA866 has been observed leveraging malicious Google advertisements and SEO poisoning to infect victims.&nbsp;</p><p>The hyperlinks in these cases pointed to entry points into the 404 TDS. The 404 TDS is a traffic distribution system that enables adversaries to deploy rapidly changing infrastructure which is used to direct potential victims to malicious content, in many cases, malware.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p><p>In the case of 404 TDS, the URLs accessed typically return an <code>HTTP/404</code> error code, but a meta refresh is used to redirect victims to additional intermediary servers. These intermediary servers are typically responsible for identifying/querying information about the visiting systems to determine whether to redirect them to the malicious content or simply to a benign destination such as a search engine or email provider.&nbsp;</p><p>In cases where malicious TDS redirection occurs, victims are delivered malicious payloads, which in the case of analyzed TA866 activity, are typically the malicious JavaScript-based downloaders used to initiate the infection process.&nbsp;</p><h1 id="infection-chains-and-tooling">Infection chains and tooling&nbsp;</h1><p>The most commonly observed infection chain associated with intrusions we attribute to TA866 is typified by the use of multiple distinct stages of custom malware, each responsible for conducting different actions to facilitate additional data gathering, reconnaissance and enable the threat actor flexibility in determining if a given infected system is a high-value target and whether they should operate further in compromised environments. </p><p>We have observed cases where extended periods of time elapse between when the threat actor gains initial access and persistence within compromised environments and the delivery of additional payloads, followed by the conduction of post-compromise activity within the environment. Over time we have observed variations in the infection chains used following initial compromise and assess that TA866 likely chooses to deploy tools in specific situations or target environments as needed while operating towards their longer-term mission objective(s). While variations do exist, we have observed consistent use of various tooling over the past couple of years, as described in the following sections.&nbsp;</p><h2 id="javascript-downloaders">JavaScript downloaders&nbsp;</h2><p>In most observed cases, the infection process begins with the delivery of a malicious JavaScript downloader via the distribution process(es) previously described. This downloader is responsible for retrieving the next stage of the infection chain, which is often MSI packages containing a malware payload called WasabiSeed. The obfuscation used to hide the JavaScript being executed has varied across campaigns over time. An example of one is shown below.&nbsp;</p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card"><img src="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/2024/10/image.png" class="kg-image" alt="" loading="lazy" width="1750" height="872" srcset="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/size/w600/2024/10/image.png 600w, https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/size/w1000/2024/10/image.png 1000w, https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/size/w1600/2024/10/image.png 1600w, https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/2024/10/image.png 1750w" sizes="(min-width: 720px) 720px"></figure><p>This code is responsible for initiating an HTTPS connection to retrieve and execute the WasabiSeed MSI package. In this case, the URL hosting the MSI package was:&nbsp;</p><p>hxxps[:]//perfectsystems-ltd[.]com/x-css/cd.msi</p>

This is performed by a VBScript included in the MSI package delivered to infected systems.&nbsp;</p><p>During execution, the MSI creates a subfolder within <code>%PROGRAMDATA%</code> and copies a malicious VBScript into this location. The name of the subdirectory and VBScript file varies across analyzed samples.&nbsp;</p><p>A <code>CustomAction[.]idt</code> is defined, which executes the VBScript using <code>wscript[.]exe</code> when the MSI is run. The VBScript is stored in a CAB archive contained within the MSI package. Persistence is achieved via the use of an LNK shortcut that is dropped into the Startup directory on the system, ensuring that WasabiSeed is executed each time the system reboots. When run, it continuously reaches out to obtain arbitrary payloads in the form of MSI packages that are then executed by MsiExec to infect systems with additional malware.&nbsp;</p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card"><img src="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/2024/10/image-1.png" class="kg-image" alt="" loading="lazy" width="1750" height="556" srcset="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/size/w600/2024/10/image-1.png 600w, https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/size/w1000/2024/10/image-1.png 1000w, https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/size/w1600/2024/10/image-1.png 1600w, https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/2024/10/image-1.png 1750w" sizes="(min-width: 720px) 720px"></figure><p>The URL used by this payload retrieval process is randomized using the drive serial number of the infected system, making it unique to each system. This continuous polling allows the delivery of arbitrary payloads at the discretion of the threat actor at any point following initial access. In most cases, we observed subsequent delivery of an additional MSI containing a malware tool called Screenshotter.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p><h2 id="screenshotter">Screenshotter&nbsp;</h2><p>Screenshotter is a malware family used to generate periodic screenshots from infected systems which are transmitted to the threat actor over HTTP. We have observed the delivery of multiple variants of Screenshotter and have identified implementations of the malware in a variety of programming languages, including JavaScript and Python.&nbsp;</p><p>We also identified an implementation of Screenshotter using an AutoHotKey script, likely to enable this functionality directly within AHK Bot, which is also often delivered during the infection process and described in the next section.&nbsp;</p><p>In both the JavaScript and Python implementations of Screenshotter, the malware is delivered within an MSI package.
