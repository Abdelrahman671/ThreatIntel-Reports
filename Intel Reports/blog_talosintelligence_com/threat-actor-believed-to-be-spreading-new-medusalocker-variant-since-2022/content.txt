    <h1 class="text-center">Threat actor believed to be spreading new MedusaLocker variant since 2022</h1>

    <div class="text-center m-1">
        <div class="post-author">
            <span>By </span>
                <a href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/author/tiago-pereira/">Tiago Pereira</a>, 
                <a href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/author/arnaud/">Arnaud Zobec</a>
        </div>

        <br/>
        <time class="post-datetime" datetime="October 3, 2024 06:00">
            Thursday, October 3, 2024 06:00
        </time>

        <div class="m-3">
                            <a href="/category/ransomware/" class="category primary-category">
                                ransomware
                            </a>
                                                    <a href="/category/threats/" class="category primary-category">

                            </a>

                            </a>
                                </div>
    </div>

    <section class="post-content-wrapper mt-5">
        <div class="post-content">
            <ul><li>Cisco Talos has discovered a financially motivated threat actor, active since 2022, recently observed delivering a MedusaLocker ransomware variant.&nbsp;</li><li>Intelligence collected by Talos on tools regularly employed by the threat actor allows us to see an estimate of the amount and countries of origin of this group’s victims. This actor has been active since at least late 2022 and targets organizations worldwide, although the number of victims was higher than average in EU countries until mid-2023 and, since then, in South American countries.</li><li>This threat actor was observed distributing a MedusaLocker ransomware variant known as “BabyLockerKZ.” This variant is compiled with a PDB path containing the word “paid_memes” which is also present in other tools observed during the attacks, presumably by the same author.</li><li>Talos has new information on the attacker’s tools, including BabyLockerKz and attacker TTPs and IOCs to assist in detecting and preventing further attacks.</li></ul><p>Talos has recently observed an attack leading to the deployment of a MedusaLocker ransomware variant known as “BabyLockerKZ.” The distinguishable techniques — including consistently storing the same set of tools in the same location on compromised systems, the use of tools that have the PDB path with the string “paid_memes,” and the use of a lateral movement tool named “checker” — used in the attack led us to take a deeper look to try to understand more about this threat actor.&nbsp;</p><p>This attacker uses several publicly known attack tools and living-off-the-land binaries (LoLBins), a set of tools built by the same developer (possibly the attacker) to assist in credential theft and lateral movement in compromised organizations. These tools are mostly wrappers around publicly available tools that include additional functionality to streamline the attack process and provide graphical or command-line interfaces.&nbsp;</p><p>The same developer built the MedusaLocker variant used in the initial attack. This variant that uses the same chat and leak site URLs contains several differences to the original MedusaLocker ransomware, such as a different autorun key or an extra public and private key set stored in the registry. Based on the name of the autorun key, the attackers call this variant “BabyLockerKZ.”&nbsp;</p><p>We assess with medium confidence that the actor is financially motivated, likely working as an IAB or an affiliate of a ransomware cartel, and has been carrying out attacks since at least 2022. Our telemetry indicates that the actor opportunistically targeted many victims worldwide. In late 2022 and early 2023, most victims were in European countries, but since the first quarter of 2023, the group’s focus shifted toward South American countries and, as a result, the number of victims per month almost doubled.</p><h1 id="tracking-babylockerkz-across-the-globe">Tracking BabyLockerKZ across the globe</h1><p>Intelligence collected by Talos on tools regularly employed by the threat actor allows us to estimate the number of, and the countries of origin of the victims. Although this is unlikely to capture all of the adversary’s activities, it still provides a look at a specific window of activity.</p><p>The actor has been active since at least October 2022. At that time, the targets were mostly located in European countries such as France, Germany, Spain or Italy. During the second&nbsp; quarter of 2023, the attack volume per month almost doubled, and the group shifted its focus toward South American countries such as Brazil, Mexico, Argentina and Colombia, as shown in the chart below. The attacks kept a steady volume of around 200 unique IPs compromised per month until the first quarter of 2024 when the attacks decreased.</p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card"><img src="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/2024/10/medusa-chart.jpg" class="kg-image" alt="" loading="lazy" width="1800" height="1022" srcset="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/size/w600/2024/10/medusa-chart.jpg 600w, https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/size/w1000/2024/10/medusa-chart.jpg 1000w, https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/size/w1600/2024/10/medusa-chart.jpg 1600w, https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/2024/10/medusa-chart.jpg 1800w" sizes="(min-width: 720px) 720px"></figure><p>The actor has consistently compromised a large number of organizations, often more than 100 per month, since at least 2022. This reveals the professional and highly aggressive nature of the attacks and is coherent with the activity we would expect from an IAB or ransomware affiliate.</p><h1 id="attacker-ttps-and-tools">Attacker TTPs and tools</h1><p>During the attack leading to the deployment of the BabyLockerKZt, the adversary used several publicly known attack tools and others that could be unique to this actor. The group frequently used the Music, Pictures or Documents user folders of compromised systems to store attack tools. For example, the following paths were used to store tools during this attack:</p><ul><li>c:\users\&lt;user&gt;\music\advanced_port_scanner_2.5.3869.exe</li><li>c:\users\&lt;user&gt;\music\hrsword\hrsword install.bat</li><li>c:\users\&lt;user&gt;\music\killav\build.004\disabler.exe</li><li>c:/users/&lt;user&gt;/music/checker/checker(222).exe</li><li>c:/users/&lt;user&gt;/music/checker/invoke-thehash.ps1</li><li>c:/users/&lt;user&gt;/music/checker/checker (222).exe</li><li>c:/users/&lt;user&gt;/music/checker/invoke-smbexec.ps1</li><li>c:/users/&lt;user&gt;/music/checker/invoke-wmiexec.ps1</li><li>c:/users/&lt;user&gt;/appdata/roaming/ntsystem/ntlhost.exe.exe</li><li>c:/users/&lt;user&gt;/appdata/local/temp/advanced port scanner 2/advanced_port_scanner.exe</li><li>c:/users/&lt;user&gt;/appdata/local/temp/is-juad3.tmp/advanced_port_scanner_2.5.3869.tmp</li></ul><p>These are similar to a previous attack leading to MedusaLocker ransomware, documented by <a href="https://asec.ahnlab.com/en/48940/"><u>ASEC</u></a> in February 2023, which our telemetry suggests was a more active period for this threat actor.</p><p>Some of the publicly known tools used by the attacker are:</p><ul><li>HRSword_v5.0.1.1.rar: A tool used to disable AV and EDR software.</li><li>Advanced_Port_Scanner_2.5.3869.exe: A network-scanning tool with several additional features to map internal networks and devices.</li><li>Netscan.exe: SoftPerfect Network Scanner: A tool similar to Advanced Port Scanner.</li><li>Processhacker.exe: Process Monitoring and administration software. Allows a TA to enumerate and control processes running on the infected endpoint.</li><li>PCHunter64.exe: A tool similar to processhacker.</li><li>Mimikatz: A tool to dump Windows user credentials from memory.</li></ul><p>While most of the tools the attacker uses are publicly available, they also use some tools that are not widely distributed that streamline the attack process by automating the interaction between popular attack tools (e.g., Mimikatz, Invoke-the-hash, PSEXEC, RDP) and by adding convenient functionality and interfaces. One of these tools, called “Checker” used in an attack that deployed BabyLockerKZ, consisted of pivotal characteristics of BabyLockerKZ, the “Checker” tool has a PDB path containing the string “<strong>paid_memes</strong>”. Pivoting off this string, we identified files on VirusTotal, of which most are BabyLockerKZ samples. We also discovered several other tools, which we’ll outline below.</p><h2 id="checker-tool">Checker tool</h2><p>Checker (E:\<strong>paid_memes</strong>\wmi_smb_rdp_checker\Release\checker.pdb) is an app that bundles several other freely available apps and provides a GUI for management of credentials as the attackers proceed with lateral movement. In particular it contains a set of tools:</p><ul><li>Remote Desktop Plus</li><li>PSEXEC</li><li>MIMIKATZ</li></ul><p>And a set of scripts based on the <a href="https://github.com/Kevin-Robertson/Invoke-TheHash"><u>Invoke-TheHash tool</u></a>.</p><p>The tool also contains a GUI, as shown below, and a database to store the credentials.</p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card"><img src="https://lh7-rt.googleusercontent.com/docsz/AD_4nXfjiW3fsyDxyUZOWORVrBr9p_4kQhBXq9dZZIjcUliNNJtP_09MWgUG8JJTBgnHGyMkncRSV_j6cYtndUUlAuy8ywcRIyFLvZOTrqEBh3GD7UWkrxQts1GD4GszfrsnTnWDOMkeUraVld_A9yWUk0ynWuw?key=DVE37h6YIpCMeCZlcKyX-Q" class="kg-image" alt="" loading="lazy" width="624" height="416"></figure><p>As the image illustrates, the tool can be used to scan IPs for valid credentials using several protocols/techniques (PSEXEC, RDP, SMB and WMI) and is prepared to import data from lists of hosts and some of the tools in the attacker toolset, such as Mimikatz, as well as an advanced port scanner. The tool can also decrypt hashes and offers the convenience of a GUI to store a database of the hosts and respective credentials that have been obtained or verified.</p><h2 id="pth-project">PTH project</h2><p>The PTH (D:\Projects\<strong>paid_memes</strong>\PTH\Release\PTH.pdb) name suggests the pass-the-hash technique to use NTLM hashes to authenticate remotely without having to crack the password. Looking at its resources it embeds:</p><ul><li>Invoke-SMBClient.ps1</li><li>Invoke-SMBEnum.ps1</li><li>Invoke-SMBExec.ps1</li><li>Invoke-TheHash.ps1</li><li>Invoke-WMIExec.ps1</li></ul><p>These were also used in the checker tool and are part of Invoke-TheHash. According to the author:&nbsp;</p><p><em>“Invoke-TheHash contains PowerShell functions for performing pass the hash WMI and SMB tasks. WMI and SMB connections are accessed through the .NET TCPClient. Authentication is performed by passing an NTLM hash into the NTLMv2 authentication protocol. Local administrator privilege is not required client-side.”</em></p><h2 id="mimik-tool">MIMIK tool</h2><p>MIMIK (D:\Projects\<strong>paid_memes</strong>\mimik\Release\stub_mimik.pdb) is a wrapper around Mimikatz and rclone that can be used to steal credentials and automatically upload them to an attacker-controlled server. The following image shows the terminal output for the tool.</p><p>The following command lines are examples of commands executed via the tool:</p><ul><li>64.exe privilege::debug sekurlsa::logonPasswords token::elevate lsadump::sam full exit&nbsp;</li><li>C:\Users\user\Desktop\64.exe 64.exe "privilege::debug" "sekurlsa::logonPasswords" "token::elevate" "lsadump::sam full" exit&nbsp;</li><li>64.exe "privilege::debug" "sekurlsa::logonPasswords" "token::elevate" "lsadump::sam full" exit</li><li>C:\Users\user\Desktop\rclone.exe rclone rcd --rc-no-auth --bwlimit=30M</li><li>C:\Users\user\Desktop\rclone.exe rclone rc operations/stat</li></ul><h1 id="babylockerkz">BabyLockerKZ</h1><p>BabyLockerKZ is a variant of MedusaLocker that has been around at least since late 2023 and has been analyzed by other researchers, although not specifically called out as a MedusaLocker variant with this name.&nbsp;</p><p>A <a href="https://www.cynet.com/blog/dynamic-analysis-hazard-ransomware/"><u>Cynet blog post</u></a> on the malware used the name “Hazard” for a MedusaLocker variant (named after the extension used for encrypted files) and mentions the existence of the BabyLockerKZ registry key.&nbsp;</p><p>Another post from <a href="https://whitehat.eu/medusalocker-ransomware-part-2/"><u>Whitehat</u></a> mentions the existence of PAIDMEMES PUBLIC and PRIVATE registry keys on a MedusaLocker sample.&nbsp;</p><p>This variant has not been given much attention outside of that, though, possibly because it’s highly similar to MedusaLocker or because it uses the same chat and leak sites as MedusaLocker. But there are several notable differences between BabyLockerKZ and MedusaLocker, such as:</p><ul><li>No {8761ABBD-7F85-42EE-B272-A76179687C63} mutex.</li><li>No MDSLK reg key.</li><li>The PAIDMEMES Public and private keys.</li><li>The BabyLockerKZ run key.</li></ul><p>The use of the PAIDMEMES public and private keys is unclear. In their post, Whitehat mentioned that they believe the keys aren’t necessary for the encryption process, as the Linux version doesn’t use them. Further research into the use of these keys might be a topic for another blog post.</p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card"><img src="https://lh7-rt.googleusercontent.com/docsz/AD_4nXcEYkLT85jeeDYQoe4eTuWqVHmBbT8hEAAUsNSRkF_EbLafZ6SAa78eevTAvKIHLCDOwKkHMq_kPk_zXfKsromjyrauCkYkgUz9qe9ciBozJ_bJTIHxpPOHB8h5mqlnREJWtKr2eXe2Y_4UwlcXiKyCnR8?key=DVE37h6YIpCMeCZlcKyX-Q" class="kg-image" alt="" loading="lazy" width="624" height="548"></figure><h1 id="coverage">Coverage</h1><p>Ways our customers can detect and block this threat are listed below.</p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card"><img src="https://lh7-rt.googleusercontent.com/docsz/AD_4nXex3iSXINRzhb7l_4nC8Lojkxk6jLEjLPMhmoYJxRpzyii3XCvwhG5IyvsP8oEJuyJPa50pYlRonrGxZlA41512RfuaKoEYXS4-QCzQT-cV9X-0fA9bwWqcJVt8GpIZqG6p_EGnd4-gFWNZF_z6z_5GOY8?key=DVE37h6YIpCMeCZlcKyX-Q" class="kg-image" alt="" loading="lazy" width="624" height="127"></figure><p><a href="https://www.cisco.com/c/en/us/products/security/amp-for-endpoints/index.html"><u>Cisco Secure Endpoint</u></a> (formerly AMP for Endpoints) is ideally suited to prevent the execution of the malware detailed in this post.
