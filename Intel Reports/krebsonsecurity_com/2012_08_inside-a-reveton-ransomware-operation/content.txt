					<h1 class="entry-title">Inside a &#8216;Reveton&#8217; Ransomware Operation</h1>

							<div class="entry-content">
												<p>The <strong>U.S Federal Bureau of Investigation</strong> is warning about an uptick in online extortion scams that impersonate the FBI and frighten people into paying fines to avoid prosecution for supposedly downloading child pornography and pirated content. This post offers an inside look at one malware gang responsible for orchestrating such scams.</p>
<div id="attachment_16396" style="width: 295px" class="wp-caption alignright"><a class="lightbox" href="https://krebsonsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/08/reveton.png"><img aria-describedby="caption-attachment-16396" decoding="async" class=" wp-image-16396" title="reveton" src="https://krebsonsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/08/reveton-285x319.png" alt="Reveton ransomware scam impersonating FBI" width="285" height="278" /></a><p id="caption-attachment-16396" class="wp-caption-text">Reveton ransomware scam page impersonating the FBI</p></div>
<p>In <a title="http://www.fbi.gov/news/stories/2012/august/new-internet-scam/new-internet-scam?utm_campaign=email-Immediate&amp;utm_medium=email&amp;utm_source=fbi-top-stories&amp;utm_content=129647" href="http://www.fbi.gov/news/stories/2012/august/new-internet-scam/new-internet-scam?utm_campaign=email-Immediate&amp;utm_medium=email&amp;utm_source=fbi-top-stories&amp;utm_content=129647" target="_blank">an alert</a> published last week, the FBI said that <a title="www.ic3.gov" href="http://www.ic3.gov" target="_blank">The Internet Crime Complaint Center</a> &#8212; a partnership between the FBI and the <a title="http://www.nw3c.org/" href="http://www.nw3c.org/" target="_blank">National White Collar Crime Center</a> &#8212; was &#8220;getting inundated with complaints&#8221; from consumers targeted or victimized by the scam, which uses drive-by downloads to hijack host machines. The downloaded malware displays a threatening message (see image to the right) and blocks the user from doing anything else unless he pays the fine or finds a way to remove the program.</p>
<p>The FBI alert said the attacks have surged with the help of a &#8220;new drive-by virus&#8221; called <strong>Reveton</strong>; in fact, Reveton and its ilk are hardly new. These types of attacks have been around for years, but traditionally have targeted European users. The scam pages used in the attacks mimic official notices from various national police or investigatory agencies, corresponding to the country in which the victim resides. For a breakdown of these Reveton-related ransomware scam pages by country, see <a title="https://www.botnets.fr/index.php/Reveton" href="https://www.botnets.fr/index.php/Reveton" target="_blank">this comprehensive gallery set up at botnets.fr</a>.</p>
<p><a title="http://www.microsoft.com/security/portal/Threat/Encyclopedia/Entry.aspx?Name=Trojan:Win32/Reveton.A" href="http://www.microsoft.com/security/portal/Threat/Encyclopedia/Entry.aspx?Name=Trojan:Win32/Reveton.A" target="_blank">Reveton.A</a> is blamed in these most recent attacks, and the FBI said it appears Reveton is being distributed in conjunction with <strong>Citadel</strong>, an offshoot of the ZeuS Trojan that I have <a title="Collaboration Fuels Rapid Growth of Citadel Trojan" href="http://krebsonsecurity.com/2012/02/collaboration-feuls-rapdid-growth-of-citadel-trojan/" target="_blank">written</a> <a title="Citadel-trojan-touts-trouble-ticket-system/" href="http://krebsonsecurity.com/2012/01/citadel-trojan-touts-trouble-ticket-system/" target="_blank">about</a> on several occasions. It is certainly possible that crooks are using Citadel to deploy Reveton, but as I&#8217;ll illustrate below, it seems more likely that the attackers in these cases are using exploit kits like <strong>BlackHole</strong> to plant both threats on victim PCs.</p>
<p><span style="text-decoration: underline;">INSIDE A REVETON MALWARE GANG</span></p>
<div id="attachment_16413" style="width: 610px" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><a class="lightbox" href="https://krebsonsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/08/revetondist.png"><img aria-describedby="caption-attachment-16413" decoding="async" loading="lazy" class="size-large wp-image-16413" title="revetondist" src="https://krebsonsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/08/revetondist-600x306.png" alt="" width="600" height="306" srcset="https://krebsonsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/08/revetondist-600x306.png 600w, https://krebsonsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/08/revetondist-285x145.png 285w, https://krebsonsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/08/revetondist.png 1205w" sizes="(max-width: 600px) 100vw, 600px" /></a><p id="caption-attachment-16413" class="wp-caption-text">Operations of one Reveton crime group. Source: &#8216;Kafeine,&#8217; from botnets.fr.</p></div>
<p>At least that&#8217;s the behavior that&#8217;s been observed by a ragtag group of researchers that has been tracking Reveton activity for many months. Some of the researchers are associated with botnets.fr, but they&#8217;ve asked to remain nameless because of the sensitivity of their work. One of them, who goes by the screen name &#8220;Kafeine,&#8221; said much of the Reveton activity traces back to a group that is controlling the operation using <a title="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reverse_proxy" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reverse_proxy" target="_blank">reverse proxies</a> at dozens of servers scattered across data centers globally (see <a title="http://krebsonsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/08/Reveton-Distribution-Scheme-1st-May-20111.pdf" href="http://krebsonsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/08/Reveton-Distribution-Scheme-1st-May-20111.pdf" target="_blank">this PDF</a> for a more detailed look at the image above).</p>
<p>Kafeine said the groups involved in spreading Reveton are constantly fine-tuning all aspects of their operations, from the scam pages to solidifying their back-end hosting infrastructure. The latest versions of Reveton, for example, serve the scam pages from an encrypted (https://) connection, and only cough up the pages when an infected machine visits and sends a special request.<span id="more-16393"></span></p>
<p>Some readers may have a hard time understanding how such schemes could be successful. To those folks, I say consider the lucrative operations of the <a title="http://krebsonsecurity.com/2011/08/huge-decline-in-fake-av-following-credit-card-processing-shakeup/" href="http://krebsonsecurity.com/2011/08/huge-decline-in-fake-av-following-credit-card-processing-shakeup/" target="_blank">once-mighty</a> <a title="http://krebsonsecurity.com/2011/03/chronopays-scareware-diaries/" href="http://krebsonsecurity.com/2011/03/chronopays-scareware-diaries/" target="_blank">scareware</a> <a title="http://krebsonsecurity.com/2011/08/fake-antivirus-industry-down-but-not-out/" href="http://krebsonsecurity.com/2011/08/fake-antivirus-industry-down-but-not-out/" target="_blank">industry</a>, which similarly hijacks infected machines with warnings about malware until the victim relents and pays for some worthless and fake cleaner program.</p>
<p>Kafeine shared a couple of screen shots of two similar and recent ransomware scams (not Reveton-related) targeting European users that shows just how successful these scams can be. Both of these images were obtained when security researchers stumbled upon statistics pages maintained by the criminal groups running the scheme.</p>
<div id="attachment_16403" style="width: 295px" class="wp-caption alignright"><a class="lightbox" href="https://krebsonsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/08/weelsof1.png"><img aria-describedby="caption-attachment-16403" decoding="async" loading="lazy" class="size-medium wp-image-16403" title="weelsof1" src="https://krebsonsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/08/weelsof1-285x148.png" alt="" width="285" height="148" srcset="https://krebsonsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/08/weelsof1-285x148.png 285w, https://krebsonsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/08/weelsof1-600x311.png 600w, https://krebsonsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/08/weelsof1.png 826w" sizes="(max-width: 285px) 100vw, 285px" /></a><p id="caption-attachment-16403" class="wp-caption-text">Ransomware earnings on 5/17/2012</p></div>
<p>The one on the right, for instance, shows that the attackers managed to get their malware installed on 2,116 PCs in France, and of those, only 3.7 percent &#8212; 79 victims &#8212; opted to pay to rid their machines of the ransomware. But those 79 victims each paid $100, earning the miscreants 7,800 Euros.</p>
<p>That&#8217;s the haul from just one country; bear in mind that this stats page shows the total take from a single day (May 17, 2012). According to these stats, at least 322 people from all countries they ran the scam in opted to pay the ransom that day, earning the attackers more than €28,000 (~$34,500)! The next day (the screen shot below left), the miscreants earned €43,750 (~$54,000).</p>
<div id="attachment_16404" style="width: 295px" class="wp-caption alignleft"><a class="lightbox" href="https://krebsonsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/08/weelsof2.png"><img aria-describedby="caption-attachment-16404" decoding="async" loading="lazy" class="size-medium wp-image-16404" title="weelsof2" src="https://krebsonsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/08/weelsof2-285x141.png" alt="" width="285" height="141" srcset="https://krebsonsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/08/weelsof2-285x141.png 285w, https://krebsonsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/08/weelsof2-600x297.png 600w, https://krebsonsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/08/weelsof2.png 806w" sizes="(max-width: 285px) 100vw, 285px" /></a><p id="caption-attachment-16404" class="wp-caption-text">Ransomware earnings 5/18/2012</p></div>
<p>Unlike scareware scams, ransomware schemes do not rely on credit card payments from victims &#8212; a key pressure point for squashing affiliate programs that help spread this crud. Most previous ransomware schemes have used alternative payment systems such as <a title="ukash.com" href="http://ukash.com" target="_blank">Ukash</a> and <a title="paysafecard.com" href="http://paysafecard.com" target="_blank">Paysafe</a>. The Reveton attacks that spoof the FBI instruct victims to pay their &#8220;fines&#8221; via <a title="moneypak.com" href="http://moneypak.com" target="_blank">MoneyPak</a>, which allows people who don&#8217;t have bank accounts to send money and pay bills at participating businesses. MoneyPak cards are available for purchase at Wal-Mart, CVS and other retailers, and can be reloaded with cash, and can be used to send money to <strong>PayPal</strong> accounts, prepaid credit cards, and to pay bills for some cell phone companies and <strong>DirectTV</strong>.</p>
<p>I mentioned earlier that most of these Reveton attacks that have been tracked so far used versions of the BlackHole exploit kit to deploy the malware. These are kits that are stitched into hacked or malicious Web sites, so that all visiting browsers are checked for a variety of insecure, outdated plugins, from <strong>Flash</strong> to <strong>Java</strong> to <strong>Adobe Reader</strong>. Browsers that are found vulnerable will be handed a Trojan downloader that fetches Reveton and most likely a copy of the password-stealing Citadel/ZeuS Trojan.</p>
<p>Kafeine and his fellow researchers recently gained access to one of the three main BlackHole exploit panels used by a Reveton malware gang. The screen shot below shows the BlackHole administration page; in the upper left portion of the image, we get a sense of how much traffic these crooks see on any given day. It shows that in just one day, the exploit kit was sent more than 187,000 potential victims, and that more than 11,000 of those were successfully seeded with Reveton. The &#8220;exploits&#8221; stats in the upper right portion of the image show that, once again, <a title="http://krebsonsecurity.com/2012/07/new-java-exploit-to-debut-in-blackhole-exploit-kits/" href="http://krebsonsecurity.com/2012/07/new-java-exploit-to-debut-in-blackhole-exploit-kits/" target="_blank">insecure and outdated installations</a> of <strong>Java</strong> remain by far the most popular vehicle for exploiting PCs.</p>
<div id="attachment_16426" style="width: 610px" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><a class="lightbox" href="https://krebsonsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/08/revetonBHEKit.png"><img aria-describedby="caption-attachment-16426" decoding="async" loading="lazy" class="size-large wp-image-16426" title="revetonBHEKit" src="https://krebsonsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/08/revetonBHEKit-600x548.png" alt="" width="600" height="548" srcset="https://krebsonsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/08/revetonBHEKit-600x548.png 600w, https://krebsonsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/08/revetonBHEKit-285x260.png 285w, https://krebsonsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/08/revetonBHEKit.png 804w" sizes="(max-width: 600px) 100vw, 600px" /></a><p id="caption-attachment-16426" class="wp-caption-text">A recent screenshot of a BlackHole exploit kit panel used by a group spreading Reveton. Source: Kafeine from botnets.fr</p></div>
<p>A number of Web sites include instructions for removing the Reveton malware without having to pay the ransom (here&#8217;s <a title="http://www.f-secure.com/v-descs/trojan_w32_reveton.shtml" href="http://www.f-secure.com/v-descs/trojan_w32_reveton.shtml" target="_blank">one example</a>). But it&#8217;s important for readers to understand that if you have been hit by a ransomware attack, the ransomware component is almost certainly just the most visible of the threats that reside on your system. For one thing, Kafeine said, the latest Reveton versions will steal all passwords stored on the victim&#8217;s PC. What&#8217;s more, the FBI&#8217;s report indicates Reveton is being bundled with Citadel, which is an extremely powerful and advanced family of malware that can be quite difficult to remove.</p>
<p>Attacks such as Reveton illustrate the need to have a solid plan for backing up your data, because the surest way to clean a machine infected with the likes of Reveton is to completely reinstall Windows (from the <a title="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Master_boot_record" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Master_boot_record" target="_blank">Master Boot Record</a> on up). The most advanced ransomware threats (the subject of a future post) will steal your passwords and then encrypt all of your important files before demanding a ransom payment.</p>

			<section class="comment-content comment">
				<p>That&#8217;s good advice. It&#8217;s very similar to what I do. I advise the backup procedures be started when the PC is first purchased, otherwise reinstall OS or restore to factory state. Here&#8217;s the process for other readers.</p>
<p>1. Purchase PC or get it to factory state.<br />
2. Before online, strip any unneeded bloat/crapware &amp; turn on all security features/services.<br />
 2-1. Be sure to strip Java and Adobe Reader<br />
3. Get online &amp; run Microsoft&#8217;s updates. Do nothing until these finish. Reboot as necessary.<br />
4. If you bought security software, install it now &amp; update it immediately.<br />
5. Get the needed third party apps. Good browser &amp; security plugins first. Then Foxit PDF, security suite, backup app, DivX, VLC, Office/OpenOffice, 7-Zip&#8230; and so on.<br />
6. Run the update procedure on all of them.<br />
7. I also say run each once to get past the &#8220;first use&#8221; stuff, do file associations, set long term settings, etc.<br />
8. Get offline &amp; immediately do a full backup. Ideally, your backup software can also split the backup into pieces that can each fit on a DVD. This ensures malware can&#8217;t infect backup on, say, a USB hard disk.<br />
 8-1. If your software supports it, create a boot CD for the backup system so you can do a backup or restore without loading windows. It&#8217;s safer.</p>
<p>(Also, OS&#8217;s do weird stuff behind the scenes sometimes. Or all the time, depending on who you ask. Making sure the OS is &#8220;off&#8221; by using the boot disc might eliminate any problems the OS might cause that have nothing to do with malware &amp; corrupt your backup anyway.)</p>
<p>Regarding OS vs Data, I agree with RSH that it&#8217;s best to keep them separate. Many keep them on separate partitions. Put the OS &amp; main apps on one, then your music etc on the other. I typically do a disk image style backup for the OS partition &amp; a file-level backup for data. There are some good free tools available, with step by step guides online, for creating partitions. It&#8217;s easy enough for a non-expert to do. Just don&#8217;t change it after making a disk image backup. 😉</p>
<p>Maintaining the &#8220;Good&#8221; Backup. It&#8217;s a pain, but here&#8217;s a safe procedure.</p>
<p>1. Backup all Data to an external hard disk or something. Remember bookmarks, contacts, whatever.<br />
2. Make notes of any important settings you need to manually set later.<br />
3. Use one of the boot CD&#8217;s to boot into your backup software. Recover your last good backup from it. (Preferrably from DVD&#8217;s)<br />
4. Microsoft/Windows Update first. Then update 3rd party software.<br />
5. Install any new software &amp; manually apply recent settings.<br />
6. Import bookmarks, contacts, etc.<br />
7. Use boot CD to make a new full backup from this system.</p>
<p>The idea is you start in a known &#8220;clean&#8221; copy. Apply all updates to make it safer. Then, import and set everything to bring it up to recent situation. Then, make a full backup again.</p>

				<cite class="fn">na </cite><a href="https://krebsonsecurity.com/2012/08/inside-a-reveton-ransomware-operation/#comment-97547"><time datetime="2012-08-16T03:25:27-04:00">August 16, 2012</time></a>			</header><!-- .comment-meta -->

			
			<section class="comment-content comment">
				<p>Brain, you have some pretty significant errors/typo/incorrect information in this blog post&#8230; </p>
<p>At first glance one would assume the &#8220;Conversion&#8221; meant percent of installs that yielded pins. However unless these guys are using servers that don&#8217;t know how to count properly it is not very difficult to see this is not the case.</p>
<p>For example from your first screenshot:</p>
<p>From Austria there was 375 installs and 11 Pins so the % of installs which yielded pins was 11/375 or 2.93% which != 2.4% found in the &#8220;Conversion&#8221; field.<br />
From Sweden there was 735 installs and 45 Pins so the % of installs which yielded pins was 45/735 or 6.12% which != 3.61% found in the &#8220;Conversion&#8221; field.<br />
From France there was 2116 installs and 79 pins so the % of installs which yielded pins was 79/2116 or 3.73% which != 3.69% found in the &#8220;Conversion&#8221; field.<br />
&#8230;<br />
and so on, none of the %s line up with a percent based on the # of installs that yielded pins for any given country.</p>
<p>Then the question is left to ask, what is this mystery % all about? And the answer: Many people enter fake pins instead of paying to unlock. If you have never looked at a panel like this before (it is common to have a % or total # of pins that work out of submitted pins) then it would be easy to assume otherwise.</p>
<p>Now that you understand the &#8220;Conversion&#8221; tab stands for a percent of valid pins out of all submitted for that country see below.</p>
<p>For the first day in question there was a total of 28,100 EUR recorded this would only be the income for that day if EVERY pin was valid. However, you can see to the right of the &#8220;Amount&#8221; field is the &#8220;Conversion&#8221; field &#8212; None of these #s are near to 100%. The final &#8220;overall&#8221; conversion rate was 2.9%. This means the profit for the day in question was 2.9% of 28,100 EUR (IE: 814.9EUR).</p>
<p>Looking at the second screenshot the total for the day was 43,750 EUR, but with a 2.84% conversion rate. So only 2.84% of 43,750 EUR or 1242.5 EUR was the income for the second day.</p>
<p>There is also another error in this post where you talked about the victims from france. See Below&#8230;</p>
<p>&#8220;malware installed on 2,116 PCs in France, and of those, only 3.7 percent — 79 victims — opted to pay to rid their machines of the ransomware. But those 79 victims each paid $100, earning the miscreants 7,800 Euros.&#8221;</p>
<p>If each victim paid $100 that would be $7900 which is 6386.41 EUR (not 7800 EUR). If the locker was ran on *EU* systems it will ask for 100 EUR, but if on *US* 100$. You can note 79 * 100 doesn&#8217;t add up to 7800. This is because along with many people entering &#8220;fake&#8221; unlock codes for their computers others will only opt to purchase a voucher worth 1/2 the requested amount or some other value. My guess would be 2/79 only bought 50 EUR vouchers which would explain how the total for 79 vouches was only 7800 EUR.</p>
