	<div id="primary" class="site-content">
		<div id="content" role="main">

			
					<article id="post-31043" class="post-31043 post type-post status-publish format-standard hentry category-sunshine category-data-breaches tag-carefirst-bluecross-blueshield-breach tag-empireblue-bcbs-breach tag-premera tag-prennera tag-threatconnect tag-we11point tag-wellpoint">
				<header class="entry-header">
					<div class="clear"></div>
	<div class="btm-wrap">
	<div class="below-title-meta">
		<div class="adt">
			
			
				
				<span class="date updated">May 21, 2015</span>
					
        </div>
		<div class="adt-comment">
		<span><a class="link-comments" href="https://krebsonsecurity.com/2015/05/carefirst-blue-cross-breach-hits-1-1m/#comments">60 Comments</a></span> 
        </div>    <div class="clear"></div>	   
    </div><!-- below title meta end -->
	</div>
		
						</header><!-- .entry-header -->
		
		
							<div class="entry-content">
												<p><strong>CareFirst BlueCross BlueShield</strong> on Wednesday said it had been hit with a data breach that compromised the personal information on approximately 1.1 million customers. There are indications that the same attack methods may have been used in this intrusion as with breaches at <strong>Anthem</strong> and <strong>Premera</strong>, incidents that collectively involved data on more than 90 million Americans.</p>
<p><a href="https://krebsonsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/carefirst.png"><img decoding="async" class="alignright  wp-image-31047" src="https://krebsonsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/carefirst.png" alt="carefirst" width="246" height="217" /></a>According to <a href="http://www.carefirstanswers.com/" target="_blank">a statement</a> CareFirst issued Wednesday, attackers gained access to names, birth dates, email addresses and insurance identification numbers. The company said the database did not include Social Security or credit card numbers, passwords or medical information. Nevertheless, CareFirst is offering credit monitoring and identity theft protection for two years.</p>
<p>Nobody is officially pointing fingers at the parties thought to be responsible for this latest health industry breach, but there are clues implicating the same state-sponsored actors from China thought to be involved in the Anthem and Premera attacks.<span id="more-31043"></span></p>
<p>As I noted in <a href="http://krebsonsecurity.com/2015/02/anthem-breach-may-have-started-in-april-2014/" target="_blank">this Feb. 9, 2015 story</a>, Anthem was breached not long after a malware campaign was erected that mimicked Anthem&#8217;s domain names at the time of the breach. Prior to its official name change at the end of 2014, Anthem was known as Wellpoint. Security researchers at cybersecurity firm <strong>ThreatConnect Inc.</strong> had uncovered a series of subdomains for we11point[dot]com (note the &#8220;L&#8217;s&#8221; in the domain were replaced by the numeral &#8220;1&#8221;) &#8212; including myhr.we11point[dot]com and hrsolutions.we11point[dot]com.</p>
<p>ThreatConnect also found that the domains were registered in April 2014 (approximately the time that the Anthem breach began), and that the domains were used in conjunction with malware designed to mimic a software tool that many organizations commonly use to allow employees remote access to internal networks.</p>
<p>On Feb. 27, 2015, ThreatConnect <a title="http://www.threatconnect.com/news/the-anthem-hack-all-roads-lead-to-china/" href="http://www.threatconnect.com/news/the-anthem-hack-all-roads-lead-to-china/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer">published more information</a> tying the same threat actors and modus operandi to a domain called “<strong>prennera[dot]com</strong>” (notice the use of the double “n” there to mimic the letter “m”).</p>
<p><a href="https://krebsonsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/tc-cfbcbs.png"><img decoding="async" loading="lazy" class="alignleft  wp-image-31052" src="https://krebsonsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/tc-cfbcbs.png" alt="tc-cfbcbs" width="288" height="237" /></a>“It is believed that the prennera[dot]com domain may have been impersonating the Healthcare provider <a href="http://www.premera.com/" rel="noreferrer">Premera Blue Cross</a>, where the attackers used the same character replacement technique by replacing the ‘m’ with two ‘n’ characters within the faux domain, the same technique that would be seen five months later with the we11point[dot]com command and control infrastructure,” ThreatConnect observed in a February 2015 blog post.</p>
<p>Turns out, the same bulk registrant in China that registered the phony Premera and Anthem domains in April 2014 also registered two Carefirst look-alike domains &#8212; <strong>careflrst[dot]com</strong> (the &#8220;i&#8221; replaced with an &#8220;L&#8221;) and <strong>caref1rst[dot]com</strong> (the &#8220;i&#8221; replaced with the number &#8220;1&#8221;).</p>
<p>Additionally, ThreatConnect has <a href="https://www.anthemfacts.com/" target="_blank">unearthed evidence</a> showing the same tactics were used on <strong>EmpireB1ue.com</strong> (note the &#8220;L&#8221; replaced with a number &#8220;1&#8221;), a domain registered April 11, 2014 (the same day as the phony Carefirst domains). <strong>EmpireBlue BlueCross BlueShield</strong> was o<a href="https://www.anthemfacts.com/" target="_blank">ne of the organizations impacted by the Anthem breach</a>.</p>

<p>As human beings we try to make sense of the world.  In doing so, we often fall to a mental error of requiring the &#8216;why&#8217; before we can accept the &#8216;what&#8217;.  In general, just because the &#8216;why&#8217; of something is not understood should not influence our assessment of the probability of a scenario.  In other words, our own understanding does not make the &#8216;what&#8217; more or less likely.</p>
<p>I often hear folks discuss the &#8216;why&#8217; as a modifier to the likelihood of an event, and it&#8217;s a huge logical mistake.  It&#8217;s whether it&#8217;s possible and the consequences, not whether we understand &#8216;why&#8217; it would happen.  Coupling them basically multiplies the odds of the event and our understanding, making the result even less clear.</p>
<p>Brian Krebs  has succumbed to this error as well (notably in his NK attribution posts), so it&#8217;s by no means a rare mistake.  Our analysts missed 9/11 in part due to dismissing an event because it had no known &#8216;why&#8217; answer to why would anyone want to do such a thing.</p>
<p>A corollary to demanding &#8216;why&#8217; to accept &#8216;what&#8217; is that we are also tricked by a false &#8216;why&#8217;.  Once we think we understand &#8216;why&#8217; we assign a greater likelihood to the event, even if the &#8216;why&#8217; is false. We all know people who just want an answer, and any answer is fine with them.. but until they get an answer they are not satisfied.  So another exploitation is to present a false but plausable &#8216;why&#8217; and it leads to errors on the flip side, or over estimating likelihood.</p>
<p>So please folks, particularly Infosec and security people, when you find yourself giving up on a scenario because of lack of clarity on &#8216;why&#8217;, resist the natural human urge to dismiss a probability of the event, particularly if the consequence of the event is large or unknown.  And when we think we know &#8216;why&#8217;, remember we can be wrong as much, if not more than if we don&#8217;t know &#8216;why&#8217; at all.</p>
<p>Sometimes, the &#8216;why&#8217; presents itself much later after an event&#8230; and sometimes never.  This is the nature of tail event risks and I would suspect that most tail events have the &#8216;it could not have been otherwise&#8217; though pre-event analysis never sees it coming.</p>
<p>One final thought for those that are interested.  When the &#8216;black hats&#8217; know folks dismiss events that have no readily answerable &#8216;why&#8217;, they can design events that exploit that behavior.  So it&#8217;s up to the Infosec and security folks to make sure we take away that bit of human behavior exploitation.</p>
<p>anyway, hope this was of use.</p>

				<p>I think you&#8217;re missing something. </p>
<p>Think about how Remote Access Trojans work.</p>
<p>Imagine an employee is authorized to do certain things, and imagine someone successfully spearfishes them with the intent to deliver malware to their computer. That computer is now compromised, they get a &#8220;your session has expired&#8221; message&#8211; something that they&#8217;re used to experiencing due to company policy. They log in and receive their 2FA message. They use it to confirm their connection. The software now borrows that confirmed connection and performs the attacks and data exfiltration. (This kind of attack has been described on this blog for banking account compromises&#8211; typically payroll)</p>
<p>2FA isn&#8217;t a panacea. In this case, an older technology from the credit card industry is more important: atypical customer pattern detection. When I make a purchase using my credit card, my credit card issuer compares all the details of the transaction against my profile, and if the transaction isn&#8217;t a good match for my profile, or perhaps for people with similar profiles, then they either block the transaction or my account in general and contact me to explain that they&#8217;ve detected a problem. </p>
<p>If the databases that the employee is authorized to access aren&#8217;t directly compromised and are capable of comparing the employee&#8217;s requests to typical requests by that employee, then they could potentially recognize a query for too much data&#8230;</p>
<p>The other technology that would help is really similar technology, but instead of having detection running between the users and the databases that holds the data, similar technology could run between the employees and the rest of the Internet. &#8212; unfortunately, since the Internet is moving to encrypted traffic for everything, this task is much harder, so the analysis would be mostly looking at time &amp; date, network destination (which is a poor indicator since any network can host compromised computers), and data volume (which means that it could catch large scale leaks, but would probably miss targeted instances).</p>
