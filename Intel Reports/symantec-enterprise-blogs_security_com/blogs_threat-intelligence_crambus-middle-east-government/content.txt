class="blog-post__title">Crambus: New Campaign Targets Middle Eastern Government</h1><h2 class="blog-post__subtitle">Iran-linked attackers compromised multiple computers and servers over the course of eight months.</h2></header><span><!----><app-paragraph-rte _nghost-ng-c2666164074><div _ngcontent-ng-c2666164074><p class="MsoNormal">The Iranian Crambus espionage group (aka OilRig, APT34) staged an eight-month-long intrusion against a government in the Middle East between February and September 2023. During the compromise, the attackers stole files and passwords and, in one case, installed a PowerShell backdoor (dubbed PowerExchange) that was used to monitor incoming mails sent from an Exchange Server in order to execute commands sent by the attackers in the form of emails, and surreptitiously forwarded results&nbsp; to the attackers. Malicious activity occurred on at least 12 computers and there is evidence that the attackers deployed backdoors and keyloggers on dozens more.</p><p class="MsoNormal">In addition to deploying malware, the attackers made frequent use of the publicly available network administration tool Plink to configure port-forwarding rules on compromised machines, enabling remote access via the Remote Desktop Protocol (RDP). There is also evidence the attackers modified Windows firewall rules in order to enable remote access.</p><h2>Background</h2><p class="MsoNormal">Crambus is a long-running Iranian espionage group that has mounted operations against targets in multiple countries, including Saudi Arabia, Israel, the United Arab Emirates, Iraq, Jordan, Lebanon, Kuwait, Qatar, Albania, the U.S., and Turkey.</p><p class="MsoNormal">The group is known to stage long-running intrusions for intelligence gathering and spying purposes. In recent years it has added a heavy social engineering component to the early stages of its attacks.</p><p class="MsoNormal">It most recently came to attention last year, when <a href="https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/security/blog/2022/09/08/microsoft-investigates-iranian-attacks-against-the-albanian-government/">Microsoft linked the group</a> to a destructive attack against the Albanian government. It assessed that Crambus was involved in gaining initial access and exfiltrating data from impacted networks. Wipers were likely then deployed by other Iran-linked actors.&nbsp;</p><h2>Toolset Used</h2><p class="MsoNormal">During this latest attack, Crambus deployed three previously undiscovered pieces of malware, along with the PowerExchange backdoor, a known backdoor that hadn’t yet been attributed to Crambus. In addition to malware, the attackers made use of a number of living-off-the-land and legitimate tools.</p><ul><li><strong>Backdoor.Tokel:</strong> Has the ability to execute arbitrary PowerShell commands and download files. The command and control (C&amp;C) address is stored in a separate, RC4 encrypted file called token.bin, which is saved in the working directory.</li><li><strong>Trojan.Dirps:</strong> Used to enumerate all files in a directory and execute PowerShell commands.</li><li><strong>Infostealer.Clipog:</strong> Information stealing malware that is capable of copying clipboard data, capturing keystrokes and logging processes where keystrokes are entered.</li><li><strong>Backdoor.PowerExchange:</strong> PowerShell-based malware that can log into an Exchange Server with hardcoded credentials and monitor for emails sent by the attackers. It uses an Exchange Server as a C&amp;C. Mails received with “@@" in the subject contain commands sent from the attackers which allows them to execute arbitrary PowerShell commands, write files and steal files.
