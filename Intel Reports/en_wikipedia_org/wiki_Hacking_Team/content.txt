<p><b>Hacking Team</b> was a <a href="/wiki/Milan" title="Milan">Milan</a>-based <a href="/wiki/Information_technology" title="Information technology">information technology</a> company that sold offensive intrusion and <a href="/wiki/Surveillance" title="Surveillance">surveillance</a> capabilities to governments, law enforcement agencies and corporations.<sup id="cite_ref-spies_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-spies-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Its "<i>Remote Control Systems</i>" enable governments and corporations to monitor the communications of internet users, decipher their <a href="/wiki/Cryptography" title="Cryptography">encrypted</a> files and emails, record <a href="/wiki/Skype" title="Skype">Skype</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Voice_over_IP" title="Voice over IP">Voice over IP</a> communications, and remotely activate microphones and camera on target computers.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The company has been criticized for providing these capabilities to governments with poor <a href="/wiki/Human_rights" title="Human rights">human rights</a> records,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> though HackingTeam states that they have the ability to disable their software if it is used unethically.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The Italian government has restricted their licence to do business with countries outside Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>HackingTeam employs around 40 people in its Italian office, and has subsidiary branches in <a href="/wiki/Annapolis" class="mw-redirect" title="Annapolis">Annapolis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-know_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-know-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Its products are in use in dozens of countries across six continents.<sup id="cite_ref-Jeffries_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jeffries-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>

<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Company_foundation">Company foundation</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=HackingTeam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Company foundation"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>HackingTeam was founded in 2003 by Italian entrepreneurs  Vincenzetti and Valeriano Bedeschi.  In 2007 the company was invested by two Italian VC: Fondo Next and Innogest.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>The Milan police department learned of the company. Hoping to use its tool to spy on Italian citizens and listen to their Skype calls, the police contacted Vincenzetti and asked him to help.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> HackingTeam became "the first sellers of commercial hacking software to the police”.
</p><p>According to former employee Byamukama Robinhood, the company began as security services provider, offering <a href="/wiki/Penetration_testing" class="mw-redirect" title="Penetration testing">penetration testing</a>, auditing and other defensive capabilities to clients.<sup id="cite_ref-war_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-war-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Byamukama states that as malware and other offensive capabilities were developed and accounted for a larger percentage of revenues, the organization pivoted in a more offensive direction and became increasingly compartmentalized. Byamukama claims fellow employees working on aspects of the same platform – for example, Android exploits and payloads – would not communicate with one another, possibly leading to tensions and strife within the organization.<sup id="cite_ref-war_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-war-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>In February 2014, a report from <a href="/wiki/Citizen_Lab" title="Citizen Lab">Citizen Lab</a> identified the organisation to be using hosting services from <a href="/wiki/Linode" title="Linode">Linode</a>, <a href="/wiki/Telecom_Italia" class="mw-redirect" title="Telecom Italia">Telecom Italia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rackspace" class="mw-redirect" title="Rackspace">Rackspace</a>, NOC4Hosts and <a href="/wiki/Bullet_proof_hosting" class="mw-redirect" title="Bullet proof hosting">bullet proof hosting</a> company <a href="/w/index.php?title=Santrex&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Santrex (page does not exist)">Santrex</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>On 5 July 2015 the company suffered a major data breach of customer data, software code, internal documents and e-mails. (<i>See: <a href="#2015_data_breach">§ 2015 data breach</a></i>)
</p><p>On 2 April 2019 HackingTeam was acquired by InTheCyber Group to create Memento Labs.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Products_and_capabilities">Products and capabilities</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=HackingTeam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Products and capabilities"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Hacking Team enables clients to perform remote monitoring functions against citizens via their <a href="/wiki/Remote_administration_tool" class="mw-redirect" title="Remote administration tool">RCS (remote control systems)</a>, including their Da Vinci and Galileo platforms:<sup id="cite_ref-spies_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-spies-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Covert collection of emails, text message, phone call history and address books</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Keystroke_logging" title="Keystroke logging">Keystroke logging</a></li>
<li>Uncover search history data and take screenshots</li>
<li>Record audio from phone calls</li></ul>
<dl><dd><ul><li>Capture audio and video stream from device memory to bypass <a href="/wiki/Cryptography" title="Cryptography">cryptography</a> of <a href="/wiki/Skype" title="Skype">Skype</a> sessions<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li>
<li>Use microphones on device to collect ambient background noise and conversations</li></ul></dd></dl>
<ul><li>Activate phone or computer cameras</li>
<li>Hijack telephone GPS systems to monitor target's location</li>
<li>Infect target computer's <a href="/wiki/UEFI" title="UEFI">UEFI</a> <a href="/wiki/BIOS" title="BIOS">BIOS</a> <a href="/wiki/Firmware" title="Firmware">firmware</a> with a <a href="/wiki/Rootkit" title="Rootkit">rootkit</a><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li>
<li>Extract WiFi passwords<sup id="cite_ref-skiddies_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-skiddies-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li>
<li>Exfiltrate <a href="/wiki/Bitcoin" title="Bitcoin">Bitcoin</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Cryptocurrency_wallet" title="Cryptocurrency wallet">cryptocurrency wallet</a> files to collect data on local accounts, contacts and transaction histories<sup id="cite_ref-coin_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-coin-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li></ul>
<p>HackingTeam uses advanced techniques to avoid draining cell phone batteries, which could potentially raise suspicions, and other methods to avoid detection.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>18<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>19<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>The malware has payloads for <a href="/wiki/Android_(operating_system)" title="Android (operating system)">Android</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-skiddies_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-skiddies-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/BlackBerry" title="BlackBerry">BlackBerry</a>, Apple <a href="/wiki/IOS" title="IOS">iOS</a>, <a href="/wiki/Linux" title="Linux">Linux</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mac_OS_X" class="mw-redirect" title="Mac OS X">Mac OS X</a>, <a href="/wiki/Symbian" title="Symbian">Symbian</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Windows" title="Microsoft Windows">Microsoft Windows</a>, <a href="/wiki/Windows_Mobile" title="Windows Mobile">Windows Mobile</a> and <a href="/wiki/Windows_Phone" title="Windows Phone">Windows Phone</a> class of <a href="/wiki/Operating_system" title="Operating system">operating systems</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-C3_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-C3-20"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>20<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>RCS is a management platform that allows operators to remotely deploy exploits and payloads against targeted systems, remotely manage devices once compromised, and exfiltrate data for remote analysis.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Controversies">Controversies</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=HackingTeam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Controversies"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Use_by_repressive_governments">Use by repressive governments</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=HackingTeam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Use by repressive governments"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>HackingTeam has been criticized for selling its products and services to governments with poor human rights records, including <a href="/wiki/Sudan" title="Sudan">Sudan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bahrain" title="Bahrain">Bahrain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Venezuela" title="Venezuela">Venezuela</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Saudi_Arabia" title="Saudi Arabia">Saudi Arabia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-repressive_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-repressive-21"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>21<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>In June 2014, a <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> panel monitoring the implementation of sanctions on Sudan requested information from HackingTeam about their alleged sales of software to the country in contravention of United Nations weapons export bans to Sudan. Documents leaked in the 2015 data breach of HackingTeam revealed the organization sold Sudanese National Intelligence and Security Service access to their "Remote Control System" software in 2012 for 960,000 Euros.<sup id="cite_ref-repressive_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-repressive-21"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>21<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>In response to the United Nations panel, the company responded in January 2015 that they were not currently selling to Sudan. In a follow-up exchange, HackingTeam asserted that their product was not controlled as a weapon, and so the request was beyond the scope of the panel. There was no need for them to disclose previous sales, which they considered confidential business information.<sup id="cite_ref-repressive_21-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-repressive-21"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>21<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>The U.N. disagreed. "The view of the panel is that as such software is ideally suited to support military electronic intelligence (ELINT) operations it may potentially fall under the category of 'military ... equipment' or 'assistance' related to prohibited items," the secretary wrote in March. "Thus its potential use in targeting any of the belligerents in the Darfur conflict is of interest to the Panel."<sup id="cite_ref-repressive_21-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-repressive-21"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>21<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>22<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>In the fall of 2014, the Italian government abruptly froze all of HackingTeam's exports, citing human rights concerns. After lobbying Italian officials, the company temporarily won back the right to sell its products abroad.<sup id="cite_ref-repressive_21-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-repressive-21"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>21<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="2015_data_breach">2015 data breach</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=HackingTeam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 2015 data breach"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>On July 5, 2015, the <a href="/wiki/Twitter" title="Twitter">Twitter</a> account of the company was compromised by an unknown individual who published an announcement of a <a href="/wiki/Data_breach" title="Data breach">data breach</a> against HackingTeam's computer systems. The initial message read, "<i>Since <a href="/wiki/Nothing_to_hide_argument" title="Nothing to hide argument">we have nothing to hide</a>, we're publishing all our e-mails, files, and source code ...</i>" and provided links to over 400 <a href="/wiki/Gigabyte" title="Gigabyte">gigabytes</a> of data, including alleged internal e-mails, invoices, and <a href="/wiki/Source_code" title="Source code">source code</a>; which were leaked via <a href="/wiki/BitTorrent" title="BitTorrent">BitTorrent</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mega_(service)" title="Mega (service)">Mega</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>23<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> An announcement of the data breach, including a link to the bittorrent seed, was retweeted by <a href="/wiki/WikiLeaks" title="WikiLeaks">WikiLeaks</a> and by many others through social media.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>24<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ibtimes.co.uk_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ibtimes.co.uk-25"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>25<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>The material was voluminous and early analysis appeared to reveal that HackingTeam had invoiced the <a href="/wiki/Lebanese_Army" class="mw-redirect" title="Lebanese Army">Lebanese Army</a><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>26<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Sudan" title="Sudan">Sudan</a> and that spy tools were also sold to <a href="/wiki/Bahrain" title="Bahrain">Bahrain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kazakhstan" title="Kazakhstan">Kazakhstan</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ibtimes.co.uk_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ibtimes.co.uk-25"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>25<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> HackingTeam had previously claimed they had never done business with Sudan.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>27<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>

</p><p>Also revealed in leaked data was HackingTeam employees' use of weak passwords, including 'P4ssword', 'wolverine', and 'universo'.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>34<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>After a few hours without response from HackingTeam, member Christian Pozzi tweeted the company was working closely with police and "<i>what the attackers are claiming regarding our company is not true.</i>"<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>35<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>36<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> He also claimed the leaked archive "contains a virus" and that it constituted "false info".<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>37<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Shortly after these tweets, Pozzi's Twitter account itself was apparently compromised.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>38<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Responsibility for this attack was claimed by the hacker known as "Phineas Fisher" (or Phisher) on Twitter.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>39<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Phineas has previously attacked spyware firm <a href="/wiki/Gamma_International" class="mw-redirect" title="Gamma International">Gamma International</a>, who produce malware, such as <a href="/wiki/FinFisher" title="FinFisher">FinFisher</a>, for governments and corporations.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>40<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In 2016, Phineas published details of the attack, in Spanish and English, as a "how-to" for others, and explained the motivations behind the attack.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>41<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>42<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>The internal documents revealed details of HackingTeam's contracts with repressive governments.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>43<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In 2016, the Italian government again revoked the company's license to sell spyware outside of Europe without special permission.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>44<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Use_by_Mexican_drug_cartels">Use by Mexican drug cartels</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=HackingTeam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Use by Mexican drug cartels"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Corrupt Mexican officials have helped drug cartels obtain state-of-the-art spyware (including Hacking Team spyware). The software  has been used to target and intimidate Mexican journalists by drug cartels and cartel-entwined government actors.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>45<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Customer_list">Customer list</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=HackingTeam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Customer list"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>HackingTeam's clientele include not just governments, but also corporate clients such as <a href="/wiki/Barclays" title="Barclays">Barclays</a> and <a href="/wiki/BT_Group" title="BT Group">British Telecom</a> (BT) of the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Deutsche_Bank" title="Deutsche Bank">Deutsche Bank</a> of <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-spies_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-spies-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>A full list of HackingTeam's customers were leaked in the 2015 breach. Disclosed documents show HackingTeam had 70 current customers, mostly military, police, federal and provincial governments. The total company revenues disclosed exceeded 40 million <a href="/wiki/Euro" title="Euro">Euros</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>46<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>47<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>48<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>49<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>50<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>51<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>On Sep 8, 2021, SentinelLABS released a research report about a Turkish threat actor EGoManiac, that used Remote Control System (RCS), software from the Italian infosec firm Hacking Team, which was operated between 2010 and 2016 and campaign run by Turkish TV journalists at OdaTV for spying Turkish police.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>52<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
