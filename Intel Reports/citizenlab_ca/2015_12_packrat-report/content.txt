                <section itemprop="articleBody" class="article-body mb4 mt4 pt2 bt b--light-gray">
                  <h2 class="lh-solid mb3">Summary</h2>
<p class="mt0">This report describes an extensive malware, phishing, and disinformation campaign active in several Latin American countries, including Ecuador, Argentina, Venezuela, and Brazil. The nature and geographic spread of the targets seems to point to a sponsor, or sponsors, with regional, political interests. The attackers, whom we have named <b>Packrat</b>, have shown a keen and systematic interest in the political opposition and the independent press in so-called ALBA countries (Bolivarian Alternative for the Americas), and their recently allied regimes. These countries are linked by a trade agreement as well as a cooperation on a range of non-financial matters.</p>
<p>After observing a wave of attacks in Ecuador in 2015, we linked these attacks to a campaign active in Argentina in 2014. The targeting in Argentina was discovered when the attackers <a href="https://theintercept.com/2015/08/21/inside-the-spyware-campaign-against-argentine-troublemakers-including-alberto-nisman/" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener">attempted to compromise the devices of Alberto Nisman and Jorge Lanata</a>. &nbsp;Building on what we had learned about these two campaigns, we then traced the group&rsquo;s activities back as far as 2008.</p>
<p>This report brings together many of the pieces of this campaign, from malware and phishing, to command and control infrastructure spread across Latin America. It also highlights fake online organizations that Packrat has created in Venezuela and Ecuador. &nbsp;Who is responsible? We assess several scenarios, and consider&nbsp;the most likely to be that&nbsp;Packrat is sponsored by a state actor or actors, given their apparent lack of concern about discovery, their targets, and their persistence. &nbsp;However, we do not conclusively attribute Packrat to a particular sponsor.</p>
<h2 class="lh-solid mb3">Part 1:&nbsp;Packrat&rsquo;s Seven Years of Activity</h2>
<p class="mt0">The authors on this report have been independently investigating malware and phishing campaigns in Latin America. &nbsp;This report is the result of discovering that the cases we have been investigating are linked by a common threat actor with targeting in several countries, including Venezuela, Ecuador, Argentina, and Brazil. We refer to this threat actor as<b> Packrat</b>, to highlight their preference for packed, commodity Remote Access Trojans (RATs), and their retention of the same domains and servers over many years.</p>
<figure class="center mw-100 ba b--light-gray" style="width:624px;"><div class="tc pa2 bg-white"><a href="https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2015/12/1-packrat_known_targeting.png" class="pointer"><img fetchpriority="high" decoding="async" class="size-full wp-image-26690" src="https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2015/12/1-packrat_known_targeting.png" alt="Image 1: Known targets of Packrat" width="624" height="571" title="Packrat: Seven Years of a South American Threat Actor 1" srcset="https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2015/12/1-packrat_known_targeting.png 624w, https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2015/12/1-packrat_known_targeting-300x275.png 300w, https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2015/12/1-packrat_known_targeting-180x165.png 180w, https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2015/12/1-packrat_known_targeting-372x340.png 372w, https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2015/12/1-packrat_known_targeting-217x199.png 217w" sizes="(max-width: 624px) 100vw, 624px" /></a></div><figcaption class="f5-ns f6 black-70 pa2 bg-light-gray">Image 1:&nbsp;Some of Packrat&rsquo;s&nbsp;known targets and activity types</figcaption></figure>
<p>Packrat has systematically targeted high profile political figures, journalists, and others in several countries with malware and phishing. In total we uncovered 12 different malware command and control domains, and over 30 samples of malware stretching over a seven year time period. Packrat also favors an interesting strategy: create and maintain fake opposition groups and news organizations, then use these to distribute malware and conduct phishing attacks.</p>
<p>Some of these organizations exist in name only, while others have a more elaborate online presence. Packrat has also created elaborate fake news organizations without any evidence we can find of malware or phishing activity.</p>
<figure class="center mw-100 ba b--light-gray" style="width:624px;"><div class="tc pa2 bg-white"><a href="https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2015/12/2-major_activities1.png" class="pointer"><img decoding="async" class="size-full wp-image-26692" src="https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2015/12/2-major_activities1.png" alt="Image 2: Major activities of Packrat" width="624" height="393" title="Packrat: Seven Years of a South American Threat Actor 2" srcset="https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2015/12/2-major_activities1.png 624w, https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2015/12/2-major_activities1-300x189.png 300w, https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2015/12/2-major_activities1-180x113.png 180w, https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2015/12/2-major_activities1-540x340.png 540w, https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2015/12/2-major_activities1-297x187.png 297w" sizes="(max-width: 624px) 100vw, 624px" /></a></div><figcaption class="f5-ns f6 black-70 pa2 bg-light-gray">Image 2: Packrat&rsquo;s major activities</figcaption></figure>
<p>We chart Packrat&rsquo;s activities back to at least 2008. Through correlation of network infrastructure, we identified several waves of activity, coupled with changes in tools and tactics. This section provides a brief chronology of Packrat&rsquo;s network infrastructure and activities. For a detailed chronology of the malware used, see&nbsp;<strong>3. The Evolution of Packrat&rsquo;s Implants</strong>.</p>
<h3 class="lh-solid mb3">Packrat&rsquo;s Greatest Hits</h3>
<p class="mt0"><strong>2008-2013</strong><br>
Tools and infrastructure used by Packrat suggest that they have been active since at least 2008. During this period, Packrat used hosting services in Brazil, and some of their malware samples were uploaded from Brazilian IP space to popular online virus scanning services. Some of the messages they sent also contained Brazilian bait content. While this is suggestive of Brazilian targeting, we have yet to find confirmed victims from this period.</p>
<p><strong>2014</strong><br>
By 2014, Packrat was targeting high-profile Argentine lawyer Alberto Nisman, and well-known investigative journalist and television news host, Jorge Lanata. Maximo Kirchner, son of Argentina&rsquo;s president, also announced that he was targeted. The screenshot he released of the phishing email he received is consistent with what we have seen, although we have not been able to verify his claims. In addition, a number of phishing domains with Ecuadorian and Venezuelan targeting that we identified became active during this period.</p>
<p><strong>2015</strong><br>
2015 seems to have marked an extensive campaign of phishing and malware attacks targeting civil society and public figures, including parliamentarians in Ecuador. We observed a range of phishing domains and attacks, often using fake organizations during this period. We also found fake organizations and possible disinformation campaigns with targets in Ecuador, Venezuela and the Venezuelan diaspora.</p>
<h3 class="lh-solid mb3">1.1 Nisman and the Argentine Cases</h3>
<p class="mt0">In January 2015, controversial Argentine prosecutor Alberto Nisman was found dead of a gunshot under <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/interactive/2015/02/07/world/americas/argentina-alberto-nisman-case.html?_r=0" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener">suspicious circumstances</a>. Argentine news reported that a malicious file was found on his Android phone by the Buenos Aires Metropolitan Police forensic lab. The <a href="https://theintercept.com/2015/08/21/inside-the-spyware-campaign-against-argentine-troublemakers-including-alberto-nisman/" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener">file was named</a> &ldquo;<strong>estrictamente secreto y confidencial.pdf.jar</strong>,&rdquo; or &ldquo;strictly secret and confidential&rdquo; in English.</p>
<p>An <a href="https://www.virustotal.com/en/file/aa9aa05af8df2cc99eb936e2d17623a68abdbb60606bb097379457c4a3760116/analysis/" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener">identically titled file was uploaded to VirusTotal</a> from Argentina on the 29th of May, 2015. The file was a remote access toolkit, known as AlienSpy, which allowed an attacker the ability to record the activities of a target, access their email, their webcam, and more. However, the file was built for the Windows operating system, and could not have infected Nisman&rsquo;s Android&nbsp;phone.</p>
<p>The <a href="https://theintercept.com/2015/08/21/inside-the-spyware-campaign-against-argentine-troublemakers-including-alberto-nisman/" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener">initial analysis of the alienspy implant&nbsp;by Morgan Marquis</a><a href="https://theintercept.com/2015/08/21/inside-the-spyware-campaign-against-argentine-troublemakers-including-alberto-nisman/" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener">&ndash;</a><a href="https://theintercept.com/2015/08/21/inside-the-spyware-campaign-against-argentine-troublemakers-including-alberto-nisman/" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Boire</a> revealed the command and control server of the attackers to be <strong>deyrep24.ddns.net</strong>. In addition to the malware apparently used to target Nisman, Lanata, and Kirchner (see below), three other samples<a href="#ftn1" name="_ftnref1" class="pointer"><sup>[1]</sup></a> were found which used deyrep24.ddns.net as a command and control domain. One of these, <strong>3 MAR PROYECTO GRIPEN.docx.jar</strong>, was a build of AlienSpy (See <strong>Packrat&rsquo;s Implants</strong>) which masqueraded as a document containing communication between Ecuadorian President Rafael Correa and Ecuador&rsquo;s Ambassador to Sweden concerning the acquisition of fighter jets.</p>
<p>After <a href="https://theintercept.com/2015/08/21/inside-the-spyware-campaign-against-argentine-troublemakers-including-alberto-nisman/" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener">the finding was made public</a>, other targets came forward. Prominent investigative journalist and television host Jorge Lanata <a href="http://pastebin.com/iiAGSMYc" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener">revealed</a> that he too had been targeted by the same malware. The president&rsquo;s son, Maximo Kirchner, also <a href="http://www.buenosairesherald.com/article/196727/an%C3%ADbal-m%C3%A1ximo-kirchner-received-same-virus-as-lanata" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener">claimed</a> to have been targeted. We were unable to verify Kirchner&rsquo;s&nbsp;claim, however, a screenshot showing his targeting was <a href="http://www.ambito.com/noticia.asp?id=803749" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener">included in a report</a> of this claim:</p>
<figure class="center mw-100 ba b--light-gray" style="width:500px;"><div class="tc pa2 bg-white"><a href="https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2015/12/3-screenshot.png" class="pointer"><img decoding="async" class="size-full wp-image-26694" src="https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2015/12/3-screenshot.png" alt="Image 3: A screenshot showing Maximo Kirschner&#039;s targeting" width="404" height="282" title="Packrat: Seven Years of a South American Threat Actor 3" srcset="https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2015/12/3-screenshot.png 404w, https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2015/12/3-screenshot-300x209.png 300w, https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2015/12/3-screenshot-180x126.png 180w, https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2015/12/3-screenshot-285x199.png 285w" sizes="(max-width: 404px) 100vw, 404px" /></a></div><figcaption class="f5-ns f6 black-70 pa2 bg-light-gray">Image 3: A screenshot included in <a href="http://www.ambito.com/noticia.asp?id=803749" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener">this report </a>showing Maximo Kirchner&rsquo;s targeting</figcaption></figure>
<p>The email which he claims to have received has an attachment named &ldquo;Estrictamente Secreto y Confidencial.pdf.jar&rdquo; (size 67.3kb) which is the same as the malware sent to Nisman and Lanata. Additionally, the sender&rsquo;s email address (<a href="/cdn-cgi/l/email-protection#d7b4bbb6a2b3beb8b5b8b9b6b3beb8efef97b0bab6bebbf9b4b8ba" class="pointer"><span class="__cf_email__" data-cfemail="90f3fcf1e5f4f9fff2fffef1f4f9ffa8a8d0f7fdf1f9fcbef3fffd">[email&#160;protected]</span></a>) purports to be well-known judge Claudio Bonadio. This similar to the targeting of Lanata, who also received an email also claiming to be from Claudio Bonadio (<a href="/cdn-cgi/l/email-protection#e281848786cc808d8c83868b8da2858f838b8ecc818d8f" class="pointer"><span class="__cf_email__" data-cfemail="1b787d7e7f357974757a7f72745b7c767a727735787476">[email&#160;protected]</span></a>).</p>
<h3 class="lh-solid mb3">1.2 Ecuadorian Campaigns</h3>
<p class="mt0">In 2015, we began independently receiving a growing number of reports of phishing attacks via e-mail and SMS targeting journalists and other public figures in Ecuador. Some emails we examined had no political content, but were simple credential phishing for social media and email providers, like Gmail. Others, however, had explicit political content concerning a range of political figures and issues in Ecuador. Further investigation revealed an extensive campaign, as well as many fake organizations (See <strong>Section 6: Possible Deception Operations</strong>).</p>
<p>One of the authors developed a Gmail search query for strings associated with the attacks (See <strong>Appendix A: The Search Query</strong>). We shared this query with many potential targets, resulting in hits for phishing attacks, as well as suspicious Microsoft Word (DOCX) files sent to a range of journalists and public figures. These documents contained embedded RATs written in Java, including Adzok and AlienSpy (See <strong>Packrat&rsquo;s Implants</strong>). Subsequently, using indicators found in the JAR files, as well as an updated Gmail query we were able to identify a larger set of malicious files and domains used by Packrat (See <strong>Appendix B: Malware Samples</strong>).</p>
<p>We found a dense web of interconnections between phishing and malware sites. Sites often shared registration information, or were hosted from the same servers. We determined that the malware samples typically communicated with <strong>daynews.sytes.net</strong>, which is linked to the Argentine cases. Ultimately, investigation of this infrastructure also revealed malware and infrastructure in Brazil, and fake sites in Venezuela.</p>
<h3 class="lh-solid mb3">1.3 Shared Command &amp; Control Infrastructure</h3>
<p class="mt0">This section describes Packrat&rsquo;s command and control infrastructure in narrative form, <strong>Appendix B</strong> provides a full list of Command &amp; Control domains along with the related binaries and malware families.</p>
<p>Packrat&rsquo;s <strong>deyrep24.ddns.net</strong> domain was created on November 7th, 2014, and at the time of Nisman&rsquo;s targeting, pointed to the IP address: <strong>50.62.133.49. </strong> This IP address belongs to a GoDaddy range for dedicated hosting, and on March 3rd, 2015, the domain moved to another GoDaddy IP: <strong>192.169.243.65</strong>. Passive DNS records revealed that at the same time as this IP was being used by the <strong>deyrep24.ddns.net</strong> command and control domain, it was being used by the domain <strong>daynews.sytes.net</strong> which had been created on March 1st, 2015. Over the course of our joint investigation we found 5 samples of malware using this domain which were used to target journalists and civil society in Ecuador (See <strong>Appendix D: Seeding Domains </strong>for a larger list).</p>

<h2 class="lh-solid mb3">Part 2: Recent Malware Attacks in Ecuador</h2>
<p class="mt0">Packrat is active in many countries, but it is in Ecuador that we were able to gather the most systematic evidence of their activities, as well as connect directly with targets and victims. We are also tracking active attacks against Ecuadorian targets at the time of writing.</p>

<p>Using email inbox search queries that we shared with potential targets (See: <strong>Appendix A</strong>), as well as analysis of malware databases and seeding infrastructure, we collected a diverse set of malware and phishing attacks targeting journalists, public figures, politicians, and other prominent individuals (see: <strong>5. Packrat&rsquo;s Persistent Phishing Campaigns</strong> for examples).</p>
<h3 class="lh-solid mb3">2.1 Previous Reports of Packrat Malware in Ecuador</h3>
<p class="mt0">There are public reports, as well as social media mentions, that point to politically-linked malware attacks Ecuador by Packrat. For example, Ecuadorian freedom of expression organization <a href="http://www.fundamedios.org/alertas/periodistas-y-activistas-sufren-repetidos-intentos-de-hackeo/" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Fundamedios reported that</a> public figures, satirical news organizations, the director of Fundamedios, and others, had received suspicious messages and phishing attempts. Fundamedios <a href="http://www.fundamedios.org/alertas/periodistas-y-activistas-ecuatorianos-atacados-con-el-mismo-software-espia-de-la-computadora-del-fiscal-nisman/" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener">later updated their reporting</a> to note that <a href="https://www.accessnow.org/" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Access Now</a> had stated that some of these attacks shared command and control infrastructure with the malware that was reportedly used to target&nbsp;Nisman. There are also indications on Twitter of phishing attacks and malware. We have been able to link many of these reports to Packrat.</p>
<h3 class="lh-solid mb3">2.2 Common Techniques</h3>
<p class="mt0">We observed a range of social engineering techniques used to send malware to Ecuadorian targets. In the cases where we observed seeding, we found that the malware was often accompanied by political bait content, frequently relevant to Ecuador&rsquo;s opposition. In other cases, the seeding was personalized to the intended victim. The most common delivery mechanism was via Microsoft Word DOCX files containing malicious Java. However, in other cases, attackers used fake updates.</p>
<p><strong>Common Seeding Techniques</strong></p>
<div class="about-author pa3 br1 f5 lh-copy mv1 bg-washed-yellow ba b--mid-gray">
<ul>
<li class="mt2">Emailed as attached malicious files</li>
<li class="mt2">As links to malware hosted on sites controlled by the attackers</li>
<li class="mt2">On Google Drive or Onedrive</li>
<li class="mt2">Popups or fake update notifications on politically themed / lookalike sites</li>
</ul>
</div>
<p>Packrat often uses email senders and websites in its social engineering that appear similar to real persons and organizations. For example, they registered ecuadorenvivo.co, which looks like the genuine domain of the Ecuador En Vivo news website (ecuadorenvivo.com). Packrat then sent e-mails purporting to be e-mail news updates (a practice by the real Ecuador En Vivo) from the ecuadorenvivo.co domain.</p>
<p>Packrat also sometimes creates identical paths to real news stories, and hides them under clickable links. For example:</p>

<h3 class="lh-solid mb3">2.3 Three Attacks in Detail</h3>
<p class="mt0">To illustrate Packrat&rsquo;s approach, this section describes three recent attacks in detail. The attacks date from between Spring and Fall 2015. Targets of these attacks include Ecuadorian journalists and public figures.</p>
<h4 class="lh-solid mb3">2.3.1 Attack 1: Email from a fake opposition movement</h4>
<p class="mt0">Throughout April 2015, multiple targets received e-mails from the &ldquo;Movimento Anti Correista&rdquo; (English: &ldquo;Anti Correa Movement&rdquo;), a fictitious group (based on open source searches and consultation with individuals familiar with the region) that purports to be opponents of Ecuador&rsquo;s current president, Rafael Correa. The emails contained a Microsoft Word DOCX attachment containing Adzok malware (<strong>See: Section 3. The Evolution of Packrat&rsquo;s Implants</strong>), as well as text and graphics to bolster the fiction.</p>

<h2 class="lh-solid mb3">3. The Evolution of Packrat&rsquo;s Implants</h2>
<p class="mt0">Over the past seven years Packrat has used several different types of malware, much of it off-the-shelf RATs, such as Cybergate, Xtreme, AlienSpy, and Adzok. While these malware families are known to researchers, Packrat typically obfuscates their malware using a range of tools, including: an unknown VB6 crypter, AutoIt3Wrapper, UPX, PECompact, PEtite, and Allatori Obfuscator. This layer of obfuscation means that Packrat&rsquo;s attacks frequently escaped detection by antivirus when the attacks were deployed. This section describes these tools, roughly grouped into distinct time periods.</p>
<figure class="center mw-100 ba b--light-gray" style="width:624px;"><div class="tc pa2 bg-white"><a href="https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2015/12/12-malware_families.png" class="pointer"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="size-full wp-image-26708" src="https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2015/12/12-malware_families.png" alt="Image 12: Packrat malware families" width="624" height="668" title="Packrat: Seven Years of a South American Threat Actor 12" srcset="https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2015/12/12-malware_families.png 624w, https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2015/12/12-malware_families-280x300.png 280w, https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2015/12/12-malware_families-180x193.png 180w, https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2015/12/12-malware_families-318x340.png 318w, https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2015/12/12-malware_families-186x199.png 186w" sizes="auto, (max-width: 624px) 100vw, 624px" /></a></div><figcaption class="f5-ns f6 black-70 pa2 bg-light-gray">Image 12: Packrat malware families</figcaption></figure>
<h3 class="lh-solid mb3">3.1. 2008-2014: Packed RATS, mostly CyberGate</h3>
<p class="mt0">Between 2008 and 2014, Packrat made extensive use of off-the-shelf RATs encapsulated in AutoIt3Wrapper, a runtime packer. This packer is written in AutoIt, a compilable scripting language for automating tasks in Windows. The use of an initial obfuscation layer seems to have been enough to thwart or at least misguide detection, as well as leverage some basic anti-debugging techniques.</p>
<p>The majority of implants that are then dropped and executed appear to be CyberGate RAT. In 2013 and 2014, Packrat seems to have adopted XtremeRAT as well. Cybergate and Xtreme are both written in Delphi and <a href="https://www.fireeye.com/blog/threat-research/2014/02/xtremerat-nuisance-or-threat.html" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener">share code</a> with each other and other Delphi based RATs, SpyNet and Cerberus.</p>
<p>Many of these attacks included embedded decoy Office documents that are opened at execution of the implant, likely in the context of a targeted attack. Among the documents we found are r&eacute;sum&eacute;s of purported Brazilian citizens, as well as purported payment receipts of the Association of Lawyers of Sao Paulo, Brazil.</p>

<h4 class="lh-solid mb3">3.1.1 Analysis of CyberGate RAT</h4>
<p class="mt0">The CyberGate RAT samples we analyzed were, as mentioned above, typically wrapped in a layer of AutoIt. Code and strings found in the binary indicate that it is based on the Spy-Net RAT version 2.6. This RAT was developed by a Brazilian hacker using the handle&nbsp;spynetcoder and is outlined on the Spy-Net RAT <a href="https://spynet-rat-officiel.blogspot.com/" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener">&lsquo;official&rsquo; website</a>.</p>
<p><strong>CyberGate&rsquo;s Infection Routine</strong><br>
After unpacking from the applied runtime packer, CyberGate runs its second stage, which is likeley the infection routine. This injects the third stage, a DLL, into a running process. Once implanted, CyberGate then deploys a range of techniques for persistence and monitoring.</p>
<p>The third stage module picks from three execution paths (based on mutexes, which can also be set by a prior infection):</p>
<ul>
<li class="mt2">Password gathering (mutex: &ldquo;_x_X_PASSWORDLIST_X_x_&rdquo;)</li>
<li class="mt2">Block mouse and keyboard input to any other application (mutex: &ldquo;_x_X_BLOCKMOUSE_X_x_&rdquo;)</li>
<li class="mt2">Infection routine</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>CyberGate Anti Analysis</strong><br>
The infection routine comes with a set of anti-analysis features packaged in a single function. CyberGate searches for a range of virtual and sandbox environments.<a href="#ftn3" class="pointer"><sup>[3]</sup></a> It also checks for user space debuggers through the IsDebuggerPresent API, and for SoftICE and Syser through their respective pipes. The malware performs breakpoint detection on the function entries of the listed anti-analysis features by checking whether the first byte of each function equals &lsquo;CC&rsquo;, the bytecode indicating a breakpoint.</p>
<figure class="center mw-100 ba b--light-gray" style="width:500px;"><div class="tc pa2 bg-white"><a href="https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2015/12/15-cybergate.png" class="pointer"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="size-full wp-image-26714" src="https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2015/12/15-cybergate.png" alt="Image 15: CyberGate Anti Analysis" width="377" height="138" title="Packrat: Seven Years of a South American Threat Actor 15" srcset="https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2015/12/15-cybergate.png 377w, https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2015/12/15-cybergate-300x110.png 300w, https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2015/12/15-cybergate-180x66.png 180w, https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2015/12/15-cybergate-297x109.png 297w" sizes="auto, (max-width: 377px) 100vw, 377px" /></a></div><figcaption class="f5-ns f6 black-70 pa2 bg-light-gray">Image 15: CyberGate Anti Analysis</figcaption></figure>
<p><strong>CyberGate Process Injection</strong><br>
The infection routine fetches the encrypted implant from the resource section, and upon decryption attempts to inject its implant into the Windows system shell process (explorer.exe). If this fails, CyberGate launches an explorer.exe process on its own, injects its implant into it, and then completes the setup. Additionally, another instance of the CyberGate implant is injected into a default browser process, which runs invisibly.</p>
<p>The infection routine drops a copy of itself into different directories, depending on the Windows version: /System, /Windows, or /Program Files. The implant&rsquo;s name varies: taskhost.exe, regedit.exe, or taskmgr.exe are all common. The infection routine also writes a copy of the encrypted implant into the %TEMP% directory and names it XX&ndash;XX&ndash;XX.txt.</p>
<p>To achieve persistence, the second stage writes registry keys so that CyberGate is run at startup:</p>
<div class="about-author pa3 br1 f5 lh-copy mv1 bg-washed-yellow ba b--mid-gray">HKLM\SOFTWARE\Microsoft\Active Setup\Installed Components\%GUID%\StubPath: &ldquo;C:\WINDOWS\System32\regedit.exe&rdquo;<br>
HKLM\SOFTWARE\Microsoft\Windows\CurrentVersion\policies\Explorer\Run\Policies: &ldquo;C:\WINDOWS\System32\regedit.exe&rdquo;<br>
HKLM\SOFTWARE\Microsoft\Windows\CurrentVersion\Run\msconfig: &ldquo;C:\WINDOWS\System32\regedit.exe&rdquo;</div>
<p><strong>Password Collection</strong><br>
If tasked with password collection, the second stage binary grabs passwords from a range of locations, including: the No-ip Dynamic Update Client (DUC), MSN messenger, Firefox, and Internet Explorer. The credentials are collected from the Windows Registry, browser profiles, the RAS dial up settings, Local Security Authority (LSA) settings, MS ProtectedStorage, MS IntelliForms, and the credential store.</p>
<p><strong>CyberGate&rsquo;s Functionality</strong><br>
The CyberGate implant runs two instances. The first runs in the default browser process, and acts as the monitoring component. Meanwhile, the explorer.exe instance serves as &lsquo;watchdog,&rsquo; ensuring persistence and making sure the infector binary on disk doesn&rsquo;t disappear.</p>

<h4 class="lh-solid mb3">3.1.2 Analysis of XTremeRAT</h4>
<p class="mt0">XTremeRAT is commercial off-the-shelf malware, often available cracked, and used to monitor victim machines. While used by apolitical hackers, XTremeRAT has been extensively used by <a href="https://www.usenix.org/system/files/conference/usenixsecurity14/sec14-paper-marczak.pdf" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener">government-linked malware groups</a> to target the opposition during the ongoing Syrian Civil War, as well as by <a href="https://www.fireeye.com/blog/threat-research/2014/06/molerats-here-for-spring.html" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener">other politically-motivated groups</a> in the Middle East and North Africa. It has also <a href="https://blogs.mcafee.com/executive-perspectives/tool-talk-unleashing-validedge-on-xtremerat/" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener">been</a> <a href="https://www.sans.org/reading-room/whitepapers/malicious/xtremerat-unicode-breaks-35897" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener">extensively</a> <a href="https://malware.lu/articles/2012/07/22/xtreme-rat-analysis.html" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener">analyzed</a>, and we encourage interested readers to review some of these analyses.</p>
<p>While often packed, XTreme RAT itself has limited stealth and persistence functionality. Its monitoring capabilities are also straightforward. The versions we analyze here have no code obfuscation. XTreme Rat is implemented as client/server architecture, where the infected machine acts as server, while the C&amp;C component is the client.</p>
<p><strong>This version of Xtreme Rat&rsquo;s capabilities include:</strong></p>
<div class="about-author pa3 br1 f5 lh-copy mv1 bg-washed-yellow ba b--mid-gray">
<ul>
<li class="mt2">Logging keystrokes</li>
<li class="mt2">Logging the name of the foreground desktop application</li>
<li class="mt2">Sniffing the clipboard for passwords</li>
<li class="mt2">Downloading and executing binaries via HTTP, presumably to install second stage malware</li>
</ul>
</div>

The Xtreme RAT implant sniffs the clipboard contents via the keylogger window, using a clipboard viewer that it also installs. <p>Possible injections of explorer.exe by XTreme RAT:</p>
<ul>
<li class="mt2"><strong>A &lsquo;watchdog&rsquo; thread</strong> to restore persistence keys, and to locate and run the infector binary. To increase stealth, the dropping module changes the timestamp of the dropped infector.</li>
<li class="mt2">A thread for <strong>deleting of XTreme RAT&rsquo;s files on disk</strong></li>
<li class="mt2"><strong>The entire keylogging code and FTP push functionality</strong></li>
</ul>
<h3 class="lh-solid mb3">3.2: 2014- 2015 AlienSpy Dominates</h3>
<p class="mt0">Over the past two years, Packrat has been using an evolving family of off-the-shelf malware known as AlienSpy. The software began as the free RAT &ldquo;<a href="http://www.symantec.com/connect/blogs/cross-platform-frutas-rat-builder-and-back-door" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Frutas</a>,&rdquo; and was <a href="http://www.symantec.com/connect/blogs/targeted-attacks-delivering-fruit" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener">identified in 2013</a> during a campaign in Mexico. This was subsequently adapted for commercial sale as the &ldquo;Premium RAT&rdquo; <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20130213044621/http:/adwind.com.mx/" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Adwind</a>. Adwind could be purchased for $75 for a single license, and up to $250 for multiple licenses. Then, by 2013, AdWind was renamed <a href="http://blog.crowdstrike.com/adwind-rat-rebranding/" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener">UNRECOM</a> (UNiversal REmote COntrol Multi-platform), and was <a href="http://blog.crowdstrike.com/adwind-rat-rebranding/" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener">detected</a> in targeted attacks in the Middle East.</p>
<p>The software was most recently branded &ldquo;AlienSpy,&rdquo; and was <a href="http://www.fidelissecurity.com/sites/default/files/FTA_1015_Alienspy_FINAL.pdf" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener">again</a> <a href="http://contagiodump.blogspot.ch/2014/11/alienspy-java-rat-samples-and-traffic.html" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener">found</a> by security researchers in targeted spying operations. At the time of this report, the latest re-packaging of this RAT is known as <a href="https://jsocket.org/" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener">JSocket</a>. For the purposes of this report, we&rsquo;ll refer to all variants of this spyware as &ldquo;AlienSpy.&rdquo; AlienSpy is a relatively full-featured RAT with a range of features, such as recording the victim&rsquo;s keystrokes, audio eavesdropping via a device&rsquo;s built-in microphone, remote viewing of the desktop, and the ability to turn on a victim&rsquo;s webcam &ldquo;without user notification.&rdquo; Alienspy has been extensively analyzed by malware researchers, including reports by <a href="https://www.proofpoint.com/us/threat-insight/post/You-Dirty-RAT" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener">ProofPoint </a>and <a href="https://www.fidelissecurity.com/sites/default/files/FTA_1015_Alienspy_FINAL.pdf" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Fidelis.</a></p>
<h4 class="lh-solid mb3">3.2.1 Packrat&rsquo;s Alienspy Deployment</h4>
<p class="mt0">From 2014 to early 2015, Packrat&rsquo;s preferred technique was to send AlienSpy implants as attachments in phishing emails with the extension &lsquo;.pdf.jar.&rsquo; The default setting in Windows is to hide file extensions, thus making it appear to be a &ldquo;.pdf&rdquo; file. With some minor differences, all the samples from this time period are built in a similar manner. There&rsquo;s an outer .jar (Java archive) file containing a folder named META-INF and two files: Favicon.ico and Principal.class. Upon execution, Principal.class unzips the contents of &ldquo;Favicon.ico&rdquo; (not an icon file, but a .zip archive), and looks for a filename containing &ldquo;.jar&rdquo;.</p>

<h4 class="lh-solid mb3">3.2.2 Adzok makes an appearance</h4>
<p class="mt0">Between 2014-2015, Packrat also used Adzok &ndash; Invisible Remote Administrator. Similar to AlienSpy in functionality, the java-based Adzok is apparently from Bolivia. The premium version costs $990, but it appears that Packrat is using the &ldquo;free&rdquo; version. <h2 class="lh-solid mb3">4. Packrat&rsquo;s Persistent Phishing Campaigns</h2>
<p class="mt0">Packrat is active in phishing, often against the same groups and individuals whom they target with malware. For example, when examining one target of malware attacks, we found this individual had been targeted by dozens of phishing attempts by Packrat during the same period. The same domains and fake identities that Packrat uses to seed malware are also used to serve phishing, although Packrat also maintains dedicated phishing sites and servers. While phishing e-mails appeared to be regularly sent, we also observed particular cases of the phishing apparently sent to targets in response to contacts they made during our investigation.</p>
<p>We have been able to achieve the most systematic visibility of Packrat&rsquo;s campaign against Ecuadorian targets, but have found evidence of targeting in neighboring countries including Venezuela. Packrat uses both e-mails and social media messages, as well as SMSes to send phishing messages.</p>

<h3 class="lh-solid mb3">4.1 Non-Politically Themed Phishing Content</h3>
<p class="mt0">One of the most common phishing techniques is lookalike communications from popular webmail and social media sites containing requests for password verification, notifications of unauthorized logins, and so on. Packrat uses a wide range of templates for the major email providers, including Gmail, Yahoo and Hotmail. A majority of the e-mails are in Spanish. The messages are typically personalized to the targets, including both their names and e-mail addresses.</p>

<h4 class="lh-solid mb3">4.1.2 Example Non-Political Phishing SMSes</h4>

In other cases, the attackers push harder, warning the targeted user that their accounts will be terminated if they fail to follow the link. In at least one case we observed that the messages contained improperly formatted e-mail addresses.</p>

<h3 class="lh-solid mb3">4.2 Politically Themed Phishing</h3>
<p class="mt0">We observed a wide range of e-mails and messages that contained content with political overtones. Packrat takes two basic approaches in the attacks we have examined. First approach: <strong>create&nbsp;</strong><strong>fake political and media organizations</strong>. Second approach: <strong>impersonate well-known groups and high-profile individuals</strong>. Attacks typically took the form of messages and e-mails containing information either in &lsquo;solidarity,&rsquo; upsetting information, or other relevant news. A majority of what we observed seemed to be crafted to appeal to opposition elements.</p>
<p>While much of the phishing seems to mimic common free webmail and social media sites, in specific cases, Packrat mimicked e-mail services of high-profile targets, like the Ecuadorian National Assembly.</p>
<h4 class="lh-solid mb3">4.2.1 Targeting Ecuadorian Parliamentarians</h4>

This malicious site prompts the target(s) to enter their email credentials, which are captured via formmail, a technique seen repeatedly in the phishing pages we identified (see below).</p>

<p class="mt0">Several moves by Packrat gave us access to some more systematic information about the scope of their attacks. During much of 2015, we observed that Packrat regularly used the same bit.ly link in a range of attacks.</p>

<p>A majority of clicks (322) came from direct link clicks, rather than link shares elsewhere. While this particular bitly link was not extensively clicked on social media sites, Packrat does use social media for some seeding.</p>
<h3 class="lh-solid mb3">4.5&nbsp;Note:&nbsp;Lots of Phishing, Not All of it Linkable</h3>
<p class="mt0">The investigation yielded a high volume&nbsp;of &nbsp;Spanish-language phishing attacks against the same individuals that Packrat targeted, however for a number of reasons we were unable to link much of the&nbsp;phishing activity to Packrat. &nbsp;One of the most notable and high volume campaigns that we identified used the domain <strong>gmail.com.msg07.xyz</strong>&nbsp;and masqueraded &nbsp;as a range of account notifications from popular providers including Gmail. &nbsp;Typically these messages displayed as originating&nbsp;from e-mail addresses like &ldquo;<a href="/cdn-cgi/l/email-protection" class="__cf_email__" data-cfemail="6608094b1403151609080203142615131616091412010b070f574805090b">[email&#160;protected]</a>.&rdquo; &nbsp;&nbsp;In some cases, individuals received these messages almost weekly over long periods of time.</p>
<h2 class="lh-solid mb3">5. Possible Deception Operations</h2>
<p class="mt0">Not all of the domains associated with this group appear to have been built to spread malware, or to trick victims into entering their passwords. Several domains are extensively built out, and currently maintained, which appear to be designed to convey the impression of active, news media sites. Some of these sites contain &lsquo;original&rsquo; news stories and &lsquo;leaks&rsquo; about political figures. At least two of these sites seem Venezuela-specific, while a third is Ecuador-oriented.</p>
<p>What makes these three fake organizations exceptionally interesting is that we have found no evidence that they are used to seed malware or conduct phishing, either directly, or as pretexts for messages. While it may be that we simply lack visibility on the targeting, it may be that the pages and identities serve another function. They may be attempts to seed false information, or might serve as watering holes to attract individuals that Packrat or its sponsors wish to monitor. They may also be coupled with other operations on which we have no visibility.</p>

<h2 class="lh-solid mb3">Part 6: The Challenge of Attribution</h2>
<p class="mt0">The evidence presented in this report points to a coordinated and persistent campaign with a regional focus. Naturally, this raises questions about attribution. This section assesses two competing hypotheses: <strong>Hypothesis 1: Packrat is State-Sponsored</strong> and <strong>Hypothesis 2: Packrat is Not State-Sponsored</strong>. For each hypothesis we provide one or more scenarios that we consider plausible versions of the hypothesis.</p>
<h3 class="lh-solid mb3">6.1 Hypothesis 1: Packrat is State-Sponsored</h3>
<h4 class="lh-solid mb3">6.1.2 Examining the Target List</h4>
<p class="mt0">The list of known targets is full of influential people whose activities could have an impact on the domestic and regional political standing of regimes in several countries. Where we have been able to identify individuals who have been targeted, we find vocal, strong regime critics and independent journalists in Ecuador and Argentina. Interestingly, Packrat has also targeted parliamentarians, and others within the government of Ecuador. This diversity of targets with (possibly) opposing positions is a common theme in the data.</p>
<p>In other cases, we find phishing and malware-seeding websites, emails and messages with obviously political themes. Packrat created fake political organizations, whose identities, logos, and websites are then used to seed phishing attacks and malware. These materials seem designed to appeal primarily to both critics and (some) members of governments in Ecuador and Venezuela. We also believe that there are targets in other countries, including Brazil, but have limited information about who they are.</p>
<p>These are the kinds of targets who would be of interest to an intelligence or security service in the region, especially one not equipped to directly and passively monitor all of the communications of their targets. The multiple nations reflected in the seeding and targeting suggest, further, that the sponsor(s) of this activity would be interested in the opponents of several regimes.</p>
<h4 class="lh-solid mb3">6.1.3 Motivation for Disinformation Campaigns</h4>
<p class="mt0">We see a range of fake websites touching on political themes, but not obviously used to seed malware. While some of these sites share the same registrations as malware serving sites, they differ by having extensive content, and show no evidence of having hosted malicious files or phishing pages, or of having been used as pretexts for campaigns, as is common with Packrat. The content reflects extensive effort, as does the backstopping with identities on social media sites.</p>
<p>There seem to be three possible explanations for these pages. First, the pages may be an attempt to create credible fake organizations that can be used to actively promote disinformation, interspersed with real news. Second, the pages may serve as honeypots, used to attract targets, either to identify and possibly manipulate them, or to build trust that could then be used in malware attacks. Finally, the pages may be part of information gathering operations that have other components of which we are not aware.</p>
<p>While it would not be surprising for a political movement or well-funded set of interests to&nbsp;engage in these activities&nbsp;in one country, Packrat has done so in multiple countries. It is not clear what group beyond a state, would be interested in this kind of activity, and have the resources to support it.</p>
<h4 class="lh-solid mb3">6.1.4 Ability to Pay</h4>
<p class="mt0">The hosting and domain registrations necessary to maintain this campaign infrastructure for more than seven years clearly have associated costs. The&nbsp;human labor associated with creating and maintaining the many fake websites we have observed is also costly. This is especially true for the websites with substantial &ldquo;original&rdquo; content, such as PanCaliente. Finally, the extensive and personalized targeting clearly reflects substantial human effort.</p>
<p>The cost of this campaign suggests that Packrat is either well-resourced, or has sponsors who can pay for server space and time, as well as the costs of Packrat&rsquo;s operators. We see no evidence of targeting with out-of-scope seeding materials, such as industry, business or the financial sector. Given the cost of the campaign, it is difficult to see who, beyond a state or an organized entity with political aspirations, would both want the information, and be in a position to pay for it.</p>
<h4 class="lh-solid mb3">6.1.5 Clues That Packrat Feels &lsquo;Safe&rsquo;</h4>
<p class="mt0">When the first reports were published on the Nisman and Argentine targeting in early 2015, some of Packrat&rsquo;s infrastructure was exposed. Nevertheless, much of the infrastructure has remained online. From a pragmatic point of view this makes sense. If Packrat had successful implants operational at that time, taking the infrastructure offline would close that access. Packrat would have had to engage in a process of pushing out updated implants to the infected computers that connected to a new host. This time-consuming process would also be prone to failure and possible detection.</p>
<p>If Packrat were afraid of criminal punishment in their country of residence, it would have been natural to take the servers offline once exposed. Their interlinked infrastructure can be used to investigate, as we did, the broader structure of their campaigns. In the hands of a law enforcement agency, these active servers could potentially be traced back to the responsible parties.</p>
<p>The fact that the servers remain online suggests that Packrat is operating purely on practical concerns, which do not include fear of the authorities. We take this to suggest that they may enjoy a degree of protection in the country or countries in which they operate.</p>
<p>While it is less conclusive, their taunts of a Citizen Lab researcher also suggest a degree of confidence that these actions will not have consequence for them. Given their decision to keep their infrastructure online, this researcher may not have been the first (or the last) to bother them by analyzing one of their attacks.</p>
<h4 class="lh-solid mb3">6.1.6 Two Scenarios of State Sponsorship</h4>
<p class="mt0">In this section we present two possible scenarios for state sponsorship. The data we have presented in this report could be explained by either of these possibilities (or by the scenario outlined for Hypothesis 2: Non State Sponsor).</p>
<p><strong><u>Scenario 1: A Single State Sponsor</u></strong><br>
There are several possible explanations for the targeting we see. It is possible that Packrat is working for a single intelligence or security service, and that these activities all reflect that service&rsquo;s targeting. Such a service would, potentially, wish to keep tabs on many groups, including both its opponents, the opponents of friendly governments, and possibly friendly governments themselves.</p>
<p>The strong representation of ALBA (Bolivarian Alternative for the Americas) countries, and their recent &ldquo;fellow-travelers&rdquo; (Argentina included, although it has recently changed ruling parties), among the locations of targeting may be instructive. The leaders of these countries are widely seen as political allies on the leftist spectrum, although Argentina&rsquo;s most recent <a href="https://www.theguardian.com/world/2015/nov/22/argentina-election-exit-polls-buenos-aires-mauricio-macri" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener">election in November 2015</a> elected a president who seems to reject this relationship. The governments of Ecuador and Venezuela, meanwhile, are especially close allies, although it is&nbsp;unclear how this will evolve given <a href="https://www.theguardian.com/world/2015/dec/07/venezuela-parliamentary-election-opposition-win-political-prisoners-amnesty" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Venezuela&rsquo;s very recent elections</a>.</p>
<p>Some may jump to interpret public reports of malware attacks targeting opponents of the Ecuadorian government as conclusive evidence of official Ecuadorian government involvement with Packrat. They may also point to previous reports that Ecuador&rsquo;s domestic intelligence agency reportedly used the commercial trojan made by Hacking Team to <a href="http://www.foxbusiness.com/markets/2015/08/06/apnewsbreak-leaked-hacking-team-emails-suggest-ecuador-illegally-spied-on/" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener">target at least one dissident</a>. However, the presence of targets within the Ecuadorian parliament and possibly elsewhere in the government<strong>&nbsp;challenges any simplistic theory of Ecuadorian government sponsorship</strong>, but does not completely rule it out as intra-governmental rivalries or other dynamics could be at play.</p>
<p><strong><u>Scenario 2: Mercenary Work for One or More Government Sponsors</u></strong><br>
The range and diversity of regional targets, which includes the opponents of several regimes, as well as those same governments supporters in some cases, could be taken as indicating that the threat actor group is reusing the same infrastructure for campaigns on behalf of multiple client governments at once. It is possible, for example, that Packrat is in the &ldquo;address book&rdquo; of multiple clients, and reuses the same infrastructure for multiple campaigns.</p>
<h3 class="lh-solid mb3">6.2 Hypothesis 2: Packrat is Not a State Actor</h3>
<p class="mt0">Although some of the evidence we have outlined above provides circumstantial support for state sponsorship, other features of Packrat&rsquo;s activity are not so clear. This section briefly lays out the most salient of these pieces of evidence: lack of technical sophistication. We evaluate this fact, and then briefly note a potential scenario for non-state sponsorship.</p>
<h4 class="lh-solid mb3">6.2.1 Lack of Use of technically sophisticated tools</h4>
<p class="mt0">The malware used by this campaign is primarily Commercial Off-The-Shelf (COTS) RATs, not boutique or tailor-made implants. Nor is it the commercial malware sold to governments by companies like FinFisher and Hacking Team. Additionally, the attackers do not make use of exploits for their malware seeding. The lack of exploits very likely hampers the efficacy of many attacks. For example some of the bait documents instruct victims to double click on an icon in the document. This is cumbersome, and is more likely to result in failed attacks or discovery. A threat actor with the direct support of a government might have access to more sophisticated malware, and exploits.</p>
<p>However, it is well documented that not all government-linked malware groups make use of sophisticated malware or exploits. <a href="https://targetedthreats.net/" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Prior research</a> by Citizen Lab, for example, has shown that state-sponsored groups targeting civil society often use the minimum necessary technical sophistication in their campaigns. Why use more sophisticated or esoteric technique when simpler tools suffice? Other research on government-linked hacking groups in the Middle East also suggests that these groups persist in exploit-less targeting, perhaps because it is inexpensive, and that it nevertheless achieves a minimum satisfactory level of infections.</p>
<p>Ultimately, the reliance on COTS malware and lack of exploits does not enable us to draw many conclusions. However, it does have one feature worth noting: by extensively obfuscating COTS malware, the attackers are nevertheless able to deliver malware that is effective at evading detection while simultaneously being difficult to attribute. That said, since obfuscated malware is also common in criminal hacking, the pairing of crimeware and obfuscators does not tell either way towards this hypothesis.</p>
<h4 class="lh-solid mb3">6.2.2: Scenario: Non-State Group</h4>
<p class="mt0">It is impossible to reject the possibility that Packrat is a criminal or non-state group. Such a group could, in theory, be a supporter of an opposition political movement, or have other interests in the critics of regimes. There are a range of powerful non-state groups in South America, including cartels and others involved in illegal trafficking, who would certainly have the ability to pay for these operations. Nevertheless, given the particular targets we are aware of, we are unsure of why these individuals would be of primary interest to groups involved in illegal activities.</p>
<p>Another possibility, also unfalsifiable, is that a non-government group with political ambitions is responsible for Packrat. Such a group might be particularly interested in potential allies or sources of instability, as well as governments.</p>
<h3 class="lh-solid mb3">6.3 Adjudicating between competing hypotheses</h3>
<p class="mt0">Ultimately, this report does not provide sufficient data to conclusively adjudicate between hypotheses. However, we think that the best fit, which is still circumstantial, is that the ultimate recipient of the information collected by Packrat is likely one or more governments in the region.</p>
<h2 class="lh-solid mb3">7. Conclusion</h2>
<p class="mt0">This report described a seven year campaign with targets in several Latin American countries. While there are many well-known threat actors in Latin America, many of the most visible are engaged in cybercrime. What distinguishes Packrat is the extensive and often ingenious targeting of political figures, journalists and others. They are also distinguished by their ability to remain active over such an extended period, seemingly unfazed by discovery.</p>
<p>Packrat highlights the extent to which multi-year campaigns can be run using limited technical sophistication, and a lot of creativity. From a technical perspective, they rely almost entirely on off-the-shelf RATs and packers to evade antivirus detection. Where they excel is in the time and effort spent to create detailed and moderately convincing fake organizations to seed their malware.</p>
<p>Their persistence, and their willingness to keep using domains even after they are exposed suggests that exposure of their infrastructure is not an existential threat. Their threats and taunts are similarly brazen. This strongly suggests, but does not prove, that Packrat operates with a perception of safety.</p>
<p>Ultimately, this report does not conclusively attribute Packrat&rsquo;s activities, however we hope that by exposing their activities, we have provided encouragement to others to continue to follow the thread.</p>
<h3 class="lh-solid mb3">Note: The Precarious Position of Media in Latin America</h3>
<p class="mt0">While we discourage a direct link between specific incidents of non-digital harassment of journalists and Packrat, the case highlights the difficult situation faced by journalists and freedom of expression supporters in countries like Ecuador.</p>
<p>In Ecuador, for example, <a href="https://www.hrw.org/americas/ecuador" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener">numerous</a> <a href="https://freedomhouse.org/report/freedom-press/2013/ecuador" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener">observers</a>, including the <a href="http://www.ohchr.org/EN/NewsEvents/Pages/DisplayNews.aspx?NewsID=16453&amp;LangID=E" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener">United Nations Special Rapporteur for Freedom of Expressio</a>n, have expressed concern that the freedoms afforded to journalists continue to be constricted. In Ecuador journalists, <a href="http://www.economist.com/news/americas/21595946-government-orders-newspaper-correct-critical-cartoon-drawn-and-quartered" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener">and even cartoonists</a>, have faced apparent retaliation for political speech. Journalist Martha Roldos, for example, had personal e-mails exposed in the press, and recordings of her conversations played in public. Freedom of expression and journalism organization Fundamedios, which has documented <a href="https://www.theguardian.com/global-development-professionals-network/2015/sep/09/ngo-alert-ecuadorian-government-orders-press-freedom-group-to-disband" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener">over 600 attacks against journalists</a> from 2008-2012, has <a href="http://www.ohchr.org/EN/NewsEvents/Pages/DisplayNews.aspx?NewsID=16453&amp;LangID=E" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener">face forced shutdown</a>. Interestingly, both of these individuals were also targets of Packrat. While we have no evidence linking the perpetrators of these actions to Packrat, nor reason to believe the sponsor is the same, the targeted malware that we describe only adds to the threats these individuals and organizations face.</p>
