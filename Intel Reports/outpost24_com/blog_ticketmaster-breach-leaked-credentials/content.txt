					<h1 class="entry-title">TicketMaster breach: Leaked credentials are the golden ticket once again</h1>					<div class="post-info">
						<span class="post-category">
							<a href="https://outpost24.com/blog/category/research-and-threat-intel/">Research &amp; Threat Intel</a>							</span>
							<span class="post-date">17 Jun 2024</span>
						</div>
					</div>
											<div class="article-author">
							<span class="written-by">Written By</span>
																							<div class="author-container">
									<span class="author-name"><a href="https://outpost24.com/blog/authors/krakenlabs/">KrakenLabs</a></span>
									<span class="author-title">Threat Intelligence Team, Outpost24</span>
								</div>
													</div>
										
<p>It had already been a challenging few weeks for Live Nation Entertainment, Inc. as they faced down a lawsuit from <a href="https://www.justice.gov/atr/media/1353101/dl" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">The Justice Department</a> regarding anti-competitive practices. Things got worse at the end of May when a cybercriminal known as “<strong><em>SpidermanData</em></strong>” claimed to have breached a huge database of 560 million records (including personal and financial data) belonging to TicketMaster Entertainment, LLC – a Live Nation company.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>



<p>Outpost24’s Threat Intelligence team, KrakenLabs, has been analyzing the situation and looking into the role of leaked credentials. We’ll walk through what happened and share some advice on avoiding the same fate for your own organization.&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-how-was-the-breach-discovered-nbsp-nbsp"><span class="ez-toc-section" id="how-was-the-breach-discovered"></span>How was the breach discovered?&nbsp;&nbsp;<span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h2>



<p>On May 27<sup>th</sup>, a user nicknamed “<strong><em>SpidermanData</em></strong>” put a database up for sale on the underground <strong>“forum</strong> <strong>Exploit”</strong> that contained info from 560 million users<strong>.&nbsp;</strong> They alleged the data belonged to Live Nation and TicketMaster (both companies fall under Live Nation Entertainment, Inc.). The user had registered on the forum on that same day and has shown no further activity beyond that original post.&nbsp;</p>



<p>The next day, on May 28<sup>th</sup>, a known threat actor named “<strong><em>ShinyHunters</em></strong>” posted the same content<strong> </strong>on the underground forum<strong> “BreachForums”</strong>. This forum had been seized on May 15<sup>th</sup> by law enforcement authorities earlier this year. However, its administrators (with <em>ShinyHunters</em> among them) managed to get it back online on May 28<sup>th</sup>, exactly the same day that the Live Nation/TicketMaster database was put up for sale <sup>[</sup><a href="https://x.com/DarkWebInformer/status/1795237523238551694" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener"><sup>1</sup></a><sup>] [</sup><a href="https://x.com/vxunderground/status/1796217510544527857" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener"><sup>2</sup></a><sup>]</sup>.&nbsp;</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-how-did-the-attackers-gain-access-nbsp-nbsp"><span class="ez-toc-section" id="how-did-the-attackers-gain-access"></span>How did the attackers gain access?&nbsp;&nbsp;<span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h2>



<p>Researchers from vx-underground managed to speak with individuals involved in the alleged breach, and on May 30<sup>th</sup><strong> </strong>they published <sup>[</sup><a href="https://x.com/vxunderground/status/1796063116574314642" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener"><sup>3</sup></a><sup>]</sup> this claim: “<em>Sometime in April, an unidentified Threat Group was able to get access to TicketMaster AWS instances by pivoting from a Managed Service Provider (MSP). The TicketMaster breach was not performed by ShinyHunters group. ShinyHunters is the individual and/or group which posted the auction of the data, they are acting as a proxy for the Threat Group responsible for the compromise.”</em>&nbsp;</p>



<p>On June 1<sup>st</sup>, these same researchers said it had been confirmed that the MSP in question was <strong>Snowflake</strong>, an American cloud computing–based data cloud company. The individuals who claimed responsibility for the breach claimed said had gained access via an infostealer.&nbsp;</p>



<p>On May 31<sup>st</sup>, security company <strong>Hudson Rock</strong> confirmed that a Snowflake employee’s credentials were stolen via a <strong>Lumma Stealer</strong> campaign. The information provided by the firm was based on a conversation in Telegram with the alleged threat actor. Security company <strong>White Intel</strong> corroborated that the account was compromised as well. Along with the TicketMaster compromise, the threat actor claimed authorship of other relevant data breaches that had been disclosed recently: <a href="https://securityaffairs.com/163956/data-breach/shinyhunters-claims-santander-breach.html" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">Santander Bank</a>, Anheuser-Busch, State Farm, Mitsubishi, Progressive, Neiman Marcus, Allstate, and Advance Auto Part.&nbsp;</p>



<p>It’s worth noting both posts from these security firms have now been removed due to ambiguities <sup>[</sup><a href="https://www.linkedin.com/posts/hudson-rock_activity-7203433945919578113-RH05/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener"><sup>4</sup></a><sup>] [</sup><a href="https://x.com/whiteintel_io/status/1796794483339669968" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener"><sup>5</sup></a><sup>]</sup> after Snowflake’s statements about the incident. However, an archived version of the Hudson Rock article can still be reached <sup>[</sup><a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20240531140540/https:/hudsonrock.com/blog/snowflake-massive-breach-access-through-infostealer-infection" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener"><sup>6</sup></a><sup>]</sup>.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-what-have-ticketmaster-and-snowflake-said-nbsp-nbsp"><span class="ez-toc-section" id="what-have-ticketmaster-and-snowflake-said"></span>What have TicketMaster and Snowflake said?&nbsp;&nbsp;<span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h2>



<p>On May 31<sup>st</sup>, Live Nation Entertainment, Inc. (TicketMaster’s parent company) filed an 8-K form to inform the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) <sup>[</sup><a href="https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1335258/000133525824000081/lyv-20240520.htm" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener"><sup>7</sup></a><sup>]</sup> that on May 20<sup>th</sup>, they “<em>identified unauthorized activity within a third-party cloud database environment containing Company data (primarily from its TicketMaster L.L.C. subsidiary)</em>”.&nbsp;</p>



<p>After being accused of acting as an unwilling entry vector, Snowflake, together with third-party cybersecurity experts, CrowdStrike and Mandiant, published their own joint statement on June 2<sup>nd</sup>. They said they were involved in an ongoing investigation into a targeted threat campaign against some Snowflake customer accounts <sup>[</sup><a href="https://community.snowflake.com/s/question/0D5VI00000Emyl00AB/detecting-and-preventing-unauthorized-user-access" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener"><sup>8</sup></a><sup>]</sup>. Among their key preliminary findings, they claim that they “<em>had not been able to identify as the cause of the activity, a vulnerability, misconfiguration, or breach of Snowflake’s platform and neither a compromised credential of current or former Snowflake personnel</em>.”&nbsp;</p>



<p>Snowflake did not deny what security firms had been saying over the previous days and confirmed they found evidence that “<em>a threat actor obtained personal credentials to and accessed demo accounts belonging to a former Snowflake employee.”</em> However, they said this account would not have given access to sensitive data. Furthermore, they claim the reason it became compromised was because was it was a demo account not secured with Okta or multi-factor authentication (MFA), unlike the usual set-up for Snowflake’s corporate and production systems.&nbsp;</p>



<p>Snowflake does confirm that behind the current issue there “a<em>ppears to be a targeted campaign directed at users with single-factor authentication,” </em>and that<em> “as part of this campaign, threat actors have leveraged credentials previously purchased or obtained through infostealing malware”</em>. With these two key points, Snowflake are saying although they haven’t been compromised, they would have detected an active campaign against their clients where threat actors were using credentials previously compromised with infostealers to target accounts that have not been properly secured with multi-factor authentication.&nbsp;</p>



<p>The company did not provide a public list of customers that they believe might have been affected but claimed to have promptly informed them. In addition, to assist its clients in investigating potential threat activity based on their findings, they provided some guidelines for clients to follow <sup>[</sup><a href="https://community.snowflake.com/s/article/Communication-ID-0108977-Additional-Information" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener"><sup>9</sup></a><sup>]</sup>.&nbsp;</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-what-conclusions-can-we-draw-nbsp"><span class="ez-toc-section" id="what-conclusions-can-we-draw"></span>What conclusions can we draw?&nbsp;<span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h2>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-1-both-snowflake-and-live-nation-ticketmaster-have-been-compromised-nbsp"><span class="ez-toc-section" id="1-both-snowflake-and-live-nation-ticketmaster-have-been-compromised"></span>1. <strong>Both Snowflake and Live Nation (TicketMaster) have been compromised.</strong>&nbsp;<span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h3>



<p>One employee of Snowflake got their credentials stolen via a Lumma Stealer campaign, likely on October 5<sup>th</sup>, 2023 <sup>[6]</sup>. The compromised credentials belong to a demo account that was not behind Okta or Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) <sup>[8]</sup>. This account was compromised and threat actors were able to access it. However, this account did not have access to sensitive data. Live Nation (TicketMaster) was compromised through a third-party cloud database environment containing company data <sup>[7]</sup>.&nbsp;</p>



<p>Despite both compromises being true, what hasn’t been confirmed is the relationship between them. Snowflake has confirmed <sup>[8]</sup> that they have detected an active campaign against their clients where threat actors would be using credentials previously compromised with infostealers to target accounts that have not been properly secured with multi-factor authentication. However, they have denied that the cause of this activity would be a compromised credential of current or former Snowflake personnel.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>



<p>Live Nation has not confirmed nor denied yet if the access to the third-party cloud database environment containing company data was done using a compromised credential and neither if the account had MFA enabled or not.&nbsp;</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-2-we-can-t-confirm-the-impact-of-the-targeted-campaign-nbsp"><span class="ez-toc-section" id="2-we-cant-confirm-the-impact-of-the-targeted-campaign"></span><strong>2. We can’t confirm the impact of the targeted campaign.</strong>&nbsp;<span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h3>



<p>Hudson Rock mentioned <sup>[6]</sup> at least nine potential victims related to this attack campaign. Security researcher Kevin Beaumont confirmed on June 1<sup>st</sup>, that six major organizations were running Snowflake incidents <sup>[</sup><a href="https://cyberplace.social/@GossiTheDog/112538298122679069" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener"><sup>10</sup></a><sup>]</sup>. One of these companies would be Advance Auto Parts, whose data had already been put up for sale on BreachForums on June 5<sup>th</sup> <sup>[</sup><a href="https://x.com/H4ckManac/status/1798342651407663254" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener"><sup>11</sup></a><sup>]</sup>. Also citing Beaumont, Snowflake offers a free trial where anybody can sign up and upload data <sup>[</sup><a href="https://cyberplace.social/@GossiTheDog/112536407633131499" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener"><sup>12</sup></a><sup>]</sup>, and their authentication setup would not be an easy task <sup>[</sup><a href="https://cyberplace.social/@GossiTheDog/112552067562285307" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener"><sup>13</sup></a><sup>]</sup>.&nbsp;</p>



<p>If threat actors are indeed targeting Snowflake’s customers by leveraging credentials previously purchased or obtained through infostealing malware, it’s very likely that more customers will be affected. For these credentials to work out, these customers must also have single-factor authentication enabled, something more likely due to the complications highlighted by researchers for enabling the MFA.&nbsp;</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-3-identity-based-attacks-are-in-the-spotlight-over-the-last-couple-of-months-nbsp"><span class="ez-toc-section" id="3-identity-based-attacks-are-in-the-spotlight-over-the-last-couple-of-months"></span><strong>3</strong>. <strong>Identity-based attacks are in the spotlight over the last couple of months.</strong>&nbsp;<span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h3>



<p>Citing the alleged threat actors, vx-underground mentioned <sup>[3]</sup> “<em>Sometime in April an unidentified Threat Group was able to get access to TicketMaster AWS instances by pivoting from a Managed Service Provider.” </em>Coincidentally, Okta security published on May 28, 2024, a warning for “<em>suspicious activity that started on April 15</em>” and that was related to endpoints used to support a cross-origin authentication feature in their Customer Identity Cloud (CIC) prone to being targeted by threat actors orchestrating credential-stuffing attacks <sup>[</sup><a href="https://sec.okta.com/articles/2024/05/detecting-cross-origin-authentication-credential-stuffing-attacks" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener"><sup>13</sup></a><sup>]</sup>.&nbsp;</p>



<p>Okta’s advisory comes right after Cisco Talos confirmed they were actively monitoring a global increase in brute-force attacks against a variety of targets, since at least March 18<sup>th</sup>, 2024 <sup>[</sup><a href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/large-scale-brute-force-activity-targeting-vpns-ssh-services-with-commonly-used-login-credentials/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener"><sup>14</sup></a><sup>]</sup>. Based on their findings, the brute-forcing attempts were this time using generic usernames and valid usernames for specific organizations. It’s important to highlight that we’re not establishing a relation between all these advisories, but rather just pointing out the likelihood of the campaign that Snowflake mentioned in their statement.&nbsp;</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-4-credentials-are-almost-always-the-root-cause-nbsp"><span class="ez-toc-section" id="4-credentials-are-almost-always-the-root-cause"></span>4. <strong>Credentials are (almost) always the root cause!</strong>&nbsp;<span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h3>



<p>Identity-based attacks can be achieved through brute-force but it’s more efficient to directly use a previously-compromised credential. The benefits of the second option are likely one of the reasons behind the massive spurge of infostealers infections nowadays. Recent reporting from Mandiant <sup>[</sup><a href="https://cloud.google.com/blog/products/identity-security/a-year-in-the-cybersecurity-trenches-with-mandiant-managed-defense" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener"><sup>14</sup></a><sup>]</sup> and Kaspersky <sup>[</sup><a href="https://www.kaspersky.com/about/press-releases/2024_data-stealing-malware-infections-increased-sevenfold-since-2020-kaspersky-experts-say" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener"><sup>15</sup></a><sup>]</sup>&nbsp; cite this type of malware as the most prevalent during the past year, with nearly 10 million devices falling victim to infostealers during 2023.&nbsp;</p>



<p>To accommodate the huge amount of stolen information, the current credential theft ecosystem has also evolved and welcomed other business models such as the traffers organizations (check Outpost24 KrakenLabs analyst’s <a href="https://outpost24.com/blog/traffers-and-the-growing-threat-against-credentials/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">The Rising Threat of Traffers</a> report for further information) together with other sharing platforms like the legitimate messaging application Telegram.&nbsp;</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-5-who-s-really-behind-the-attacks-nbsp"><span class="ez-toc-section" id="5-whos-really-behind-the-attacks"></span>5. <strong>Who’s really behind the attacks?</strong>&nbsp;<span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h3>



<p>Some researchers have dared to mention a possible implication of “<em>Scattered Spider</em>” <sup>[</sup><a href="https://cyberknow.substack.com/p/navigating-the-ticketmaster-data" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener"><sup>16</sup></a><sup>]</sup> in this new campaign. This idea would not be far-fetched since this group has already been involved in carrying out identity-based attacks. However, so far, we have not been able to gather conclusive information that would allow us to confirm this hypothesis.&nbsp;</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-secure-your-organization-against-leaked-credentials-nbsp-nbsp"><span class="ez-toc-section" id="secure-your-organization-against-leaked-credentials"></span>Secure your organization against leaked credentials&nbsp;&nbsp;<span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h2>
