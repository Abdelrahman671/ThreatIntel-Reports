<p>Why would OurMine want to target WikiLeaks?  Well, there isn&#8217;t much love lost between them, following the alleged personal details of members of the hacking group were published online was followed by a <a href="https://hotforsecurity.bitdefender.com/blog/wikileaks-knocked-offline-in-ongoing-feud-between-ourmine-hacking-gang-and-anonymous-14918.html">DDoS attack against the whistle-blowing site</a> in 2016.</p>
<p>The good news for WikiLeaks this time was that things weren&#8217;t quite as bad as they might have first appeared.</p>
<p><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" src="https://grahamcluley.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/09/wikileaks-170.jpeg" alt="Wikileaks " width="170" height="201" class="alignright size-full wp-image-273363" />You see, the WikiLeaks website hadn&#8217;t been hacked.</p>
<p>Instead, OurMine had managed to alter WikiLeaks&#8217;s DNS records (held by a third-party registrar) to direct anyone who tried to visit wikileaks.org to visit a different IP address which definitely <em>wasn&#8217;t</em> under the control of Julian Assange and his cronies.</p>
<p>Was that too nerdy for you?  Think of it this way.  The internet has &#8216;telephone directories&#8217;, known as DNS (Domain Name System) records, that translate website names (such as wikileaks.org) into a numeric address (such as 95.211.113.131) that the internet understands.</p>
<p>Change the numbers in the telephone directory, and anyone trying to get to wikileaks.org could end up somewhere else entirely.</p>
<p>This kind of domain hijacking isn&#8217;t a new threat &#8211; past victims have included such well-known services as <a href="https://grahamcluley.com/whatsapp-hacked-offline/">WhatsApp</a> and anti-virus firm <a href="https://grahamcluley.com/avg-website-palestinian-hackers/">AVG</a> &#8211; but it&#8217;s a very effective way to embarrass an organisation publicly, and even (in the worst cases) attempt to scam visitors or <a href="https://www.theregister.co.uk/2017/07/13/swiss_domain_name_hijack/" rel="nofollow" title="Link to The Register article">redirect them to malware</a>.</p>
<p>And while your website&#8217;s domain records are under someone else&#8217;s control it&#8217;s not only possible that your website visitors are being redirected, but also that emails being sent to your organisation are being sent somewhere else entirely too.</p>
<p>We don&#8217;t know how OurMine managed to access WikiLeaks&#8217;s DNS records, but past experience has shown that their typical modus operandi is simply to log in using their victim&#8217;s password.</p>
<p>If that happened in this case then it&#8217;s possible that someone at WikiLeaks was either phished, had an easy-to-guess password, fell victim to some spyware, or made the mistake of reusing the same password in different places.</p>
<p>Alternatively, the attackers might have used social engineering to trick WikiLeaks&#8217;s DNS provider into handing over the credentials, or simple requested that a password reset link be sent to a compromised email address.</p>
<p>For this reason, many DNS registrars offer additional levels of security beyond passwords &#8211; such as registry locking and two-factor authentication (2FA) &#8211; to better protect the critical data they store about your website.</p>
<p>For instance, the DNS registrar I use for grahamcluley.com is DNSimple which has been <a href="https://support.dnsimple.com/articles/two-factor-authentication/" rel="nofollow" title="Link to DNSimple article">supporting 2FA</a> since 2012.</p>
<p><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" src="https://grahamcluley.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/09/dnsimple-2fa.jpeg" alt="Dnsimple 2fa" width="600" height="433" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-273332" srcset="https://grahamcluley.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/09/dnsimple-2fa.jpeg 600w, https://grahamcluley.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/09/dnsimple-2fa-300x217.jpeg 300w" sizes="auto, (max-width: 709px) 85vw, (max-width: 909px) 67vw, (max-width: 984px) 61vw, (max-width: 1362px) 45vw, 600px" /></p>
<p>Even if a malicious hacker has managed to determine the password for your company&#8217;s 2FA-protected account at the DNS registry, they shouldn&#8217;t be able to access it because they don&#8217;t know your one-time numeric PIN.</p>
<p>Of course, it&#8217;s always possible that an attacker might exploit a vulnerability in your DNS registrar&#8217;s systems to fiddle with your website&#8217;s records, perhaps without needing to know your password or bypass 2FA.  But such attacks are by their very nature much rarer.</p>
<p>If you own a website, take advantage of the security features that your DNS registrar offers you &#8211; or find an alternative registrar who will do more to protect your account.</p>
<p>And if you&#8217;re the administrator of the WikiLeaks website, just be grateful that OurMine was feeling more mischievous than malicious &#8211; as this attack could have been much more serious.</p>
