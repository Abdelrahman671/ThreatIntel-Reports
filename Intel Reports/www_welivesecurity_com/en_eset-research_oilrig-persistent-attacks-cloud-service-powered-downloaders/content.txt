    <h1 class="page-headline">OilRig’s persistent attacks using cloud service-powered downloaders</h1>    <p class="sub-title">ESET researchers document a series of new OilRig downloaders, all relying on legitimate cloud service providers for C&C communications</p>
    
    <div class="article-authors d-flex flex-wrap"><div class="article-author d-flex"><a href="/en/our-experts/zuzana-hromcova/" title="Zuzana Hromcová"><picture><source srcset="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/tn/-x45/wls/2023/07/zuzana-hromcova-2.jpeg" media="(max-width: 768px)" /><img class="author-image me-3"  src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/tn/-x45/wls/2023/07/zuzana-hromcova-2.jpeg" alt="Zuzana Hromcová"  /></picture></a><div class="author-text"><p><a href="/en/our-experts/zuzana-hromcova/" title="Zuzana Hromcová"><b>Zuzana Hromcová</b></a></p></div></div><div class="article-author d-flex"><a href="/en/our-experts/adam-burgher/" title="Adam Burgher"><picture><source srcset="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/tn/-x45/wls/2021/06/Adam-Burgher.jpg" media="(max-width: 768px)" /><img class="author-image me-3"  src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/tn/-x45/wls/2021/06/Adam-Burgher.jpg" alt="Adam Burgher"  /></picture></a><div class="author-text"><p><a href="/en/our-experts/adam-burgher/" title="Adam Burgher"><b>Adam Burgher</b></a></p></div></div></div>

        <picture><source srcset="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/tn/-x266/wls/2023/2023-12/oilrig-eset-research-cloud-downloaders.jpeg" media="(max-width: 768px)" /><source srcset="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/tn/-x425/wls/2023/2023-12/oilrig-eset-research-cloud-downloaders.jpeg" media="(max-width: 1120px)" /><img class="hero-image"  src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/tn/-x700/wls/2023/2023-12/oilrig-eset-research-cloud-downloaders.jpeg" alt="OilRig’s persistent attacks using cloud service-powered downloaders"  /></picture>    </div>

                    <div class="article-body">
    <p>ESET researchers analyzed a growing series of OilRig downloaders that the group has used in several campaigns throughout 2022, to maintain access to target organizations of special interest &ndash; all located in Israel. These lightweight downloaders, which we named SampleCheck5000 (SC5k v1-v3), OilCheck, ODAgent, and OilBooster, are notable for using one of several legitimate cloud service APIs for C&amp;C communication and data exfiltration: the Microsoft Graph OneDrive or Outlook APIs, and the Microsoft Office Exchange Web Services (EWS) API.</p>
<p>In all cases, the downloaders use a shared (email or cloud storage) OilRig-operated account to exchange messages with the OilRig operators; the same account is typically shared by multiple victims. The downloaders access this account to download commands and additional payloads staged by the operators, and to upload command output and staged files.</p>
<p>We discovered the earliest of the series, SC5k (v1) downloader, in November 2021, when it was used in OilRig&rsquo;s Outer Space campaign, documented in our <a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/en/eset-research/oilrigs-outer-space-juicy-mix-same-ol-rig-new-drill-pipes/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">recent blogpost</a>. In the current blogpost, we focus on all of the SC5k successors that OilRig developed throughout 2022, with a new variation introduced every few months; we will also take a closer look at the mechanisms employed by these downloaders. We also compare these downloaders to other OilRig backdoors that use email-based C&amp;C protocols, and that were reported earlier this year by <a href="https://www.trendmicro.com/en_us/research/23/b/new-apt34-malware-targets-the-middle-east.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Trend Micro</a> (MrPerfectionManager) and <a href="https://symantec-enterprise-blogs.security.com/blogs/threat-intelligence/crambus-middle-east-government" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Symantec</a> (PowerExchange).</p>
<p>Finally, this blogpost also expands on our <a href="https://events.sentinelone.com/event/1bdc4a79-98f4-4913-a8d6-efb9a6796cce/websitePage:9107aab7-3c7b-47d0-bad4-4f85cf7fcecd" target="_blank" rel="noopener">LABScon 2023 presentation</a>, where we drilled down into how OilRig keeps access to selected Israeli organizations: all of the downloaders studied in this blogpost were deployed in networks that were previously affected by multiple OilRig tools, which underlines the fact that OilRig is persistent in targeting the same organizations, and determined to keep its foothold in compromised networks.</p>
<blockquote>
<p><strong>Key points of this blogpost:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li><em>OilRig actively developed and used a series of downloaders with a similar logic throughout 2022: three new downloaders &ndash; ODAgent, OilCheck, OilBooster &ndash; and newer versions of the SC5k downloader.</em></li>
<li><em>The downloaders use various legitimate cloud service APIs for C&amp;C communication and data exfiltration: Microsoft Graph OneDrive API, Microsoft Graph Outlook API, and Microsoft Office EWS API.</em></li>
<li><em>Targets, all in Israel, included an organization in the healthcare sector, a manufacturing company, a local governmental organization, and other organizations.</em></li>
<li><em>All targets were previously affected by multiple OilRig campaigns.</em></li>
</ul>
</blockquote>
<h2>Attribution</h2>
<p>OilRig, also known as APT34, Lyceum, Crambus, or Siamesekitten, is a cyberespionage group that has been active since at least 2014 and is <a href="https://media.defense.gov/2019/Oct/18/2002197242/-1/-1/0/NSA_CSA_TURLA_20191021%20VER%203%20-%20COPY.PDF" target="_blank" rel="noopener">commonly believed</a> to be based in Iran. The group targets Middle Eastern governments and a variety of business verticals, including chemical, energy, financial, and telecommunications.</p>
<p>OilRig carried out the DNSpionage campaign in <a href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/2018/11/dnspionage-campaign-targets-middle-east.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener">2018</a> and <a href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/2019/04/dnspionage-brings-out-karkoff.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener">2019</a>, which targeted victims in Lebanon and the United Arab Emirates. In 2019 and 2020, OilRig continued its attacks with the <a href="https://www.mandiant.com/resources/hard-pass-declining-apt34-invite-to-join-their-professional-network">HardPass</a> campaign, which used LinkedIn to target Middle Eastern victims in the energy and government sectors. In 2021, OilRig updated its <a href="https://vblocalhost.com/uploads/VB2021-Kayal-etal.pdf">DanBot</a> backdoor and began deploying the <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20230129145433/https:/www.prevailion.com/latest-targets-of-cyber-group-lyceum/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Shark, Milan</a>, and Marlin backdoors, as mentioned in the <a href="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/en/papers/threat-reports/eset_threat_report_t32021.pdf" target="_blank" rel="noopener">T3 2021</a> issue of the ESET Threat Report. In 2022 and 2023, the group carried out several attacks against local government entities and healthcare organizations in Israel, using its new backdoors <a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/en/eset-research/oilrigs-outer-space-juicy-mix-same-ol-rig-new-drill-pipes/">Solar and Mango</a>. In 2023, OilRig targeted organizations in the Middle East with the <a href="https://symantec-enterprise-blogs.security.com/blogs/threat-intelligence/crambus-middle-east-government" target="_blank" rel="noopener">PowerExchange</a> and <a href="https://www.trendmicro.com/en_us/research/23/b/new-apt34-malware-targets-the-middle-east.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener">MrPerfectionManager</a> backdoors, and related tools to harvest internal mailbox account credentials and then to leverage these accounts for exfiltration.</p>
<p>We attribute SC5k (v1-v3), ODAgent, OilCheck, and OilBooster downloaders to OilRig with a high level of confidence, based on these indicators:</p>
<ul>
<li>Targets:
<ul>
<li>These downloaders were deployed exclusively against Israeli organizations, which aligns with typical OilRig targeting.</li>
<li>The observed verticals of the victims also align with OilRig&rsquo;s interests &ndash; for example, we have seen OilRig previously targeting the <a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/en/eset-research/oilrigs-outer-space-juicy-mix-same-ol-rig-new-drill-pipes/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Israeli healthcare sector</a>, as well as the <a href="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/en/papers/threat-reports/eset-apt-activity-report-q2-2023-q3-2023.pdf" target="_blank" rel="noopener">local government sector in Israel</a>.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>Code similarities:
<ul>
<li>The SC5k v2 and v3 downloaders evolved naturally from the initial version, which was previously used in an OilRig <a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/en/eset-research/oilrigs-outer-space-juicy-mix-same-ol-rig-new-drill-pipes/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Outer Space campaign</a>. ODAgent, OilCheck and OilBooster share similar logic, and all use various cloud service providers for their C&amp;C communications, as do <a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/en/eset-research/oilrigs-outer-space-juicy-mix-same-ol-rig-new-drill-pipes/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">SC5k</a>, <a href="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/en/papers/threat-reports/eset_threat_report_t32021.pdf" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Marlin</a>, <a href="https://symantec-enterprise-blogs.security.com/blogs/threat-intelligence/crambus-middle-east-government" target="_blank" rel="noopener">PowerExchange</a>, and <a href="https://www.trendmicro.com/en_us/research/23/b/new-apt34-malware-targets-the-middle-east.html">MrPerfectionManager</a>.</li>
<li>While not unique to OilRig, these downloaders have a low level of sophistication and are often unnecessarily noisy on the system, which is a practice we previously observed in its <a href="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/en/papers/threat-reports/eset_threat_report_t32021.pdf" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Out to Sea campaign</a>.</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>In February 2022, we detected a new OilRig downloader, which we named ODAgent based on its filename: <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">ODAgent.exe</span>. ODAgent is a C#/.NET downloader that, similar to OilRig&rsquo;s <a href="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/en/papers/threat-reports/eset_threat_report_t32021.pdf" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Marlin backdoor</a>, uses the Microsoft OneDrive API for C&amp;C communications. Unlike Marlin, which supports a comprehensive list of backdoor commands, ODAgent&rsquo;s narrow capabilities are limited to downloading and executing payloads, and to exfiltrating staged files.</p>
<p>ODAgent was detected in the network of a manufacturing company in Israel &ndash; interestingly, the same organization was previously affected by OilRig&rsquo;s <a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/en/eset-research/oilrigs-outer-space-juicy-mix-same-ol-rig-new-drill-pipes/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">SC5k downloader</a>, and later by another new downloader, OilCheck, between April and June 2022. SC5k and OilCheck have similar capabilities to ODAgent, but use cloud-based email services for their C&amp;C communications.</p>
<p>Throughout 2022, we observed the same pattern being repeated on multiple occasions, with new downloaders being deployed in the networks of previous OilRig targets: for example, between June and August 2022, we detected the OilBooster, SC5k v1, and SC5k v2 downloaders and the <a href="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/en/papers/threat-reports/eset_threat_report_t32021.pdf" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Shark backdoor</a>, all in the network of a local governmental organization in Israel. Later we detected yet another SC5k version (v3), in the network of an Israeli healthcare organization, also a previous OilRig victim.</p>
<p>SC5k is a C#/.NET application whose purpose is to download and execute additional OilRig tools using the Office Exchange Web Services (EWS) API. The new versions introduced changes to make retrieval and analysis of the malicious payloads harder for analysts (SC5k v2), and new exfiltration functionality (SC5k v3).</p>
<p><a name="_Hlk152075292"></a>All the downloaders, summarized in Figure 1, share a similar logic but have different implementations and show growing complexity over time, alternating C#/.NET binaries with C/C++ applications, varying the cloud service providers misused for the C&amp;C communication, and other specifics.</p>
<figure class="image"><img title="Figure 1. Timeline of OilRig&rsquo;s downloaders" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2020/03/figure-1-timeline-oilrig-downloaders.png" alt="Figure 1 Timeline OilRig downloaders" width="" height="" />
<figcaption><em>Figure 1. Timeline of OilRig&rsquo;s downloaders</em></figcaption>
</figure>
<p>OilRig has only used these downloaders against a limited number of targets, all located in Israel and, according to ESET telemetry, all of them were persistently targeted months earlier by other OilRig tools. As it is common for organizations to access Office 365 resources, OilRig&rsquo;s cloud service-powered downloaders can thus blend more easily into the regular stream of network traffic &ndash; apparently also the reason why the attackers chose to deploy these downloaders to a small group of especially interesting, repeatedly victimized targets.</p>
<p>As of this writing, the following (exclusively Israeli, as noted above) organizations were affected:</p>
<ul>
<li>a manufacturing company (SC5k v1, ODAgent, and OilCheck),</li>
<li>a local governmental organization (SC5k v1, OilBooster, and SC5k v2),</li>
<li>a healthcare organization (SC5k v3), and</li>
<li>other unidentified organizations in Israel (SC5k v1).</li>
</ul>
<p>Unfortunately, we don&rsquo;t have information about the initial attack vector used to compromise the targets discussed in this blogpost &ndash; we can&rsquo;t confirm whether the attackers have been able to successfully compromise the same organizations repeatedly, or if they somehow managed to keep their foothold in the network in between deploying various tools.</p>
<h2><a name="_Toc152577456"></a>Technical analysis</h2>
<p>In this section, we provide a technical analysis of OilRig&rsquo;s downloaders used throughout 2022, with the details of how they abuse various cloud storage services and cloud-based email providers for their C&amp;C communications. All of these downloaders follow a similar logic:</p>
<ul>
<li>They use a shared (email or cloud storage) account to exchange messages with the OilRig operators; the same account can be used against multiple victims.</li>
<li>They access this account to download commands and additional payloads staged by the operators, and to upload command output and staged files.</li>
</ul>
<p>In our analysis, we focus on these characteristics of the downloaders:</p>
<ul>
<li>Specifics of the network communication protocol (e.g., Microsoft Graph API vs. Microsoft Office EWS API).</li>
<li>The mechanism used to distinguish between different attacker-staged and downloader-uploaded messages in the shared account, including the mechanism to distinguish between messages uploaded from various victims.</li>
<li>Specifics of how the downloaders process commands and payloads are downloaded from the shared account.</li>
</ul>
<p>Table 1 summarizes and compares how the individual downloaders implement these characteristics; we then analyze the first (SC5k) and the most complex (OilBooster) downloaders in detail as examples of tools abusing cloud-based email services and cloud storage services, respectively.</p>

<h3><a name="_Toc152577457"></a>SC5k downloader</h3>
<p>The SampleCheck5000 (or SC5k) downloader is a C#/.NET application, and the first in a series of OilRig&rsquo;s lightweight downloaders that use legitimate cloud services for their C&amp;C communication. We briefly documented the first variant in our <a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/en/eset-research/oilrigs-outer-space-juicy-mix-same-ol-rig-new-drill-pipes/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">recent blogpost</a>, and have since discovered two newer variants.</p>
<p>All SC5k variants use the Microsoft Office EWS API to interact with a shared Exchange mail account, as a way to download additional payloads and commands, and to upload data. Email drafts and their attachments are the primary vehicle for the C&amp;C traffic in all the versions of this downloader, but the later versions increase the complexity of this C&amp;C protocol (SC5k v3) and add detection evasion capabilities (SC5k v2). This section focuses on highlighting these differences.</p>
<h4>Exchange account used for C&amp;C communication</h4>
<p>At runtime, SC5k connects to a remote Exchange server via the EWS API to obtain additional payloads and commands to execute from an email account shared with the attacker (and usually other victims). By default, a Microsoft Office 365 Outlook account is accessed via the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">https://outlook.office365.com/EWS/Exchange.asmx </span>URL using hardcoded credentials, but some SC5k versions also have the capability to connect to other remote Exchange servers when a configuration file is present with a hardcoded name (<span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">setting.key</span>, <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">set.idl</span>) and the corresponding credentials inside.</p>
<p>We have seen the following email addresses used by SC5k versions for C&amp;C communication, the first of which gave the downloader its name:</p>
<ul>
<li><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">samplecheck5000@outlook.com</span></li>
<li><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">FrancesLPierce@outlook.com</span></li>
<li><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">SandraRCharles@outlook.com</span></li>
</ul>
<p>In SC5k v2, the default Microsoft Exchange URL, email address, and password are not included in the main module &ndash; instead, the downloader&rsquo;s code has been split into multiple modules. We have detected only variations of the main application, which logs into a remote Exchange server, iterates through emails in the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">Drafts </span>directory, and extracts additional payloads from their attachments. However, this application depends on two external classes that were not present in the detected samples and are probably implemented in the missing module(s):</p>
<ul>
<li>The class <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">init </span>should provide an interface to obtain the email address, username, and password required to log into the remote Exchange account, and other configuration values from the other module.</li>
<li>The class <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">structure </span>should implement functions used for encryption, compression, executing downloaded payloads, and other helper functions.</li>
</ul>
<p>These changes were likely introduced to make retrieval and analysis of the malicious payloads harder for analysts, as the two missing classes are crucial for identifying the Exchange account used for malware distribution.</p>
<h4>C&amp;C and exfiltration protocol</h4>
<p>In all versions, the SC5k downloader repeatedly logs into a remote Exchange server using the <a href="https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/dotnet/api/microsoft.exchange.webservices.data.exchangeservice?view=exchange-ews-api" target="_blank" rel="noopener">ExchangeService</a> .NET class in the <a href="https://docs.microsoft.com/en-us/dotnet/api/microsoft.exchange.webservices.data?view=exchange-ews-api" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Microsoft.Exchange.WebServices.Data</a> namespace to interact with the EWS API. Once connected, SC5k reads email messages with attachments in the Drafts directory to extract attacker commands and additional payloads. Conversely, in each connection, SC5k exfiltrates files from a local staging directory by creating new email drafts in the same email account. The path to the staging directory varies across samples.</p>
<p>Of interest is the way both the operators and various instances of this downloader can distinguish between the different types of drafts in the shared email account. For one, each email draft has a &lt;victimID&gt; incorporated, which allows the same Exchange account to be used for multiple OilRig victims:</p>
<ul>
<li>For v1 and v2, the downloader transmits the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">&lt;victimID&gt;</span> as a custom attribute of the email draft via the <a href="https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/dotnet/api/microsoft.exchange.webservices.data.item.setextendedproperty?view=exchange-ews-api" target="_blank" rel="noopener">SetExtendedProperty</a> method.</li>
<li>For v3, the downloader incorporates the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">&lt;victimID&gt;</span> into the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">From </span>field of the email draft.</li>
</ul>

<h3><a name="_Toc152577458"></a>Other OilRig tools using email-based C&amp;C protocol</h3>
<p>Besides SC5k, other notable OilRig tools have been discovered subsequently (in 2022 and 2023) that abuse APIs of legitimate cloud-based email services for exfiltration and both directions of their C&amp;C communication.</p>
<p>OilCheck, a C#/.NET downloader discovered in April 2022, also uses draft messages created in a shared email account for both directions of the C&amp;C communication. Unlike SC5k, OilCheck uses the <a href="https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/graph/use-the-api" target="_blank" rel="noopener">REST-based Microsoft Graph API</a> to access a shared Microsoft Office 365 Outlook email account, not the <a href="https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/exchange/client-developer/web-service-reference/ews-reference-for-exchange" target="_blank" rel="noopener">SOAP-based Microsoft Office EWS API</a>. While SC5k uses the built-in <a href="https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/dotnet/api/microsoft.exchange.webservices.data.exchangeservice?view=exchange-ews-api" target="_blank" rel="noopener">ExchangeService</a> .NET class to create the API requests transparently, OilCheck builds the API requests manually. The main characteristics of OilCheck are summarized in Table 1 above.</p>
<p>Earlier in 2023, two other OilRig backdoors were publicly documented: MrPerfectionManager (<a href="https://www.trendmicro.com/en_us/research/23/b/new-apt34-malware-targets-the-middle-east.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Trend Micro</a>, February 2023) and PowerExchange (<a href="https://symantec-enterprise-blogs.security.com/blogs/threat-intelligence/crambus-middle-east-government" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Symantec</a>, October 2023), both using email-based C&amp;C protocols to exfiltrate data. A notable difference between these tools and OilRig&rsquo;s downloaders studied in this blogpost is that the former use the victimized organization&rsquo;s Exchange server to transmit email messages from and to the attacker&rsquo;s email account. In contrast: with SC5k and OilCheck, both the malware and the operator accessed the same Exchange account and communicated by creating email drafts, never sending an actual message.</p>
<p>In any case, the new findings confirm the trend of OilRig shifting away from the previously used HTTP/DNS-based protocols to using legitimate cloud service providers as a way to hide its malicious communication and to mask the group&rsquo;s network infrastructure, while still experimenting with various flavors of such alternative protocols.</p>
<h3><a name="_Toc152577459"></a>OilBooster downloader</h3>
<p>OilBooster is a 64-bit portable executable (PE) written in Microsoft Visual C/C++ with statically linked OpenSSL and Boost libraries (hence the name). Like OilCheck, it uses the <a href="https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/graph/api/overview?view=graph-rest-1.0" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Microsoft Graph API</a> to connect to a Microsoft Office 365 account. Unlike OilCheck, it uses this API to interact with a OneDrive (not Outlook) account controlled by the attackers for C&amp;C communication and exfiltration. OilBooster can download files from the remote server, execute files and shell commands, and exfiltrate the results.</p>
<h4>Overview</h4>
<p>Upon execution, OilBooster hides its console window (via the ShowWindow API) and verifies that it was executed with a command line argument; otherwise it terminates immediately.</p>
<p>OilBooster then builds a <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">&lt;victimID&gt;</span> by combining the compromised computer&rsquo;s hostname and username: <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">&lt;hostname&gt;-&lt;username&gt;</span>. This identifier is later used in the C&amp;C communication: OilBooster creates a specific subdirectory on the shared OneDrive account for each victim, which is then used to store backdoor commands and additional payloads (uploaded by the operators), command results, and exfiltrated data (uploaded by the malware). This way, the same OneDrive account can be shared by multiple victims.</p>

On the other end of the C&amp;C protocol, OilBooster uploads command results and exfiltrated data as files with the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">.xls </span>and <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">.xlsx</span> extensions, respectively. Note that these are not genuine Microsoft Office files, but rather JSON files with XOR-encrypted and base64-encoded values.</p>

<h4>Network communication</h4>
<p>For C&amp;C communication and exfiltration, OilBooster uses the Microsoft Graph API to access the shared OneDrive account, using a variety of HTTP GET, POST, PUT, and DELETE requests to the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">graph.microsoft.com</span> host over the standard 443 port. For brevity, we will also refer to these requests as OneDrive API requests. The encrypted communication is facilitated by the statically linked OpenSSL library, which handles the SSL communication.</p>
<p>To authenticate with the OneDrive account, OilBooster first obtains the <a href="https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/azure/active-directory/develop/v2-oauth2-auth-code-flow">OAuth2 access token</a> from the Microsoft identity platform (the authorization server) by sending a POST request with the following body over port 443 to <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">login.microsoftonline.com/common/oauth2/v2.0/token</span>, using hardcoded credentials:</p>
<pre class="language-markup"><code>client_id=860b23a7-d484-481d-9fea-d3e6e129e249
&amp;redirect_uri=https://login.live.com/oauth20_desktop.srf
&amp;client_secret=&lt;redacted&gt;
&amp;refresh_token=&lt;redacted&gt;
&amp;grant_type=refresh_token</code></pre>
<p>OilBooster obtains a new access token this way, which will be used in the Authorization header of the subsequent OneDrive API requests, along with a new refresh token. OilBooster also has a backup channel to request a new refresh token from its C&amp;C server after 10 consecutive unsuccessful connections to the OneDrive server. As shown in Figure 7, the new token can be acquired by sending a simple HTTP GET request on port 80 to <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">host1[.]com/rt.ovf</span> (a legitimate, likely compromised website), which should be followed by the new refresh token in cleartext in the HTTP response.</p>

<h3>Downloader loop</h3>
<p>In the downloader loop, OilBooster repeatedly connects to the shared OneDrive account to <a href="https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/graph/api/driveitem-list-children" target="_blank" rel="noopener">obtain a list of files</a> with the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">.docx</span> and <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">.doc</span> extensions in the victim-specific subdirectory named <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">&lt;victimID&gt;/items/</span> by sending an HTTP GET request over port 443 to this URL:</p>
<p><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">graph.microsoft.com/v1.0/me/drive/root:/&lt;victimID&gt;/items:/children?$filter=endsWith(name,'.doc')%20or%20endsWith(name,'.docx')&amp;$select=id,name,file</span></p>
<p>If the connection is not successful (the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">HTTP_STATUS_DENIED</span> response status) after 10 attempts, OilBooster connects to its fallback C&amp;C server, <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">host1[.]com/rt.ovf</span>, to acquire a new refresh token, as discussed earlier.</p>
<p>Alternatively, if the specified directory does not yet exist (<span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">HTTP_STATUS_NOT_FOUND</span>), OilBooster first registers the victim on the shared OneDrive account by sending an HTTP POST request over port 443 to this URL: <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">graph.microsoft.com/v1.0/me/drive/items/root:/&lt;victimID&gt;:/children</span> with the JSON string <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">{"name": "items","folder":{}}</span> as the request body, as shown in <!-- [if supportFields]> REF _Ref144118341 \h <![endif]-->Figure 9. This request creates the whole directory structure<span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;"> &lt;victimID&gt;/items </span>at the same time, which will later be used by the attackers to store commands and additional payloads disguised as <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">.doc</span> and <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">.docx</span> files.</p>
<figure class="image"><img title="Figure 9. OilBooster first downloads each file from the OneDrive account and deletes it from OneDrive after processing the file.</p>
<p>Finally, after going through all the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">.doc </span>and <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">.docx </span>files downloaded from the OneDrive subdirectory, OilBooster records the last connection timestamp (the current GMT time) by creating a new file named setting.ini in the victim&rsquo;s OneDrive subdirectory, via an HTTP PUT request on port 443 made to this URL: <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">graph.microsoft.com/v1.0/me/drive/root:/&lt;victimID&gt;/setting.ini:/content</span>.</p>
<h5>Processing .doc files</h5>
<p>Files with the<span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;"> .doc</span> extension downloaded from the shared OneDrive account are in fact JSON files with encrypted commands to be executed on the compromised host. Once a <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">&lt;filename&gt;.doc</span> is downloaded, OilBooster parses the values named <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">s</span> (part of the decryption key) and <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">c</span> (encrypted command) from the file content. It first base64 decodes, then XOR decrypts the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">c</span> value, using a key that is created by appending the last two characters of the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">s</span> value to the last two characters of <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">&lt;filename&gt;</span>.</p>
<p>After decryption, OilBooster executes the command line in a new thread using the CreateProcessW API, and reads the command result via an unnamed pipe connected to the process. OilBooster then uploads the command result to the shared OneDrive account as a new file named <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">&lt;filename&gt;.xls</span> by sending an HTTP PUT request over port 443 to <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">graph.microsoft.com/v1.0/me/drive/root:/&lt;victimID&gt;/items/&lt;filename&gt;.xls:/content</span>.</p>
<h5><a name="_Ref146047510"></a>Processing .docx files</h5>
<p>Files with the<span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;"> .docx</span> extension downloaded from the shared OneDrive account are in fact compressed and encrypted files named <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">&lt;filename&gt;.&lt;original extension&gt;.docx </span>that will be dropped and unpacked on the compromised system. OilBooster first downloads the encrypted file to the local directory named <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">&lt;currentdir&gt;\items</span>, using the original full filename.</p>
<p>In the next step, it reads and decrypts the file content using an XOR cipher with <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">.&lt;original extension</span>&gt; as the decryption key, and drops it in the same directory into a file named <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">&lt;filename&gt;.&lt;original extension&gt;.doc</span>, while the first one is deleted. Finally, OilBooster reads and gzip decompresses the decrypted file, drops the result in the same directory as a file named <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">&lt;filename&gt;.&lt;original extension&gt;</span>, and deletes the other one.</p>
<p>Note the unnecessary creation of several files in the process &ndash; this is typical for OilRig. We previously described the group&rsquo;s noisy operations on compromised hosts in its <a href="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/en/papers/threat-reports/eset_threat_report_t32021.pdf" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Out to Sea campaign</a>.</p>
<h4>Exfiltration loop</h4>
<p>In the exfiltration thread, OilBooster loops over the contents of the local directory named <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">&lt;currentdir&gt;\tempFiles</span>, and uploads the file contents to the victim&rsquo;s folder on the shared OneDrive account. Each file is processed in this way:</p>
<ul>
<li>OilBooster gzip compresses the original file <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">&lt;filename&gt;.&lt;original extension&gt;</span> and writes the result to a file named <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">&lt;filename&gt;.&lt;original extension&gt;.xlsx </span>in the same directory.</li>
<li>It then encrypts the compressed file using an XOR cipher and<span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;"> .&lt;original extension&gt;</span> as the key. If there is no file extension, <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">4cx</span> is used as the default key.</li>
</ul>
<p>Finally, the encrypted file is uploaded to the OneDrive account, and the local file is deleted.</p>
<h4>ODAgent downloader: OilBooster&rsquo;s precursor</h4>
<p>ODAgent is a C#/.NET application that uses the Microsoft Graph API to access an attacker-controlled OneDrive account for C&amp;C communication and exfiltration &ndash; in short, ODAgent is loosely a C#/.NET precursor of OilBooster. Similar to OilBooster, ODAgent repeatedly connects to the shared OneDrive account and lists the contents of the victim-specific folder to obtain additional payloads and backdoor commands.</p>
<p>As shown in Figure 10, ODAgent then parses the metadata for each remote file. Subsequently, it uses the value of the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">mimeType</span> key associated with the file to distinguish between backdoor commands (formatted as JSON files) and encrypted payloads &ndash; this is unlike OilBooster, which uses file extensions for that distinction. After processing a file locally, ODAgent deletes the original from the remote OneDrive directory via the OneDrive API.</p>

It first derives the session key by XORing the provided secret with the hardcoded value <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">15a49w@]</span>. If the original file has a <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">.c</span> extension, its content is also gzip decompressed (and the extension is then dropped from the filename).</p>
<p>At the end of each connection, ODAgent uploads the contents of the local directory<span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;"> &lt;currentdir&gt;\i</span> to the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">&lt;victimID&gt;/i</span> directory on the shared OneDrive account, preserving the original filenames with the added <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">.c</span> extension.</p>

<h2><a name="_Toc152577460"></a>Conclusion</h2>
<p>Throughout 2022, OilRig developed a series of new downloaders, all using a variety of legitimate cloud storage and cloud-based email services as their C&amp;C and exfiltration channels. These downloaders were deployed exclusively against targets in Israel &ndash; often against the same targets within a few months. As all of these targets were previously affected by other OilRig tools, we conclude that OilRig uses this class of lightweight but effective downloaders as its tool of choice to maintain access to networks of interest.</p>
<p>These downloaders share similarities with MrPerfectionManager and PowerExchange backdoors, other recent additions to OilRig&rsquo;s toolset that use email-based C&amp;C protocols &ndash; except that SC5k, OilBooster, ODAgent, and OilCheck use attacker-controlled cloud service accounts, rather than the victim&rsquo;s internal infrastructure. All these activities confirm an ongoing switch to legitimate cloud service providers for C&amp;C communication, as a way to hide the malicious communication and mask the group&rsquo;s network infrastructure.</p>
<p>On par with the rest of OilRig&rsquo;s toolset, these downloaders are not particularly sophisticated, and are, again, unnecessarily noisy on the system. However, the continuous development and testing of new variants, the experimenting with various cloud services and different programming languages, and the dedication to re-compromise the same targets over and over again, makes OilRig a group to watch out for.</p>
