<p><b>October 26, 2023</b></p>
<p id="main"><newslead> In early September, Doctor Web <a href="https://news.drweb.com/show/?lng=en&i=14743" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">published</a> a study of <a href="https://vms.drweb.com/search/?q=Android.Pandora.2&lng=en"><b>Android.Pandora.2</b></a>, a backdoor that creates a botnet of infected devices and can carry out DDoS attacks at the command of threat actors. In the middle of the month, our specialists <a href=" https://news.drweb.com/show/?lng=en&i=14748" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">informed</a> users about malicious programs from the <a href="https://vms.drweb.com/search/?q=Android.Spy.Lydia&lng=en"><b>Android.Spy.Lydia</b></a> family. These multi-functional spyware trojans target Iranian users. Members of this family are camouflaged as a financial platform for online trading; they can perform various malicious actions at the command of attackers. This includes intercepting and sending SMS, collecting information about user phonebook contacts, hijacking clipboard contents, loading phishing websites, and so on. The <a href="https://vms.drweb.com/search/?q=Android.Spy.Lydia&lng=en"><b>Android.Spy.Lydia</b></a> trojans can be used in a variety of fraudulent schemes and to steal personal data. Moreover, with their help, threat actors can steal their victims’ money.</newslead></p>
<p>According to detection statistics collected by Dr.Web for Android, in September 2023, Android malware was less active, compared to the previous month. For instance, <a href="https://vms.drweb.com/search/?q=Android.HiddenAds&lng=en"><b>Android.HiddenAds</b></a> and <a href="https://vms.drweb.com/search/?q=Android.MobiDash&lng=en"><b>Android.MobiDash</b></a> adware trojans were detected 11.73% and 26.30% less often, respectively. The number of spyware trojan attacks decreased by 25.11%, <a href="https://vms.drweb.com/search/?q=Android.Locker&lng=en"><b>Android.Locker</b></a> attacks by 10.52%, and banking malware by 4.51%. At the same time, Android device owners encountered unwanted adware programs 14.32% more often.</p>
<p>Many new threats were uncovered on Google Play over the course of September. Among them were <a href="https://vms.drweb.com/search/?q=Android.FakeApp&lng=en"><b>Android.FakeApp</b></a> trojan apps used in different fraudulent schemes, <a href="https://vms.drweb.com/search/?q=Android.Joker&lng=en"><b>Android.Joker</b></a> trojans, which subscribe victims to paid services, and also <a href="https://vms.drweb.com/search/?q=Android.HiddenAds&lng=en"><b>Android.HiddenAds</b></a> adware trojans.</p>

<h2 id="monthdanger">Mobile threat of the month</h2>
<p>In September, Doctor Web <a href="https://news.drweb.com/show/?i=14743&lng=en" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">presented</a> the details of its <a href="https://vms.drweb.com/search/?q=Android.Pandora.2&lng=en"><b>Android.Pandora.2</b></a> malware analysis; this trojan primarily targets Spanish-speaking users. The first cases of attacks involving it were <a href="https://news.drweb.com/show/review/?i=14695&lng=en#month" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">recorded</a> in March 2023.</p>

<p>This malicious program infects Smart TVs and television boxes with the Android TV operating system via compromised firmware or when users install trojanized versions of software for illegally watching videos online. </p>

<p>The main function of <a href="https://vms.drweb.com/search/?q=Android.Pandora.2&lng=en"><b>Android.Pandora.2</b></a> is to perform various types of DDoS attacks at the command of cybercriminals. In addition, this trojan can perform a number of other actions, like installing its own updates and replacing the system hosts file.</p>

<p>A study performed by Doctor Web’s malware analysts revealed that when creating this trojan, virus writers borrowed from the authors of <a href="https://vms.drweb.com/search/?q=Linux.Mirai&lng=en"><b>Linux.Mirai</b></a>, taking part of its code and using it as the basis for their trojan. Since 2016, <a href="https://vms.drweb.com/search/?q=Linux.Mirai&lng=en"><b>Linux.Mirai</b></a> has been widely used to infect IoT (the “Internet of things”) devices and to perform DDoS attacks on various websites.</p>

<h2 id="googleplay">Threats on Google Play</h2>
<p>In September, Doctor Web’s malware analysts uncovered many new malicious apps on Google Play. Among them were trojans that displayed intrusive ads. Threat actors distributed them under the guise of such games as Agent Shooter (<a href="https://vms.drweb.com/search/?q=Android.HiddenAds&lng=en"><b>Android.HiddenAds</b></a><b>.3781</b>), Rainbow Stretch (<a href="https://vms.drweb.com/search/?q=Android.HiddenAds&lng=en"><b>Android.HiddenAds</b></a><b>.3785</b>), Rubber Punch 3D (<a href="https://vms.drweb.com/search/?q=Android.HiddenAds&lng=en"><b>Android.HiddenAds</b></a><b>.3786</b>), and Super Skibydi Killer (<a href="https://vms.drweb.com/search/?q=Android.HiddenAds&lng=en"><b>Android.HiddenAds</b></a><b>.3787</b>). Once these trojans were installed on Android devices, they tried to hide from users. For this, they replaced their icons, located on the home screen menu, with transparent versions and also changed their names so they were left blank. In addition, they could pretend to be a Google Chrome browser by replacing their own icons with the corresponding copy. When users tap on such an icon, these trojans launch the browser and continue to operate in the background. This allows them to become less noticeable and reduces the likelihood of their premature removal. Moreover, if these malicious programs stop working, users will restart them, thinking that they are launching a browser.</p>
