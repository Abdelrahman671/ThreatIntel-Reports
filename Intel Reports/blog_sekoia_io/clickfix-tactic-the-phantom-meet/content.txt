<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-context">Context</h2>



<p>In May 2024, a new social engineering tactic called ClickFix emerged, featuring a ClearFake cluster that the Sekoia Threat Detection &amp; Research (TDR) team closely monitored and analysed in a private report entitled <em>FLINT 2024-027 &#8211; New widespread ClearFake variant abuses PowerShell and clipboard</em>. This tactic involves displaying fake error messages in web browsers to deceive users into copying and executing a given malicious PowerShell code, finally infecting their systems.</p>



<p>Proofpoint researchers, who named this tactic ClickFix, reported<sup data-fn="0be63006-3353-4f2d-a64d-cf84af0757e6" class="fn"><a id="0be63006-3353-4f2d-a64d-cf84af0757e6-link" href="#0be63006-3353-4f2d-a64d-cf84af0757e6">1</a></sup> that the initial access broker TA571 leveraged it in email phishing campaigns since March 2024. These campaigns primarily used HTML files disguised as Word documents, displaying a fake error window that prompts users to install malware such as Matanbuchus, DarkGate, or NetSupport RAT via a PowerShell script.</p>



<p>In recent months, multiple malware distribution campaigns have leveraged the ClickFix lure to spread Windows and macOS infostealers, botnets, and remote access tools. This is in line with the growing, ongoing trend of distributing malware through the drive-by download technique. Sekoia analysts assess that several intrusion sets recently adopted this tactic, presumably to evade antivirus software scanning and browser security features, aiming to improve attackers’ infection rates.&nbsp;</p>



<p>In this blog post, we provide a <strong>chronological overview of the observed ClickFix campaigns</strong>. We further share technical details about a <strong>ClickFix cluster that uses fake Google Meet video conference pages to distribute infostealers</strong>, targeting both Windows and macOS systems. Sekoia analysts successfully associated this cluster impersonating Google Meet with two <strong>cybercrime groups: &#8220;<em>Slavic Nation Empire (SNE)&#8221;</em> and &#8220;<em>Scamquerteo</em>&#8220;</strong>. These groups are sub-teams of the cryptocurrency scam teams &#8220;<em>Marko Polo</em>&#8221; and &#8220;<em>CryptoLove</em>&#8220;, respectively.</p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-victimology-of-clickfix-clusters">Victimology of ClickFix clusters</h3>



<p>While many of these campaigns reportedly aim to broadly target multiple sectors &#8211; using websites compromised by ClearFake or through extensive phishing efforts &#8211; some are designed to target specific verticals.</p>



<p>For instance, Proofpoint identified<sup data-fn="d26f72ab-8a63-443f-bb05-9595e527e828" class="fn"><a id="d26f72ab-8a63-443f-bb05-9595e527e828-link" href="#d26f72ab-8a63-443f-bb05-9595e527e828">2</a></sup> a ClickFix cluster targeting transport and logistics companies in North America from at least May to August 2024. This campaign uses websites that impersonate transport and fleet operations management software.</p>



<p>Additionally, the GitHub issues campaign mainly targeted developers to spread Lumma Stealer by falsely reporting security vulnerabilities, thereby impacting thousands of public code repositories and exploiting developers’ trust in GitHub notifications. The goal of this large-scale operation was likely to opportunistically gather a significant amount of sensitive developer data, which can be used for more targeted attacks in the future.&nbsp;</p>



<p>Recent campaigns uncovered by Sekoia analysts appear to continuously target both businesses and individuals, using opportunistic lures such as fake Google Meet pages and Facebook groups.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-investigation-of-clickfix-clusters">Investigation of ClickFix clusters</h2>



<p>The following section provides a detailed analysis of one of the clusters discovered by Sekoia analysts.</p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-windows-users-targeted-with-stealc-and-rhadamanthys">Windows users targeted with Stealc and Rhadamanthys</h3>

Using a Python script<sup data-fn="179bc3f8-0cfa-46cf-880e-1bf1f7920573" class="fn"><a id="179bc3f8-0cfa-46cf-880e-1bf1f7920573-link" href="#179bc3f8-0cfa-46cf-880e-1bf1f7920573">3</a></sup>, we deobfuscated it and obtained the following VBScript.</p>

<p>Upon execution, the VBS script performs the following actions:</p>



<ol class="wp-block-list">
<li>It terminates its parent process (<em>mshta.exe</em>).</li>



<li>It downloads two executables (<em>stealc.exe</em> and <em>ram.exe</em>) using <em>bitsadmin</em>. After a two-seconds delay, it notifies the C2 server (<em>webapizmland[.]com</em>) about the success or failure of running the executables.</li>



<li>It retrieves the victim’s public IP address using the service <em>api.ipify[.]org</em> and sends it to the C2 server along the execution status.</li>
</ol>



<p>The two executables <em>stealc.exe</em> (SHA256: <em>a834be6d2bec10f39019606451b507742b7e87ac8d19dc0643ae58df183f773c</em>) and <em>ram.exe</em> (SHA256: <em>2853a61188b4446be57543858adcc704e8534326d4d84ac44a60743b1a44cbfe</em>)<em> </em>are the Stealc and Rhadamanthys payloads respectively, both protected by the HijackLoader crypter.</p>



<p>In this campaign, the Stealc C2 server is <em>“hxxp://95.182.97[.]58/84b7b6f977dd1c65.php</em>” and the Rhadamanthys C2 server is “<em>hxxp://91.103.140[.]200:9078/3936a074a2f65761a5eb8/6fmfpmi7.fwf4p</em>”. Both IP addresses were already known by our CTI database following the Sekoia.io C2 Trackers monitoring routine, as we proactively track the C2 infrastructure of these two infostealer families sold as Malware-as-a-Service.</p>



<p>Notably, the name of the Stealc botnet &#8220;<em>sneprivate24</em>&#8221; suggests that the traffer<sup data-fn="ef19f51d-b89d-4bb2-8b3e-25492f77b611" class="fn"><a id="ef19f51d-b89d-4bb2-8b3e-25492f77b611-link" href="#ef19f51d-b89d-4bb2-8b3e-25492f77b611">4</a></sup> group &#8220;<em>Slavic Nation Empire</em> (SNE)&#8221; was behind this campaign. Further details about this association can be found in the section &#8220;Traffers teams operating this ClickFix cluster&#8221;.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-macos-users-targeted-by-amos-stealer">MacOS users targeted by AMOS Stealer</h3>



<p>For macOS users, clicking on the “Try Fix” button results in downloading the file <em>Launcher_v1.94.dmg</em> (SHA256: <em>94379fa0a97cc2ecd8d5514d0b46c65b0d46ff9bb8d5a4a29cf55a473da550d5</em>), using the following HTTP requests:</p>



<ol class="wp-block-list">
<li>A GET request to <em>hxxps://carolinejuskus[.]com/kusaka.php?call=launcher</em>, where the server responds with a second URL in the HTTP header Location.</li>



<li>A GET request to <em>hxxps://carolinejuskus[.]com/f9dfbcf6a999/7cc2f5dc3c76/load.51f8527e20dcb05ffd8586b853937a8a.php?call=launcher</em>, which returns the malicious payload.</li>
</ol>



<p>We identified the payload <em>Launcher_v1.94.dmg</em> (SHA256: 94379fa0a97cc2ecd8d5514d0b46c65b0d46ff9bb8d5a4a29cf55a473da550d5) as AMOS Stealer, which communicates with its C2 server at “<em>hxxp://85.209.11[.]155/joinsystem</em>”.</p>



<p>Sekoia actively tracks this infrastructure characterised by the <em>/kusaka.php</em> endpoint. Since at least May 2024, this endpoint is used in campaigns redirecting users from malicious websites to download the AMOS Stealer. It is likely used to protect the payload from unwanted traffic, such as downloads by bots or scans by security products.</p>



<p>We identified the following domain names associated with this macOS malware distribution infrastructure:</p>



<figure class="wp-block-table aligncenter"><table class="has-fixed-layout"><tbody><tr><td class="has-text-align-center" data-align="center">alienmanfc6[.]com<br>apunanwu[.]com<br>bowerchalke[.]com<br>carolinejuskus[.]com<br>cautrucanhtuan[.]com<br>cphoops[.]com<br>dekhke[.]com<br>iloanshop[.]com<br>kansaskollection[.]com<br>lirelasuisse[.]com<br>mdalies[.]com<br>mensadvancega[.]com<br>mishapagerealty[.]com<br>modoodeul[.]com<br>pabloarruda[.]com<br>pakoyayinlari[.]com<br>patrickcateman[.]com<br>phperl[.]com<br>stonance[.]com<br>utv4fun[.]com</td></tr></tbody></table></figure>



<p>Given the variety of initial malicious websites redirecting to this infrastructure, we assess with high confidence that it is shared among multiple threat actors. They collaborate within a centralised traffers team to share certain resources, including this infrastructure and the AMOS Stealer, which is also sold as Malware-as-a-Service.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-traffers-teams-operating-this-clickfix-cluster">Traffers teams operating this ClickFix cluster</h3>



<h4 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-slavic-nation-empire-sne-a-sub-group-of-marko-polo">Slavic Nation Empire (SNE): a sub-group of Marko Polo</h4>



<p>The attacker’s server hosts an interesting JavaScript code at <em>hxxp://77.221.157[.]170:3004/server.js</em><sup data-fn="67d60d4c-ed50-46db-ae7e-8e9521f7aa38" class="fn"><a id="67d60d4c-ed50-46db-ae7e-8e9521f7aa38-link" href="#67d60d4c-ed50-46db-ae7e-8e9521f7aa38">5</a></sup>, which is a backend code related to this distribution infrastructure. In brief, this JavaScript connects to a MongoDB database to retrieve <em>worker</em>’s information, and sends statistics to two Telegram bots when users visited the malicious Google Meet websites and successfully downloaded the payload. We would like to thank the cybersecurity researcher Karol Paciorek from the CSIRT KNF team for sharing this discovery with us<sup data-fn="02787884-6b34-40ab-82ca-6d411bfcc17e" class="fn"><a id="02787884-6b34-40ab-82ca-6d411bfcc17e-link" href="#02787884-6b34-40ab-82ca-6d411bfcc17e">6</a></sup>.</p>

<p>Cybercriminals frequently use Telegram bots to monitor their activities, especially when this involves working in a team and collaborating with affiliates (traffers/workers).</p>



<p>Based on our analysis of this cluster’s activities and the messages shared between the threat actors operating and using it, Sekoia analysts advance the following hypothesis:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>The threat actor <strong><em>sparkhash</em> deployed the GMeet cluster for the benefit of the traffers team</strong> <strong>&#8220;<em>Slavic Nation Empire (SNE)</em>&#8220;</strong> in charge of generating traffic to this cluster.</li>
</ul>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>This team of traffers could be <strong>administered by the threat actor </strong><strong><em>Alexmen</em></strong><strong> </strong>who oversees the<strong> distribution clusters activities </strong>and possibly manages infostealers licences, relying on external services.</li>
</ul>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>The <strong>traffers</strong>, also known as affiliates or workers, <strong>spread the malicious URLs to potential victims, redirecting them to this cluster</strong>. For example, the cybercriminal going by the handle <em>web3huntereth</em> may have infected a victim, or himself as part of a test, in Poland, as indicated by the download statistics from the Telegram bot.</li>
</ul>



<p>TDR confidently associate this <strong>cluster impersonating Google Meet with the traffers team “<em>Slavic Nation Empire</em></strong><em><strong> (SNE</strong></em><strong><em>)</em></strong>”, also known as “<em>Slavice Nation Land</em>”. This team provides its members <strong>a comprehensive kit for sophisticated scams</strong> targeting users of cryptocurrency assets, Web3 applications, decentralised finance, and NFT. The kit includes landing pages impersonating software and video conferencing webpages, along with infostealers, drainers, and automation tools to coordinate attacks.</p>



<p>The traffers team &#8220;<em>Slavic Nation Empire (SNE)</em>&#8221; is a sub-group of the <strong>cryptocurrency scam team “<em>Marko Polo</em>”</strong> and part of the Russian-speaking cybercrime ecosystem. <p>Both traffers teams, &#8220;<em>Slavic Nation Empire (SNE)</em>&#8221; and &#8220;<em>Scamquerteo</em>&#8220;, <strong>use the same ClickFix template</strong> that impersonates Google Meet. This discovery suggests that these teams<strong> share materials</strong>, also known as &#8220;landing project&#8221;, as well as <strong>infrastructure</strong>.</p>



<p>Sekoia analysts assess with medium confidence that <strong>both teams use the same cybercrime service to supply them with this fake Google Meet cluster</strong>, that remains unknown at the time of writing. Additionally, it is likely that a third party manages their infrastructure or registers their domain names.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-conclusion">Conclusion</h2>



<p>ClickFix is an <strong>emerging social engineering tactic first observed in 2024</strong>. As of September 2024, <strong>several intrusion sets already adopted it </strong>to widely distribute malware through email phishing campaigns, compromised websites, and distribution infrastructures.</p>



<p>The ClickFix tactic <strong>deceives users into downloading and running malware</strong> on their machines <strong>without involving a web browser for download or requiring manual file execution</strong>. It makes it possible to bypass web browser security features, such as Google Safe Browsing, and to appear less suspicious to unsuspecting corporate and individual users.</p>



<p>The ClickFix cluster analysed in this blog post employs a decoy that could be particularly <strong>devastating in campaigns targeting organisations that use Google Workspace</strong>, especially Google Meet. The investigation into the traffers team distributing this cluster suggests that it primarily targets cryptocurrency assets, Web3 applications, decentralised finance, and NFT users. However, we believe that similar social engineering techniques could be employed in other malware distribution campaigns.</p>
