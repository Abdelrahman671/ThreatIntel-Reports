<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="introduction">Introduction</h2>



<p>Check Point Research unraveled an ongoing surveillance operation by Iranian entities that has been targeting Iranian expats and dissidents for years. While some individual sightings of this attack were previously reported by other researchers and journalists, our investigation allowed us to connect the different campaigns and attribute them to the same attackers.</p>
<p><strong>Among the different attack vectors we found were:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Four variants of Windows infostealers intended to steal the victim&#8217;s personal documents as well as access to their Telegram Desktop and KeePass account information</li>
<li>Android backdoor that extracts two-factor authentication codes from SMS messages, records the phone&#8217;s voice surroundings and more</li>
<li>Telegram phishing pages, distributed using fake Telegram service accounts</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>The above tools and methods appear to be mainly used against Iranian minorities, anti-regime organizations and resistance movements such as:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Association of Families of Camp Ashraf and Liberty Residents (AFALR)</li>
<li>Azerbaijan National Resistance Organization</li>
<li>Balochistan people</li>
</ul>

<h2 id="initial_infection"><a name="_Toc50691499"></a>Initial Infection</h2>
<p>We first encountered a document with the name <code>وحشت_رژیم_از_گسترش_کانونهای_شورشی.docx</code>, which roughly translates to <code>The Regime Fears the Spread of the Revolutionary Cannons.docx</code>. The title of the document was in fact referring to the ongoing struggle between the Iranian regime and the Revolutionary Cannons, a Mujahedin-e Khalq movement.</p>
<p>Mujahedin-e Khalq, or The People&#8217;s Mujahedin of Iran, is an anti-regime organization whose aim is to free Iran from its current leadership. In 1986, Mujahedin-e Khalq (MEK) started building their new headquarters, which later became known as Camp Ashraf, near the Iraqi town of Khalis. However, years of political tension in Iraq eventually led to the transfer of the camp&#8217;s residents to a new, remote, and unlikely destination: Albania.</p>
<p>The above document leverages the external template technique, allowing it to load a document template from a remote server, which in this case was <code>afalr-sharepoint[.]com</code>.</p>
<p><img decoding="async" class="aligncenter wp-image-24168 size-full" src="//research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/image.png" alt="" width="624" height="74" srcset="https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/image.png 624w, https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/image-300x36.png 300w" sizes="(max-width: 624px) 100vw, 624px" /></p>
<p style="text-align: center;">Figure 1: Remote template</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>Curious by this website, we set out to discover more about it. At first, we found a handful of tweets from accounts opposing the Iranian regime mentioning a very similar SharePoint site, which the website in the document was likely impersonating:</p>
<p><img fetchpriority="high" decoding="async" width="590" height="261" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-24171" src="//research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/image3.png" alt="" srcset="https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/image3.png 590w, https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/image3-300x133.png 300w" sizes="(max-width: 590px) 100vw, 590px" /></p>
<p style="text-align: center;">Figure 2: Tweets mentioning similar website</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img decoding="async" class="aligncenter  wp-image-24172" src="//research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/image4.png" alt="" width="657" height="422" srcset="https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/image4.png 879w, https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/image4-300x193.png 300w, https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/image4-768x494.png 768w" sizes="(max-width: 657px) 100vw, 657px" /></p>

<h2 id="infection_chain"><a name="_Toc50691500"></a>Infection Chain</h2>
<p>After the victim opens the document and the remote template is downloaded, the malicious macro code in the template executes a batch script which tries to download and execute the next stage payload from <code>afalr-sharepoint[.]com</code>:</p>
<p><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="aligncenter  wp-image-24173" src="//research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/image5.png" alt="" width="935" height="493" srcset="https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/image5.png 1794w, https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/image5-300x158.png 300w, https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/image5-1024x540.png 1024w, https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/image5-768x405.png 768w, https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/image5-1536x810.png 1536w" sizes="(max-width: 935px) 100vw, 935px" /></p>
<p style="text-align: center;">Figure 4: Infection chain</p>
<p>The payload then checks if Telegram is installed on the infected machine, and if so it proceeds to extract three additional executables from its resources:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>BOBC3953C59DA7870</strong> &#8211; Loader, executed by <code>RunDLL</code>, injects the main payload into <code>explorer.exe</code></li>
<li><strong>CO9D5A739B85C37C1</strong> – Infostealer payload</li>
<li><strong>Updater.exe</strong> &#8211; Modified Telegram updater</li>
</ul>
<p><strong> </strong></p>
<h2 id="payload_analysis"><a name="_Toc50691501"></a>Payload Analysis</h2>
<p>The main features of the malware include:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Information Stealer </strong>
<ul>
<li>Uploads relevant Telegram files from victim&#8217;s computer. These files allow the attackers to make full usage of the victim&#8217;s Telegram account</li>
<li>Steals information from KeePass application</li>
<li>Uploads any file it could find which ends with pre-defined extensions</li>
<li>Logs clipboard data and takes desktop screenshots</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li><strong>Module Downloader</strong>
<ul>
<li>Downloads and installs several additional modules.</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li><strong>Unique Persistence </strong>
<ul>
<li>Implements a persistence mechanism based on Telegram’s internal update procedure</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<p>&nbsp;</p>

<h3 id="cnc_communication"><a name="_Toc50691504"></a>C&amp;C Communication</h3>
<p>The malware uses SOAP for its communication purposes. SOAP is a simple XML-based data structure for web-service communication. The API in SOAP web-services is public and can be observed by accessing the website from a browser:</p>
<p><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" width="674" height="322" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-24176" src="//research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/image8.png" alt="" srcset="https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/image8.png 674w, https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/image8-300x143.png 300w" sizes="(max-width: 674px) 100vw, 674px" /></p>
<p style="text-align: center;">Figure 7: SOAP API in C&amp;C website</p>
<p><strong> </strong></p>
<p>The messages (commands) can be divided into the following categories:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Authentication</strong>:
<ul>
<li><em><code>HelloWorld</code></em> &#8211; Authentication message</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><strong>Module Downloader: </strong>
<ul>
<li><code>DownloadFileSize</code> &#8211; Checks whether a module should be downloaded</li>
<li><em><code>DownloadFile</code></em> &#8211; Downloads a module from the remote server</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><strong>Data Exfiltration: </strong>
<ul>
<li><em><code>UploadFileExist</code></em> &#8211; Checks whether a specific victim file has been uploaded</li>
<li><em><code>UploadFile</code></em> &#8211; Uploads a specific victim file</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<p><strong> </strong></p>
<p><strong>Authentication</strong></p>
<p>The first message for a valid communication tunnel should be <code>HelloWorld</code>, which implements a simple Username/Password authentication. The credentials are hard-coded in the sample, and the SOAP response for that message contains a session ID which must be used for the remainder of the session.</p>
<p><strong> </strong></p>
<p><strong>Module Downloader</strong></p>
<p>The program tries fetching updates for its current modules and also download several additional modules.</p>
<p>Some of the additional missing modules that could not be fetched:</p>
<ul>
<li><code>D07C9D5A79B85C331.dll</code></li>
<li><code>EO333A57C7C97CDF1</code></li>
<li><code>EO3A7C3397CDF57C1</code></li>
</ul>
<p><strong> </strong></p>
<p><strong>Data Exfiltration </strong></p>
<p>The core functionality of the malware is to steal as much information as it can from the target device. The payload targets two main applications<strong>: Telegram Desktop</strong> and <strong>KeePass</strong>, the famous password manager.</p>
<p>Once the relevant Telegram Desktop and KeePass files have been uploaded, the malware enumerates any relevant file it can find on the victim&#8217;s computer which has one of the following extensions: <code>.txt;.csv;.kdbx;.xls;.xlsx;.ppt;.pptx;</code>. For each such file, the malware then uploads it after encoding the file to base64.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h3 id="persistence"><a name="_Toc50691505"></a>Persistence</h3>
<p>The malware uses a unique persistence method which is tied to the <strong>Telegram update procedure</strong>.</p>
<p>Periodically, it copies the Telegram main executable into <code>Telegram Desktop\tupdates</code>, which triggers an update procedure for the Telegram application once it starts. The hidden trick of the malware&#8217;s persistence method is changing the default Telegram updater file &#8211; <code>Telegram Desktop\Updater.exe</code>, with one of its dropped payloads (more specifically &#8211; <code>CO79B3A985C5C7D30</code>). The most notable changed feature of that updater is running the payload again:</p>
<p><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="aligncenter wp-image-24177 size-full" src="//research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/image9.png" alt="" width="923" height="192" srcset="https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/image9.png 923w, https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/image9-300x62.png 300w, https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/image9-768x160.png 768w" sizes="(max-width: 923px) 100vw, 923px" /></p>

<p>The list of targets we observed reflects some of the internal struggles in Iran and the motives behind this attack. <h2 id="conclusion"><a name="_Toc50691515"></a>Conclusion</h2>
<p>Following the tracks of this attack revealed a large-scale operation that has largely managed to remain under the radar for at least six years. According to the evidence we gathered, the threat actors, who appear to be operating from Iran, take advantage of multiple attack vectors to spy on their victims, attacking victims’ personal computers and mobile devices.</p>
<p>Since most of the targets we identified are Iranians, it appears that similarly to other <a href="https://research.checkpoint.com/2018/domestic-kitten-an-iranian-surveillance-operation/">attacks</a> attributed to the Islamic Republic, this might be yet another case in which Iranian threat actors are collecting intelligence on potential opponents to the regiment.<br /><br /></p>

<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h3 id="pc_backdoor_variants_analysis"><a name="_Toc50691517"></a>PC Backdoor Variants Analysis</h3>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h4 id="telb_variant"><a name="_Toc50691518"></a>TelB Variant</h4>
<p>&#8220;TelB&#8221; is the latest variant we encountered, and its analysis shown above. We named it as such because of the next debug string: <code>D:\Aslan\Delphi\TelB\BMainWork\SynCryptoEN.pas</code>.</p>
<h4> </h4>
<h4 id="telbandext_variant">TelAndExt Variant</h4>
<p>This variant is probably the older version of &#8220;TelB&#8221;, and has been active mostly during 2019 and 2020. It shares the following properties and techniques with the newer versions:</p>
<ul>
<li>Developed in Delphi</li>
<li>Shares a great amount of code with the &#8220;TelB&#8221; variant</li>
<li>Focuses on the Telegram Desktop application</li>
<li>Similar persistence and update methods</li>
<li>Uses FTP instead of SOAP for data exfiltration</li>
</ul>
<p>We named this variant &#8220;TelAndExt&#8221; because of the next debug string: <code>D:\Aslan\Delphi\TelAndExt\TelegramUpdater\SynCryptoEN.pas</code></p>
<h4> </h4>
<h4 id="python_variant"><a name="_Toc50691519"></a>Python Info-stealer Variant Analysis</h4>
<p>We discovered several samples which use the following methods:</p>
<ul>
<li>Two-layer SFX (self-extracted archive) which extract several .bat/.vbs/.nfs/.conf files.</li>
<li>Persistence method by copying the executable (ends with <code>.nfs</code>) to<br /><code>%appdata%\Microsoft\Windows\Start Menu\Programs\Startup\audio-driver.exe</code></li>
<li>Executable name is <code>speaker-audio.exe</code> or <code>keyboard-EN.exe</code>, depending on the sample.</li>
<li>The executable was created with Pyinstaller.</li>
<li>Downloads a second-stage payload under the name of <code>net-update.exe</code></li>
<li>Before being uploaded, the data is encrypted using library <code>pyAesCrypt</code> with a hard-coded password.</li>
</ul>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><strong>Info stealing</strong></p>
<p>According to our analysis, the script communicates with an FTP server using hard-coded credentials, and steals the following data:</p>
<ul>
<li>Telegram Desktop application related files.</li>
<li>Paltalk NG application related files.</li>
<li>Chrome, Firefox and Edge related data.</li>
<li>Any file which ends with extensions listed in a given configuration. If no configuration is given, it searches for files with the following extensions: <code>.txt;.docx;.doc;.exe;.jpg;.html;.zip;.pdf</code></li>
</ul>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><strong>Operation</strong></p>
<p>During our investigation, we saw several Python info-stealers that communicate with the same FTP server, but store the stolen information in different pages under different aliases.</p>
<p>We suspect this is how the malware authors operate:</p>
<ul>
<li>Choose a target, and create a designated folder in the FTP server for them.</li>
<li>Build a sample customized for the target, with a unique AES key and FTP credentials for information uploading.</li>
<li>Deliver the weaponized executable via one of the infection chain vectors.</li>
</ul>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><strong>Second-stage Payload &#8211; HookInjEx</strong></p>
<p>One of its core functionalities is fetching a second-stage payload. If the designated FTP folder contains a file named <code>net-update.exe</code>, then it downloads and executes it.</p>
<p>We analyzed few of those <code>net-update.exe</code> samples and found a complete overlap with the &#8220;HookInjEx&#8221; variant below, making it a targeted advanced payload.  </p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h4 id="hookinjex_variant"><a name="_Toc50691520"></a>HookInjEx Variant</h4>
<p>This variant has been in use since 2014, and has 32-bit and 64-bit versions. Over time, the variant evolved and added some features while also changed the names of the different components in its infection chain.</p>
<p><strong> </strong></p>
<p><strong>Infection Chain</strong></p>
<p>We found two main types of infection chains:</p>
<ol>
<li><strong>SFX</strong> (self-extracting archive) which contains all the components. It drops all of them into a folder and then executes the main loader &#8211; <code>DrvUpdt.exe</code> (<code>ehtmlh.exe</code> in older versions).</li>
<li>Fake <strong>SCR </strong>file that is functioning as an executable. In order to look like a legitimate SCR file, the loader contains a <strong>decoy</strong> – a JPEG/PPTX/DOC file as a resource (<code>Resource_1</code>), which is loaded upon running.</li>
</ol>

<p><strong>Persistence</strong></p>
<p>The malware sets the registry key which is in the <code>&lt;Reg&gt;</code> value of the configuration file (which is almost always the key <strong>RunOnce</strong>) to the following values &#8211; it sets the name to <code>SunJavaHtml</code> or <code>DevNicJava</code> and the value is <code>DrvUpdt.exe 11</code>. That way the malware knows it was already executed.</p>
<p>In older versions, the malware used to drop a file named either <code>rreegg.exe</code> or <code>Capdev.exe</code>, which was added to <strong>RunOnce</strong>, and in turn executed <code>DrvUpdt.exe 11</code></p>
<p><strong> </strong></p>
<p><strong>Info stealing</strong></p>
<p>Main feature of the malware is stealing information from the victim&#8217;s computer and send it to the C2 using FTP.</p>
<p>The malware steals different types of data:</p>
<ul>
<li>Opera/Chrome/Firefox login data.</li>
<li>Firefox information: profiles, keys and db files.</li>
<li>The output of <code>tracert www.google.com</code></li>
<li>WHOIS information (based on the <code>&lt;Ws1&gt;</code> value).</li>
<li>Screenshots and title of the foreground window.</li>
<li>Waveform-audio recording for a minute.</li>
<li>Files from removable drivers. The types of files are based on the <code>&lt;cpy&gt;</code> tags in the file <code>Devufl1.tmp</code> (<code>winufl1.tmp</code> in older versions). In some versions, that logic was implemented in a file named <code>uflscan.exe</code>.<br />Interestingly, if driver&#8217;s name is one of the following: <code>A65RT52WE3F</code>, <code>09353536557</code> or <code>transcend20276</code>, the malware ends the thread. We believe it to be a debug code (Fig. X) that stayed and its purpose is to prevent the malware from gathering files from the author&#8217;s computers.</li>
<li>Files from other drives based on the <code>&lt;cpy&gt;</code> tags in the file <code>Devufl2.tmp</code> (<code>winufl2.tmp</code> in older versions ).</li>
<li>Keylogging and clipboard data from various formats – drag and drop/ <code>CF_HDROP</code>, <code>CF_TEXT</code>,  <code>CF_TEXT</code>, <code>CF_UNICODETEXT</code>,  virtual key codes, <code>CF_OEMTEXT</code>, <code>CF_LOCALE</code>, <code>CF_BITMAP</code> and <code>CF_DIB</code>.</li>
<li>Capture using webcam (<code>tcwin.exe</code> in older versions).</li>
<li>Since 2018 &#8211; Telegram Desktop data.</li>
</ul>

<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><strong>C2 communication</strong></p>
<p>This variant uploads files to its C2 domain using the FTP protocol. The FTP domain is placed in the configuration file inside the <code>&lt;S_n&gt;</code> tag.</p>
<p>The connection starts with authentication using the password and username from the configuration file.</p>
<p>The malware then creates a directory according to the <code>&lt;F_f&gt;</code> tag and a subdirectory inside it with the user id it generated before. The user id is generated from the network adapter’s info that was written into the file <code>Mcdata.dat</code> (<code>PAdata.dat</code> In older versions).</p>
<p>After that, the connection continues with <code>TYPE I</code> and <code>PASV</code> commands before storing the files with the <code>STOR</code> command.</p>
<p>The variant also contacts other domains to update its different components. The domains are placed in the configuration file inside <code>&lt;U_U1&gt;</code>, <code>&lt;U_U2&gt;</code> and <code>&lt;U_U3&gt;</code> tags. The files are downloaded using <code>URLDownloadToFileW</code> from the given URLs, the <code>user_id</code> is included in the URLs.</p>
<p><strong> </strong></p>
<p><strong>String Obfuscation</strong></p>

<h3 id="android_backdoor_analysis"><a name="_Toc50691521"></a>Android Backdoor Analysis</h3>
<p>The first activity is <code>MainActivity</code>, which is responsible for presenting the user with the decoy content and requesting permissions to perform privileged activity. It also starts a background service called <code>MainService</code>, and launches the second <code>MainActivityFake</code> (<code>GmailActivity</code>) when the server sends a command to do so.</p>
<p><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="aligncenter  wp-image-24198" src="//research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/image30.png" alt="" width="327" height="581" srcset="https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/image30.png 541w, https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/image30-169x300.png 169w" sizes="(max-width: 327px) 100vw, 327px" /></p>
<p style="text-align: center;">Figure 23: User is requested to allow a set of permissions</p>
<p><strong>Data Collection</strong></p>
<p>Once the <code>GmailActivity</code> launches the <code>MainService</code>, it in turn is responsible for the following tasks: Timer registration, configuration monitoring, showing fake notification (described below) and <strong>sensitive data collection</strong>.</p>
<p>During this initial data collection process, the following information is read and prepared:</p>
<ul>
<li>Installed applications list</li>
<li>Accounts information</li>
<li>SMS messages</li>
<li>Contacts information</li>
</ul>
<p>The rest of the information is collected on demand, once a command is received from a C&amp;C server:</p>
<ul>
<li>Voice recording &#8211; A 30 seconds recording by default.</li>
<li>Google credentials &#8211; The server triggers an authentication phishing attempt.</li>
</ul>
<p><strong> </strong></p>
<p><strong>Google Credentials Theft</strong></p>
<p>Upon receiving the proper command from the C&amp;C server, a Google login page will be displayed to the victim, by activating the <code>MainActivityFake</code> (<code>GmailActivity</code>).</p>
<p><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="aligncenter  wp-image-24200" src="//research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/image32.jpeg" alt="" width="372" height="397" srcset="https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/image32.jpeg 1080w, https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/image32-281x300.jpeg 281w, https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/image32-958x1024.jpeg 958w, https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/image32-768x821.jpeg 768w" sizes="(max-width: 372px) 100vw, 372px" /></p>
<p style="text-align: center;">Figure 24: Google login page</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>At this point the user is presented with a legitimate <code>accounts.google.com</code> login page, inside Android&#8217;s WebView. In order to steal the typed-in credentials, Android&#8217;s JavascriptInterface is used, alongside a timer which periodically retrieves the information from the username and password input fields.</p>
<p><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" width="1408" height="211" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-24201" src="//research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/image33.png" alt="" srcset="https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/image33.png 1408w, https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/image33-300x45.png 300w, https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/image33-1024x153.png 1024w, https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/image33-768x115.png 768w" sizes="(max-width: 1408px) 100vw, 1408px" /></p>
<p style="text-align: center;">Figure 25: Periodic retrieval of Google account credentials</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>&#8220;<strong>Google protect is enabled&#8221;</strong></p>
<p>As we previously mentioned, one of its core functionalities is to turn on the microphone and record the surroundings. In order to achieve this goal in a real-time manner, the application needs to have its service running in the background.</p>
<p>Any Android application that wants to perform such action, is required to post an ongoing notification to the user, which will alert the user of the uninitiated activity on the device. In order to circumvent this issue, the malware developers chose to display the user with a fake notification of &#8220;<strong>Google protect is enabled</strong>&#8220;.</p>
<p><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" width="1153" height="355" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-24202" src="//research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/image34.png" alt="" srcset="https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/image34.png 1153w, https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/image34-300x92.png 300w, https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/image34-1024x315.png 1024w, https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/image34-768x236.png 768w" sizes="(max-width: 1153px) 100vw, 1153px" /></p>

<p>The result is an always-on decoy notification masquerading as &#8220;Google protect&#8221;.</p>
<p><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="aligncenter  wp-image-24203" src="//research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/image35.png" alt="" width="424" height="204" srcset="https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/image35.png 790w, https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/image35-300x144.png 300w, https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/image35-768x369.png 768w" sizes="(max-width: 424px) 100vw, 424px" /></p>

<p><strong>C&amp;C Communication</strong></p>
<p>The malware uses regular HTTP to communicate with the C&amp;C servers. It sends the initial request to <code>alarabiye[.]net</code>, and proceeds to communicate with <code>gradleservice[.]info</code> in order to get configuration, commands and status updates.</p>
<p>In order to upload all the sensitive information, the malware uses FTPS with hard-coded credentials.</p>
<p><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" width="753" height="341" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-24204" src="//research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/image36.png" alt="" srcset="https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/image36.png 753w, https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/image36-300x136.png 300w" sizes="(max-width: 753px) 100vw, 753px" /></p>

<p>In addition, the sensitive files are encrypted using the AES algorithm, with a pre-configured passphrase before being uploaded to the FTP server,</p>
<p><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" width="946" height="468" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-24205" src="//research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/image37.png" alt="" srcset="https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/image37.png 946w, https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/image37-300x148.png 300w, https://research.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/image37-768x380.png 768w" sizes="(max-width: 946px) 100vw, 946px" /></p>

<p><strong> </strong></p>
<p><strong>Two Factor Exfiltration by SMS</strong></p>
<p>One of the unique functionalities in this malicious application is forwarding any SMS starting with the prefix <code>G-</code> (The prefix of Google two-factor authentication codes), to a phone number that it receives from the C&amp;C server.</p>
<p>Furthermore, all incoming SMS messages from Telegram, and other social network apps, are also automatically sent to the attackers phone number.</p>

function loadImg(element){
    var bg = '';
	switch (screenW) {
		case  'mobile':
			bg = element.getAttribute('data-bg-mobile');
			if(bg)
				break; 
		case 'tablet':
			bg = element.getAttribute('data-bg-tablet');
			if(bg)
				break; 
		case 'laptop':
			bg = element.getAttribute('data-bg-laptop');
			if(bg)
				break; 
		default: 
			bg = element.getAttribute('data-bg');
	}
	if(bg)
		element.style.backgroundImage = 'url(' + bg + ')';	
}
