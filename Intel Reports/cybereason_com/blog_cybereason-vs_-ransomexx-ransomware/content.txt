      <h1><span id="hs_cos_wrapper_name" class="hs_cos_wrapper hs_cos_wrapper_meta_field hs_cos_wrapper_type_text" style="" data-hs-cos-general-type="meta_field" data-hs-cos-type="text">Cybereason vs. RansomEXX Ransomware</span></h1>
      <div class="cr-mln__post-author-share">
      <div id="hubspot-author_data" class="hubspot-editable cr-mln__post-meta" data-hubspot-form-id="author_data" data-hubspot-name="Blog Author">
        
          <span class="descriptor">Written By</span>
          <p><span class="author">Cybereason Nocturnus</span></p>
         
      </div>
    </div>
     
    </div>
    
    <!-- Sticky Author and Social Box -->
    
    <!-- END Sticky Author and Social Box -->
      <div class="container-is-blog columns is-multiline page-center cr-mln__blog-post--body">
        
        <div class="column is-7-fullhd is-7-desktop is-10-tablet is-10-mobile is-offset-1-fullhd is-offset-1-desktop is-offset-1-tablet is-offset-1-mobile">
          <span id="hs_cos_wrapper_post_body" class="hs_cos_wrapper hs_cos_wrapper_meta_field hs_cos_wrapper_type_rich_text" style="" data-hs-cos-general-type="meta_field" data-hs-cos-type="rich_text"><p><span style="color: #000000;">Over the last few months, the </span><a href="https://www.cybereason.com/company/nocturnus"><span>Cybereason Nocturnus Team</span></a><span style="color: #000000;"> has been tracking the activity around the </span><a href="https://malpedia.caad.fkie.fraunhofer.de/details/win.ransomexx"><span>RansomEXX</span></a><span style="color: #000000;"> ransomware. It has been active since 2018, but came to fame in 2020 in attacks on major organizations such as the Texas Department of Transportation. RansomEXX started as a Windows variant, but a </span><a href="https://securelist.com/ransomexx-trojan-attacks-linux-systems/99279/"><span>Linux variant</span></a><span style="color: #000000;"> was discovered earlier this year.</span></p>
<!--more-->
<h2 style="font-size: 36px; line-height: 1.75;"><span style="color: #000000;">Key Findings</span></h2>
<p style="line-height: 1.5;"><strong><span style="color: #1f2e33;">Human-operated targeted attacks:</span></strong><span style="color: #1f2e33;"> RansomEXX is being used as a part of multi-staged human-operated attacks targeting various government related entities and tech companies. It is being delivered as a secondary payload after initial compromise of the targeted network.</span></p>
<p style="line-height: 1.5;"><strong><span style="color: #1f2e33;">Disables security products:</span></strong><span style="color: #1f2e33;"> The Windows variant has a functionality that was </span><a href="https://www.cybereason.com/blog/cybereason-vs.-clop-ransomware"><span>seen before</span></a><span style="color: #1f2e33;"> in other ransomware, disabling various security products for a smooth execution on the infected machine.</span></p>
<p style="line-height: 1.5;"><strong><span style="color: #1f2e33;">Multi-Platform: </span></strong><span style="color: #1f2e33;">RansomEXX started solely as a Windows variant, but later a Linux variant was added to the arsenal, sharing similarities with its predecessor.&nbsp;</span></p>
<p style="line-height: 1.5;"><strong><span style="color: #1f2e33;">Fileless ransomware: </span></strong><span style="color: #1f2e33;">RansomEXX is usually delivered as a secondary in-memory payload without ever touching the disk, which makes it harder to detect.&nbsp;</span></p>
<p style="line-height: 1.75;"><strong><span style="color: #1f2e33;">Detected and prevented:</span></strong><span style="color: #1f2e33;"> The </span><a href="https://www.cybereason.com/platform#graphic"><span>Cybereason Defense Platform</span></a><span style="color: #1f2e33;"> fully detects and prevents the RansomEXX ransomware.</span></p>
<div class="hs-embed-wrapper" data-service="fast.wistia" data-script-embed="true" data-responsive="true" style="position: relative; overflow: hidden; width: 100%; height: auto; padding: 0px; max-width: 658px; min-width: 256px; display: block; margin: auto; max-height: 370px;"><div class="hs-embed-content-wrapper"><script src="https://fast.wistia.com/embed/medias/87en0ktmm8.jsonp" async>&nbsp;</script><script src="https://fast.wistia.com/assets/external/E-v1.js" async></script><div class="wistia_responsive_padding" style="padding:56.25% 0 0 0;position:relative;"><div class="wistia_responsive_wrapper" style="height:100%;left:0;position:absolute;top:0;width:100%;"><span class="wistia_embed wistia_async_87en0ktmm8 popover=true popoverAnimateThumbnail=true videoFoam=true" style="display:inline-block;height:100%;position:relative;width:100%">&nbsp;</span></div></div></div></div>
<h2 style="font-size: 36px; line-height: 1;">&nbsp;</h2>
<h2 style="font-size: 36px; line-height: 1.75;"><span style="color: #000000;">Background</span></h2>
<p><span style="color: #000000;">TheRansomEXX family, also known as Defray777 and Ransom X, runs as a solely in-memory payload that is not&nbsp; dropped to disk, making it highly evasive. RansomEXX was involved in three major attacks in 2020 against </span><a href="https://www.bleepingcomputer.com/news/security/new-ransom-x-ransomware-used-in-texas-txdot-cyberattack/"><span>Texas TxDOT</span></a><span style="color: #000000;"> in May of 2020, against </span><a href="https://www.bleepingcomputer.com/news/security/business-technology-giant-konica-minolta-hit-by-new-ransomware/"><span>Konica Minolta</span></a><span style="color: #000000;"> in the end of July, and against </span><a href="https://www.bleepingcomputer.com/news/security/brazils-court-system-under-massive-ransomexx-ransomware-attack/"><span>Brazil's court system</span></a><span style="color: #000000;"> in the beginning of November.&nbsp;</span></p>
<p><span style="color: #000000;">In addition, last December RansomEXX operators published </span><a href="https://www.zdnet.com/article/hackers-leak-data-from-embraer-worlds-third-largest-airplane-maker/"><span>stolen credentials from Embraer</span></a><span style="color: #000000;">, one of the largest aircraft makers in the world, on its own leaks website as part of the ongoing </span><a href="https://www.infosecurity-magazine.com/blogs/double-extortion-ransomware/"><span>double extortion trend</span></a><span style="color: #000000;">.</span></p>
<p><span style="color: #000000;">In mid 2020, a Linux variant of RansomEXX emerged. This variant, despite sharing </span><a href="https://securelist.com/ransomexx-trojan-attacks-linux-systems/99279/"><span>similarities</span></a><span style="color: #000000;"> with the Windows variant, is </span><a href="https://kc.mcafee.com/corporate/index?page=content&amp;id=KB93665"><span>simpler</span></a><span style="color: #000000;"> than its predecessor and lacks many features such as disabling security software and command and control communication. There are decryptors for both variants, and the threat actors send paying victims a private key to decode their files.</span></p>
<h2 style="font-size: 36px; line-height: 1.75;"><span style="color: #000000;">RansomEXX Analysis</span></h2>
<p><span style="color: #000000;">This analysis focuses on the Windows variant of RansomEXX, which can be classified&nbsp; as fileless malware because it is </span><a href="https://www.andreafortuna.org/2017/12/08/what-is-reflective-dll-injection-and-how-can-be-detected/"><span>reflectively loaded</span></a><span style="color: #000000;"> and executed in memory without touching the disk. Analysis of this sample reveals that it is partially obfuscated but includes indicative information such as the “ransome.exx” string that can be seen hard coded in the binary:</span></p>
