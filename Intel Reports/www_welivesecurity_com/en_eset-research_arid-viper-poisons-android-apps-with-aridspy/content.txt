    <h1 class="page-headline">Arid Viper poisons Android apps with AridSpy</h1>    <p class="sub-title">ESET researchers discovered Arid Viper espionage campaigns spreading trojanized apps to Android users in Egypt and Palestine</p>
    
    <div class="article-authors d-flex flex-wrap"><div class="article-author d-flex"><a href="/en/our-experts/lukas-stefanko/" title="Lukas Stefanko"><picture><source srcset="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/tn/-x45/wls/2023/07/lukas-stefanko.jpeg" media="(max-width: 768px)" /><img class="author-image me-3"  src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/tn/-x45/wls/2023/07/lukas-stefanko.jpeg" alt="Lukas Stefanko"  /></picture></a><div class="author-text"><p><a href="/en/our-experts/lukas-stefanko/" title="Lukas Stefanko"><b>Lukas Stefanko</b></a></p></div></div></div>

    <div class="hero-image-container">
        <picture><source srcset="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/tn/-x266/wls/2024/6-2024/arid-viper/arid-viper-aridspy-android-apps-malware.jpeg" media="(max-width: 768px)" /><source srcset="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/tn/-x425/wls/2024/6-2024/arid-viper/arid-viper-aridspy-android-apps-malware.jpeg" media="(max-width: 1120px)" /><img class="hero-image"  src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/tn/-x700/wls/2024/6-2024/arid-viper/arid-viper-aridspy-android-apps-malware.jpeg" alt="Arid Viper poisons Android apps with AridSpy"  /></picture>    </div>

                    <div class="article-body">
    <p>ESET researchers have identified five campaigns targeting Android users with trojanized apps. Most probably carried out by the Arid Viper APT group, these campaigns started in 2022 and three of them are still ongoing at the time of the publication of this blogpost. They deploy multistage Android spyware, which we named AridSpy, that downloads first- and second-stage payloads from its C&amp;C server to assist it avoiding detection. The malware is distributed through dedicated websites impersonating various messaging apps, a job opportunity app, and a Palestinian Civil Registry app. Often these are existing applications that had been trojanized by the addition of AridSpy&rsquo;s malicious code.</p>
<div>
<blockquote><strong>Key points of the blogpost:</strong>
<ul>
<li>ESET Research discovered three-stage Android malware, which we named AridSpy, being distributed via five dedicated websites.</li>
<li>AridSpy&rsquo;s code is in some cases bundled into applications that provide legitimate functionality.</li>
<li>While the first stage of AridSpy has been documented previously, here we also provide a full analysis of its previously unknown later stages.</li>
<li>AridSpy is a remotely controlled trojan that focuses on user data espionage.</li>
<li>We detected six occurrences of AridSpy, in Palestine and Egypt.</li>
<li>We attribute AridSpy with medium confidence to the Arid Viper APT group.</li>
</ul>
</blockquote>
<p>Arid Viper, also known as APT-C-23, Desert Falcons, or Two-tailed Scorpion, is a cyberespionage group that has been active <a href="https://securelist.com/the-desert-falcons-targeted-attacks/68817/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">since at least 2013</a>. Known for targeting countries in the Middle East, the group has drawn attention over the years for its vast arsenal of malware for <a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/2020/09/30/aptc23-group-evolves-its-android-spyware/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Android</a>, <a href="https://about.fb.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/04/Technical-threat-report-Arid-Viper-April-2021.pdf" target="_blank" rel="noopener">iOS</a>, and <a href="https://unit42.paloaltonetworks.com/unit42-targeted-attacks-middle-east-using-kasperagent-micropsia/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Windows</a> platforms. We reported on the group and its then-newest spyware in a previous <a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/2020/09/30/aptc23-group-evolves-its-android-spyware/">blogpost</a>.</p>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>ESET Research identified five Arid Viper campaigns targeting Android users. These campaigns delivered malware via dedicated websites from which victims could download and manually install an Android application. Three apps provided on these websites are legitimate apps trojanized with malicious code that we named AridSpy, whose purpose is espionage. You can see the overview scheme in Figure 1.</p>
<figure class="image"><img title="" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2024/6-2024/arid-viper/figure1.png" alt="Figure 1. Infiltration overview" width="" height="" />
<figcaption><em>Figure 1. Infiltration overview</em></figcaption>
</figure>
<p>AridSpy was first analyzed by <a href="https://www.zimperium.com/blog/new-advanced-android-malware-posing-as-system-update/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Zimperium </a>in 2021; at the time, the malware only consisted of a single stage, with all the malicious code implemented in the trojanized application.</p>
<p>The second occurrence of AridSpy that ESET Research identified was being used in <a href="https://twitter.com/ESETresearch/status/1596222232384401408" target="_blank" rel="noopener">2022</a> (and later <a href="https://mp.weixin.qq.com/s/48Atw1b6Oe7A-vlsKHYWwg" target="_blank" rel="noopener">analyzed by 360 Beacon Labs</a> in December 2022), where the malware operators targeted the FIFA World Cup in Qatar. Impersonating one of the many Kora applications, the campaign deployed the Kora442 app bundled with AridSpy. As in the case of the sample analyzed by Zimperium, the malware still only had one stage at this time.</p>
<p>In <a href="https://www.ctfiot.com/106664.html">March 2023</a>, 360 Beacon Labs analyzed another Android campaign operated by Arid Viper and found a connection between the Kora442 campaign and the Arid Viper group, based on use of the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">myScript.js</span> file mentioned in Figure 1. We found the same connection in the campaigns discussed in this blogpost (as explained in the <em>Attribution </em>section). It has proven to be a useful indicator to identify additional Arid Viper distribution websites.</p>
<p>In August 2023 we logged a detection of AridSpy in our telemetry and investigated further. We identified targets in Palestine and Egypt. New in these campaigns, AridSpy was turned into a multistage trojan, with additional payloads being downloaded from the C&amp;C server by the initial, trojanized app.</p>
<p>At the time of this publication, three out of the five discovered campaigns are still active; the campaigns used dedicated websites to distribute malicious apps impersonating NortirChat, LapizaChat, and ReblyChat, and the تطبيق المشغل (machine translation: Operator application; we will refer to this as the job opportunity app) and السجل المدني الفلسطيني (machine translation: Palestinian Civil Registry) apps. We discovered the following distribution websites via our telemetry, VirusTotal, and pivoting on the shared myScript.js script using the <a href="https://en.fofa.info/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">FOFA network search engine</a> (which is an alternative to Shodan and Censys):</p>
<ul>
<li><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">lapizachat[.]com</span></li>
<li><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">reblychat[.]com</span></li>
<li><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">nortirchats[.]com</span></li>
<li><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">pariberychat[.]com</span> (inactive)</li>
<li><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">renatchat[.]com</span> (inactive)</li>
</ul>
<p>Parallel to our investigation, the FOFA research team published a <a href="https://medium.com/@fofabot/practical-fofa-asset-expansion-apt-c-23-android-malware-7964b6625c6d" target="_blank" rel="noopener">blogpost</a> that discusses discovering seven distribution websites with the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">myScript.js</span> JavaScript file responsible for retrieving the download paths for Arid Viper payloads. Four of these websites distributed various versions of AridSpy. The following two were previously unknown to us:</p>
<ul>
<li><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">clemochat[.]com</span></li>
<li><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">voevanil[.]com</span></li>
</ul>
<p>In this blogpost, we focus on AridSpy payloads that we could obtain from all the confirmed active distribution websites listed above.</p>
<p>Note that these malicious apps have never been offered through Google Play and are downloaded from third-party sites. To install these apps, the potential victim is requested to enable the non-default Android option to install apps from unknown sources.</p>
<h3>Victimology</h3>
<p>Altogether we detected six occurrences of AridSpy in our telemetry, from Palestine and Egypt. The majority of the spyware instances registered in Palestine were for the malicious Palestinian Civil Registry app, with one other detection not being part of any campaign mentioned in this blogpost. We then found the same first-stage payload but with a different package name in Egypt. There was also another first-stage payload detected in Egypt, one that uses the same C&amp;C servers as the samples in the LapizaChat and job opportunity campaigns.</p>
<h3>Attribution</h3>
<p>We attribute AridSpy to Arid Viper with medium confidence, based on these indicators:</p>
<ul>
<li>AridSpy targeted organizations in Palestine and Egypt, which fits a subset of Arid Viper&rsquo;s typical targeting.</li>
<li>Multiple AridSpy distribution websites use a unique, malicious JavaScript file named <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">myScript.js</span>, which has been previously linked to Arid Viper by <a href="https://www.ctfiot.com/106664.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener">360 Beacon Labs</a> and <a href="https://medium.com/@fofabot/practical-fofa-asset-expansion-apt-c-23-android-malware-7964b6625c6d" target="_blank" rel="noopener">FOFA</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">myScript.js</span> was first discovered and linked to Arid Viper in <a href="https://www.ctfiot.com/106664.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener">360 Beacon Labs</a>&rsquo; March 30<sup>th</sup>, 2023 <a href="https://www.ctfiot.com/106664.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener">analysis</a> of a different Android campaign operated by Arid Viper. The (unnamed) malicious Android code used in that campaign was <a href="https://research.checkpoint.com/2020/hamas-android-malware-on-idf-soldiers-this-is-how-it-happened/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">previously attributed</a> to the Arid Viper group. <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">myScript.js</span> was found on one of the distribution websites used in the campaign. The purpose of this JavaScript code was to download a malicious Android app hosted on the distribution server.</p>
<p>Figure 2 shows the part of the code that registers the handler for clicks on the website&rsquo;s Download button, and Figure 3 displays JavaScript code that generates file paths to download the malicious app.</p>

<p>As pointed out by 360 Beacon Labs, this same JavaScript code was also used in the campaign that targeted the FIFA World Cup in Qatar with an earlier version of AridSpy, which <a href="https://twitter.com/ESETresearch/status/1596222496121925633" target="_blank" rel="noopener">we reported</a> in 2022. In both campaigns, the distribution websites used this specific <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">myScript.js</span> script to retrieve a malicious app from a server, although the final payload was different.</p>
<p>Finally, we found a very similar piece of JavaScript on the distribution websites for the campaigns discussed in this blogpost, distributing NortirChat, LapizaChat, and ReblyChat. During our investigation, this linkage was independently confirmed by the research team of the FOFA search engine, who found seven of the same distribution websites that contained the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">myScript.js</span> responsible for downloading Android AridSpy, and <a href="https://medium.com/@fofabot/practical-fofa-asset-expansion-apt-c-23-android-malware-7964b6625c6d" target="_blank" rel="noopener">attributed this malware</a> to Arid Viper.</p>
<p>We have not been able to link the JavaScript code used in these campaigns to any legitimate or open-source project, which leads us to believe that this script is most likely specific to various Arid Viper campaigns distributing Android malware.</p>
<p>It is possible that Arid Viper reused this distribution method, but switched to a new tool, AridSpy, for its new campaigns, since the (unnamed) malware family the group used before was <a href="https://www.ctfiot.com/106664.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener">disclosed and analyzed</a> by various researchers and security companies.</p>
<p>Interestingly, we also discovered a different version of <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">myScript.js</span> on the AridSpy distribution site, masquerading as a Palestinian Civil Registry app. In this case, the script had the same purpose but not the same JavaScript code: instead of downloading AridSpy, this script just returned a hardcoded link to AridSpy.</p>
<p>This version of the script is based on a script <a href="https://netparadis.com/javascript-tutorial-44-ajax/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">available online</a>, contrary to the earlier versions that appear to use a custom-developed <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">myScript.js</span> file. When the earlier versions of <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">myScript.js</span> were disclosed and attributed to Arid Viper, the threat actors most likely changed its code to avoid their new code being connected to the group.</p>
<h2>Technical analysis</h2>
<h3>Initial access</h3>
<p>The distribution mechanism is very similar for all campaigns mentioned in this section. In order to gain initial access to the device, the threat actors try to convince their potential victim to install a fake, but functional, app. Once the target clicks the site&rsquo;s Download button, <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">myScript.js</span>, hosted on the same server, is executed to generate the correct download file path for the malicious AridSpy. This script makes an AJAX request to <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">api.php</span> located on the same server and returns a specific file directory and name.</p>

The malicious app claims to offer general information about the residents of Palestine, such as name, place of residence, date of birth, ID number, and other information. This campaign provides a malicious Android app available for download from <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">palcivilreg[.]com</span>, registered on May 30<sup>th</sup>, 2023; see Figure 6.</p>

<p>All analyzed Android apps from these campaigns contain similar malicious code, and download first- and second-stage payloads; our analysis focuses on the NortirChat and LapizaChat campaigns, where we were able to obtain the final payloads.</p>

<p>The campaigns mostly deploy legitimate apps that have been trojanized. In the analyzed LapizaChat and NortirChat cases, malicious functionality responsible for downloading a payload is implemented in the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">apputils</span> subpackage inserted into the legitimate messaging apps, as can be seen in Figure 11.</p>

In all cases that we observed, when a security app was installed on the device, the server returned the value <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">0</span> and payloads were not downloaded.</p>

<h4>First-stage payload</h4>
<p>During installation of the malicious update, the first-stage payload displays app names such as <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">Play Manager</span> or <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">Service Google</span>. This payload works separately, without the necessity of having the trojanized app installed on the same device. This means that if the victim uninstalls the initial trojanized app, for example LapizaChat, AridSpy will not be in any way affected.</p>
<p>Functionality-wise, the first-stage payload is similar to the trojanized application. It is responsible for downloading the second-stage payload, which is then dynamically loaded and executed. The first-stage payload downloads an AES-encrypted second-stage payload from a hardcoded URL and controls its further execution.</p>
<h4>Second-stage payload</h4>
<p>The second-stage payload is a Dalvik executable (<span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">dex</span>); based on our observations, it always has the name <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">prefLog.dex</span>. The malicious functionality is implemented in this stage; however, it is operated by the first-stage payload, which loads it whenever necessary.</p>
<p>AridSpy uses a Firebase C&amp;C domain for receiving commands, and a different, hardcoded C&amp;C domain, for data exfiltration. We reported the Firebase servers to Google, since it provides the service.</p>
<p>When payloads are downloaded and executed, AridSpy sets listeners to monitor when the device screen is on and off. If the victim locks or unlocks the phone, AridSpy will take a picture using the front camera and send it to the exfiltration C&amp;C server. Pictures are taken only if it is more than 40 minutes since the last picture was taken and the battery level is above 15%. By default, these pictures are taken using the front camera; however, this can be changed by receiving a command from the Firebase C&amp;C server to use the rear camera. Images are archived in the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">data.zip</span> file on internal storage and uploaded to the exfiltration C&amp;C server.</p>
<p>AridSpy has a feature intended to avoid network detection &ndash; specifically C&amp;C communication. It can deactivate itself, as AridSpy states in the code, by changing the exfiltration C&amp;C server used for data upload to a dummy hardcoded <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">androidd[.]com</span> domain (a currently registered typosquat). This action occurs based on a command received from the Firebase C&amp;C server. The dummy domain would probably look more legitimate, is not flagged as malicious, and might not trigger network detection systems.</p>
<p>Data exfiltration is initiated either by receiving a command from the Firebase C&amp;C server or when a specifically defined event is triggered. These events are defined in <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">AndroidManifext.xml</span> and are caused when actions occur, such as: internet connectivity changes, the app is installed or uninstalled, a phone call is made or received, an SMS message is sent or received, a battery charger is connected or disconnected, or the device reboots.</p>
<p>If any of these events occurs, AridSpy starts to gather various victim data and uploads it to the exfiltration C&amp;C server. It can collect:</p>
<ul>
<li>device location,</li>
<li>contact list,</li>
<li>call logs,</li>
<li>text messages,</li>
<li>thumbnails of photos,</li>
<li>thumbnails of recorded videos,</li>
<li>recorded phone calls,</li>
<li>recorded surrounding audio,</li>
<li>malware-taken photos,</li>
<li>file structure of external storage,</li>
<li>six WhatsApp databases (<span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">wa.db-wal</span>, <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">wa.db-shm</span>, <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">wa.db</span>, <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">msgstore.db-wal</span>, <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">msgstore.db-shm</span>, <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">msgstore.db</span>) that contain exchanged messages and user contacts, if the device is rooted,</li>
<li>bookmarks and search history from the default browser and Chrome, Samsung Browser, and Firefox apps if installed,</li>
<li>data in the clipboard,</li>
<li>files from external storage with file size smaller than 30 MB and extensions <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">.pdf, .doc, .docx, .xls, .xlsx, .ppt, .pptx, and .opus</span>,</li>
<li>thumbnails from the Samsung Gallery app stored in the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">/storage/emulated/0/Android/data/com.sec.android.gallery3d/cache/</span> directory,</li>
<li>all received notifications,</li>
<li>Facebook Messenger and WhatsApp communication, and</li>
<li>logs of all text visible by misusing Accessibility services.</li>
</ul>
<p>Besides waiting for events to occur, the Arid Viper operator can extract specific information and upload it immediately to the exfiltration C&amp;C server by sending commands to the compromised device. AridSpy can receive commands from its Firebase C&amp;C server to obtain data or to control the malware. Operators can exfiltrate:</p>
<ul>
<li>device location,</li>
<li>contact list,</li>
<li>text messages,</li>
<li>call logs,</li>
<li>thumbnails of photos,</li>
<li>thumbnails of recorded videos,</li>
<li>a specific image from external storage based on an ID received from the Firebase C&amp;C server,</li>
<li>a specific video from external storage based on an ID received from the Firebase C&amp;C server,</li>
<li>recorded audio,</li>
<li>images taken on demand,</li>
<li>a specific file by file path received from the C&amp;C, and</li>
<li>device info such as whether Facebook Messenger and WhatsApp apps are installed, device storage, battery percentage, internet connection, Wi-Fi connection data, screen on or off status, and the time zone.</li>
</ul>
<p>By receiving control commands, it can:</p>
<ul>
<li>deactivate communication by replacing the exfiltration C&amp;C domain with the dummy value <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">androidd[.]com</span>,</li>
<li>activate communication by replacing the dummy <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">androidd[.]com</span> C&amp;C domain with another domain name,</li>
<li>allow data upload when on a mobile data plan, and</li>
<li>change the exfiltration C&amp;C server for data upload.</li>
</ul>
<p>AridSpy can snoop on user activity by keylogging all text visible and editable in any application. On top of that, it specifically focuses on Facebook Messenger and WhatsApp communications, which are stored and exfiltrated separately. To accomplish this task, it misuses built-in accessibility services to record all text visible and uploads it to the exfiltration C&amp;C server. Examples of stored WhatsApp communications can be seen in Figure 14.</p>

All exfiltrated data is saved using specific filenames that might contain file IDs, filenames, time stamps, location, phone number, and AridSpy version. This string is hardcoded and appended to every file. The files are then uploaded to the exfiltration C&amp;C server and removed from the device.</p>

<p>The Version column of the table suggests that the malware is regularly maintained.</p>
<p>It is worth mentioning that the trojanized malicious apps used for the Palestinian Civil Registry and job opportunity campaigns have implemented malicious functionality that is then also provided in the second-stage payload. It seems very unusual to download a payload if the same functionality is already included. The duplicated malicious functionality doesn&rsquo;t seem to be an intended behavior, as it is not implemented in samples for other campaigns; rather, it might be code left over from a time before the malware was updated to provide two additional stages. Even so, these two trojanized apps can receive commands and spy on victims without needing additional payloads. Naturally, the second-stage payload carries the latest updates and malicious code changes, which can be pushed to other ongoing campaigns.</p>
<h2>Conclusion</h2>
<p>Five campaigns, most likely operated by the Arid Viper APT group, distribute Android spyware, which we've named AridSpy, via dedicated websites, with AridSpy&rsquo;s malicious code implanted into various trojanized apps. This malware family has two additional stages that are downloaded from a C&amp;C server. The purpose of the second-stage payload is espionage via victim data exfiltration. AridSpy also has a hardcoded internal version number that differs in these five campaigns and from other samples disclosed before. This information suggests that AridSpy is maintained and might receive updates or functionality changes.</p>
