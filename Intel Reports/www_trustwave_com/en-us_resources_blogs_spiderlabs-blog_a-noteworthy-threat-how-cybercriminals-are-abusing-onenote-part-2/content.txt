          <h1>A Noteworthy Threat: How Cybercriminals are Abusing OneNote – Part 2</h1>          
          
        </div>
        <div class="blog-details-header-right">
          
            
            <a href="#" class="theme-changer">Change theme to light</a>
            
            
            <img src="https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Web/Spiders/2024/spider_01-mob.png?width=450&amp;height=250&amp;name=spider_01-mob.png" loading="eager" alt="A Noteworthy Threat: How Cybercriminals are Abusing OneNote – Part 2" class="hero-spider">
            
          
        </div>
      </div>
      <div class="toc-content content-wrapper">
        
        <div class="wrapper-main_content">
          
          <div class="blog-details-metadata">
            <span class="date-time">
              <time datetime="2023-03-08 06:00:00">March 08, 2023</time>
            </span>
            <span class="minread">6 Minute Read</span>
            
              <span class="blog-author">by Bernard Bautista</span>
            
          </div> 
          
          
          <div class="wrapper-blog-content">
            <span id="hs_cos_wrapper_post_body" class="hs_cos_wrapper hs_cos_wrapper_meta_field hs_cos_wrapper_type_rich_text" style="" data-hs-cos-general-type="meta_field" data-hs-cos-type="rich_text"><p style="font-weight: 400;">In <a href="/en-us/resources/blogs/spiderlabs-blog/a-noteworthy-threat-how-cybercriminals-are-abusing-onenote-part-1/">part one</a>, we examined how threat actors abuse a OneNote document to install an<span>&nbsp;</span><span>infostealer</span>. Part 2 of this series discusses an<span>&nbsp;</span><span>AsyncRAT</span><span>&nbsp;</span>infection chain while detailing important parts of the code. We’ll also quickly analyze other notable malware strains such as<span>&nbsp;</span><span>Qakbot</span><span>&nbsp;</span>and<span>&nbsp;</span><span>RemcosRAT</span>.</p>
<!--more-->
<h3><strong>AsyncRAT</strong></h3>
<p style="font-weight: 400;">This campaign starts with threat actors emailing potential victims claiming that they have an unpaid invoice. The email has a sense of urgency trying to push victims to open the OneNote attachment.</p>
<p><a class="asset-img-link" href="https://21158977.fs1.hubspotusercontent-na1.net/hubfs/21158977/Imported_Blog_Media/image002-Jul-06-2023-11-49-44-7570-PM.jpg" style="display: inline;"><img loading="lazy" src="https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Web/Blogs/SpiderLab/19668_image002.webp?width=624&amp;height=325&amp;name=19668_image002.webp" alt="19668_image002" width="624" height="325" style="display: block; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto; height: auto; max-width: 100%; width: 624px;" srcset="https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Web/Blogs/SpiderLab/19668_image002.webp?width=312&amp;height=163&amp;name=19668_image002.webp 312w, https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Web/Blogs/SpiderLab/19668_image002.webp?width=624&amp;height=325&amp;name=19668_image002.webp 624w, https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Web/Blogs/SpiderLab/19668_image002.webp?width=936&amp;height=488&amp;name=19668_image002.webp 936w, https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Web/Blogs/SpiderLab/19668_image002.webp?width=1248&amp;height=650&amp;name=19668_image002.webp 1248w, https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Web/Blogs/SpiderLab/19668_image002.webp?width=1560&amp;height=813&amp;name=19668_image002.webp 1560w, https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Web/Blogs/SpiderLab/19668_image002.webp?width=1872&amp;height=975&amp;name=19668_image002.webp 1872w" sizes="(max-width: 624px) 100vw, 624px"></a></p>
<p style="font-weight: 400; text-align: center;"><em><strong>Figure 1</strong>. MailMarshal console showing the suspicious batch file, images and text contained in the OneNote notebook.</em></p>
<p style="font-weight: 400;">The OneNote document contains only one section with a ‘click to view document’ button image placed right above the batch script. When a user clicks the button image, the batch script is implicitly clicked and executed, prompting a security warning that recipients often ignore. As a trick to increase the click rate, threat actors purposely arrange copies of the script across the width of the button image.</p>

<p style="font-weight: 400;">The script copies a PowerShell executable to the current working directory and then renames it as<span>&nbsp;</span><em>skyy.bat.exe</em>. <h3>.NET Loader</h3>
<p style="font-weight: 400;">Let’s now examine the entry point. The loader dynamically resolves Windows API functions using a combination of<span>&nbsp;</span><span><em>GetProcAddress</em></span><span>&nbsp;</span>and<span>&nbsp;</span><span><em>LoadLibrary</em></span><span>&nbsp;</span>calls.</p>
<p style="font-weight: 400;">As a quick note, some Windows API<span>&nbsp;</span><span>names</span><span>&nbsp;</span>and notable strings are encrypted using the AES-CBC algorithm and then base64-encoded. The key and initialization vector (IV)<span>&nbsp;</span><span>is</span><span>&nbsp;</span>hardcoded in the executable, and the AES decryption function in the loader requires three arguments, namely: input, key and IV. The same key-IV pair is also used to decrypt the main payload.</p>

<p style="font-weight: 400;"><span>Moving on,</span><span>&nbsp;</span>the loader first resolves the memory address of<span>&nbsp;</span><span><em>VirtualProtect</em></span><span>&nbsp;</span>followed by the<span>&nbsp;</span><span><em>AmsiScanBuffer</em></span><span>&nbsp;</span>API function from their respective libraries. <p style="font-weight: 400;">Eventually, the main payload is reflectively loaded in memory and executed using the<span>&nbsp;</span><span>Assembly.Load</span><span>&nbsp;</span>function.</p>
<p style="font-weight: 400;">At the end of the loader routine, a command line process is started with arguments consisting of the concatenation of encrypted strings and the calling process referring to<span>&nbsp;</span><em>skyy.bat.exe,<span>&nbsp;</span></em>which is a PowerShell copy. <p style="font-weight: 400;"><span>Qakbot</span><span>&nbsp;</span>continues to infest enterprise networks and users by tapping into new delivery methods, this time abusing OneNote to its advantage.</p>
<p style="font-weight: 400;">Another campaign abusing OneNote delivers<span>&nbsp;</span><span>Remcos</span><span>&nbsp;</span>RAT.<span>&nbsp;</span><span>Remcos</span><span>&nbsp;</span>is a commercial remote access tool with robust features out-of-the-box which appeals to threat actors.</p>
<p style="font-weight: 400;">The email lure disguised as a property information notice from a construction company comes with a OneNote document. An executable embedded in the OneNote hides behind the ‘<em>click to view document’</em><span>&nbsp;</span>button.</p>
<p><a class="asset-img-link" href="https://21158977.fs1.hubspotusercontent-na1.net/hubfs/21158977/Imported_Blog_Media/image024-2.jpg" style="display: inline;"><img loading="lazy" src="https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Web/Blogs/SpiderLab/19679_image024.webp?width=479&amp;height=276&amp;name=19679_image024.webp" alt="19679_image024" width="479" height="276" style="display: block; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto; height: auto; max-width: 100%; width: 479px;" srcset="https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Web/Blogs/SpiderLab/19679_image024.webp?width=240&amp;height=138&amp;name=19679_image024.webp 240w, https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Web/Blogs/SpiderLab/19679_image024.webp?width=479&amp;height=276&amp;name=19679_image024.webp 479w, https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Web/Blogs/SpiderLab/19679_image024.webp?width=719&amp;height=414&amp;name=19679_image024.webp 719w, https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Web/Blogs/SpiderLab/19679_image024.webp?width=958&amp;height=552&amp;name=19679_image024.webp 958w, https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Web/Blogs/SpiderLab/19679_image024.webp?width=1198&amp;height=690&amp;name=19679_image024.webp 1198w, https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Web/Blogs/SpiderLab/19679_image024.webp?width=1437&amp;height=828&amp;name=19679_image024.webp 1437w" sizes="(max-width: 479px) 100vw, 479px"></a></p>
<p style="font-weight: 400; text-align: center;"><em><strong>Figure 12</strong>. This lure sports a simpler design like the AsyncRAT campaign, but this OneNote has an embedded executable.</em></p>
<p style="font-weight: 400;">When we examine the executable, it appears to be a Delphi-based loader known as<span>&nbsp;</span><span>ModiLoader</span>. Upon executing, the loader excludes the path<span>&nbsp;</span><em>C:\Users</em><span>&nbsp;</span>from AV scanning. Doing so prevents the AV scanner from blocking the execution of the payload. Eventually, the loader downloads a binary from a remote URL leading to<span>&nbsp;</span><span>Remcos</span><span>&nbsp;</span>RAT being installed on the victim’s machine.</p>
<p style="font-weight: 400;"><span>Remcos</span><span>&nbsp;</span>RAT configuration.</p>
<ul>
<li style="font-weight: 400;"><span></span>Version:<span>&nbsp;</span><span>Remcos</span><span>&nbsp;</span>3.1.0 Pro</li>
<li style="font-weight: 400;"><span></span>C2 Servers:</li>
<li style="font-weight: 400;"><span></span>184[.]75[.]221[.]171:5129</li>
<li style="font-weight: 400;"><span></span>199[.]249[.]230[.]27:5129</li>
<li style="font-weight: 400;"><span></span>185[.]103[.]96[.]143:5129</li>
<li style="font-weight: 400;"><span></span>185[.]104[.]184[.]43:5129</li>
<li style="font-weight: 400;"><span></span>Mutex: -O1VWSP</li>
</ul>
<h3>Conclusion</h3>
<p style="font-weight: 400;">In this analysis, we observed the shift to OneNote documents as the initial payload for AsyncRAT delivery. We explored the stages of the infection chain, highlighting defense evasion techniques such as memory patching to bypass antivirus, file obfuscation, and reflective code loading.</p>
<p style="font-weight: 400;">Overall, the extent of defense evasion techniques exhibited shows how aggressively the threat actors are attempting to increase the effectiveness of their attacks and make them more difficult to detect and analyze.</p>
<p style="font-weight: 400;">From what we have seen, any files can be easily embedded in OneNote.<span>&nbsp;&nbsp;</span>Together with tricky social engineering techniques, threat actors can successfully take control of a target’s system and steal sensitive data. Furthermore, OneNote documents do not include ‘Protected View’ and Mark-of-the-Web (MOTW) protection increasing the risk of exposure to potentially malicious files and making it attractive to cybercriminals.</p>
<p style="font-weight: 400;">These campaigns show that threat actors are quick to adapt to new and trending methods, riding the wave to achieve their malicious goals. We expect a wave of campaigns leveraging OneNote to continue for a while yet.</p>
<p style="font-weight: 400;"><a href="https://www.trustwave.com/en-us/resources/blogs/spiderlabs-blog/onenote-spear-phishing-campaign/">Please continue to Part 3 of our OneNote research where we discuss a OneNote spearphishing campaign.</a></p>
