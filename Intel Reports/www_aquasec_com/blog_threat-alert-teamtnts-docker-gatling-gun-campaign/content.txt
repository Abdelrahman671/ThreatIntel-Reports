			<div class="main_content_column match_blog_width">
				<div class="content">
					
					<p aria-level="2"><span data-contrast="auto">Long time no see, Aqua Nautilus researchers have identified a new campaign in the making by TeamTNT, a notorious hacking group. In this campaign, TeamTNT appears to be returning to its roots while preparing for a large-scale attack on cloud native environments. The group is currently targeting exposed Docker daemons to deploy Sliver malware, a cyber worm, and cryptominers, using compromised servers and Docker Hub as the infrastructure to spread their malware.</span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;134245418&quot;:true,&quot;134245529&quot;:true,&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559738&quot;:40,&quot;335559739&quot;:0,&quot;335559740&quot;:240}"> </span></p>
<p aria-level="2"><span id="more-23151"></span></p>
<p aria-level="2"><span data-contrast="auto">In this campaign, TeamTNT is leveraging n<span class="ui-provider a b c d e f g h i j k l m n o p q r s t u v w x y z ab ac ae af ag ah ai aj ak" dir="ltr">ative capabilities in cloud environments</span> by appending compromised Docker instances to a Docker Swarm and utilizing Docker Hub to store and distribute their malware. They are also renting the victims&#8217; computational power to third parties, effectively earning money indirectly from cryptomining without the hassle of managing it themselves. In addition, they have adopted new hacking tools by replacing their traditional Tsunami backdoor with the stealthier Sliver malware. In this blog, we will explore the campaign and its components.</span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;134245418&quot;:true,&quot;134245529&quot;:true,&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559738&quot;:40,&quot;335559739&quot;:0,&quot;335559740&quot;:240}"> </span></p>
<h3><span data-contrast="none">The attack flow</span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;134245418&quot;:true,&quot;134245529&quot;:true,&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559738&quot;:40,&quot;335559739&quot;:0,&quot;335559740&quot;:240}"> </span></h3>
<p><span data-contrast="auto">We are currently witnessing the preparation of TeamTNT&#8217;s cloud based infrastructure for a large-scale attack. The group is using both compromised web servers and Docker Hub registries to disseminate malware, aiming to deploy cryptominers or rent out computational power to third parties.</span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559739&quot;:0,&quot;335559740&quot;:240}"> </span></p>
<p><span data-teams="true"><span class="ui-provider a b c d e f g h i j k l m n o p q r s t u v w x y z ab ac ae af ag ah ai aj ak" dir="ltr">There are clear indicators of a TeamTNT campaign, including their characteristics, naming conventions, choice of tools, and familiar infrastructure. They even returned using their retired domain <code>teamtnt.red</code>, which was terminated in 2022.</span></span></p>
<p><span data-contrast="auto">Below you can see the entire attack flow:</span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559739&quot;:0,&quot;335559740&quot;:240}"> </span></p>
<div id="attachment_23154" style="width: 1410px" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><a href="https://www.aquasec.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/10/TeamTNT-Gutling-Gun.jpg" class="modal-inline-image"><img fetchpriority="high" decoding="async" aria-describedby="caption-attachment-23154" class="wp-image-23154 size-full" src="https://www.aquasec.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/10/TeamTNT-Gutling-Gun.jpg" alt="Figure 1: The entire attack flow " width="1400" height="1007" srcset="https://www.aquasec.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/10/TeamTNT-Gutling-Gun.jpg 1400w, https://www.aquasec.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/10/TeamTNT-Gutling-Gun-300x216.jpg 300w, https://www.aquasec.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/10/TeamTNT-Gutling-Gun-1024x737.jpg 1024w, https://www.aquasec.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/10/TeamTNT-Gutling-Gun-768x552.jpg 768w, https://www.aquasec.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/10/TeamTNT-Gutling-Gun-140x101.jpg 140w" sizes="(max-width: 1400px) 100vw, 1400px" /></a><p id="caption-attachment-23154" class="wp-caption-text">Figure 1: The entire attack flow</p></div>
<p><span data-contrast="auto">Throughout the past seven years of observing TeamTNT’s campaigns, we have consistently seen four key elements:</span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559739&quot;:0,&quot;335559740&quot;:240}"> </span></p>
<ol>
<li><strong>External and Local Lateral Movement:</strong> Aggressive detection and infection methods using tools like <strong>Masscan</strong> and <strong>ZGrab</strong> and local searches to propagate the infection across additional servers within the target’s network.</li>
<li><strong>Resource Hijacking:</strong> This campaign focuses on cryptominer deployment and the evolution of selling compromised infrastructure to others, avoiding the overhead of running their own cryptomining operation.</li>
<li><strong>Command and Control (C2):</strong> In this campaign, TeamTNT is using <strong>Sliver malware</strong>, which may replace the previously used <strong>Tsunami malware</strong>. In earlier campaigns, researchers had gained insights into TeamTNT’s operations by infiltrating their IRC servers, but Sliver makes this much harder. Having said that the new infrastructure also include a Tsunami malware capability.</li>
<li><strong>Cloud Tools:</strong> TeamTNT has always experimented with cloud native open-source software (OSS) and offensive security tools (OSTs). In this campaign, they are using Docker Hub to store and spread malware, and Sliver for control and exploitation.</li>
</ol>
<p>Additionally, TeamTNT continues to use their established naming conventions, such as <code>Chimaera</code>,<code>TDGG</code>, and <code>bioset</code> (for C2 operations), which reinforces the idea that this is a classic TeamTNT campaign.<span data-ccp-props="{&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559739&quot;:0,&quot;335559740&quot;:240}"> </span></p>
<div id="attachment_23155" style="width: 1410px" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><a href="https://www.aquasec.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/10/Screenshot-2024-10-22-at-22.16.06.jpg" class="modal-inline-image"><img decoding="async" aria-describedby="caption-attachment-23155" class="wp-image-23155 size-full" src="https://www.aquasec.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/10/Screenshot-2024-10-22-at-22.16.06.jpg" alt="Figure 2: TeamTNT’s signature ASCII art " width="1400" height="689" srcset="https://www.aquasec.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/10/Screenshot-2024-10-22-at-22.16.06.jpg 1400w, https://www.aquasec.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/10/Screenshot-2024-10-22-at-22.16.06-300x148.jpg 300w, https://www.aquasec.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/10/Screenshot-2024-10-22-at-22.16.06-1024x504.jpg 1024w, https://www.aquasec.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/10/Screenshot-2024-10-22-at-22.16.06-768x378.jpg 768w, https://www.aquasec.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/10/Screenshot-2024-10-22-at-22.16.06-140x69.jpg 140w" sizes="(max-width: 1400px) 100vw, 1400px" /></a><p id="caption-attachment-23155" class="wp-caption-text">Figure 2: TeamTNT’s signature ASCII art</p></div>
<h4 aria-level="3"><b><span data-contrast="none">Initial access</span></b><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;134245418&quot;:true,&quot;134245529&quot;:true,&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559738&quot;:40,&quot;335559739&quot;:0,&quot;335559740&quot;:240}"> </span></h4>
<p><span data-contrast="auto">The campaign gains initial access by exploiting exposed Docker daemons on ports 2375, 2376, 4243, and 4244 (there are also indications to exploit K8s clusters in the future). The attack script, which scans for these ports, is known as the </span><b><span data-contrast="auto">Docker Gatling Gun</span></b><span data-contrast="auto">. It targets a wide range of IP addresses (~16.7 million) and deploys a container from TeamTNT’s compromised Docker Hub account, running an </span><b><span data-contrast="auto">Alpine Linux</span></b><span data-contrast="auto"> image with malicious commands. The image executes an initial script called <code>TDGGinit.sh</code> (short for </span><b><span data-contrast="auto">TeamTNT&#8217;s Docker Gatling Gun init shell script</span></b><span data-contrast="auto">).</span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559739&quot;:0,&quot;335559740&quot;:240}"> </span></p>
<div id="attachment_23186" style="width: 778px" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><a href="https://www.aquasec.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/10/carbon-2024-10-22T223522.137.jpg" class="modal-inline-image"><img decoding="async" aria-describedby="caption-attachment-23186" class="wp-image-23186 size-large" src="https://www.aquasec.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/10/carbon-2024-10-22T223522.137-1024x345.jpg" alt="Figure 3: The function TDGG " width="768" height="259" srcset="https://www.aquasec.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/10/carbon-2024-10-22T223522.137-1024x345.jpg 1024w, https://www.aquasec.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/10/carbon-2024-10-22T223522.137-300x101.jpg 300w, https://www.aquasec.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/10/carbon-2024-10-22T223522.137-768x258.jpg 768w, https://www.aquasec.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/10/carbon-2024-10-22T223522.137-140x47.jpg 140w, https://www.aquasec.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/10/carbon-2024-10-22T223522.137.jpg 1400w" sizes="(max-width: 768px) 100vw, 768px" /></a><p id="caption-attachment-23186" class="wp-caption-text">Figure 3: The function TDGG</p></div>
<h4 aria-level="3"><b><span data-contrast="none">Sliver malware</span></b><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;134245418&quot;:true,&quot;134245529&quot;:true,&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559738&quot;:40,&quot;335559739&quot;:0,&quot;335559740&quot;:240}"> </span></h4>
<p><span data-contrast="auto">A new tool used in this campaign is the </span><b><span data-contrast="auto">Sliver malware</span></b><span data-contrast="auto">, which replaces the previously used </span><b><span data-contrast="auto">Tsunami backdoor</span></b><span data-contrast="auto">. Sliver is an open-source, cross-platform,<span class="ui-provider a b c d e f g h i j k l m n o p q r s t u v w x y z ab ac ae af ag ah ai aj ak" dir="ltr">adversary emulation</span> and red-team framework. Its implants support </span><b><span data-contrast="auto">Command and Control (C2)</span></b><span data-contrast="auto"> over multiple protocols, including </span><b><span data-contrast="auto">mTLS</span></b><span data-contrast="auto">, </span><b><span data-contrast="auto">WireGuard</span></b><span data-contrast="auto">, </span><b><span data-contrast="auto">HTTP(S)</span></b><span data-contrast="auto">, and </span><b><span data-contrast="auto">DNS</span></b><span data-contrast="auto">, and are dynamically compiled with per-binary asymmetric encryption keys. It can be used to execute commands and deliver payloads, including in-memory execution.</span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559739&quot;:0,&quot;335559740&quot;:240}"> </span></p>
<div id="attachment_23156" style="width: 1410px" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><a href="https://www.aquasec.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/10/Screenshot-2024-10-23-at-1.08.52.jpg" class="modal-inline-image"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" aria-describedby="caption-attachment-23156" class="wp-image-23156 size-full" src="https://www.aquasec.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/10/Screenshot-2024-10-23-at-1.08.52.jpg" alt="Figure 4: The C2 generate function in the Sliver binary " width="1400" height="144" srcset="https://www.aquasec.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/10/Screenshot-2024-10-23-at-1.08.52.jpg 1400w, https://www.aquasec.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/10/Screenshot-2024-10-23-at-1.08.52-300x31.jpg 300w, https://www.aquasec.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/10/Screenshot-2024-10-23-at-1.08.52-1024x105.jpg 1024w, https://www.aquasec.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/10/Screenshot-2024-10-23-at-1.08.52-768x79.jpg 768w, https://www.aquasec.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/10/Screenshot-2024-10-23-at-1.08.52-140x14.jpg 140w" sizes="(max-width: 1400px) 100vw, 1400px" /></a><p id="caption-attachment-23156" class="wp-caption-text">Figure 4: The C2 generate function in the Sliver binary</p></div>
<p><span data-contrast="auto">TeamTNT also maintains a list of compromised victims on their web servers (as seen in previous campaigns) and has experimented with adding compromised servers to a </span><b><span data-contrast="auto">Docker Swarm</span></b><span data-contrast="auto">. Docker Swarm is a native clustering and orchestration tool that allows Docker nodes to be managed as a single system.</span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559739&quot;:0,&quot;335559740&quot;:240}"> </span></p>
<p><span data-teams="true"><span class="ui-provider a b c d e f g h i j k l m n o p q r s t u v w x y z ab ac ae af ag ah ai aj ak" dir="ltr">We also found references to TeamTNT&#8217;s past campaigns. For example, in the current campaign one container image referenced the domain <b>solscan[.]life</b>, along with the path <code>Chimaera</code>, which was part of a previous TeamTNT campaign in 2021.</span></span></p>
<div id="attachment_23157" style="width: 1410px" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><a href="https://www.aquasec.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/10/Screenshot-2024-10-24-at-12.10.58.jpg" class="modal-inline-image"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" aria-describedby="caption-attachment-23157" class="wp-image-23157 size-full" src="https://www.aquasec.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/10/Screenshot-2024-10-24-at-12.10.58.jpg" alt="Figure 5: Chimaera’s campaign Twit taken from X.com " width="1400" height="468" srcset="https://www.aquasec.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/10/Screenshot-2024-10-24-at-12.10.58.jpg 1400w, https://www.aquasec.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/10/Screenshot-2024-10-24-at-12.10.58-300x100.jpg 300w, https://www.aquasec.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/10/Screenshot-2024-10-24-at-12.10.58-1024x342.jpg 1024w, https://www.aquasec.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/10/Screenshot-2024-10-24-at-12.10.58-768x257.jpg 768w, https://www.aquasec.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/10/Screenshot-2024-10-24-at-12.10.58-140x47.jpg 140w" sizes="(max-width: 1400px) 100vw, 1400px" /></a><p id="caption-attachment-23157" class="wp-caption-text">Figure 5: Chimaera’s campaign Twit taken from X.com <span style="font-size: 16px;"> </span></p></div>
<p><span data-contrast="auto">On the website there’s infrastructure for a third wave of Chimaera, targeting more initial access vectors, than solely exposed docker daemons.</span></p>
<p><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559739&quot;:0,&quot;335559740&quot;:240}"><span class="ui-provider a b c d e f g h i j k l m n o p q r s t u v w x y z ab ac ae af ag ah ai aj ak" dir="ltr">In the Chimeara capmaign (<a title="https://solscan[.life/chimaera/sh" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">https[:]//solscan[.]life/chimaera/sh</a>) it appears that TeamTNT are actively targeting SSH, Jupyter, Docker, Kubernetes misconfigurations, as well as credentials of SSH, AWS, Docker, s3cfg, GitHub, Shodan, gcloud, Ngrok, Pidgin, FileZilla, HexChat, MoneroGuiWallet, CloudFlared, davfs2, PostgreSQL, smbClients.</span></span></p>
<p><span data-teams="true"><span class="ui-provider a b c d e f g h i j k l m n o p q r s t u v w x y z ab ac ae af ag ah ai aj ak" dir="ltr">Furthermore, in this campaign TeamTNT is also using anondns (<strong>AnonDNS</strong> or Anonymous DNS is a concept or service designed to provide anonymity and privacy when resolving DNS queries), in order to point to their web server. They use <code>devnull.anondns.net</code> to point on IP address 45.154.2.77.</span></span></p>
