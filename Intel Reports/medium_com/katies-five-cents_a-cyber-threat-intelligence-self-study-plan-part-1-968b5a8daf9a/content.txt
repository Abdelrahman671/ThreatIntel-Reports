(in part…)</figcaption></figure><h1 id="e269" class="oo op gu bf oq or os ot ou ov ow ox oy oz pa pb pc pd pe pf pg ph pi pj pk pl bk">How to use this plan</h1><p id="1565" class="pw-post-body-paragraph mx my gu mz b na pm nc nd ne pn ng nh ni po nk nl nm pp no np nq pq ns nt nu gn bk">Honestly, however it helps you! :) I wrote this with the idea that someone wanting to learn about CTI could work through it section-by-section, since some concepts build on each other. Be forewarned, if you do this entire thing, it’s going to take a lot of time and effort! I’ve included resources to read and watch, things to do, and questions to think about, as well as a few optional paid resources if you want more. (Please note that some of the links directly download a PDF.) Maybe you work through this in order, but maybe you just need to learn more on specific sections. I highly recommend taking notes as you go as a way to reinforce the material and hold yourself accountable for thinking through and answering the questions—the critical thinking is key to all of this. To be a good CTI analyst, you can’t just consume something, you have to think about what you think of it and how you can apply it. Consider how you want to organize your notes before you start (a notebook, a binder with dividers, Google Docs, OneNote, or whatever else works for you). If you’re new to this industry, I recommend finding someone to help you out as you have questions.</p><p id="c10a" class="pw-post-body-paragraph mx my gu mz b na nb nc nd ne nf ng nh ni nj nk nl nm nn no np nq nr ns nt nu gn bk">I’ll be breaking this plan up as I have time to write each section. I haven’t completely decided on topics for future sections, but here are a couple I plan to include: tools/TIPs, pivoting, open source research, evaluating reports, clustering, indicators/TTPs, threat modeling, assessments/uncertainty, and dissemination/writing.</p><p id="0ddc" class="pw-post-body-paragraph mx my gu mz b na nb nc nd ne nf ng nh ni nj nk nl nm nn no np nq nr ns nt nu gn bk">Part 1 topics include:</p><ul class=""><li id="277b" class="mx my gu mz b na nb nc nd ne nf ng nh ni nj nk nl nm nn no np nq nr ns nt nu pr ps pt bk">Intelligence</li><li id="37d2" class="mx my gu mz b na pu nc nd ne pv ng nh ni pw nk nl nm px no np nq py ns nt nu pr ps pt bk">Cyber Threat Intelligence</li><li id="826f" class="mx my gu mz b na pu nc nd ne pv ng nh ni pw nk nl nm px no np nq py ns nt nu pr ps pt bk">Requirements</li><li id="c925" class="mx my gu mz b na pu nc nd ne pv ng nh ni pw nk nl nm px no np nq py ns nt nu pr ps pt bk">The CTI Community</li><li id="b030" class="mx my gu mz b na pu nc nd ne pv ng nh ni pw nk nl nm px no np nq py ns nt nu pr ps pt bk">Frameworks and Models</li></ul><h1 id="32e8" class="oo op gu bf oq or os ot ou ov ow ox oy oz pa pb pc pd pe pf pg ph pi pj pk pl bk">Intelligence</h1><p id="b673" class="pw-post-body-paragraph mx my gu mz b na pm nc nd ne pn ng nh ni po nk nl nm pp no np nq pq ns nt nu gn bk">CTI represents the convergence of two communities: intelligence and cybersecurity. Good CTI analysts will understand fundamentals about intelligence and bring those principles to their work. Sometimes I’ve seen analysts struggle to bring together “traditional intelligence” and cybersecurity concepts, so I recommend doing this section first, and being relentless about considering how it applies to everything that follows.</p><h2 id="6002" class="pz op gu bf oq qa qb dy ou qc qd ea oy ni qe qf qg nm qh qi qj nq qk ql qm qn bk">Read and watch</h2><ul class=""><li id="9b9f" class="mx my gu mz b na pm nc nd ne pn ng nh ni po nk nl nm pp no np nq pq ns nt nu pr ps pt bk">Read about the 9 principles of <a class="af nv" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20201102212401/https://www.cia.gov/library/kent-center-occasional-papers/pdf/OPNo5.pdf" rel="noopener ugc nofollow" target="_blank">Sherman Kent’s analytic doctrine</a>, starting with “1. Focus on Policymaker Concerns” on page 9 (you can skim the rest). Sherman Kent is a figure you should know about, and these principles of his analytic doctrine are core to what I think an intelligence analyst should be.</li><li id="bfc1" class="mx my gu mz b na pu nc nd ne pv ng nh ni pw nk nl nm px no np nq py ns nt nu pr ps pt bk">Read Richards Heuer’s <a class="af nv" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20201206081245/https://www.cia.gov/library/center-for-the-study-of-intelligence/csi-publications/books-and-monographs/psychology-of-intelligence-analysis/PsychofIntelNew.pdf" rel="noopener ugc nofollow" target="_blank">Psychology of Intelligence Analysis</a>. This is a great intro to several important principles about how we think as intel analysts — including cognitive biases and structured analytic techniques.</li><li id="023a" class="mx my gu mz b na pu nc nd ne pv ng nh ni pw nk nl nm px no np nq py ns nt nu pr ps pt bk">Read about the <a class="af nv" href="https://www.intelligencecareers.gov/icintelligence.html" rel="noopener ugc nofollow" target="_blank">Intelligence Cycle</a>.</li></ul><h2 id="f00b" class="pz op gu bf oq qa qb dy ou qc qd ea oy ni qe qf qg nm qh qi qj nq qk ql qm qn bk">Things to do</h2><ul class=""><li id="f087" class="mx my gu mz b na pm nc nd ne pn ng nh ni po nk nl nm pp no np nq pq ns nt nu pr ps pt bk">Go research Sherman Kent and Richards Heuer — why were they considered important in this field (especially in Western intelligence)? Who are other figures that are important in intelligence, especially from a <em class="qo">non</em>-Western perspective? (go research and find at least one)</li><li id="3984" class="mx my gu mz b na pu nc nd ne pv ng nh ni pw nk nl nm px no np nq py ns nt nu pr ps pt bk">Scroll around <a class="af nv" href="https://yourbias.is/" rel="noopener ugc nofollow" target="_blank">this cognitive biases site</a> and go research cognitive biases to find a few that weren’t in Heuer’s book.</li><li id="dd94" class="mx my gu mz b na pu nc nd ne pv ng nh ni pw nk nl nm px no np nq py ns nt nu pr ps pt bk">Identify your own cognitive biases in everyday life. This is HARD, but you can do it if you’re honest with yourself. Tip: anything that makes you angry is a great place to look for biases. Write down your cognitive biases when you find them.</li></ul><h2 id="d272" class="pz op gu bf oq qa qb dy ou qc qd ea oy ni qe qf qg nm qh qi qj nq qk ql qm qn bk">Questions to think about</h2><ul class=""><li id="58c5" class="mx my gu mz b na pm nc nd ne pn ng nh ni po nk nl nm pp no np nq pq ns nt nu pr ps pt bk">Why do intelligence analysts consider Sherman Kent’s analytic doctrine to be so important? Can you think of situations you’ve seen — whether in intelligence or elsewhere — where people have not followed these principles? What happened?</li><li id="fdb6" class="mx my gu mz b na pu nc nd ne pv ng nh ni pw nk nl nm px no np nq py ns nt nu pr ps pt bk">As you go through the rest of this self-study, when do you see analysts adhering to Kent’s analytic doctrine? When do you see them NOT adhering to it? What is the result?</li><li id="4cf7" class="mx my gu mz b na pu nc nd ne pv ng nh ni pw nk nl nm px no np nq py ns nt nu pr ps pt bk">Why does the intelligence cycle exist? What makes intelligence different from raw data?</li></ul><h2 id="5141" class="pz op gu bf oq qa qb dy ou qc qd ea oy ni qe qf qg nm qh qi qj nq qk ql qm qn bk">Optional paid resource</h2><ul class=""><li id="3448" class="mx my gu mz b na pm nc nd ne pn ng nh ni po nk nl nm pp no np nq pq ns nt nu pr ps pt bk">If you’re looking to learn more about the US Intelligence Community, I recommend Mark Lowenthal’s “Intelligence: From Secrets to Policy”. Go research news around the APT1 report — why was that so important?</li><li id="9c99" class="mx my gu mz b na pu nc nd ne pv ng nh ni pw nk nl nm px no np nq py ns nt nu pr ps pt bk">Research and take a few notes on at least one major CTI event or intrusion you can find prior to 2013 (when the APT1 report came out). (If you need a place to get started check out <a class="af nv" href="https://www.cfr.org/cyber-operations/" rel="noopener ugc nofollow" target="_blank">this site.</a>) What was noteworthy about the event/intrusion you chose? Can you find any similar events/intrusions (for example, involving similar actors or TTPs) that occurred after it?</li></ul><h2 id="340e" class="pz op gu bf oq qa qb dy ou qc qd ea oy ni qe qf qg nm qh qi qj nq qk ql qm qn bk">Questions to think about</h2><ul class=""><li id="b118" class="mx my gu mz b na pm nc nd ne pn ng nh ni po nk nl nm pp no np nq pq ns nt nu pr ps pt bk">What principles do you see from the intelligence section that you see also apply to CTI? How do you think CTI is the same or different from traditional intelligence?</li><li id="8511" class="mx my gu mz b na pu nc nd ne pv ng nh ni pw nk nl nm px no np nq py ns nt nu pr ps pt bk">How long has CTI been around as a discipline? As you go through this curriculum, think about who you see as key people and organizations who have driven the development of CTI.</li><li id="e2bc" class="mx my gu mz b na pu nc nd ne pv ng nh ni pw nk nl nm px no np nq py ns nt nu pr ps pt bk">How does the traditional intelligence cycle translate to CTI?</li></ul><h2 id="3e40" class="pz op gu bf oq qa qb dy ou qc qd ea oy ni qe qf qg nm qh qi qj nq qk ql qm qn bk">Optional paid resources</h2><ul class=""><li id="3fe7" class="mx my gu mz b na pm nc nd ne pn ng nh ni po nk nl nm pp no np nq pq ns nt nu pr ps pt bk">If you’re looking for a good book on CTI, I highly recommend Rebekah Brown and Scott Roberts’ <a class="af nv" href="https://www.oreilly.com/library/view/intelligence-driven-incident-response/9781491935187/" rel="noopener ugc nofollow" target="_blank">Intelligence-Driven Incident Response</a>.</li><li id="c3b1" class="mx my gu mz b na pu nc nd ne pv ng nh ni pw nk nl nm px no np nq py ns nt nu pr ps pt bk">If you’re looking for paid CTI courses, I recommend <a class="af nv" href="https://www.sans.org/cyber-security-courses/cyber-threat-intelligence/" rel="noopener ugc nofollow" target="_blank">SANS FOR578</a> (full disclosure: I teach it!), Joe Slowik’s <a class="af nv" href="https://paralus.co/training/" rel="noopener ugc nofollow" target="_blank">Operational Threat Intelligence course</a>, or any of <a class="af nv" href="https://school.threatintel.academy/" rel="noopener ugc nofollow" target="_blank">Sergio Caltagirone’s courses</a>. <a class="af nv" href="https://chrissanders.org/training/investigationtheory/" rel="noopener ugc nofollow" target="_blank">Chris Sanders’ Investigation Theory course</a> is more forensics-based, but the principles he teaches are key to those you should develop as a CTI analyst.</li></ul><h1 id="757e" class="oo op gu bf oq or os ot ou ov ow ox oy oz pa pb pc pd pe pf pg ph pi pj pk pl bk">Requirements</h1><p id="15d8" class="pw-post-body-paragraph mx my gu mz b na pm nc nd ne pn ng nh ni po nk nl nm pp no np nq pq ns nt nu gn bk">These are so important that they get their own section, though some of this content is covered in the above CTI section.</p><h2 id="0218" class="pz op gu bf oq qa qb dy ou qc qd ea oy ni qe qf qg nm qh qi qj nq qk ql qm qn bk">Read and watch</h2><ul class=""><li id="8cf1" class="mx my gu mz b na pm nc nd ne pn ng nh ni po nk nl nm pp no np nq pq ns nt nu pr ps pt bk">Read Scott Roberts’ <a class="af nv" href="https://sroberts.medium.com/cti-squadgoals-setting-requirements-41bcb63db918" rel="noopener">blog post</a> on requirements.</li><li id="40c9" class="mx my gu mz b na pu nc nd ne pv ng nh ni pw nk nl nm px no np nq py ns nt nu pr ps pt bk">Watch Andreas Sfakianakis’ <a class="af nv" href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kGqnCR6XOhQ" rel="noopener ugc nofollow" target="_blank">2020 CTI Summit presentation</a>.</li><li id="f676" class="mx my gu mz b na pu nc nd ne pv ng nh ni pw nk nl nm px no np nq py ns nt nu pr ps pt bk">Read Pasquale Stirparo’s <a class="af nv" href="https://isc.sans.edu/forums/diary/Defining+Threat+Intelligence+Requirements/21519/" rel="noopener ugc nofollow" target="_blank">blog post</a>.</li></ul><h2 id="3406" class="pz op gu bf oq qa qb dy ou qc qd ea oy ni qe qf qg nm qh qi qj nq qk ql qm qn bk">Things to do</h2><ul class=""><li id="688c" class="mx my gu mz b na pm nc nd ne pn ng nh ni po nk nl nm pp no np nq pq ns nt nu pr ps pt bk">Choose a CTI team at an organization— you can imagine one or use a real one you know of (if you’re trying to get a CTI job at a company, choosing that org would be great for this exercise). Write down strategic, operational, and tactical intelligence requirements you think that they might have based on your research (or made-up information) about that company.</li></ul><h2 id="7319" class="pz op gu bf oq qa qb dy ou qc qd ea oy ni qe qf qg nm qh qi qj nq qk ql qm qn bk">Questions to think about</h2><ul class=""><li id="3237" class="mx my gu mz b na pm nc nd ne pn ng nh ni po nk nl nm pp no np nq pq ns nt nu pr ps pt bk">Why are requirements so important in CTI? What do you think would happen if a team did not have requirements?</li></ul><h1 id="29a2" class="oo op gu bf oq or os ot ou ov ow ox oy oz pa pb pc pd pe pf pg ph pi pj pk pl bk">The CTI Community</h1><p id="2ae9" class="pw-post-body-paragraph mx my gu mz b na pm nc nd ne pn ng nh ni po nk nl nm pp no np nq pq ns nt nu gn bk">If you want to get involved in CTI, it’s important to get to know the community, including key people and stakeholders. Like anything in life, everyone has different perspectives, backgrounds, and motivations. You should get to know people in the community, while also realizing that we all have our own cognitive biases (see how this builds on earlier material?), so you should always think critically about the perspectives someone presents — and whether you agree or disagree (always doing so respectfully without resorting to ad hominem attacks).</p><h2 id="b3f6" class="pz op gu bf oq qa qb dy ou qc qd ea oy ni qe qf qg nm qh qi qj nq qk ql qm qn bk">Things to do</h2><ul class=""><li id="f957" class="mx my gu mz b na pm nc nd ne pn ng nh ni po nk nl nm pp no np nq pq ns nt nu pr ps pt bk">Create a Twitter account (it doesn’t have to be in your real name) and go find people to find in CTI to follow. If you’re not sure who to follow, check out me (@likethecoins) and see who I re-tweet and interact with, or follow some people I mention in this post. From your Twitter research, figure out what hash tags the CTI community uses. Follow these hash tags — you might find it easier to use <a class="af nv" href="https://help.twitter.com/en/using-twitter/how-to-use-tweetdeck" rel="noopener ugc nofollow" target="_blank">TweetDeck</a> to do this.</li><li id="da30" class="mx my gu mz b na pu nc nd ne pv ng nh ni pw nk nl nm px no np nq py ns nt nu pr ps pt bk">Create an RSS feed of some resources you see those Twitter accounts share. You could use Feedly’s free offerings to do this, or many <a class="af nv" href="https://zapier.com/blog/best-rss-feed-reader-apps/" rel="noopener ugc nofollow" target="_blank">other free RSS feed options</a>. Building a good RSS feed is essential for many CTI roles, as it will allow you to stay up to date with new blog posts and reports. Remember that your RSS feed will grow and change over time based on new sources you find and your needs (requirements!!!), so don’t worry about getting it “perfect” to start.</li></ul><h2 id="bbf4" class="pz op gu bf oq qa qb dy ou qc qd ea oy ni qe qf qg nm qh qi qj nq qk ql qm qn bk">Questions to think about</h2><ul class=""><li id="eda1" class="mx my gu mz b na pm nc nd ne pn ng nh ni po nk nl nm pp no np nq pq ns nt nu pr ps pt bk">What people and companies make up the “CTI community”? (For example, vendors are one group— what are others?) How does the “CTI community” overlap with other parts of the cybersecurity community like DFIR?</li><li id="9287" class="mx my gu mz b na pu nc nd ne pv ng nh ni pw nk nl nm px no np nq py ns nt nu pr ps pt bk">What roles do CTI analysts and CTI companies play in the community? What are their backgrounds and what do they do? Do they have a specific angle or agenda? What role does media play in the CTI community?</li><li id="296d" class="mx my gu mz b na pu nc nd ne pv ng nh ni pw nk nl nm px no np nq py ns nt nu pr ps pt bk">What are analysts discussing about CTI on Twitter? Who seems to represent what perspectives? What in their background might lead them to have those perspectives?</li><li id="a765" class="mx my gu mz b na pu nc nd ne pv ng nh ni pw nk nl nm px no np nq py ns nt nu pr ps pt bk">What are key events and conferences that happen in the CTI community? Which are free and how much do they cost? What approach/angle do different events take?</li></ul><h1 id="2598" class="oo op gu bf oq or os ot ou ov ow ox oy oz pa pb pc pd pe pf pg ph pi pj pk pl bk">Frameworks and Models</h1><p id="2b24" class="pw-post-body-paragraph mx my gu mz b na pm nc nd ne pn ng nh ni po nk nl nm pp no np nq pq ns nt nu gn bk">The “big three” frameworks and models I recommend you know about are the Cyber Kill Chain, the Diamond Model, and MITRE ATT&amp;CK(R), though there are several others as well. It’s important for CTI analysts to understand what these are and how they can be applied, as well as what to be cautious of when applying them. Concepts from these frameworks and models will also be sprinkled throughout this self-study plan.</p><h2 id="3ed6" class="pz op gu bf oq qa qb dy ou qc qd ea oy ni qe qf qg nm qh qi qj nq qk ql qm qn bk">Read and watch</h2><ul class=""><li id="1295" class="mx my gu mz b na pm nc nd ne pn ng nh ni po nk nl nm pp no np nq pq ns nt nu pr ps pt bk">Read Chris Sanders’ <a class="af nv" href="https://chrissanders.org/2019/05/infosec-mental-models/" rel="noopener ugc nofollow" target="_blank">blog on mental models</a>.</li><li id="f430" class="mx my gu mz b na pu nc nd ne pv ng nh ni pw nk nl nm px no np nq py ns nt nu pr ps pt bk">Read the Lockheed Martin Cyber Kill Chain(R) <a class="af nv" href="https://www.lockheedmartin.com/content/dam/lockheed-martin/rms/documents/cyber/LM-White-Paper-Intel-Driven-Defense.pdf" rel="noopener ugc nofollow" target="_blank">paper</a>.</li><li id="86e4" class="mx my gu mz b na pu nc nd ne pv ng nh ni pw nk nl nm px no np nq py ns nt nu pr ps pt bk">If you’re a Star Wars fan, read this <a class="af nv" href="https://threatconnect.com/blog/diamond-model-threat-intelligence-star-wars/" rel="noopener ugc nofollow" target="_blank">ThreatConnect blog</a> on applying Star Wars to the Diamond Model. (If you’re not a fan, you can obviously still read it, it just might not make as much sense to you!)</li><li id="19f7" class="mx my gu mz b na pu nc nd ne pv ng nh ni pw nk nl nm px no np nq py ns nt nu pr ps pt bk">Read the <a class="af nv" href="https://www.activeresponse.org/wp-content/uploads/2013/07/diamond.pdf" rel="noopener ugc nofollow" target="_blank">Diamond Model paper</a>. It’s dense, but it’s awesome, so break it up into parts if you need to.</li><li id="0100" class="mx my gu mz b na pu nc nd ne pv ng nh ni pw nk nl nm px no np nq py ns nt nu pr ps pt bk">Click around the <a class="af nv" href="http://veriscommunity.net/" rel="noopener ugc nofollow" target="_blank">VERIS website</a> and skim various sections — maybe the “Quick Start” section or whatever section helps you learn what VERIS is and why you might use it.</li></ul><h2 id="0743" class="pz op gu bf oq qa qb dy ou qc qd ea oy ni qe qf qg nm qh qi qj nq qk ql qm qn bk">Things to do</h2><ul class=""><li id="06a0" class="mx my gu mz b na pm nc nd ne pn ng nh ni po nk nl nm pp no np nq pq ns nt nu pr ps pt bk">Take the <a class="af nv" href="https://attack.mitre.org/resources/training/cti/" rel="noopener ugc nofollow" target="_blank">ATT&amp;CK for CTI training</a> I created with my former teammate Adam Pennington, designed to take about four hours. (Note that it’s based on a previous version of ATT&amp;CK, but you’ll still get the key points.)</li><li id="030c" class="mx my gu mz b na pu nc nd ne pv ng nh ni pw nk nl nm px no np nq py ns nt nu pr ps pt bk">Find at least one example of a team (perhaps a cybersecurity vendor) applying each of the frameworks or models you learned about above. Think about your opinion of the pros and cons of how they applied it.</li><li id="edb3" class="mx my gu mz b na pu nc nd ne pv ng nh ni pw nk nl nm px no np nq py ns nt nu pr ps pt bk">Go look for examples of combining two or more of these frameworks. (Hint: Palo Alto’s Unit 42 team has a good one, though there are others!)</li></ul><h2 id="cf4b" class="pz op gu bf oq qa qb dy ou qc qd ea oy ni qe qf qg nm qh qi qj nq qk ql qm qn bk">Questions to think about</h2><ul class=""><li id="b981" class="mx my gu mz b na pm nc nd ne pn ng nh ni po nk nl nm pp no np nq pq ns nt nu pr ps pt bk">Why do CTI analysts use frameworks and models? When would each of these frameworks or models be useful?</li><li id="bfaa" class="mx my gu mz b na pu nc nd ne pv ng nh ni pw nk nl nm px no np nq py ns nt nu pr ps pt bk">What are the limitations of frameworks and models?
