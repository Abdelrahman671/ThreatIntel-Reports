<p style="font-weight: 400;">Volexity has identified several long-running and currently active campaigns undertaken by the threat actor Volexity tracks as EvilBamboo (formerly named Evil Eye) targeting Tibetan, Uyghur, and Taiwanese individuals and organizations. These targets represent three of the <a href="https://www.govinfo.gov/content/pkg/CHRG-109hhrg22579/pdf/CHRG-109hhrg22579.pdf">Five Poisonous Groups</a> of Chinese Communist Party (CCP).</p>
<p style="font-weight: 400;">Volexity has tracked the activities of EvilBamboo for more than five years and continues to observe new campaigns from this threat actor. In <a href="https://www.volexity.com/blog/2019/09/02/digital-crackdown-large-scale-surveillance-and-exploitation-of-uyghurs/">September 2019</a>, Volexity described the deployment of a reconnaissance framework and custom Android malware targeting both the Uyghur and Tibetan communities. In <a href="https://www.volexity.com/blog/2020/04/21/evil-eye-threat-actor-resurfaces-with-ios-exploit-and-updated-implant/">April 2020</a>, Volexity detailed attacks by this threat actor against iOS devices, using a Safari exploit to infect Uyghur users with custom iOS malware.</p>
<p style="font-weight: 400;">Key highlights from Volexity’s recent investigations include the following:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Android targeting:</strong> Development of three custom Android malware families, BADBAZAAR, BADSIGNAL, and BADSOLAR, to infect CCP adversaries is ongoing.</li>
<li><strong>Fake websites and social media profiles:</strong> The attacker has created fake Tibetan websites, along with social media profiles, likely used to deploy browser-based exploits against targeted users.</li>
<li><strong>Building communities to facilitate malware distribution:</strong> Partly through impersonating existing popular communities, the attacker has built communities on online platforms, such as Telegram, to aid in distribution of their malware.</li>
<li><strong>iOS apps: </strong>Volexity discovered credible evidence of malicious iOS apps being successfully distributed via Apple’s App Store.</li>
<li><strong>Deployment of custom JavaScript profiling framework: </strong>Volexity observed the use of a custom JS profiling toolkit by the attackers specifically geared towards identifying devices running iOS and believes this could have been used to selectively deliver malware. Volexity also encountered evidence suggesting continued use of IRONSQUIRREL.</li>
</ul>
<p style="font-weight: 400;">With a high level of confidence, Volexity attributes this activity to EvilBamboo, a threat actor operating in the interest of the Chinese state. The content in this blog is the amalgamation of several reports sent to Volexity <a href="https://www.volexity.com/services-overview/threat-intelligence/">Threat Intelligence</a> customers in June 2023 and presented at <a href="https://www.labscon.io/speakers/paul-rascagneres/">LABScon 2023</a>.</p>
<h2>Android Malware: A Tale of Three BAD Brothers</h2>
<p style="font-weight: 400;">Volexity continues to predominantly track EvilBamboo’s campaigns through their prolific use of Android spyware. EvilBamboo currently employs at least three different Android spyware families, which Volexity tracks as BADBAZAAR, BADSIGNAL, and BADSOLAR. Each is inserted as a backdoor into legitimate applications. A recent <a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/en/eset-research/badbazaar-espionage-tool-targets-android-users-trojanized-signal-telegram-apps/">blog post by ESET</a> discusses BADSIGNAL, which they track under the name “BADBAZAAR”, a moniker first used in a 2022 report <a href="https://www.lookout.com/threat-intelligence/article/uyghur-surveillance-campaign-badbazaar-moonshine">by Lookout</a>. While the two malware families do share some code, they also appear to be divergent in their development and functionality. Both naming decisions are reasonable, but the reader should be aware of conflicting naming decisions regarding this malware.</p>

<p style="font-weight: 400;">The crux of the links between these families from a malware code point of view lies in two functions, <mark style="background-color: lightgrey;">GetOperatorName()</mark> and <mark style="background-color: lightgrey;">DeviceInfo()</mark>. The first function, <mark style="background-color: lightgrey;">GetOperatorName()</mark>, is used to get the GSM operator (Figure 1). The second function, <mark style="background-color: lightgrey;">DeviceInfo()</mark>, is used to generate the JSON object containing the information of the infected terminal.</p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><img decoding="async" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-2915" src="https://www.volexity.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/09/image001.png" alt="" width="1100" height="230" srcset="https://www.volexity.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/09/image001.png 1100w, https://www.volexity.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/09/image001-300x63.png 300w, https://www.volexity.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/09/image001-1024x214.png 1024w, https://www.volexity.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/09/image001-768x161.png 768w" sizes="(max-width: 1100px) 100vw, 1100px" /></p>
<p style="font-weight: 400; text-align: center;"><em>Figure 1. <mark style="background-color: lightgrey;">GetOperatorName()</mark> function</em></p>
<p style="font-weight: 400;">Both functions appear to be derived from a public source, as versions of each function are present in other APKs online. However, Volexity was unable to find either function in its exact form in the public domain. For example, <mark style="background-color: lightgrey;">DeviceInfo()</mark> is remarkably similar to one available from a public <a href="https://github.com/Leney/IpChangeDemo/blob/master/app/src/main/java/com/example/dell/ipchangedemo/net/NetUtil.java#L105">GitHub page</a>, while <mark style="background-color: lightgrey;">GetOperatorName()</mark> is similar to several <a href="https://github.com/chenshouyin/DevNote/blob/c9505e06f97c340f9b741622418d07c14727d9af/%E7%A7%BB%E5%8A%A8%E5%BC%80%E5%8F%91%E7%AF%87/Java/Android%E5%BC%80%E5%8F%91%E5%B7%A5%E5%85%B7%E7%B1%BB%E6%94%B6%E9%9B%86/CommonUtilLibrary-master/CommonUtilLibrary-master/CommonUtil/src/main/java/com/jingewenku/abrahamcaijin/commonutil/AppSysMgr.java#L176">publicly available code snippets</a>. Searching for any APK containing <strong>both</strong> functions yielded only malware related to the developer of these applications.</p>
<p style="font-weight: 400;">In addition to this key code overlap, Volexity was also able to link use of the different malware families together through analysis of attacker infrastructure patterns &amp; distribution methods.</p>
<p style="font-weight: 400;">Analysis of each of the malware families is given in an <a href="#Appendix">Appendix</a>.</p>
<h3>Mobile Malware Distribution</h3>
<h4>Forum Threads</h4>
<p style="font-weight: 400;">Since at least January 17, 2023, EvilBamboo has been targeting Taiwanese users via distribution of BADBAZAAR through multiple threads on a <a href="https://apk.tw/thread-1024826-1-1.html">Taiwanese APK sharing forum</a>. The main thread has over 100,000 views and claims to be sharing a cracked version of the popular <a href="https://whoscall.com/en">Whoscall</a> Android application. The legitimate Whoscall app helps identify spam calls and messages. Its Taiwanese-based developer, Gogolook Co. Ltd, claims the app has had over 100 million downloads.</p>
<p style="font-weight: 400;">A translated screenshot from the thread is included below (Figure 2).</p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><img decoding="async" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-2916" src="https://www.volexity.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/09/image002.png" alt="" width="484" height="712" srcset="https://www.volexity.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/09/image002.png 484w, https://www.volexity.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/09/image002-204x300.png 204w" sizes="(max-width: 484px) 100vw, 484px" /></p>
<p style="font-weight: 400; text-align: center;"><em>Figure 2. Screenshot of the thread on a Taiwanese APK sharing forum promoting Whoscall</em></p>
<p style="font-weight: 400;">At the bottom of the forum post, a link is included for users to download the App. This link, which is updated each time EvilBamboo releases a new version of the APK, leads to a QR code that currently leads to a Dropbox link hosting the latest version of the APK. In the past, the link has led to a Google Drive URL run by an account called <em>“TibetOne”.</em></p>
<h4>Fake Websites and Social Media</h4>
<p style="font-weight: 400;">A primary method EvilBamboo uses to support distribution of its Android spyware is establishing websites that lend legitimacy to their apps. Since January 16, 2023, EvilBamboo has distributed the BADSIGNAL spyware through a fake website, <mark style="background-color: lightgrey;">www.signalplus[.]org</mark>, that was created to aid in the distribution of BADSIGNAL. As the name suggests, it is a backdoored version of the Signal app (Figures 3)</p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="aligncenter wp-image-2917 size-large" src="https://www.volexity.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/09/image003-1024x747.png" alt="" width="1024" height="747" srcset="https://www.volexity.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/09/image003-1024x747.png 1024w, https://www.volexity.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/09/image003-300x219.png 300w, https://www.volexity.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/09/image003-768x560.png 768w, https://www.volexity.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/09/image003.png 1099w" sizes="(max-width: 1024px) 100vw, 1024px" /></p>
<p style="font-weight: 400; text-align: center;"><em>Figure 3. Website </em><em><mark style="background-color: lightgrey;">www.signalplus[.]org</mark></em><em> used to distribute BADSIGNAL</em></p>
<p style="font-weight: 400;">In addition to the backdoored version of Signal, Volexity uncovered historic samples where BADSIGNAL code was used to backdoor other applications. Two notable examples of this involve backdooring the Telegram application. As with the Signal variant, EvilBamboo created fake websites to distribute these samples, <mark style="background-color: lightgrey;">www.flygram[.]org</mark> (Figure 4) and <mark style="background-color: lightgrey;">www.groupgram[.]org</mark>. There is also a <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=awbjkwCMQ7E">promotional YouTube video</a> for the backdoored FlyGram app. These samples appear to have been active since June 2020, and they implement the same style of command-and-control (C2) communication through a REST API on port 4432.</p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-2918" src="https://www.volexity.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/09/image004.png" alt="" width="742" height="438" srcset="https://www.volexity.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/09/image004.png 742w, https://www.volexity.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/09/image004-300x177.png 300w" sizes="(max-width: 742px) 100vw, 742px" /></p>

Due to misconfiguration of the C2 server, it was possible to enumerate the API endpoints used by the FlyGram variant, which showed the threat actor had configured API endpoints for an iOS version of the app (Figure 5).</p>
<p><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-2919" src="https://www.volexity.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/09/image005.png" alt="" width="724" height="302" srcset="https://www.volexity.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/09/image005.png 724w, https://www.volexity.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/09/image005-300x125.png 300w" sizes="(max-width: 724px) 100vw, 724px" /></p>

<p style="font-weight: 400;">Another site created to assist with distribution of malware is <mark style="background-color: lightgrey;">allwhatsapp[.]net</mark>, which hosts variations of BADBAZAAR (Figure 6).</p>

<h2>Fake Websites Leading to Malicious JavaScript</h2>
<p style="font-weight: 400;">A user who routinely shares BADSOLAR samples via the <em>“Tibetanphone" </em>Telegram group also shared a link to <mark style="background-color: lightgrey;">ignitetibet[.]net</mark>. This website is currently active and hosted on infrastructure (<mark style="background-color: lightgrey;">45.154.12[.]80</mark>) that has overlaps with <mark style="background-color: lightgrey;">uyghurinfo[.]net</mark>, which also shares a distinct registration pattern with several of the BADSOLAR C2 domains. Further, the same IP address hosts a website that has a simple survey regarding Taiwan’s independence <mark style="background-color: lightgrey;">tw.tinmf[.]org</mark>.</p>
<p style="font-weight: 400;">One article published on <mark style="background-color: lightgrey;">ignitetibet[.]net</mark> in March 2023 attempts to load two additional resources (Figure 10).</p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-2924" src="https://www.volexity.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/09/image010.png" alt="" width="1593" height="69" srcset="https://www.volexity.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/09/image010.png 1593w, https://www.volexity.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/09/image010-300x13.png 300w, https://www.volexity.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/09/image010-1024x44.png 1024w, https://www.volexity.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/09/image010-768x33.png 768w, https://www.volexity.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/09/image010-1536x67.png 1536w" sizes="(max-width: 1593px) 100vw, 1593px" /></p>
<p style="font-weight: 400; text-align: center;"><em>Figure 10. Additional resources loaded when viewing a March 2023 article posted on <mark style="background-color: lightgrey;">ignitetibet[.]net</mark></em></p>
<p style="font-weight: 400;">The request to the URL on port 9001 received no response at the time of analysis; however, the second resource (<mark style="background-color: lightgrey;">jquery.min.js</mark>) loaded an obfuscated profiling script, which Volexity refers to as JMASK. JMASK is a custom profiler that is <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minification_(programming)">minified</a> and obfuscated through the use of <a href="https://www.educative.io/answers/how-to-insert-unicode-in-javascript">Unicode declarations</a> of each string, which are declared in reverse. In summary, the purpose of JMASK is as follows:</p>
<ul>
<li>Collect basic device information, such as the time zone, language, and screen resolution.</li>
<li>List the user’s Ethereum accounts if they are running the MetaMask extension. The technique used to do this will not work in MetaMask extension versions newer than Q2 2020.</li>
<li>Fingerprint the browser using <a href="https://browserleaks.com/canvas#how-does-it-work">canvas-based fingerprinting</a>. Curiously, the implementation fills the canvas used for fingerprinting with a reference to a Chinese-language Github account named <em>“Eular”</em> (Figure 11). It is unclear why this string was chosen.</li>
</ul>
<p style="text-align: center;"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-2925" src="https://www.volexity.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/09/image011.png" alt="" width="841" height="394" srcset="https://www.volexity.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/09/image011.png 841w, https://www.volexity.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/09/image011-300x141.png 300w, https://www.volexity.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/09/image011-768x360.png 768w" sizes="(max-width: 841px) 100vw, 841px" /></p>
<p style="font-weight: 400; text-align: center;"><em>Figure 11. Canvas function used to fingerprint a user’s browser referencing a Chinese-language Github account named “Eular”</em></p>
<p style="font-weight: 400;">There is no automated loading of any additional JavaScript. Instead, Volexity hypothesizes that the profiling script was used to produce lists of potentially valid victim IP addresses, and that access to the likely exploitation code on port 9001 was gated based on this list. Another page on <mark style="background-color: lightgrey;">ignitetibet[.]net</mark> contained an iframe element linking to <mark style="background-color: lightgrey;">hxxps://jindjjdtc[.]com/HxtDp2fORTSU.html</mark>. At the time of analysis, this link was not live.</p>
<p style="font-weight: 400;">Based on the URI schema used, Volexity assesses with low confidence that this URL hosted IRONSQUIRREL due its similarity to other in-the-wild URIs used by EvilBamboo which hosted the same <a href="https://www.volexity.com/blog/2020/04/21/evil-eye-threat-actor-resurfaces-with-ios-exploit-and-updated-implant/">framework</a>. In later versions of JMASK observed, the script was adjusted to specifically identify specific versions of Apple devices rendering the page based on a <a href="https://github.com/joyqi/mobile-device-js/blob/master/device.js">publicly available project</a>.</p>
<p style="font-weight: 400;">A third site of note, <mark style="background-color: lightgrey;">tibetone[.]org</mark>, shares the same registration pattern as the BADSOLAR C2 and <mark style="background-color: lightgrey;">uyghurinfo[.]net</mark>. This site is promoted on <a href="https://www.reddit.com/r/tibet/comments/hs4tpp/find_inner_peace_and_meaning_in_our_life/">Reddit</a> and <a href="https://twitter.com/TenzinNima11/status/1280402216407281664">Twitter</a> by the same personas mentioned previously that promoted BADBAZAAR and BADSOLAR. This site also appeared as the name of a likely malicious iOS app associated with BADSOLAR, as well as a Google account used to distribute the BADBAZAAR variant targeting Taiwanese individuals. The site has associated <a href="https://www.facebook.com/TibetOne/">Facebook</a> and <a href="https://www.youtube.com/@tibetone8438">YouTube</a> accounts that are likely controlled by EvilBamboo as part of their effort to create communities to distribute malicious applications.</p>
<p style="font-weight: 400;">All three sites appear to be run by EvilBamboo. <h2>Conclusion</h2>
<p style="font-weight: 400;">This blog details a series of long-running campaigns by the EvilBamboo threat actor targeting groups of interest that are seen as a threat by the CCP, both domestically and internationally. These campaigns largely rely on users installing backdoored apps, which highlights both the importance of only installing apps from trusted authors and the lack of effective security mechanisms to stop backdoored apps making their way on to official app stores. It is worth noting that for some users, installing applications from untrusted sources is not unusual, as often their language is not officially supported by application developers.</p>
<p style="font-weight: 400;">Compromise of mobile devices enables the collection of large amounts of highly sensitive information about individuals, which can put them— and those close to them— at risk. EvilBamboo is actively using three mobile spyware families, BADBAZAAR, BADSIGNAL and BADSOLAR, as part of their active collection apparatus to surveil these individuals and presumably weaponize this information to further their objectives against these targeted ethnicities.</p>
<p style="font-weight: 400;">EvilBamboo’s creation of fake websites, and the personas tailored to the specific groups they target, has been a key aspect of their operations, enabling them to build trusted communities that provide further avenues to target individuals with their spyware or for other exploitation. The threat actor has co-opted the trust users have in legitimate platforms, such as YouTube, Reddit, Twitter, Instagram, Facebook, and other online forums, to add legitimacy to these operations. A list of the profiles on these platforms observed is given <a href="https://github.com/volexity/threat-intel/blob/main/2023/2023-09-22%20EvilBamboo/attachments/evilbamboo-distribution.csv">here</a>.</p>

<h3><strong>BADBAZAAR Analysis</strong></h3>
<p style="font-weight: 400;">Credit for first identifying BADBAZAAR belongs to Lookout. Their <a href="https://www.lookout.com/threat-intelligence/article/uyghur-surveillance-campaign-badbazaar-moonshine">November 2022 blogpost</a> detailed its use to target Uyghur and other individuals of Muslim faith. Many samples discovered by Volexity in its research do not deviate significantly from Lookout’s existing writeup. However, one variation shared in the context of the WhosCall application is worth describing.</p>
<p style="font-weight: 400;">The latest versions shared on <mark style="background-color: lightgrey;">apk[.]tw</mark> contain a new variant of BADBAZAAR with additional capabilities that allow EvilBamboo to automatically update the app. This updater functionality is implemented through the <mark style="background-color: lightgrey;">judgeUpdateOrNot</mark>function (Figure 13), which checks the installed malware version through the <mark style="background-color: lightgrey;">com.whoscall.update.CompleteReceiver</mark>class. Based on this, the code includes functions to check the <mark style="background-color: lightgrey;">version_url</mark> to see if the currently installed version of the app is the latest version (<mark style="background-color: lightgrey;">judgeUpdateOrNot</mark>), and if it should attempt to update the app (<mark style="background-color: lightgrey;">sbDownload</mark>).</p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-2927" src="https://www.volexity.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/09/image013.png" alt="" width="902" height="326" srcset="https://www.volexity.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/09/image013.png 902w, https://www.volexity.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/09/image013-300x108.png 300w, https://www.volexity.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/09/image013-768x278.png 768w" sizes="(max-width: 902px) 100vw, 902px" /></p>
<p style="font-weight: 400; text-align: center;"><em>Figure 13. <mark style="background-color: lightgrey;">judgeUpdateOrNot</mark> function</em></p>
<p style="font-weight: 400;">This functionality is separate from the main logic flow of the malicious code, which still looks to download a second-stage implant in the form of a JAR file. The second stage contains the main malicious capabilities of the spyware and allows the threat actor to issue commands to do the following:</p>
<ul>
<li>Get SMS messages stored on the terminal.</li>
<li>Get call logs.</li>
<li>Get the device information, such as the IMEI, IMSI, time zone, Wi-Fi details, etc.</li>
<li>Take photos.</li>
<li>Get the contacts list.</li>
<li>Get the installed apps.</li>
<li>List and get stored files and pictures on the device.</li>
<li>Get the location of the device.</li>
</ul>
<p style="font-weight: 400;">Each compromised device is identified by its <mark style="background-color: lightgrey;">DeviceID</mark>. In addition to these commands, the operator receives the user’s SMS messages in real time, which are automatically forwarded to the C2 server. Volexity hypothesizes that the purpose of this real-time SMS theft is to target Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) using SMS technology.</p>
<h3><strong>BADSOLAR Analysis</strong></h3>
<p style="font-weight: 400;">BADSOLAR is a malware family that is backdoored into legitimate Android applications. It appears to be used primarily with apps that are themed as Tibetan, ranging from prayer apps to Tibetan dictionaries. The malicious code is executed through the creation of a service in the <mark style="background-color: lightgrey;">MainActivity</mark> class (Figure 14).</p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-2928" src="https://www.volexity.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/09/image014.png" alt="" width="606" height="298" srcset="https://www.volexity.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/09/image014.png 606w, https://www.volexity.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/09/image014-300x148.png 300w" sizes="(max-width: 606px) 100vw, 606px" /></p>
<p style="font-weight: 400; text-align: center;"><em>Figure 14. <mark style="background-color: lightgrey;">MainActivity</mark> class</em></p>
<p style="font-weight: 400;">This ultimately executes the BADSOLAR loader that is located in the <mark style="background-color: lightgrey;">com.SolARCS.SolClient</mark> class, which serves as the inspiration behind the name of the spyware family. The C2 is stored as an encrypted string that is decoded using the DES algorithm, using the key <mark style="background-color: lightgrey;">yhnrfv</mark> (Figure 15).</p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-2929" src="https://www.volexity.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/09/image015.png" alt="" width="622" height="288" srcset="https://www.volexity.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/09/image015.png 622w, https://www.volexity.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/09/image015-300x139.png 300w" sizes="(max-width: 622px) 100vw, 622px" /></p>
<p style="font-weight: 400; text-align: center;"><em>Figure 15. C2 stored as an encrypted string which is decoded using the key <mark style="background-color: lightgrey;">yhnrfv</mark></em></p>
<p style="font-weight: 400;">The decrypted C2 address for all known samples of BADSOLAR is <mark style="background-color: lightgrey;">comeflxyr[.]com</mark>. The loader’s main function is to download a JAR file from the C2 server and load it by using the <mark style="background-color: lightgrey;">DexClassLoader()</mark> class. The screenshot below shows the loading of the <mark style="background-color: lightgrey;">CommandExecute()</mark> method located in the downloaded JAR file (Figure 16).</p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-2930" src="https://www.volexity.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/09/image016.png" alt="" width="780" height="154" srcset="https://www.volexity.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/09/image016.png 780w, https://www.volexity.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/09/image016-300x59.png 300w, https://www.volexity.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/09/image016-768x152.png 768w" sizes="(max-width: 780px) 100vw, 780px" /></p>
<p style="font-weight: 400; text-align: center;"><em>Figure 16. Loading of the <mark style="background-color: lightgrey;">CommandExecute()</mark> method</em></p>

While BADSOLAR’s developer has added some capabilities and modified the code, the original code was clearly forked from AndroRAT. The C2 address is encrypted using the same algorithm and key previously described for the loader. The method names are not obfuscated, and the functions closely match their names:</p>

<h3><strong>BADSIGNAL Analysis</strong></h3>
<p style="font-weight: 400;">In contrast to BADBAZAAR and BADSOLAR, BADSIGNAL does not download a second-stage payload. Instead, all capabilities are included in the main APK. The malicious code is loaded by extending the legitimate <mark style="background-color: lightgrey;">PassPhraseRequiredActivity</mark> class in org.thoughtcrime.securesms.<mark style="background-color: lightgrey;">MainActivity</mark>. BADSIGNAL uses a REST API on port 4432 as part of its C2 communication, with the following endpoints:</p>
<table class="table table--style-2 aligncenter" style="width: 90%; font-weight: 400;">
<thead>

</tr>

<p style="font-weight: 400;">The generic information stolen and sent to <mark style="background-color: lightgrey;">/api/values </mark> uses the same code as BADBAZAAR and BADSOLAR. One of the most interesting aspects of BADSIGNAL is how it interacts with the Signal application it has backdoored. The threat actor silently links a new device to the user’s Signal account, steals the Signal PIN code, and forces the use of a Signal proxy server. This enables EvilBamboo to read any new messages sent through the legitimate Signal app.</p>
