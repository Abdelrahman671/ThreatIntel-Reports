          <h1>Agent Tesla's New Ride: The Rise of a Novel Loader</h1>          
          
        </div>
        <div class="blog-details-header-right">
          
            
            <a href="#" class="theme-changer">Change theme to light</a>
            
            
            <img src="https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Web/Spiders/2024/spider_01-mob.png?width=450&amp;height=250&amp;name=spider_01-mob.png" loading="eager" alt="Agent Tesla's New Ride: The Rise of a Novel Loader" class="hero-spider">
            
          
        </div>
      </div>
      <div class="toc-content content-wrapper">
        
        <div class="wrapper-main_content">
          
          <div class="blog-details-metadata">
            <span class="date-time">
              <time datetime="2024-03-26 13:00:00">March 26, 2024</time>
            </span>
            <span class="minread">6 Minute Read</span>
            
              <span class="blog-author">by Bernard Bautista</span>
            
          </div> 
          
          
          <div class="wrapper-blog-content">
            <span id="hs_cos_wrapper_post_body" class="hs_cos_wrapper hs_cos_wrapper_meta_field hs_cos_wrapper_type_rich_text" style="" data-hs-cos-general-type="meta_field" data-hs-cos-type="rich_text"><p>Malware loaders, critical for deploying malware, enable threat actors to deliver and execute malicious payloads, facilitating criminal activities like data theft and ransomware. Utilizing advanced evasion techniques, loaders bypass security measures and exploit various distribution channels for extensive impact, threat groups enhance their ability to download and execute various malware types as demonstrated by Smoke Loader and GuLoader, highlighting their role in extensive malware distribution.</p>
<!--more--><p>Most recently, SpiderLabs identified a phishing email on March 8, 2024, with an attached archive that included a Windows executable disguised as a fraudulent bank payment. This action initiated an infection chain culminating in the deployment of Agent Tesla.</p>
<p>This blog provides an in-depth analysis of a newly identified loader, highlighting the attack's evasiveness and the advanced tactics, techniques, and procedures (TTPs) used in both the loader and its command and control (C2) framework. We will go through an in-depth discussion of the polymorphic nature of the loader, the use of specific user-agent strings, proxy usage, and command and control server protection that collectively enhances the loader's ability to deliver and execute the payload. While these techniques aren't new or unique individually, this new loader's effectiveness stems from its skillful combination and integration of these methods, which can potentially lead to increased infection rates and greater challenges for detection and mitigation.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h3 style="font-weight: bold;">Technical Analysis</h3>
<h4 style="font-weight: normal;">Infection Chain</h4>
<p>The following is a brief overview that illustrates the infection chain. The infection chain begins with a phishing email posing as a bank payment notification in which a disguised loader was attached as an archive file. This loader then used obfuscation to evade detection and leveraged polymorphic behavior with complex decryption methods. The loader also exhibited the capability to bypass antivirus defenses and retrieved its payload using specific URLs and user agents leveraging proxies for further obfuscate traffic. The payload itself, the Agent Tesla infostealer, is then executed entirely in memory, capturing and exfiltrating data via SMTP using compromised email accounts for discreet communication.</p>

<h4>Email Delivery</h4>
<p>The threat begins with a fake bank payment email designed to deceive recipients. Concealed within this email is an attachment named "<strong>Bank Handlowy w Warszawie - dowód wpłaty_pdf.tar.gz</strong>" masquerading as a legitimate payment receipt from a bank. This filename implies a harmless document, but it actually contains a malicious loader disguised within the tar.gz archive. This tactic is commonly employed in phishing attacks to trick recipients into unwittingly activating the malware and initiating nefarious activities.</p>

<h4>Loader</h4>
<p>The loader executable, compiled with .NET, uses obfuscation and packing to hide its functionality and evade detection.</p>
<p>Upon execution, the loader initializes its configuration by storing encrypted strings and decryption keys in separate lists within its code. The decryption begins by identifying an encrypted string's index and matching it to a specific key in another list. This index-based matching is a crucial step in decrypting the configuration data the malware needs to operate.</p>

<p>The loader's polymorphic behavior is particularly intriguing, evident through two distinct variants we observed, each utilizing a different decryption routine. This polymorphism introduces an additional layer of complexity, posing significant challenges for traditional antivirus systems.</p>
<p>Taking a closer look at the decryption routines employed by each variant reveals the intricacies of their operations:</p>
<p><strong>Variant 1 - ab9cd59d789e6c7841b9d28689743e700d492b5fae1606f184889cc7e6acadcc</strong></p>

It then generates an SHA-256 hash of the encrypted string, ensuring the hash's length matches that of the byte array from the key. Following this, it then decrypts by subtracting corresponding bytes in the key-derived byte array from those in the SHA-256 hash.</p>

<p align="center" style="font-size: 14px;"><em>Figure 8. Variant 1 utilizes a specific-user agent to fetch payload though a proxy.</em></p>
<p align="center" style="font-size: 14px;">&nbsp;</p>
<p>One variant employs an HTTP proxy server sourced from an open-source list on GitHub, specifically from<span>&nbsp;</span><strong>https://github.com/TheSpeedX/PROXY-List/blob/master/http.txt</strong>, to facilitate the download of the payload. This approach generates numerous network packets and significant noise, potentially complicating network traffic analysis and detection efforts.</p>
<p>After retrieval, the payload is not executed immediately. Instead, the loader identifies and extracts the payload from HTML content using “:::” as a delimiter to separate the malicious code. This operation segment underscores the loader's stealthy approach in handling the payload, ensuring that the malicious code remains undetected within benign-looking traffic. Following extraction, the payload is loaded into memory.</p>

<p align="center" style="font-size: 14px;">&nbsp;</p>
<p>The loader decrypts the payload using XOR encryption, with the decryption key embedded within the loader itself. It begins by converting the hexadecimal string into a byte sequence, then iterates over each byte, applying the XOR operation with the corresponding byte in the key. The two variants we observed follow a similar decryption routine to process the payload, differing only in the decryption keys used.</p>
<p align="center"><img loading="lazy" src="https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blogs/SpiderLab_Blog_Images/Figure%2010.%20Payload%20decryption%20routine.jpg?width=563&amp;height=113&amp;name=Figure%2010.%20Payload%20decryption%20routine.jpg" width="563" height="113" alt="Figure 10. Payload decryption routine" style="height: auto; max-width: 100%; width: 563px;" srcset="https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blogs/SpiderLab_Blog_Images/Figure%2010.%20Payload%20decryption%20routine.jpg?width=282&amp;height=57&amp;name=Figure%2010.%20Payload%20decryption%20routine.jpg 282w, https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blogs/SpiderLab_Blog_Images/Figure%2010.%20Payload%20decryption%20routine.jpg?width=563&amp;height=113&amp;name=Figure%2010.%20Payload%20decryption%20routine.jpg 563w, https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blogs/SpiderLab_Blog_Images/Figure%2010.%20Payload%20decryption%20routine.jpg?width=845&amp;height=170&amp;name=Figure%2010.%20Payload%20decryption%20routine.jpg 845w, https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blogs/SpiderLab_Blog_Images/Figure%2010.%20Payload%20decryption%20routine.jpg?width=1126&amp;height=226&amp;name=Figure%2010.%20Payload%20decryption%20routine.jpg 1126w, https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blogs/SpiderLab_Blog_Images/Figure%2010.%20Payload%20decryption%20routine.jpg?width=1408&amp;height=283&amp;name=Figure%2010.%20Payload%20decryption%20routine.jpg 1408w, https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blogs/SpiderLab_Blog_Images/Figure%2010.%20Payload%20decryption%20routine.jpg?width=1689&amp;height=339&amp;name=Figure%2010.%20Payload%20decryption%20routine.jpg 1689w" sizes="(max-width: 563px) 100vw, 563px"></p>
<p align="center" style="font-size: 14px;"><em>Figure 10. Payload decryption routine</em></p>
<p align="center" style="font-size: 14px;">&nbsp;</p>
<p>Once the payload is decoded and stored in memory, the loader, utilizing .NET's reflection and assembly loading capabilities, locates and invokes the main entry point of the payload, triggering the operation of the Agent Tesla infostealer. The loading and execution process takes place exclusively within the system's memory space, minimizing detection and leaving no trace on disk, thus enhancing the stealth and evasiveness of the malicious activity.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h4>Agent Tesla Infostealer</h4>
<p>Agent Tesla is executed entirely from memory, conducting malicious activities like keystroke logging, credential theft, and data exfiltration. This variant uses KoiVM protector, a virtualization-based packer that transforms .NET CIL code into virtual instructions, which is interpreted at runtime by a virtual machine, complicating both static and dynamic analysis.</p>
<p>After collecting valuable information like usernames and passwords from web browsers, Agent Tesla exfiltrates this data using the Simple Mail Transfer Protocol (SMTP), a method commonly used for sending emails.</p>
<p><img loading="lazy" src="https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blogs/SpiderLab_Blog_Images/Figure%2011.%20Embedded%20configuration%20of%20Agent%20Tesla%20infostealer.jpg?width=599&amp;height=335&amp;name=Figure%2011.%20Embedded%20configuration%20of%20Agent%20Tesla%20infostealer.jpg" width="599" height="335" alt="Figure 11. Embedded configuration of Agent Tesla infostealer" style="height: auto; max-width: 100%; width: 599px;" srcset="https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blogs/SpiderLab_Blog_Images/Figure%2011.%20Embedded%20configuration%20of%20Agent%20Tesla%20infostealer.jpg?width=300&amp;height=168&amp;name=Figure%2011.%20Embedded%20configuration%20of%20Agent%20Tesla%20infostealer.jpg 300w, https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blogs/SpiderLab_Blog_Images/Figure%2011.%20Embedded%20configuration%20of%20Agent%20Tesla%20infostealer.jpg?width=599&amp;height=335&amp;name=Figure%2011.%20Embedded%20configuration%20of%20Agent%20Tesla%20infostealer.jpg 599w, https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blogs/SpiderLab_Blog_Images/Figure%2011.%20Embedded%20configuration%20of%20Agent%20Tesla%20infostealer.jpg?width=899&amp;height=503&amp;name=Figure%2011.%20Embedded%20configuration%20of%20Agent%20Tesla%20infostealer.jpg 899w, https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blogs/SpiderLab_Blog_Images/Figure%2011.%20Embedded%20configuration%20of%20Agent%20Tesla%20infostealer.jpg?width=1198&amp;height=670&amp;name=Figure%2011.%20Embedded%20configuration%20of%20Agent%20Tesla%20infostealer.jpg 1198w, https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blogs/SpiderLab_Blog_Images/Figure%2011.%20Embedded%20configuration%20of%20Agent%20Tesla%20infostealer.jpg?width=1498&amp;height=838&amp;name=Figure%2011.%20Embedded%20configuration%20of%20Agent%20Tesla%20infostealer.jpg 1498w, https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blogs/SpiderLab_Blog_Images/Figure%2011.%20Embedded%20configuration%20of%20Agent%20Tesla%20infostealer.jpg?width=1797&amp;height=1005&amp;name=Figure%2011.%20Embedded%20configuration%20of%20Agent%20Tesla%20infostealer.jpg 1797w" sizes="(max-width: 599px) 100vw, 599px"></p>
<p align="center" style="font-size: 14px;"><em>Figure 11. Embedded configuration of Agent Tesla infostealer</em></p>
<p align="center" style="font-size: 14px;">&nbsp;</p>
<p>Threat actors often hijack compromised email accounts to carry out the exfiltration process. This method has several strategic benefits. First, it exploits the trust people have in regular email communication, making it less likely to raise suspicion. Second, it provides anonymity and makes it harder to trace the attack back to the threat actors. Finally, using existing email systems means they don't have to set up new communication channels, saving time and resources.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h3 style="font-weight: bold;">Summary</h3>
<p>The new loader observed in conjunction with Agent Tesla utilizes deceptive email attachments, such as a disguised bank payment receipt, to infiltrate systems. Compiled with .NET, it employs obfuscation and packing techniques to conceal its functionality and evade detection. The loader exhibits polymorphic behavior with distinct decryption routines. It employs methods like patching to bypass Antimalware Scan Interface (AMSI) detection and dynamically load payloads, ensuring stealthy execution and minimizing traces on disk. This loader marks a notable evolution in the deployment tactics of Agent Tesla.</p>
<p>Furthermore, given its versatility, it is likely that this loader will be utilized to deploy other types of malware beyond Agent Tesla in the future.</p>
