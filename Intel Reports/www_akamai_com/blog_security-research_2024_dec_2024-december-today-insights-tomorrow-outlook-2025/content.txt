    <p>For 10 years, Akamai has published State of the Internet (SOTI) reports — in-depth analyses of the latest cybersecurity research and trends. These reports provide valuable insights into the evolving threat landscape and cybersecurity challenges faced by organizations worldwide.</p>
<p>In our early years, these reports focused largely on broadband adoption and connection speeds, but over time they’ve shifted to cybersecurity — and each of them has been built on a collection of robust data that we’ve cultivated by protecting tens of thousands of customers in over 130 countries for more than a quarter of a century.</p>

      <h3 class="cmp-title__text">What stood out for you in 2024?<br />
</h3>
    
</div>

    


</div>
<div class="text aem-GridColumn aem-GridColumn--default--12">
<div data-cmp-is="text" class="cmp-text" data-cmp-data-layer="{&#34;text-de6e8b3db9&#34;:{&#34;@type&#34;:&#34;akamai/components/text&#34;,&#34;repo:modifyDate&#34;:&#34;2024-11-12T02:52:56Z&#34;,&#34;xdm:text&#34;:&#34;&lt;p>As I look back at 2024, several key trends stand out. One surprising phenomenon was the continued high frequency and effectiveness of DNS attacks. Akamai researchers recently &lt;a href=\&#34;/content/akamai/en/resources/state-of-the-internet/securing-apps-report-2024.html\&#34; target=\&#34;_self\&#34;>observed&lt;/a> that &lt;a href=\&#34;/content/akamai/en/glossary/what-is-a-dns-ddos-attack.html\&#34; target=\&#34;_self\&#34;>DNS&lt;/a> was a component in 60% of Layers 3 and 4 &lt;a href=\&#34;/content/akamai/en/blog/security/why-and-how-apis-web-applications-are-under-siege.html\&#34; target=\&#34;_self\&#34;>distributed denial-of-service (DDoS) attack&lt;/a> events in an 18-month period. Yet, there are &lt;a href=\&#34;/content/akamai/en/resources/product-brief/edge-dns.html\&#34; target=\&#34;_self\&#34;>solutions&lt;/a> that can eliminate many of those attacks. Many of these attacks are reflector-based, with threat actors taking advantage of improperly configured servers. Simple DNS changes can prevent this and ensure that organizations don&#39;t inadvertently become part of the problem.&lt;/p>\r\n&lt;p>We saw a resurgence of the &lt;a href=\&#34;/content/akamai/en/blog/security-research/2024-corona-mirai-botnet-infects-zero-day-sirt.html\&#34; target=\&#34;_self\&#34;>Mirai&lt;/a> botnet, which has been responsible for some of the world&#39;s largest and most complicated attacks. It&#39;s one of the most interesting attack tools; it’s highly intelligent in how it takes over endpoints and locks out other actors. Mirai was quite prominent five years ago, then it disappeared — but now it&#39;s back with a vengeance.&lt;/p>\r\n&lt;p>This development underscores the need for organizations to focus not just on the size of attacks but also on their level of sophistication. Attack campaigns are becoming much more complex. Many security vendors are building in more intelligent automation to speed up response times, which is an appropriate move given the landscape.&amp;nbsp;&lt;/p>\r\n&lt;p>Additionally, &lt;b>I firmly believe that access to security experts remains crucial&lt;/b>. Sooner or later, every medium or large enterprise will face a cyber event and resolving it will require human expertise.&lt;/p>\r\n&lt;p>On the applications side, bot &lt;a href=\&#34;/content/akamai/en/solutions/security/bot-and-abuse-protection.html\&#34; target=\&#34;_self\&#34;>credential abuse&lt;/a> continued to emerge as a major threat. These attacks, many committed by nation-state actors, are extremely challenging to mitigate. Determining whether an attacker is a bot or a human, or distinguishing between &lt;a href=\&#34;/content/akamai/en/resources/state-of-the-internet/web-scraping-report-2024.html\&#34; target=\&#34;_self\&#34;>&amp;quot;good&amp;quot; and &amp;quot;bad&amp;quot; bots&lt;/a> (Figure 1), requires experienced human experts to understand the unique customer environment and implement the appropriate protection measures.&lt;/p>\r\n&#34;}}">
    <p>As I look back at 2024, several key trends stand out. One surprising phenomenon was the continued high frequency and effectiveness of DNS attacks. Akamai researchers recently <a href="/resources/state-of-the-internet/securing-apps-report-2024" target="_self">observed</a> that <a href="/glossary/what-is-a-dns-ddos-attack" target="_self">DNS</a> was a component in 60% of Layers 3 and 4 <a href="/blog/security/why-and-how-apis-web-applications-are-under-siege" target="_self">distributed denial-of-service (DDoS) attack</a> events in an 18-month period. Yet, there are <a href="/resources/product-brief/edge-dns" target="_self">solutions</a> that can eliminate many of those attacks. Many of these attacks are reflector-based, with threat actors taking advantage of improperly configured servers. Simple DNS changes can prevent this and ensure that organizations don't inadvertently become part of the problem.</p>
<p>We saw a resurgence of the <a href="/blog/security-research/2024-corona-mirai-botnet-infects-zero-day-sirt" target="_self">Mirai</a> botnet, which has been responsible for some of the world's largest and most complicated attacks. It's one of the most interesting attack tools; it’s highly intelligent in how it takes over endpoints and locks out other actors. Mirai was quite prominent five years ago, then it disappeared — but now it's back with a vengeance.</p>
<p>This development underscores the need for organizations to focus not just on the size of attacks but also on their level of sophistication. Attack campaigns are becoming much more complex. Many security vendors are building in more intelligent automation to speed up response times, which is an appropriate move given the landscape. </p>
<p>Additionally, <b>I firmly believe that access to security experts remains crucial</b>. Sooner or later, every medium or large enterprise will face a cyber event and resolving it will require human expertise.</p>
<p>On the applications side, bot <a href="/solutions/security/bot-and-abuse-protection" target="_self">credential abuse</a> continued to emerge as a major threat. These attacks, many committed by nation-state actors, are extremely challenging to mitigate. Determining whether an attacker is a bot or a human, or distinguishing between <a href="/resources/state-of-the-internet/web-scraping-report-2024" target="_self">&quot;good&quot; and &quot;bad&quot; bots</a> (Figure 1), requires experienced human experts to understand the unique customer environment and implement the appropriate protection measures.</p>

    <p>The main trend in 2024 was the increase in hacktivism and DDoS attacks in the Europe, Middle East, and Africa (EMEA) region. In Q3, we saw a massive ramp-up of attacks that targeted Europe and the Middle East. This is clearly associated with the two regional wars that are driving much of this activity, and there is no sign that these attacks will let up.</p>
<p>Another notable development was the implementation of the <a href="/glossary/what-is-nis2" target="_self">NIS2 Directive</a>, which is focused on operational resilience. It was designed to be written into legislation by each European Union (EU) member state. The EU member states had until October 17, 2024, to transpose NIS2 into national law. Organizations in Europe need to ensure that their security infrastructures comply with these new and updated requirements that center on mitigating risk and improving resilience in the event of attack.</p>
<p>A particularly interesting event was the <a href="/blog/security-research/critical-linux-backdoor-xz-utils-discovered-what-to-know" target="_self">XZ Utils backdoor</a> (CVE-2024-3094) that exploited a vulnerability in this widely used open source library. <b>This attacker played the long game by contributing to the XZ project over the course of nearly two years to build credibility until they were able to embed malware into the open source environment</b>. This long play had echoes of the <a href="https://www.cisa.gov/news-events/alerts/2024/02/07/cisa-and-partners-release-advisory-prc-sponsored-volt-typhoon-activity-and-supplemental-living-land" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noreferrer">Volt Typhoon</a> campaign, which infiltrated DSL routers in the United States with the goal of disrupting communications.</p>
<p>Perhaps the most interesting development in DDoS attacks was a <a href="/blog/security-research/october-cups-ddos-threat" target="_self">vulnerability that the Akamai SIRT discovered </a>in the Common UNIX Printing System (CUPS), an open source printing platform. This was yet another vector that attackers could add to their arsenal to strike Europe. The capability to hit once but then create an endless stream of traffic was particularly interesting, especially given that there are thousands of vulnerable devices on the internet that use CUPS.</p>

    <p>With two regional wars still raging, I don’t believe the hacktivism threat will diminish any time soon. Indeed, Akamai continues to work with the government of Ukraine to protect their key online assets. <p>In 2025, I think we will see more focus on being cryptographically agile. That’s not going to happen tomorrow, but I think people will need to start thinking about the risk and how to address it. It’s a generational shift.</p>

    <p>As in other global regions, organizations in APJ will be adopting AI to benefit their businesses and profitability at a much more frantic pace across every industry in 2025. However, in their rush to embrace AI, it’s important that they not lose sight of essential security safeguards. We saw a similar situation more than a decade ago, during the rush to adopt cloud computing and cybersecurity. Security was often overshadowed by speed to adoption, which inevitably led to several high profile data breaches.</p>
<p>Currently, I think we are just scratching at the surface of what is possible with AI, and organizations may not fully understand the underlying potential security implications. As AI becomes more deeply embedded in business operations, efforts to secure AI will need to be at the forefront of organizations’ strategies. Unfortunately, if past activity is any indication, the likelihood of this happening is relatively low — until there is a major data breach that suddenly jolts organizations and regulators into focusing on related security frameworks and regulations.</p>

AI will lower the barriers to entry for attackers, accelerating their ability to identify and exploit vulnerabilities. At Akamai, we are already a few steps ahead by building our own AI models, exploring how to attack them, and determining what protections are needed to enhance our security solutions — which may include various implementations of AI-augmented capabilities, as well. This is an area in which we will continue to advance our capabilities in 2025 and beyond.&lt;/p>\r\n&lt;p>Finally, while people are distracted by the latest shiny new thing that is AI, it is important not to lose sight of having solid security fundamentals.&amp;nbsp;&lt;b>Attackers will still come after API endpoints, and they will still conduct phishing attacks in 2025&lt;/b>. We still need to ensure that patches are deployed in a timely manner, our safeguards are always on, and that people are continuously trained to identify and mitigate malicious activity. Because with or without AI, those threats are not going away.&lt;/p>\r\n&#34;}}">
    <p>Starting in 2025, I think we will see an increasing focus on two aspects of AI security: protecting AI systems and defending against AI-driven attacks. After all, cybercriminals are learning how to leverage AI to make their attacks more evasive, more efficient, and more effective. AI will lower the barriers to entry for attackers, accelerating their ability to identify and exploit vulnerabilities. At Akamai, we are already a few steps ahead by building our own AI models, exploring how to attack them, and determining what protections are needed to enhance our security solutions — which may include various implementations of AI-augmented capabilities, as well. This is an area in which we will continue to advance our capabilities in 2025 and beyond.</p>
<p>Finally, while people are distracted by the latest shiny new thing that is AI, it is important not to lose sight of having solid security fundamentals. <b>Attackers will still come after API endpoints, and they will still conduct phishing attacks in 2025</b>. We still need to ensure that patches are deployed in a timely manner, our safeguards are always on, and that people are continuously trained to identify and mitigate malicious activity. Because with or without AI, those threats are not going away.</p>

    <p>What stood out to me this year was how much the attack surface has grown — not only from implementing new technology but also <b>because the technology itself creates new threat actors</b>. I’m not talking about ChatGPT writing malware, that’s a whole OTHER issue. The <a href="https://www.brighttalk.com/webcast/13169/623644?channel_id=13169" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noreferrer">technical barrier to entry</a> for attackers has been lowered significantly, making the gap between defenders and attackers even more disproportionate as a result — and with generative AI and LLMs becoming the norm, that gap could effectively become a big hole.</p>
<p>Technology is advancing at speeds that were previously unthinkable: We’re not just fighting the nation-state level of sophistication, we’re also fighting any <a href="/blog/security-research/dark-frost-botnet-unexpected-author-profile" target="_self">skiddie</a> with an ax to grind. The attacker’s time to market has shrunk to mere <a href="/blog/security-research/2024-php-exploit-cve-one-day-after-disclosure" target="_self">hours</a> after disclosure, some of whom have <a href="/blog/security-research/october-cups-ddos-threat" target="_self">multiple</a> exploit options. Defenders are getting bombarded, and every year it seems to get worse. </p>

</div>

    

</div>

A significant amount of the malware we saw this year took advantage of old vulnerabilities like&amp;nbsp;&lt;a href=\&#34;/content/akamai/en/blog/security-research/fritzfrog-botnet-new-capabilities-log4shell.html\&#34; target=\&#34;_self\&#34;>Log4Shell&lt;/a>, some more than a decade old, and even some&amp;nbsp;&lt;a href=\&#34;/content/akamai/en/blog/security-research/2024-august-kubernetes-gitsync-command-injection-defcon.html\&#34; target=\&#34;_self\&#34;>without proper CVE assignment&lt;/a>. A seasoned botnet author can expand their toolkit to include&amp;nbsp;&lt;a href=\&#34;/content/akamai/en/blog/security-research/updated-kmsdbot-binary-targeting-iot.html\&#34; target=\&#34;_self\&#34;>Golang-based&lt;/a>&amp;nbsp;malware as it’s harder to obfuscate, and any person with access to a credit card can run a full-scale ransomware or DDoS attack as easily as buying a pair of shoes online.&lt;/p>\r\n&#34;}}">
    <p><b>New technology has also offered new attack paths out of old threats</b>. Threats that you previously mitigated can now have an entirely new entry point, or a vector could do more damage <a href="/blog/security-research/2024-august-vpn-post-exploitation-techniques-black-hat" target="_self">post-exploit</a>. A significant amount of the malware we saw this year took advantage of old vulnerabilities like <a href="/blog/security-research/fritzfrog-botnet-new-capabilities-log4shell" target="_self">Log4Shell</a>, some more than a decade old, and even some <a href="/blog/security-research/2024-august-kubernetes-gitsync-command-injection-defcon" target="_self">without proper CVE assignment</a>. A seasoned botnet author can expand their toolkit to include <a href="/blog/security-research/updated-kmsdbot-binary-targeting-iot" target="_self">Golang-based</a> malware as it’s harder to obfuscate, and any person with access to a credit card can run a full-scale ransomware or DDoS attack as easily as buying a pair of shoes online.</p>
