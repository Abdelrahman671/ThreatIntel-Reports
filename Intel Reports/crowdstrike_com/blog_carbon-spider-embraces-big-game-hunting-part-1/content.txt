<div class="cmp-wp-headline" id="headline-en-us-04-19-2024">
    
        <h1>CARBON SPIDER Embraces Big Game Hunting, Part 1</h1>
        
        <div class="publish_info">
            <p>August 30, 2021</p>
            <span>|</span>
            <a href="/en-us/blog/author.eric-loui---josh-reynolds/" title="Posts by Eric Loui - Josh Reynolds" rel="author">
                Eric Loui - Josh Reynolds</a>
            <span>|</span>
                
            <a href="/en-us/blog/category.counter-adversary-operations/" title="Counter Adversary Operations">Counter Adversary Operations</a>
        </div>
        <div class="post_image">
            <img width="1060" height="698" src="https://assets.crowdstrike.com/is/image/crowdstrikeinc/Blog_1060x698-10-us" class="attachment-post-thumbnail size-post-thumbnail wp-post-image" alt="" decoding="async" fetchpriority="high" srcset="/content/dam/crowdstrike/www/en-us/wp/2024/12/Blog_1060x698-10-us.jpeg 1060w" sizes="(max-width: 1060px) 100vw, 1060px"/>
        </div>
    

    
</div>

    <div style="display: none;">
        
    </div>


    
</div>
<div class="text text--blog-content aem-GridColumn aem-GridColumn--default--12">

  

<div class="cmp-text" id="text-1138b49fb3" data-cmp-name="cmp-text-" data-cmp-is="text" data-target-location="false">
    
       <span><p><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Throughout 2020, </span><a href="https://adversary.crowdstrike.com/en-US/adversary/carbon-spider.html"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">CARBON SPIDER</span></a><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> dramatically overhauled their operations. In April 2020, the adversary abruptly shifted from narrow campaigns focused entirely on companies operating point-of-sale (POS) devices to broad, indiscriminate operations that attempted to infect very many victims across all sectors. The goal of these campaigns was to conduct <a href="https://www.crowdstrike.com/cybersecurity-101/cyber-big-game-hunting/">big game hunting (BGH)</a> operations using PINCHY SPIDER’s </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">REvil</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> ransomware.</span></p>
<p> </p>
<p><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">CARBON SPIDER deepened their commitment to BGH in August 2020 by using their own ransomware, </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Darkside</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">. In November 2020, the adversary took another step into the world of BGH by establishing a </span><a href="/en-us/cybersecurity-101/ransomware/ransomware-as-a-service-raas/"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">ransomware-as-a-service (RaaS)</span></a><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> affiliate program for </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Darkside</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">, allowing other actors to use the ransomware while paying CARBON SPIDER a portion of the ransom received.<p> </p>
</span> <span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Part One of this two-part blog series details how CrowdStrike Intelligence attributed </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Darkside</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> to CARBON SPIDER. Part Two will look at CARBON SPIDER’s re-emergence after the Colonial Pipeline attack, which led to the shutdown of </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Darkside</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> RaaS and the creation of </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">BlackMatter</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> RaaS.<p> </p>
</span></p>
<p> </p>
<h2>Background</h2>
<p><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">CARBON SPIDER, commonly referred to as FIN7 and active since 2013, is one of the oldest continuously operating eCrime groups. Between 2015 and 2020, the adversary conducted low-volume campaigns targeting company POS devices, primarily in the hospitality sector. These campaigns featured a variety of malware, including the </span><a href="/en-us/blog/arrests-put-new-focus-on-carbon-spider-adversary-group/"><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Sekur</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> (</span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Carbanak</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">) RAT, </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">VB Flash</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">, </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Bateleur</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> and </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Harpy</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> (</span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">GRIFFON</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">)</span></a><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">. Using POS malware, including </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">PILLOWMINT</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">, the adversary harvested credit card track data and sold this data on criminal forums such as Joker’s Stash.</span></p>
<h2>April 2020: Target Scope Widens</h2>
<p><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">In April 2020, CARBON SPIDER abruptly shifted from narrow campaigns focused entirely on companies operating POS devices to broad, indiscriminate operations that attempted to infect large numbers of victims across all sectors. The first of these occurred on April 14, 2020, when the adversary likely compromised a legitimate email distribution service to conduct a broad spam campaign targeting thousands of recipients across numerous verticals.</span></p>
<p> </p>
<p><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">This campaign used malicious links that led to a ZIP archive hosted on <code>https&lt;:&gt;//colahasch&lt;.&gt;com/portal/app/CommerceNetwork/ view/9b25068f2941618fb9b08d6d089a47faae552c93f</code></span> <span style="	font-weight: 400;
">The ZIP archive contained a </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Leo VBS</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">, which refers to a family of obfuscated scripts that download and execute a remote payload.</span> <span style="	font-weight: 400;
">The </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Leo VBS</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> performs an HTTP GET request to <code>https&lt;:&gt;//alphalanding&lt;.&gt;com/successfully/warranty.eml?uid&#61;</code> and writes a </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">JSS Loader</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> binary to <code>%TEMP%\PaintHelper.exe</code>. </span><i>JSS Loader</i>, which has both .NET and C&#43;&#43; versions, has multiple capabilities, including the ability to load additional executables, PowerShell (PS) and JavaScript (JS) files. <span style="	font-weight: 400;
">The observed JSS Loader infection led to the download and execution of a setup VBScript from <code>https&lt;:&gt;//petshopbook&lt;.&gt;com</code>. This script installs a custom </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Sekur</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> PS stager to <code>%LOCALAPPDATA%\Microsoft\WindowsDefender\ClearTemp.ps1</code> and establishes persistence for this stager with a second VBS that is launched by a scheduled task.</span> <span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Since this campaign, CARBON SPIDER has maintained an opportunistic target scope, using phishing attachments and links to deliver </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Harpy, Leo VBS, JSS Loader, Domenus VBS</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> and </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Domenus JS</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">. </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Domenus VBS</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> and </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">JS</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> are backdoors (written in VBS and JS, respectively) that enumerate a variety of system information, capture screenshots and browser history, and can download secondary payloads from a command-and-control (C2) server. Secondary payloads can include JS, Portable Executables (PEs), DLLs and PS scripts.<p> </p>
</span> <span style="	font-weight: 400;
">CrowdStrike Intelligence observed numerous </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Domenus VBS/JS</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> phishing campaigns that made use of compromised and legitimate services to host </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Domenus</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> toolchain payloads, including compromised WordPress installations, compromised SharePoint services, compromised web servers and Google Docs.</span></p>
<p> </p>
<h2>REvil Ransomware Campaigns</h2>
<p><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">On April 28, 2020, CrowdStrike Intelligence observed a </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Domenus VBS</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> distribution campaign that used a spear-phishing email containing a Google Docs link. The resulting Google Docs page contained a second link that, when clicked, directed the user to <code>https&lt;:&gt;//chauvinistable&lt;.&gt;com/perfsecure</code>, ultimately redirecting to a compromised SharePoint site. Here, the victim encountered a ZIP file containing a </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Domenus VBS</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> file that, once opened, downloaded and executed </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Harpy</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> from <code>https&lt;:&gt;//electroncador&lt;.&gt;com/info</code>.</span></p>
<p> </p>
<p><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">The redirect URL provided from the Google Docs page <code>https&lt;:&gt;//chauvinistable&lt;.&gt;com/perfsecure</code> was hosted on the same IP address (<code>185.163.45&lt;.&gt;249</code>) resolving to a domain used by multiple </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Cobalt Strike</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> samples sharing key configuration metadata with </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Cobalt Strike</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> samples used in several </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">REvil</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> incidents. These </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Cobalt Strike</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> samples were also observed in tandem with the custom PowerShell stager for </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Sekur</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">. Separate reporting by Symantec further indicated that similar </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Cobalt Strike</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> samples were used in campaigns delivering </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">REvil</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">. Based on these multiple overlaps, CrowdStrike Intelligence assesses with moderate confidence that CARBON SPIDER was responsible for certain </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">REvil</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> campaigns, likely stemming from </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">JSS Loader</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> or </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Domenus VBS/JS</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> infections.</span></p>
<p> </p>
<h2>Darkside Ransomware Campaigns &#43; RaaS</h2>
<p><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">On July 1, 2020, CARBON SPIDER sent a phishing email with the subject “Notification: Package Status Fail.” The email purports to be from a customer who received an email from the U.S.-based delivery company UPS (Figures 1 and 2). The message body attempts to impersonate a UPS notification, but contains several grammar errors and non-idiomatic terms (e.g., “waybill”).</span></p>
<div class="wp-caption" id="attachment_44782"><img class="size-full wp-image-44782" src="/content/dam/crowdstrike/www/en-us/wp/2021/08/Picture1-8.png" alt="" width="977" height="202"/>Figure 1. CARBON SPIDER phishing email</div>
<div class="wp-caption" id="attachment_44783"><img class="size-full wp-image-44783" src="/content/dam/crowdstrike/www/en-us/wp/2021/08/Picture2-3.png" alt="" width="977" height="874"/>Figure 2. CARBON SPIDER phishing email (continued)</div>
<p><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">The link “Check” led to a Google Docs page, which contained a link that redirected to a ZIP file. The ZIP file was hosted on a likely compromised SharePoint account and contained </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Domenus VBS</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">, which downloads </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Harpy</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> from <code>https&lt;:&gt;//fashionableeder&lt;.&gt;com/info</code>. At one victim, CARBON SPIDER subsequently deployed the aforementioned custom PS </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Sekur</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> stager and profiled the Active Directory environment using the utility </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">ADFind</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">.</span></p>
<p> </p>
<p><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">In this incident, CARBON SPIDER also used the </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">KillACK</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> PS backdoor, executing the malware using both PowerShell and PowerShell ISE. </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">KillACK</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> sends host information to a C2 server (in this case, <code>againcome&lt;.&gt;com</code> or <code>besaintegration&lt;.&gt;com</code>) and executes provided PS script blocks. Multiple </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">KillACK</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> modules have been observed by CrowdStrike Intelligence, including modules for conducting self-propagation and AMSI hot-patching, as well as for executing </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Cobalt Strike</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> stagers and enumerating network information.</span> <span style="	font-weight: 400;
">On Aug. 9, 2020, CARBON SPIDER attempted to run the </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Darkside</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> ransomware with the filename <code>sleep.exe</code>. This filename may reflect an attempt to masquerade as the legitimate Windows executable with the same name.</span> <span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Following this incident, CrowdStrike Intelligence identified numerous similar </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Darkside</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> campaigns featuring distinctive CARBON SPIDER tooling, including </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Harpy, Domenus</span> <span style="	font-weight: 400;
">VBS/JS, KillACK</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> and </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Sekur</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">. The adversary also used the commodity </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Cobalt Strike</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> framework and </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Plink</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> tunneling tool in many of these campaigns. After achieving initial access, the adversary consistently seeks to harvest valid administrative credentials to enable lateral movement and uses a variety of tools and techniques for this purpose, including CrackMapExec, Kerberoasting, Mimikatz, PowerSploit and SessionGopher. In one incident, the adversary likely exploited the </span><a href="/en-us/blog/new-zerologon-dashboard-offers-greater-visibility-into-cve-2020-1472/"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">ZeroLogon vulnerability (CVE-2020-1472)</span></a><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> against a domain controller.</span> <span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Using valid credentials, CARBON SPIDER moves laterally through victim environments using RDP and occasionally SSH. The adversary typically uses PS to run </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Cobalt Strike</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> but occasionally writes </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Cobalt Strike</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> stagers or </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">KillACK</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> backdoors to disk. Occasionally, CARBON SPIDER has deployed the legitimate </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">GoToAssist</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> or </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">TightVNC</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> tools to provide redundant control of hosts.</span> <span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Similar to many other ransomware operators, CARBON SPIDER not only encrypted victim files using </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Darkside</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">, but also exfiltrated data for publication on a dedicated leak site (DLS) hosted on Tor. For exfiltration, CARBON SPIDER primarily leveraged the </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">MEGASync</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> client for hosting provider MEGA but also employed </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">GoToAssist</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">. Further, CARBON SPIDER frequently conducted </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">hypervisor jackpotting</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> by </span><a href="/en-us/blog/carbon-spider-sprite-spider-target-esxi-servers-with-ransomware/"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">encrypting ESXi servers using a version of </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Darkside</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> specifically designed for ESXi</span></a><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">.</span> <span style="	font-weight: 400;
">On Nov. 10, 2020, CARBON SPIDER announced the establishment of the </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Darkside </span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">RaaS affiliate program. The announcement, posted on two major Russian-language forums, states that the operators of </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Darkside</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> are looking for Russian-speaking affiliates who understand how to recognize and delete backups. On Nov. 11, 2020, CARBON SPIDER added a new message to their DLS concerning the new affiliate program. This announcement claims “we created the perfect product for ourselves,” indicating that </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Darkside</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> was originally exclusive to one group and not shared.</span> <span style="	font-weight: 400;
">CrowdStrike Intelligence assesses that </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Darkside</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> ransomware campaigns prior to this announcement were likely conducted by CARBON SPIDER, and that CARBON SPIDER was responsible for creating </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Darkside</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> and introducing the RaaS affiliate program. This assessment carries moderate confidence, based on:</span></p>
<p> </p>
<ul><li style="	font-weight: 400;
"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Multiple separate </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Darkside</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> incidents attributable to CARBON SPIDER</span></li><li style="	font-weight: 400;
"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Low overall volume of </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Darkside</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> campaigns</span></li><li style="	font-weight: 400;
"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">The Nov. 11, 2020 announcement described above indicating </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Darkside</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> was initially exclusive to one group</span></li><li style="	font-weight: 400;
"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">The Oct. 10, 2020 press release indicating </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Darkside</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> is operated by a single group</span></li></ul>
<p><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Subsequent to the creation of the </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Darkside</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> RaaS program, CrowdStrike Intelligence continued to observe some </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Darkside</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> campaigns almost certainly conducted by CARBON SPIDER — in addition to other campaigns operated by affiliates — featuring divergent tooling and TTPs. CARBON SPIDER’s campaigns featured the malware discussed above, in addition to heavy use of the </span><i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">Cobalt Strike</span></i><span style="	font-weight: 400;
"> post-exploitation framework.</span></p>
<h2>Conclusion</h2>
<p><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">CARBON SPIDER’s shift from POS malware to BGH ransomware attacks exemplifies a broader trend in the eCrime landscape. Numerous adversaries that previously relied on banking trojans (e.g., </span><a href="/en-us/blog/hades-ransomware-successor-to-indrik-spiders-wastedlocker/"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">INDRIK SPIDER</span></a><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">) or POS compromises (e.g., GRACEFUL SPIDER) have almost entirely reinvented themselves to focus on </span><a href="/en-us/cybersecurity-101/ransomware/"><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">ransomware</span></a><span style="	font-weight: 400;
">, reflecting how lucrative BGH campaigns are. Until the economics of cybercrime fundamentally change, it is unlikely these adversaries will alter their behavior.</span></p>

<h2>Explanation of Confidence Rating</h2>

This level of confidence is used to express that judgments carry an increased probability of being incorrect until more information is available or corroborated.</li><li><b>Low Confidence:</b><p> </p>
