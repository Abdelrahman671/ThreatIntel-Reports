<ul class="wp-block-list">

<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="Executive-Summary">Executive Summary</h2>



<p><strong>FIN7</strong> (also known as Sangria Tempest, ATK32, Carbon Spider, Coreid, ELBRUS, G0008, G0046, and GOLD NIAGARA) are a financially-motivated threat group <a href="https://blogs.blackberry.com/en/2024/04/fin7-targets-the-united-states-automotive-industry">with links to Russia</a>, that has been operating <a href="https://www.hhs.gov/sites/default/files/black-basta-threat-profile.pdf">since at least 2013</a>, who were previously thought to have been <a href="https://www.justice.gov/usao-wdwa/pr/team-western-washington-honored-investigation-and-prosecution-major-cybercrime-group">eliminated by the DOJ</a>.</p>



<p>FIN7 primarily targets US-based retail, hospitality, tech, consulting, financial services, medical equipment, media, transportation, and utilities industries using:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li><a href="https://attack.mitre.org/techniques/T1566/">T1566</a> &#8211; Spearphishing (for credentials and credit card information)</li>



<li><a href="https://attack.mitre.org/techniques/T1486/">T1486</a> &#8211; Data Encrypted for Impact (Ransomware)</li>



<li><a href="https://attack.mitre.org/techniques/T1176/">T1176</a> &#8211; Malicious Browser Extensions</li>



<li><a href="https://attack.mitre.org/techniques/T1056/003/">T1056.003</a> &#8211; Web Portal Capture</li>



<li><a href="https://attack.mitre.org/techniques/T1189/">T1189</a> &#8211; Drive-by Compromise</li>



<li><a href="https://attack.mitre.org/techniques/T1665/">T1665</a> &#8211; Hide Infrastructure</li>



<li><a href="https://attack.mitre.org/techniques/T1583/008/">T1583.008</a> &#8211; Malvertising</li>
</ul>



<p><em><strong>Our research proves the group has either resurfaced, or threat actors are repurposing FIN7 TTPs and infrastructure to propagate a fresh set of campaigns utilizing over 4000 domains and subdomains, with nearly half active in the last week.</strong></em></p>



<p>From a single origin point, Silent Push Threat Analysts have uncovered an extensive series of FIN7 campaigns, including several hundred active <strong>phishing</strong>, <strong>spoofing</strong>, <strong>shell</strong> and <strong>malware delivery</strong> domains and IPs targeting the following organizations: Louvre Museum, Meta, Reuters (and WestLaw), Microsoft 365, Wall Street Journal, Midjourney, CNN, Quickbooks, Alliant, Grammarly, Airtable, Webex, Lexis Nexis, Bloomberg, Quicken, Cisco (Webex), Zoom, Investing[.]com, SAP Concur, Google, Android Developer, Asana, Workable, SAP (Ariba), Microsoft (Sharepoint), RedFin, Manulife Insurance, Regions Bank Onepass, American Express, Twitter, Costco, DropBox, Netflix, Paycor, Harvard, Affinity Energy, RuPay, Goto[.]com, Bitwarden, and Trezor.</p>



<p>Software being targeted includes 7-zip, PuTTY, ProtectedPDFViewer, AIMP, Notepad++, Advanced IP Scanner, AnyDesk, pgAdmin, AutoDesk, Bitwarden, Rest Proxy, Python, Sublime Text, and Node.js</p>



<p>Silent Push Threat Analysts have also identified an active cybersecurity shell company &#8211; <strong>cybercloudsec[.]com</strong> &#8211; which is being used to facilitate FIN7 activity, in line with previous attack vectors.</p>



<p><em><strong>Silent Push Enterprise users have access to a dedicated&nbsp;</strong></em><a href="https://help.silentpush.com/docs/tlp-amber-reports"><em><strong>TLP Amber report,</strong></em></a><em><strong>&nbsp;containing the specific data categories, content scans and advanced DNS queries we used as well as four dedicated FIN7 </strong></em><a href="https://help.silentpush.com/docs/accessing-iofa-feeds"><em><strong>IOFA Feeds</strong></em></a><em><strong>.</strong></em></p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-background">Background</h2>



<p>FIN7 are sometimes associated with the <a href="https://attack.mitre.org/groups/G0008/">Carbanak</a> threat group (as distinct from the <a href="https://malpedia.caad.fkie.fraunhofer.de/details/win.carbanak">Carbanak malware</a> &#8211; a backdoor attack vector FIN7 have been known to use), however <a href="https://www.trendmicro.com/es_es/research/21/d/carbanak-and-fin7-attack-techniques.html">both are tracked differently</a>.</p>



<p>Numerous <a href="https://socprime.com/blog/fin7-attack-detection-russia-linked-financially-motivated-group-exploits-google-ads-to-drop-netsupport-rat-via-msix-app-installer-files/">researchers</a> and <a href="https://cloud.google.com/blog/topics/threat-intelligence/fin7-pursuing-an-enigmatic-and-evasive-global-criminal-operation">threat intelligence teams</a> have attributed FIN7 activity to Russia-linked threat actors. Prominent members of the group <a href="https://therecord.media/researchers-tie-fin7-cybercrime-family-to-clop-ransomware-microsoft">have been convicted in Russian courts</a>, or have <a href="https://www.justice.gov/usao-wdwa/pr/team-western-washington-honored-investigation-and-prosecution-major-cybercrime-group">strong ties to the region</a>.</p>



<p>In April 2021, Acting U.S. Attorney Gorman May 2023 said: “This criminal organization had <a href="https://www.justice.gov/usao-wdwa/pr/high-level-organizer-notorious-hacking-group-fin7-sentenced-ten-years-prison-scheme">more than 70 people organized into business units and teams</a>. Some were hackers, others developed the malware installed on computers, and still others crafted the malicious emails that duped victims into infecting their company systems”.</p>



<p>In May 2023, U.S. Attorney Nick Brown announced that FIN7 &#8220;<a href="https://www.justice.gov/usao-wdwa/pr/team-western-washington-honored-investigation-and-prosecution-major-cybercrime-group">as an entity is no more</a>&#8220;, after three prominent members of the group were <a href="https://www.justice.gov/usao-wdwa/united-states-vs-fedir-oleksiyovych-hladyr-united-states-vs-dmytro-valerievich-fedorov">convicted in a federal court</a> for Conspiracy to Commit Wire Fraud and Conspiracy to Commit Computer Hacking.</p>



<p>Less than a month later, Microsoft Threat Intelligence (tracking the group as &#8220;Sangria Tempest&#8221;) <a href="https://x.com/MsftSecIntel/status/1659347799442432002">observed the group</a> using OpenSSH and Impacket to move laterally and deploy Clop ransomware.</p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="Initial-discovery">Initial discovery</h2>



<p>A few weeks ago, we discovered a suspect domain that our Analysts recognized as bearing all the hallmarks of a FIN7 domain.</p>



<p>After confirming our findings by corroborating the domain with well-publicized FIN7 TTPs, we used the Silent Push platform to construct granular queries that first identified linked domains across common hosts.</p>



<p>We then confirmed true positive phishing and malware infrastructure using sandboxed browser testing and executable analysis.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="Corporate-fronts-used-for-FIN7-spearphishing">Corporate fronts used for FIN7 spearphishing</h2>



<p>FIN7 infrastructure is known to contain corporate &#8220;shell&#8221; domains masquerading as legitimate businesses online, to deploy spearphishing campaigns that inject <a href="https://cloud.google.com/blog/topics/threat-intelligence/fin7-spear-phishing-campaign-targets-personnel-involved-sec-filings">ransomware and other forms of malware</a>.</p>



<p>Previous attacks have involved FIN7 initiating contact with potential victims, before using shell domains to operate under the guise of legitimacy, when propagating attacks.</p>



<p><em><strong><em><strong>Our dedicated feeds contain over 4000 shell domains and subdomains, which is a conglomeration of everything we’ve discovered, including hundreds of websites propagating spear-phishing activity across a broad variety of industries and sectors.</strong></em></strong></em></p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="Fake-cybersecurity-company-(cybercloudsec[.]com)">Fake cybersecurity company (<strong>cybercloudsec[.]com</strong>)</h3>



<p>In 2021, three members of FIN7 were <a href="https://www.justice.gov/usao-wdwa/pr/team-western-washington-honored-investigation-and-prosecution-major-cybercrime-group">sentenced to between 5-10 years</a> each in a federal prison for their role in a multi-million dollar hacking conspiracy.</p>



<p>The <a href="https://www.justice.gov/usao-wdwa/united-states-vs-fedir-oleksiyovych-hladyr-united-states-vs-dmytro-valerievich-fedorov">indictment</a> describes the use of a fake cybersecurity company &#8211; <strong>Combi Security</strong> &#8211; as a front to recruit other threat actors, and provide an air of legitimacy to an operation that involved uploading malware onto POS terminals, credit card fraud, spearphishing, and spoofing.</p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="FIN7-shell-domains-morphing-into-phishing-websites">FIN7 shell domains morphing into phishing websites</h2>



<p>A common FIN7 TTP is to take what were formerly shell domains, and morph them into conventional spoofing websites (via redirects or on-page content) targeting users of well-known brands with phishing and malware delivery.</p>



<p>From our analysis, content is served based on a range of user-specific parameters. Domains may or may not populate based on geographic region, IP address, local time, type of connection and browser settings (e.g. JavaScript being enabled).</p>

<p>At one point in its life, the domain presented as a <strong>shell website</strong>, however when accessed live with a different set of user parameters, it returned a <strong>phishing page</strong> targeting Alliant Credit Union.</p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-rented-fin7-infrastructure-stark-industries">Rented FIN7 infrastructure (Stark Industries)</h2>



<p>FIN7 rents a large amount of dedicated IPs on a number of hosts, but primarily on <a href="https://stark-industries.solutions/">Stark Industries</a>, historically considered a bulletproof hosting provider, &#8220;dedicated IPs&#8221; here meaning those IP ranges with verified FIN7 domains and less than 200 subdomains/domains mapped to a given IP.</p>



<p>Our threat analysts have discovered numerous Stark Industries IPs that are <strong>solely dedicated to hosting FIN7 infrastructure</strong>.</p>



<p>Here&#8217;s a Silent Push Explore DNS query we ran on one such domain &#8211; <strong>103.35.191[.]28</strong>. All of the domains hosted on this IP share the common string &#8220;meet-go&#8221;, and are verified FIN7 domains with different TLDs.</p>

<p>Other dedicated hosts, not on Stark Industries, also feature a mix of FIN7 domains targeting more than one brand, across numerous industries.</p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-fin7-malware-infrastructure">FIN7 malware infrastructure</h2>



<p>Public research from organizations like <a href="https://www.esentire.com/blog/fin7-uses-trusted-brands-and-sponsored-google-ads-to-distribute-msix-payloads">Esentire</a>, <a href="https://redcanary.com/blog/threat-intelligence/msix-installers/">RedCanary</a>, and <a href="https://blogs.blackberry.com/en/2024/04/fin7-targets-the-united-states-automotive-industry">Blackberry </a>about FIN7 attack vectors includes a specific template the threat actor uses for distributing MSIX malware via Google ads and possibly other channels, describe MSIX installers linked to FIN7, and reference the FIN7 “powertrash” (a part of the MSIX payload) process each have seen.</p>



<p>Most of these web pages feature a pop-up with the phrase “Requires Browser Extension!”.</p>



<p>Here&#8217;s an example targeting Reuters News Agency on <strong>thomsonreuter[.]info</strong> (we&#8217;ve also discovered Reuters phishing infrastructure on <strong>thomsonreuter[.]pro</strong> and <strong>westlaw[.]top</strong>, targeting the Reuters <strong>Westlaw</strong> product):</p>


<div class="wp-block-image">
<figure class="aligncenter size-full"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" width="2048" height="1188" src="https://www.silentpush.com/wp-content/uploads/image-66.png" alt="FIN7 fake browser extension malware" class="wp-image-6402"/></figure></div>


<p>In <a href="https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/security/blog/2023/12/28/financially-motivated-threat-actors-misusing-app-installer/">December of 2023</a>, Microsoft also observed FIN7 using MSIX malware and a Windows Trojan, EugenLoader, to inject Carbanak &#8211; a piece of software that the group has used for a decade to deliver the Gracewire implant.</p>



<p>Our Analysts have discovered a pool of FIN7 domains which appear to be mostly used for delivering malware – these include the “Requires Browser Extension” page highlighted above.</p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-fin7-malware-payload-analysis">FIN7 malware payload analysis</h2>



<p>Let&#8217;s take a closer look at a piece of FIN7 malware. For our analysis, we&#8217;re using <strong>LexisNexis.msix </strong></p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Type: Zip archive file</li>



<li>MD5: ff25441b7631d64afefdb818cfcceec7</li>



<li>Compression: Deflate</li>
</ul>



<p>To masquerade as a trusted executable, the malware has appropriated certificate data from what appears to be a Chinese manufacturing company, <strong>Cangzhou Chenyue Electronic Technology</strong>:</p>



<pre class="wp-block-code"><code>&lt;Identity Name="LexisNexis" Publisher="CN="Cangzhou Chenyue Electronic Technology Co., Ltd.", O="Cangzhou Chenyue Electronic Technology Co., Ltd.", L=Cangzhou, S=Hebei, C=CN, SERIALNUMBER=91130922MA0G8AN920, OID.1.3.6.1.4.1.311.60.2.1.1=Cangzhou, OID.1.3.6.1.4.1.311.60.2.1.2=Hebei, OID.1.3.6.1.4.1.311.60.2.1.3=CN, OID.2.5.4.15=Private Organization" Version="4.12.98.0" /&gt;</code></pre>



<p>The malware has the following embedded configuration:</p>



<pre class="wp-block-code"><code>{

      "id": "NOTEPAD",

<pre class="wp-block-code"><code>$url = "https://www.lexisnexis.com/"

<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="Delivery-chain">Delivery chain</h3>



<p>Analysing the attack chain, it&#8217;s clear that the malware is designed to target domain-joined machines, and all the corporate data they have to offer.</p>



<p><em><strong>The malware seeks to obtain eleveated privileges, including lateral movement and access to Active Directory.</strong></em></p>



<ol class="wp-block-list" start="1">
<li>The attack starts when the script opens the LexisNexis website, either as a distraction or to mimic legitimate user activity.</li>



<li>The malware then checks if the machine is part of a domain, or in a workgroup.</li>



<li>If the machine <strong>isn&#8217;t</strong> in a workgroup, the script extracts two encrypted 7-Zip archives (password: 1234567890) and runs an executable from the extracted data</li>
</ol>
