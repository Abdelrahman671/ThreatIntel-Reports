<h2>Executive Summary</h2>
<p>New Jupyter Infostealer variants continue to evolve with simple yet impactful changes to the techniques used by the malware author. This improvement aims to avoid detection and establishes persistence, enabling the attacker to stealthily compromise victims. The Carbon Black MDR Team has contained countless Jupyter Infostealer infections over the years. This malware continues to be one of the top ten infections we’ve detected in our clients’ network primarily targeting the Education and Health sectors.</p>
<p>The team has discovered new waves of Jupyter Infostealer attacks which leverage PowerShell command modifications and signatures of private keys in attempts to pass off the malware as a legitimately signed file. Over the last two weeks, the number of Jupyter Infostealer infections that we have observed has steadily risen, now totaling 26 infections. Malware researchers such as <a href="https://squiblydoo.blog/2023/05/12/certified-bad/">SquiblydooBlog</a> have also noted the recent changes.</p>
<h2>History</h2>
<p>Jupyter Infostealer (aka Yellow Cockatoo, Solarmarker, Polazert) is a malware variant that was first detected in late 2020. It has continued to evolve, changing its delivery method to evade detection. Targeting Chrome, Edge, and Firefox browsers, Jupyter infections use SEO poisoning and search engine redirects to encourage malicious file downloads that are the initial attack vector in the attack chain. The malware has demonstrated credential harvesting and encrypted command-and-control (C2) communication capabilities used to exfiltrate sensitive data.</p>
<h2>Certificate Manipulation</h2>
<p>These files are signed with a valid certificate to further evade detection. The recent Jupyter infections utilize multiple certificates to sign their malware which, in turn, can allow trust to be granted to the malicious file, providing initial access to the victim&#8217;s machine.<br />
Recent Signers:</p>
<ul>
<li>ТОВ &#8220;Чеб&#8221;</li>
<li>ТОВ &#8220;Софт Енжін юа&#8221;</li>
<li>ТОВ “Трафік Девелоп ЮА”</li>
</ul>

<p>Crafty threat actors are particularly interested in obtaining such certificates; even security analysts may inadvertently place trust in such software due to the semblance of authenticity provided by these certificates.</p>
<h2>Common Delivery Methods</h2>
<p>Jupyter Infostealer, like many other malware, can be delivered through various methods. Common delivery methods include: malicious websites, drive-by downloads, and phishing emails. Users may unknowingly download Jupyter Infostealer when visiting compromised websites or by clicking on malicious ads. The most common applications we see used to download this malware are: Firefox, Chrome, and Edge web browsers.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><a href="https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/11/Fig2.png"><img decoding="async" loading="lazy" class="size-full wp-image-83714 alignnone aligncenter" src="https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/11/Fig2.png" alt="" width="539" height="125" srcset="https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/11/Fig2.png 539w, https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/11/Fig2-300x70.png 300w" sizes="(max-width: 539px) 100vw, 539px" /></a></p>

<p>When a user gets tricked into downloading this Infostealer, the executable can then get invoked by their browser.</p>

<h2>Fake Installer</h2>
<p>The above executables are examples of installation files created by InnoSetup &#8211; an open source compiler used to create installation packages in Windows OS. These new infections typically include this installer-bundle.exe file which retains the same hash although their file names may vary.</p>
<h3>Autodesk</h3>
<p>During our recent investigation, we identified an incident where a signed Autodesk Create Installer was deployed by the installer-bundle.exe. Autodesk, a software frequently exploited in past cyber attacks, was utilized as a Remote Desktop application on the victims’ devices.</p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><a href="https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/11/Fig3.png"><img decoding="async" loading="lazy" class="size-full wp-image-83715 aligncenter" src="https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/11/Fig3.png" alt="" width="494" height="367" srcset="https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/11/Fig3.png 494w, https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/11/Fig3-300x223.png 300w" sizes="(max-width: 494px) 100vw, 494px" /></a><br />
<em>Figure 3: Installer-bundle.exe dropping AutoDeck Create Installer</em></p>
<p>Moments after, <strong>No-Hoa-Letter-Mortgage.tmp</strong> executes <strong>powershell.exe</strong> which then makes a connection to 185[.]243.112.60, a C2 server located in the Netherlands.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><a href="https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/11/Fig4.png"><img decoding="async" loading="lazy" class="size-full wp-image-83716 aligncenter" src="https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/11/Fig4.png" alt="" width="391" height="295" srcset="https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/11/Fig4.png 391w, https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/11/Fig4-300x226.png 300w" sizes="(max-width: 391px) 100vw, 391px" /></a></p>

<p><a href="https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/11/Fig5.png"><img decoding="async" loading="lazy" class="wp-image-83717 size-full aligncenter" src="https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/11/Fig5.png" alt="" width="423" height="412" srcset="https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/11/Fig5.png 423w, https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/11/Fig5-300x292.png 300w" sizes="(max-width: 423px) 100vw, 423px" /></a></p>

<p><a href="https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/11/Fig6.png"><img decoding="async" loading="lazy" class="size-full wp-image-83718 aligncenter" src="https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/11/Fig6.png" alt="" width="936" height="650" srcset="https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/11/Fig6.png 936w, https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/11/Fig6-300x208.png 300w, https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/11/Fig6-768x533.png 768w, https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/11/Fig6-600x417.png 600w" sizes="(max-width: 936px) 100vw, 936px" /></a></p>

<p><a href="https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/11/Fig7.png"><img decoding="async" loading="lazy" class="size-full wp-image-83719 aligncenter" src="https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/11/Fig7.png" alt="" width="390" height="301" srcset="https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/11/Fig7.png 390w, https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/11/Fig7-300x232.png 300w" sizes="(max-width: 390px) 100vw, 390px" /></a></p>

<p>After a foothold is established on the user’s device, PowerShell is used to immediately establish multiple network connections to their C2 server after executing the following command:</p>
<p><a href="https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/11/Fig8.png"><img decoding="async" loading="lazy" class="size-full wp-image-83720 aligncenter" src="https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/11/Fig8.png" alt="" width="421" height="106" srcset="https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/11/Fig8.png 421w, https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/11/Fig8-300x76.png 300w" sizes="(max-width: 421px) 100vw, 421px" /></a></p>

<p>The above PowerShell command was executed to decrypt the <strong>.DAT</strong> file (0AF84CcF99e5dcA1399141fB72F2B3f2.daT) with a custom XOR key. The below image shows the snippet of the decoded PowerShell script:</p>
<p><a href="https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/11/Fig9.png"><img decoding="async" loading="lazy" class="size-full wp-image-83721 aligncenter" src="https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/11/Fig9.png" alt="" width="468" height="347" srcset="https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/11/Fig9.png 468w, https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/11/Fig9-300x222.png 300w" sizes="(max-width: 468px) 100vw, 468px" /></a></p>

<h2>Summary</h2>
<p>Jupyter Infostealer exhibits a remarkable ability to evolve and adapt.  These modifications seem to enhance its evasion capabilities, allowing it to remain inconspicuous. As cyber defenses strengthen, malicious software finds new avenues to breach and infect systems leaving us vulnerable to newer renditions of commonly seen older attacks.</p>
<h2>Managed Threat Hunting</h2>
<p>As we continue to see malware evolve, some get phased out and others adapt.  This blog post is meant to observe and document the behavioral patterns and changes of Jupyter Infostealer.  With this we have seen the improvements of the evasive abilities Jupyter uses in attempts to stay under the radar and continue to silently infect victims.</p>
<p>Carbon Black adopts a different approach, focusing on both pre and post-exploit defense. This strategy proves more effective against Jupyter Infostealer, ensuring detection, prevention, and containment.</p>
<p>Carbon Black is particularly effective against Jupyter Infostealer due to its innovative approach to endpoint security. Unlike traditional antivirus (AV) solutions that rely solely on static signatures or hashes to detect malware, Carbon Black utilizes advanced techniques and behavioral analysis for threat detection. Here’s why Carbon Black is a superior endpoint protection to use against Jupyter Infostealer:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Dynamic Detection Methods:</strong> Carbon Black employs dynamic detection methods, such as behavioral analysis and machine learning algorithms, to identify malicious behavior patterns. This proactive approach allows it to detect new and evolving threats such as Jupyter Infostealer even when their specific signatures or hashes are unknown.</li>
<li><strong>Focus on Pre and Post-Exploit Defense: </strong>Carbon Black focuses on both pre-exploit and post-exploit defense. While traditional AVs primarily concentrate on pre-exploit measures, Carbon Black also monitors activities after a potential breach.  This comprehensive approach enables it to identify and mitigate Jupyter Infostealer  malicious activities throughout the attack lifecycle.</li>
<li><strong>Adaptability to Unique Attacks:</strong> Jupyter Infostealer, being a malware-as-a-service, allows attackers to customize their attacks resulting in unique configurations for each instance. Carbon Black’s adaptive and behavioral analysis can recognize these custom configurations and detect Jupyter Infostealer variants regardless of the specific parameters set by the attacker.</li>
<li><strong>Containment Capabilities:</strong> Carbon Black not only detects malware but also offers effective containment measures. When Jupyter Infostealer is detected, Carbon Black can isolate the infected system, preventing the malware from spreading further within the network. This containment feature helps prevent widespread damage and data breaches.</li>
<li><strong>Continuous Updates and Threat Intelligence:</strong> Carbon Black continuously updates its threat intelligence database, incorporating information about emerging threats and attack techniques including those used by Jupyter Infostealer. This up-to-date knowledge enhances its ability to recognize and thwart the latest variants of the malware.</li>
<li><strong>Adaptive Response:</strong> Carbon Black’s Managed Detection and Response products provide an adaptive response mechanism. In the event of a Jupyter Infostealer attack, it can respond dynamically, adapting its defense strategies based on the evolving threat landscape. This adaptability is crucial in dealing with constantly changing malware tactics.</li>
<li><strong>Managed Threat Hunting:</strong> Carbon Black’s newly released Managed Threat Hunting product provides proactive threat hunting on emerging threats. The Managed Threat Hunting product’s unique approach to detection and response allows it to quickly detect and respond to threats, including Jupyter Infostealer .</li>
</ul>
<p>Carbon Black’s advanced, dynamic, and comprehensive approach to threat detection and response makes it highly effective against Jupyter Infostealer and other sophisticated malware threats. Carbon Black’s ability to adapt, analyze behavior, and contain attacks sets it apart as a robust solution in the fight against evolving cyber threats.</p>
