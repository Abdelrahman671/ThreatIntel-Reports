			<h1 class="entry-title">Sodinokibi (aka REvil) Ransomware</h1>
		<div class="entry-meta">
			<span class="posted-on"><a href="https://thedfirreport.com/2021/03/29/sodinokibi-aka-revil-ransomware/" rel="bookmark"><time class="entry-date published" datetime="2021-03-29T00:43:09+00:00">March 29, 2021</time></a></span>		</div><!-- .entry-meta -->

		
	</header><!-- .entry-header -->

	<div class="entry-content">
		<h3>Intro</h3>
<p>Sodinokibi (aka REvil) has been one of the most prolific ransomware as a service (RaaS) groups over the last couple years. The ransomware family was purported to be behind the <a href="https://techcrunch.com/2020/01/02/travelex-malware/">Travelex</a> intrusion and current reports point to an attack against <a href="https://www.bleepingcomputer.com/news/security/computer-giant-acer-hit-by-50-million-ransomware-attack/">Acer</a> for a reported $50 million ransom demand.</p>
<p>In March, we observed an intrusion which started with malicious spam that dropped <a href="https://malpedia.caad.fkie.fraunhofer.de/details/win.icedid">IcedID</a> (Bokbot) into the environment and subsequently allowed access to a group distributing Sodinokibi ransomware. During the intrusion the threat actors escalated privileges to Domain Administrator, exfiltrated data, and used Sodinokibi to ransom all domain joined systems.</p>
<h3>Case Summary</h3>
<p>The <a href="https://malpedia.caad.fkie.fraunhofer.de/details/win.icedid">IcedID</a> trojan was first discovered in 2017 and currently operates as an initial access broker for several <a href="https://www.trendmicro.com/en_us/research/21/a/expanding-range-and-improving-speed-a-ransomexx-approach.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener">ransomware</a> <a href="https://www.fireeye.com/blog/threat-research/2021/02/melting-unc2198-icedid-to-ransomware-operations.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener">families</a>. In our intrusion, the threat actors leveraged malicious spam using an <a href="https://tria.ge/210315-7f8el774a6/behavioral1" target="_blank" rel="noopener">xlsm document</a> which, upon opening and enabling the macro, initiated a wmic command to execute the <a href="https://malpedia.caad.fkie.fraunhofer.de/details/win.icedid">IcedID</a> trojan from a remote executable posing as a GIF image.</p>
<p>Persistence was setup using a scheduled task and discovery commands were initiated from the malware within minutes of execution. About an hour and a half after initial access, the malware pulled down Cobalt Strike Beacons from 2 different command and control servers, which were both used through-out the intrusion. Once the Cobalt Strike Beacons were established, lateral movement began, first to an Exchange server, then pivoting to other servers. We did not see the attackers interact with the Exchange application at all; and at first, it appeared the attack came from Exchange, but after careful review, we assessed the source was indeed IcedID. #ArtifactsMatter. It appears the threat actors wanted us to believe Exchange was the source of attack as they pivoted through Exchange to other systems in the domain using Cobalt Strike.</p>
<p>After compromising the Exchange server, the attackers moved to domain controllers and other systems within the environment using SMB and PowerShell Beacons executed via a remote service. The attackers were slightly slowed down by AntiVirus, which ate a couple Beacons but the attackers eventually bypassed it using a variation of their lateral movement technique.</p>
<p>Additional discovery was executed from the domain controller using AdFind and the Ping utility to test connections between the domain controller and other domain joined systems. After discovery was completed, credentials were dumped from lsass. After completing these tasks the threat actors began to establish RDP connections between various systems in the domain.</p>
<p>Three and a half hours into the intrusion, the threat actors used <a href="https://rclone.org/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Rclone</a> masquerading as a svchost executable to collect and exfiltrate the contents of network shares for use in a double extortion demand.</p>
<p>At the four hour mark, the threat actors began to move on to final objectives. They staged the ransomware executable on a domain controller and then used BITSAdmin to download it to each system in the domain. After that, the threat actors used RDP to open a cmd or PowerShell process to then execute the Sodinokibi ransomware using a particular flag -smode, which when executed, wrote a couple RunOnce registry keys and then immediately rebooted the system into Safe Mode with Networking. Encryption did not start immediately after reboot but required a user to log in, which in this case the threat actors completed by logging in after the reboot.</p>
<p>Booting into Safe Mode with Networking blocked the startup of security tools and other management agents. Networking worked, but because services couldn&#8217;t start, we were unable to remotely manage the systems using our normal tools. We believe this process would have stopped some EDR agents from starting up and possibly detecting the ransomware execution.</p>
<p>On certain systems, ransomware was executed without the -smode flag, and on other systems a dll was executed via rundll32 to encrypt the system without requiring a reboot and allowing the threat actors to remain present while the encryption process completed.</p>
<p>About 4.5 hours after initial access, the threat actors had completed their mission of encrypting all domain joined systems. The ransomware note left by the infection included a link to their site on Tor which put the price tag for decryption around $200k if paid within 7 days. If we didn&#8217;t pay within 7 days the price goes up to around $400k. The ransom is required to be paid in Monero instead of the usual Bitcoin. This may be in an effort to better shield the payments from tracing activity like those performed by <a href="https://blog.chainalysis.com/reports/ransomware-update-newly-uncovered-addresses-reveal-21m-worth-of-new-2020-ransomware-payments" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Chainaylsis</a>. The threat actors identified themselves on their site as Sodinokibi and linked to a Coveware blog to provide assurance that if paid their decryption would be successful.</p>
<h3>Services</h3>
<p>Our <a href="https://thedfirreport.com/services/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Threat Feed</a> service picked up one of the two Cobalt Strike servers one day before this intrusion occurred and the other IP was added to the feed as soon as we recognized it.</p>

<h3><a>Impact</a></h3>
<p>For the final actions, the threat actors dropped a ransomware executable on the domain controller in C:\Windows and then used BITSAdmin to deploy the executable to remote systems.</p>
<pre>C:\Windows\system32\bitsadmin.exe /transfer debjob /download /priority normal \\DOMIANCONTROLLER\c$\windows\DOMAINNAME.exe C:\Windows\DOMAINNAME.exe</pre>
<p>The -smode flag was used with the ransomware executable to set the system to reboot into Safe Mode with Networking as noted by <a href="https://twitter.com/malwrhunterteam/status/1372635361285959684">Malwarehunterteam</a>.</p>
