By cloning and destroying VMs, adversaries can evade detection and maintain access to critical systems.</p></blockquote><figure class="oa ob oc od oe of"><div class="py jf l fj"><div class="pz qa l"></div></div><figcaption class="oh ff oi nx ny oj ok bf b bg z du"><em class="ol">Table 4. These actions established persistent access and allowed the adversary to execute arbitrary commands and communicate with command-and-control servers. The adversary utilized techniques such as SSH manipulation and execution of suspicious scripts to maintain control over the compromised systems.</p><p id="3be3" class="pw-post-body-paragraph mx my gu mz b na nb nc nd ne nf ng nh ni nj nk nl nm nn no np nq nr ns nt nu gn bk">A VMware default account <em class="nv">vpxuser,</em> used VMware vSphere Management API <em class="nv">pyvmomi</em>, made seven API calls that enumerated a list of mounted and unmounted drives. The adversary pivoted back to the admin account and created three new VMs, all conforming to the local naming convention, and successfully logged into them from an internal IP address.
