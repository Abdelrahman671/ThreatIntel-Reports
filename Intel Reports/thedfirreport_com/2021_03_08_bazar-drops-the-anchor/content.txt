			<h1 class="entry-title">Bazar Drops the Anchor</h1>
		<div class="entry-meta">
			<span class="posted-on"><a href="https://thedfirreport.com/2021/03/08/bazar-drops-the-anchor/" rel="bookmark"><time class="entry-date published" datetime="2021-03-08T02:09:34+00:00">March 8, 2021</time></a></span>		</div><!-- .entry-meta -->

		
	</header><!-- .entry-header -->

	<div class="entry-content">
		<h3>Intro</h3>
<p>The malware identified as <a href="https://malpedia.caad.fkie.fraunhofer.de/details/win.anchor">Anchor</a> first entered the scene in late 2018 and has been linked to the same group as Trickbot, due to similarities in code and usage of the two different malware families in the same intrusions. In 2020 the <a href="https://malpedia.caad.fkie.fraunhofer.de/details/win.bazarbackdoor">Bazar</a> malware family entered and again many associated it with the same group behind Trickbot.</p>
<p>In an intrusion this past month we saw another link between the 3 families with a Bazar loader bringing in Anchor DNS to facilitate a full domain compromise intrusion. Over a 5 day time frame the threat actors moved from a single endpoint to full domain compromise, and while ransomware deployment was not seen in this intrusion the TTP&#8217;s used mirror what we would expect from a big game ransomware crew.</p>
<h3>Case Summary</h3>
<p>In this case we started with a DocuSign themed Excel maldoc. The excel file failed to bring down the payload but to follow the infection chain we executed the follow on loader. Once Bazar was established the malware quickly injected into the Werfault process to avoid detection. As seen in many intrusions the malware then performed some initial discovery with built-in Microsoft utilities such as Nltest.</p>
<p>About an hour after initial execution, a Cobalt Strike beacon was loaded, followed shortly by Anchor. Shortly after Cobalt Strike and Anchor were running, the attackers dumped credentials and began moving laterally, starting with a domain controller.</p>
<p>Once on the domain controller, the threat actors ran additional discovery but then went quiet. Active command and control was maintained by all three malware samples (Bazar, Cobalt Strike, Anchor DNS) over the next 4 days.</p>
<p>During that timeframe, honey documents were interacted with and additional discovery scans were executed. The threat actors were briefly active on day 3 to execute their Get-DataInfo script to collect additional information, which is usually followed closely by Ryuk ransomware.</p>
<p>However, on the fifth day the threat actors access was cut off before final objectives could be accomplished. We assess that the end goal of this intrusion was to execute domain wide ransomware.</p>

<p><a href="https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/02/anchor-01.png"><img decoding="async" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-2330" src="https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/02/anchor-01.png" alt="" width="644" height="251" srcset="https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/02/anchor-01.png 644w, https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/02/anchor-01-300x117.png 300w" sizes="(max-width: 644px) 100vw, 644px" /></a></p>
<p>DocuSign was again the social engineering format of choice.</p>

<h3><a>Exfiltration</a></h3>
<p>No exfiltration was observed but honey docs were taken off network and opened by the threat actors from remote locations. We assess that this exfiltration was performed over an encrypted C2 channel. This exfiltration has been going on for months and is rarely talked about when it comes to Wizard Spider.</p>
<h3><a>Impact</a></h3>
<p>We believe this intrusion would have ended with domain wide ransomware. The deployment of the Get-DataInfo.ps1 script and overall TTP&#8217;s used in the intrustion are consistent with threat actors associated with deployments of the Ryuk ransomware family.</p>
