    <p>You’ve read the news and heard the stories. A ransomware attack takes down a company’s operations by locking down their network drive and holding the sensitive data hostage. What was the root cause? An employee accidentally clicked on a link in a phishing email scam or downloaded a malicious attachment.</p>
<p>Email phishing attempts are an incredibly common — and all too effective — channel for delivering ransomware. These emails are crafted to appear as if they’re from legitimate sources, such as a financial institution, healthcare provider, or social media site. By appearing authentic and genuine, these emails trick users into clicking malicious links or opening infected attachments. In many cases, these emails evade basic filters and land directly in users’ inboxes.</p>
<p>Social engineering plays an important role in convincing users to download ransomware via phishing emails. Emails are carefully crafted, creating a sense of urgency or mimicking a trusted source, such as a colleague or a well-known institution. Links in phishing emails may redirect users to compromised websites or apps that silently download ransomware in the background.</p>
<p>Alternatively, attachments may contain executable files or documents programmed with dangerous macros. On the surface, these documents may look like legitimate invoices or reports, making the underlying malware difficult to spot.</p>
<p>Unsuspecting recipients fall victim to the scheme by clicking to download and opening the attachment. Then, the ransomware installs itself on the user’s device. The system breach goes unnoticed, and the malicious software begins encrypting files, rendering them inaccessible without the decryption key.</p>

    <p>Hackers may also take advantage of vulnerabilities in outdated or unpatched software. These kinds of vulnerabilities are found in web browsers or old plug-ins like Flash, Java, or PDF viewers. This is the starting point for cybercriminals to use drive-by downloads or exploit kits to silently deliver ransomware to their victims’ machines.</p>
<p>A malicious web page or pop-up notification may have embedded scripts or hidden code meant to exploit weaknesses in insecure browsers or plug-ins. This code initiates a drive-by download, which is the automatic downloading of a file in the background without any user interaction. The key vulnerability here is within the browser or plug-in, which is why keeping them updated is essential.</p>
<p>The downloaded ransomware file will continue the attack, exploiting other weaknesses in the victim's system to gain control and execute malicious code. Exploit kits automate the process of scanning systems for these vulnerabilities, whether in the browser or other outdated software. After detecting a vulnerability, the exploit kit injects the ransomware payload, often with no visible sign until files are encrypted.</p>
<p>To prevent these attacks, it’s critical to regularly update your browsers, plug-ins, and operating systems.</p>

<div class="text aem-GridColumn aem-GridColumn--default--12">
<div data-cmp-is="text" class="cmp-text" data-cmp-data-layer="{&#34;text-512fd2d309&#34;:{&#34;@type&#34;:&#34;akamai/components/text&#34;,&#34;repo:modifyDate&#34;:&#34;2024-11-01T15:26:10Z&#34;,&#34;xdm:text&#34;:&#34;&lt;p>As recent years have brought about the rise of remote work, the use of Remote Desktop Protocol (RDP) has increased significantly.     <p>As recent years have brought about the rise of remote work, the use of Remote Desktop Protocol (RDP) has increased significantly. RDP allows users to remotely access and control systems. Although it’s meant for convenience and access, the use of RDP without proper security measures can open the door for attackers.</p>
<p>Attackers begin by scanning targeted machines for exposed RDP ports. They couple this with a brute-force attack to guess valid login credentials. After gaining access to a vulnerable machine, the attackers can disable security features or alter system settings. From there, they can manually deploy their ransomware.</p>
<p>The attack vectors for exploiting these remote connections depend on:</p>
<ul>
<li><p>Weak passwords</p>
</li>
<li><p>Default (and insecure) configurations</p>
</li>
<li><p>Unpatched RDP software</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p>Once attackers have made their way inside a machine and network, they can move laterally and <a href="/blog/security/how-ransomware-actually-spreads" target="_self">spread ransomware</a>. They will then disable security software and delete backups, making recovery difficult.</p>
<p>To prevent RDP-based attacks, organizations must enforce strong passwords, use <a href="/glossary/what-is-multifactor-authentication" target="_self">multi-factor authentication (MFA)</a>, and monitor remote access logs for suspicious activity.</p>

      <h2 class="cmp-title__text">How to prevent ransomware from infiltrating your information<br />

<div class="text aem-GridColumn aem-GridColumn--default--12">

    <p>There is no single solution that you can implement to guarantee comprehensive protection against ransomware attacks.       <h3 class="cmp-title__text">Implementing advanced security measures<br />
</h3>
    
</div>

    


</div>
<div class="text aem-GridColumn aem-GridColumn--default--12">
<div data-cmp-is="text" class="cmp-text" data-cmp-data-layer="{&#34;text-d5f0d2a661&#34;:{&#34;@type&#34;:&#34;akamai/components/text&#34;,&#34;repo:modifyDate&#34;:&#34;2024-11-01T15:43:14Z&#34;,&#34;xdm:text&#34;:&#34;&lt;p>Start with a solid foundation of tools to implement advanced security measures that will stop ransomware before it infects your system. This first line of defense includes:&lt;/p>\r\n&lt;ul>\r\n&lt;li>&lt;p>&lt;b>Antivirus and anti-malware software&lt;/b>: Detects and blocks various forms of malware, including ransomware, before they can be executed on your machine&lt;/p>\r\n&lt;/li>\r\n&lt;li>&lt;p>&lt;b>Behavioral analysis and real-time monitoring&lt;/b>: Identifies and alerts you to suspicious activity in your network, preventing the installation of ransomware on targeted machines&lt;/p>\r\n&lt;/li>\r\n&lt;li>&lt;p>&lt;b>Firewalls&lt;/b>: Monitors network traffic and restricts access, reducing the likelihood of ransomware entering your network through open ports of vulnerabilities&lt;/p>\r\n&lt;/li>\r\n&lt;li>&lt;p>&lt;b>Endpoint security&lt;/b>: Ensures that individual devices are continuously monitored and protected, preventing ransomware from spreading across your organization&lt;/p>\r\n&lt;/li>\r\n&lt;li>&lt;p>&lt;a href=\&#34;/content/akamai/en/resources/video/protect-against-ransomware-zero-trust-use-case.html\&#34; target=\&#34;_self\&#34;>&lt;b>Zero Trust security&lt;/b>&lt;/a>: Enforces strict identity verification for every user or device trying to access resources, and enables granular segmentation of critical applications, minimizing the risk of ransomware spreading by assuming no one is trusted by default&lt;/p>\r\n&lt;/li>\r\n&lt;/ul>\r\n&lt;p>Keeping these solutions up-to-date is crucial to staying protected against new variants of ransomware that continue to emerge.&lt;/p>\r\n&#34;}}">
    <p>Start with a solid foundation of tools to implement advanced security measures that will stop ransomware before it infects your system. This first line of defense includes:</p>
<ul>
<li><p><b>Antivirus and anti-malware software</b>: Detects and blocks various forms of malware, including ransomware, before they can be executed on your machine</p>
</li>
<li><p><b>Behavioral analysis and real-time monitoring</b>: Identifies and alerts you to suspicious activity in your network, preventing the installation of ransomware on targeted machines</p>
</li>
<li><p><b>Firewalls</b>: Monitors network traffic and restricts access, reducing the likelihood of ransomware entering your network through open ports of vulnerabilities</p>
</li>
<li><p><b>Endpoint security</b>: Ensures that individual devices are continuously monitored and protected, preventing ransomware from spreading across your organization</p>
</li>
<li><p><a href="/resources/video/protect-against-ransomware-zero-trust-use-case" target="_self"><b>Zero Trust security</b></a>: Enforces strict identity verification for every user or device trying to access resources, and enables granular segmentation of critical applications, minimizing the risk of ransomware spreading by assuming no one is trusted by default</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p>Keeping these solutions up-to-date is crucial to staying protected against new variants of ransomware that continue to emerge.</p>

<div data-cmp-is="text" class="cmp-text" data-cmp-data-layer="{&#34;text-e59aa9f45b&#34;:{&#34;@type&#34;:&#34;akamai/components/text&#34;,&#34;repo:modifyDate&#34;:&#34;2024-11-01T15:45:48Z&#34;,&#34;xdm:text&#34;:&#34;&lt;p>Patching your software is critical for closing the security gaps that attackers often exploit. One of the most common entry points for ransomware is outdated software. This is what attackers are counting on when they attempt to exploit the known vulnerability.&lt;/p>\r\n&lt;p>&lt;a href=\&#34;https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/industry/blog/healthcare/2017/06/06/wannacry-ransomware-attack-lessons-learned/\&#34; target=\&#34;_blank\&#34; rel=\&#34;nofollow noreferrer\&#34;>WannaCry&lt;/a> was a highly publicized ransomware attack in 2017, and it exploited the server message block (SMB) of unpatched machines running Microsoft Windows.     <p>Patching your software is critical for closing the security gaps that attackers often exploit. One of the most common entry points for ransomware is outdated software. When a security vulnerability is discovered in a piece of software, the software manufacturer should release an update to patch the security issue.</p>
<p>Yet all too often, software users put off applying the fix — and sometimes there is no fix to apply. This is what attackers are counting on when they attempt to exploit the known vulnerability.</p>
<p><a href="https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/industry/blog/healthcare/2017/06/06/wannacry-ransomware-attack-lessons-learned/" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noreferrer">WannaCry</a> was a highly publicized ransomware attack in 2017, and it exploited the server message block (SMB) of unpatched machines running Microsoft Windows. In total, more than 300,000 computers across 100,000 businesses in 150 countries were infected with the ransomware.</p>
<p>By patching vulnerabilities whenever possible and updating your systems regularly, you shrink the window of exploitability, minimizing the risk that attackers will prey on known weaknesses to deliver ransomware.</p>
