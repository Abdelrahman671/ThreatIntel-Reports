    <h1 class="text-center">Player 1 Limps Back Into the Ring - Hello again, Locky!</h1>

    <div class="text-center m-1">
        <div class="post-author">
            <span>By </span>
                <a href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/author/alexander-chiu/">Alexander Chiu</a>
        </div>

        <br/>
        <time class="post-datetime" datetime="June 21, 2017 17:00">
            Wednesday, June 21, 2017 17:00
        </time>

        <div class="m-3">
                            <a href="/category/ransomware/" class="category primary-category">
                                ransomware
                            </a>
                                </div>
    </div>

    <section class="post-content-wrapper mt-5">
        <div class="post-content">
            <p><i>This post was authored by <a href="https://www.blogger.com/profile/09794908020114943712">Alex Chiu</a>, <a href="https://blogs.cisco.com/author/warrenmercer">Warren Mercer</a>, and <a href="https://twitter.com/jaesonschultz">Jaeson Schultz</a>.  <a href="https://www.linkedin.com/in/seanrichardbaird/">Sean Baird</a> and <a href="https://blogs.cisco.com/author/matthewmolyett">Matthew Molyett</a>contributed to this post.</i></p><p>Back in May, the Necurs spam botnet jettisoned Locky ransomware in favor of the new Jaff ransomware variant. However, earlier this month Kaspersky <a href="https://www.bleepingcomputer.com/news/security/decrypted-kaspersky-releases-decryptor-for-the-jaff-ransomware/">discovered</a> a vulnerability within Jaff which allowed them to create a decryptor. This turn of events seems to have caused the miscreants behind Necurs to scramble to distribute a different ransomware payload. Falling back on their old tricks, they have selected to re-distribute Locky ransomware. The malware is being transmitted via email using an .exe file encapsulated within two compressed .zip archives.</p><h2 id="the-spam-campaign-the-spam-that-is-distributing-this-ransomware-campaign-is-not-significantly-different-from-other-ransomware-spam-campaigns-that-we-have-seen-from-necurs-ransomware-oriented-spam-campaigns-from-necurs-typically-involve-order-confirmations-payment-receipts-business-documents-and-so-on-all-with-the-common-goal-of-social-engineering-victims-into-opening-the-attachment-the-messages-talos-observed-in-this-particular-campaign-are-disguised-as-fake-invoices-">The Spam Campaign The spam that is distributing this ransomware campaign is not significantly different from other ransomware spam campaigns that <a href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/jaff-ransomware">we have seen</a> from Necurs. Ransomware-oriented spam campaigns from Necurs typically involve order confirmations, payment receipts, business documents, and so on -- all with the common goal of social engineering victims into opening the attachment. The messages Talos observed in this particular campaign are disguised as fake invoices.<br></h2><!--kg-card-begin: html--><table><tbody><tr><td><a target="_blank" href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/-eyNiK605aRM/WUrZl2M0HkI/AAAAAAAABGQ/qwl28puJ1mcFIcja7euYAQG9YTqlqTQ5wCLcBGAs/s1600/image2.png"><img height="366" src="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/-eyNiK605aRM/WUrZl2M0HkI/AAAAAAAABGQ/qwl28puJ1mcFIcja7euYAQG9YTqlqTQ5wCLcBGAs/s640/image2.png" width="640"></a>
</td></tr><tr><td>An example spam message propagating Locky ransomware
</td></tr></tbody></table><!--kg-card-end: html--><p>The volume of Locky spam Necurs has sent since the start of this particular campaign is notable. In the first hour of this campaign, Talos observed that Locky spam accounted for up to 7.2% of email volume on one of our systems. While the campaign has since decreased in the number of messages being sent per minute, Necurs is still actively sending messages containing Locky, though only in small quantities.<br></p><!--kg-card-begin: html--><table><tbody><tr><td><a target="_blank" href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/-O9IsDuPG5CQ/WUrZvGUQC2I/AAAAAAAABGU/xd8ggmw2doQ7iK2tb_5ztyE62QP4DqvegCLcBGAs/s1600/image3.jpg"><img height="300" src="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/-O9IsDuPG5CQ/WUrZvGUQC2I/AAAAAAAABGU/xd8ggmw2doQ7iK2tb_5ztyE62QP4DqvegCLcBGAs/s640/image3.jpg" width="640"></a>
</td></tr><tr><td>Chart illustrating the volume of Locky spam as a percent of total email volume one of our systems observed.
</td></tr></tbody></table><!--kg-card-end: html--><p><br></p><h2 id="locky-s-metamorphosis-although-they-are-using-the-same-affiliate-id-for-this-particular-round-of-locky-the-attackers-have-altered-their-ransomware-we-were-unable-to-get-the-ransomware-to-encrypt-data-on-any-systems-running-an-operating-system-more-recent-than-windows-xp-upon-further-investigation-we-determined-that-on-systems-running-windows-7-or-later-with-data-execution-prevention-dep-would-cause-the-unpacker-to-fail-our-analysis-suggests-that-locky-has-now-added-anti-debugging-protection-to-their-configuration-instead-of-unpacking-the-configuration-when-a-debugger-is-detected-their-config-pointer-is-directed-at-kernel32-allocconsole-frustrating-any-attempts-at-analysis-in-response-to-this-new-anti-analysis-technique-talos-has-updated-lockydump-so-that-it-is-able-to-extract-the-configuration-information-from-this-latest-iteration-">Locky's Metamorphosis <br> Although they are using the same affiliate ID, for this particular round of Locky, the attackers have altered their ransomware. We were unable to get the ransomware to encrypt data on any systems running an operating system more recent than Windows XP. Upon further investigation, we determined that on systems running Windows 7 or later with Data Execution Prevention (DEP) would cause the unpacker to fail. Our analysis suggests that Locky has now added anti-debugging protection to their configuration. Instead of unpacking the configuration, when a debugger is detected their config pointer is directed at kernel32!AllocConsole, frustrating any attempts at analysis. In response to this new anti-analysis technique, Talos has updated <a href="https://www.talosintelligence.com/lockydump">LockyDump</a> so that it is able to extract the configuration information from this latest iteration.<br></h2><!--kg-card-begin: html--><table><tbody><tr><td><a target="_blank" href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/-IO66iRUAo7Y/WUrZ4FmnYjI/AAAAAAAABGY/7fRBMyeOi8cz2CLZaNHTtO6-oEoyiWtUgCLcBGAs/s1600/image1.png"><img height="396" src="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/-IO66iRUAo7Y/WUrZ4FmnYjI/AAAAAAAABGY/7fRBMyeOi8cz2CLZaNHTtO6-oEoyiWtUgCLcBGAs/s640/image1.png" width="640"></a>
</td></tr><tr><td>Locky's unpacker crashes when trying to execute stack memory on systems more recent than Windows XP.
</td></tr></tbody></table><!--kg-card-end: html--><p><br> Another notable aspect of this latest campaign was the C2 URL structure. Adversaries behind this latest Locky campaign have reused the /checkupdate path as part of the URL structure -- the same URL structure found in <a href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/locky-returns-necurs">previous</a> Locky campaigns. This is perhaps another indication that adversaries were hasty in their developing and distributing this campaign.<br></p><!--kg-card-begin: html--><table><tbody><tr><td><a target="_blank" href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/-MA9D-Bh1-lg/WUrZ9bg40jI/AAAAAAAABGc/_6xzKOgRZ7MbQ0C0GYGn8g8KCz287zZjQCLcBGAs/s1600/image4.png"><img height="198" src="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/-MA9D-Bh1-lg/WUrZ9bg40jI/AAAAAAAABGc/_6xzKOgRZ7MbQ0C0GYGn8g8KCz287zZjQCLcBGAs/s640/image4.png" width="640"></a>
</td></tr><tr><td>Threat Grid sandbox run illustrating Locky C2 communication
</td></tr></tbody></table><!--kg-card-end: html--><p><br></p><h2 id="conclusion-this-updated-version-of-locky-appears-to-have-been-hastily-deployed-and-as-a-result-it-has-not-affected-users-running-windows-operating-systems-other-than-windows-xp-the-attackers-behind-this-ransomware-are-likely-already-aware-of-this-so-we-can-expect-a-fixed-version-of-locky-to-appear-in-a-future-round-of-necurs-ransomware-spam-">Conclusion <br> This updated version of Locky appears to have been hastily deployed, and as a result it has not affected users running Windows operating systems other than Windows XP. The attackers behind this ransomware are likely already aware of this, so we can expect a fixed version of Locky to appear in a future round of Necurs' ransomware spam.</h2><p>Despite sounding like a broken record, we at Talos feel it's our duty to re-iterate that it's always risky clicking on links or opening attachments in strange email messages. Users that fail to heed this advice can easily become ransomware victims, and if the subsequent ransom is paid, the monies will no doubt fund another round of attacks. As always, organizations are encouraged to make regular backups of their data, practice restoring said data, and store your backups offline far out of the reach of potential criminals.</p><h2 id="coverage-open-source-snort-subscriber-rule-set-customers-can-stay-up-to-date-by-downloading-the-latest-rule-pack-available-for-purchase-on-snort-org-">Coverage Open Source Snort Subscriber Rule Set customers can stay up to date by downloading the latest rule pack available for purchase on <a href="https://www.snort.org/products">Snort.org</a>.</h2><p>Additional ways our customers can detect loading="lazy"></figure><p><br> Advanced Malware Protection (<a href="https://www.cisco.com/c/en/us/support/security/amp-firepower-software-license/tsd-products-support-series-home.html">AMP</a>) is ideally suited to prevent the execution of the malware used by these threat actors.</p><p><a href="https://www.cisco.com/c/en/us/products/security/cloud-web-security/index.html">CWS</a>, <a
