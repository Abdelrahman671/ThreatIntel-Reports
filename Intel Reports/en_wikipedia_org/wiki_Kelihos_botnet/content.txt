					<div id="mw-content-text" class="mw-body-content"><div class="mw-content-ltr mw-parser-output" lang="en" dir="ltr"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Computer botnet</div>
<p>
The <b>Kelihos botnet</b>, also known as <b>Hlux</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/Botnet" title="Botnet">botnet</a> mainly involved in <a href="/wiki/Spam_(electronic)" class="mw-redirect" title="Spam (electronic)">spamming</a> and the theft of <a href="/wiki/Bitcoin" title="Bitcoin">bitcoins</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Mills_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mills-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="History">History</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kelihos_botnet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The Kelihos botnet was first discovered around <span class="nowrap">December 2010</span>.<sup id="cite_ref-Ortloff_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ortloff-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Researchers originally suspected having found a new version of either the <a href="/wiki/Storm_botnet" title="Storm botnet">Storm</a> or <a href="/wiki/Waledac" class="mw-redirect" title="Waledac">Waledac</a> botnet, due to similarities in the <a href="/wiki/Modus_operandi" title="Modus operandi">modus operandi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Source_code" title="Source code">source code</a> of the bot,<sup id="cite_ref-Adair_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Adair-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Donohue_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Donohue-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> but analysis of the botnet showed it was instead a new, 45,000-<a href="/wiki/Trojan_horse_(computing)" title="Trojan horse (computing)">infected</a>-computer-strong botnet that was capable of sending an estimated <span class="nowrap">4 billion</span> spam messages a day.<sup id="cite_ref-Mills2_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mills2-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Kirk_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kirk-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In <span class="nowrap">September 2011</span>,<sup id="cite_ref-Constantin_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Constantin-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">Microsoft</a> took down the botnet in an operation codenamed "Operation b79".<sup id="cite_ref-Mills2_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mills2-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Boscovich_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Boscovich-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> At the same time, Microsoft filed civil charges against Dominique Alexander Piatti, dotFREE Group SRO and 22 <a href="/wiki/John_Doe" title="John Doe">John Doe</a> defendants for suspected involvement in the botnet for issuing 3,700 <a href="/wiki/Subdomains" class="mw-redirect" title="Subdomains">subdomains</a> that were used by the botnet.<sup id="cite_ref-Boscovich_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Boscovich-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Microsoft_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Microsoft-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> These charges were later dropped when Microsoft determined that the named defendants did not intentionally aid the botnet controllers.<sup id="cite_ref-Latif_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Latif-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Gonsalves_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gonsalves-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>In January 2012, a new version of the botnet was discovered, one sometimes referred to as Kelihos.b or Version 2,<sup id="cite_ref-Mills_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mills-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Kirk_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kirk-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Constantin_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Constantin-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> consisting of an estimated 110,000 infected computers.<sup id="cite_ref-Mills_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mills-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Warren_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Warren-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> During this same month Microsoft pressed charges against Russian citizen Andrey Sabelnikov, a former IT security professional, for being the alleged creator of the Kelihos Botnet <a href="/wiki/Sourcecode" class="mw-redirect" title="Sourcecode">sourcecode</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Gonsalves_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gonsalves-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Brewster_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brewster-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Keizer_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Keizer-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The second version of the botnet itself was shut down in <span class="nowrap">March 2012</span> by several privately owned firms by <a href="/wiki/DNS_sinkhole" title="DNS sinkhole">sinkholing</a> it – a technique which gave the companies control over the botnet while cutting off the original controllers.<sup id="cite_ref-Ortloff_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ortloff-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Donohue2_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Donohue2-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Following the shutdown of the second version of the botnet, a new version surfaced as early as April 2nd, though there is some disagreement between research groups whether the botnet is simply the remnants of the disabled Version 2 botnet, or a new version altogether.<sup id="cite_ref-Raywood_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Raywood-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Leyden_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Leyden-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> This version of the botnet currently consists of an estimated 70,000 infected computers. The Kelihos.c version mostly infects computers through Facebook by sending users of the website malicious download links. Once clicked, a <a href="/wiki/Trojan_horse_(computing)" title="Trojan horse (computing)">Trojan horse</a> named Fifesoc is downloaded, which turns the computer into a <a href="/wiki/Zombie_(computer_science)" class="mw-redirect" title="Zombie (computer science)">zombie</a>, which is part of the botnet.<sup id="cite_ref-SPAMfighter_News_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SPAMfighter_News-18"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>18<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>On 24 November 2015 a Kelihos botnet event occurred causing widespread false positives of blacklisted IPs:
</p>
<blockquote>
<p>″November 24, 2015 Widespread false positives
</p><p>Earlier today, a very large scale Kelihos botnet event occurred - by large scale, many email installations will be seeing in excess of 20% kelihos spam, and some will see their inbound email volume jump by a volume of as much as 500%. This isn't an unusual thing normally, the CBL/XBL has been successfully dealing with large scale Kelihos spam spikes like this, often daily, for years.
</p><p>The email was allegedly from the US Federal Reserve, saying something about restrictions in "U.S. Federal Wire and ACH online payments." Not only was the notice itself fraudulent, the attached Excel spreadsheet (.xls) contained macro instructions (a downloader) to download a Windows executable virus, most likely Dyreza or <a href="/wiki/Dridex" title="Dridex">Dridex</a> malware.
</p><p>The detection rules initially deployed by the CBL unfortunately were insufficiently detailed, and listed a number of IP addresses in error.″<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>19<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
</blockquote>
<p>An affidavit unsealed on 5 February 2018, showed <a href="/wiki/Apple_Inc." title="Apple Inc.">Apple's</a> unexpected role in bringing the Russian spam king to justice. <a href="/wiki/Peter_Levashov" title="Peter Levashov">Peter Levashov</a> allegedly ran the Kelihos botnet under the alias "Severa", renting out access to spammers and other cybercriminals. But despite Levashov's significant efforts at anonymity, court records show that federal agents had been surveilling his <a href="/wiki/ICloud" title="ICloud">iCloud</a> account since 20 May 2016, funneling back crucial information that may have led to his arrest. The standing federal iCloud warrant would have given authorities a running tab of <a href="/wiki/IP_address" title="IP address">IP addresses</a> used to log in to the account, which could easily have tipped them off to his vacation in <a href="/wiki/Barcelona" title="Barcelona">Barcelona, Spain</a>, and was arrested at the request of US law enforcement and extradited to the United States for prosecution.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>20<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Structure,_operations_and_spread"><span id="Structure.2C_operations_and_spread"></span>Structure, operations and spread</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kelihos_botnet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Structure, operations and spread"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The Kelihos botnet is a so-called <a href="/wiki/Peer-to-peer" title="Peer-to-peer">peer-to-peer</a> botnet, where individual botnet nodes are capable of acting as command-and-control servers for the entire botnet. In traditional non-peer-to-peer botnets, all the nodes receive their instructions and "work" from a limited set of servers&#160;– if these servers are removed or taken down, the botnet will no longer receive instructions and will therefore effectively shut down.<sup id="cite_ref-Grizzard_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Grizzard-21"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>21<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Peer-to-peer botnets seek to mitigate that risk by allowing every peer to send instructions to the entire botnet, thus making it more difficult to shut down.<sup id="cite_ref-Ortloff_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ortloff-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>The first version of the botnet was mainly involved in <a href="/wiki/DDoS#Distributed_attack" class="mw-redirect" title="DDoS">denial-of-service attacks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spam_(electronic)" class="mw-redirect" title="Spam (electronic)">email spam</a>, while version two of the botnet added the ability to steal <a href="/wiki/Bitcoin" title="Bitcoin">Bitcoin</a> wallets, as well as a program used to <a href="/wiki/Bitcoin#Mining" title="Bitcoin">mine</a> bitcoins itself.<sup id="cite_ref-Ortloff_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ortloff-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-SPAMfighter2_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SPAMfighter2-22"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>22<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Its spam capacity allows the botnet to spread itself by sending <a href="/wiki/Malware" title="Malware">malware</a> links to users in order to infect them with a Trojan horse, though later versions mostly propagate over social network sites, in particular through Facebook.<sup id="cite_ref-Raywood_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Raywood-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Jorgenson_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jorgenson-23"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>23<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> A more comprehensive list of the Kelihos spam can be found in the following research paper.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>24<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>

<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Arrest_and_extradition">Arrest and extradition</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kelihos_botnet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Arrest and extradition"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>On 2 February 2018, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Justice" title="United States Department of Justice">United States Department of Justice</a> announced that a Russian national has been extradited from <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> and will be arraigned in <a href="/wiki/Connecticut" title="Connecticut">Connecticut</a> on charges stemming from his alleged operation of the Kelihos botnet. <a href="/wiki/Peter_Levashov" title="Peter Levashov">Peter Yuryevich Levashov</a>, 37, also known as Pyotr Levashov,<sup id="cite_ref-dw030218_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dw030218-25"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>25<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Petr Levashov, Peter Severa, Petr Severa and Sergey Astakhov, of St. Petersburg, was detained on 7 April 2017 in <a href="/wiki/Barcelona" title="Barcelona">Barcelona</a>, when he was arrested by Spanish authorities based upon a criminal complaint and arrest warrant issued in the United States District of Connecticut.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>26<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> On 3 February 2018, he pleaded not guilty to the charges of <a href="/wiki/Mail_and_wire_fraud" title="Mail and wire fraud">wire and email fraud</a>, <a href="/wiki/Computer_Fraud_and_Abuse_Act#Criminal_offenses_under_the_Act" title="Computer Fraud and Abuse Act">hacking</a>, <a href="/wiki/Identity_theft_in_the_United_States" title="Identity theft in the United States">identity theft</a> and <a href="/wiki/Conspiracy_against_the_United_States" title="Conspiracy against the United States">conspiracy</a> after appearing before a federal judge in the U.S. state of <a href="/wiki/Connecticut" title="Connecticut">Connecticut</a>. He remains in detention.<sup id="cite_ref-dw030218_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dw030218-25"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>25<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In September 2018, Levashov pleaded guilty.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>27<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
