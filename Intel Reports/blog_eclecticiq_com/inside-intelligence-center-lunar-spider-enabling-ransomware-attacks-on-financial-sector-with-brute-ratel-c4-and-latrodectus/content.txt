<!--more-->
<p><span data-contrast="auto" xml:lang="EN-GB" lang="EN-GB"><span>In October 2024, EclecticIQ analysts observed a malvertising campaign employing an obfuscated JavaScript downloader known as Latrodectus [<a href="#a1" rel="noopener">1</a>] to deliver a malicious payload associated with Brute Ratel C4 (BRc4) [<a href="#a2" rel="noopener">2</a>]. Analysts assess with high confidence that this campaign is very likely linked to LUNAR SPIDER [<a href="#a3" rel="noopener">3</a>], a Russian-speaking, financially motivated threat actor group active since at least 2009. LUNAR SPIDER is responsible for developing several high-profile malware families, including IcedID [<a href="#a4" rel="noopener">4</a>] and Latrodectus. IcedID malware is often distributed via malware-as-a-service (MaaS) offerings, enabling affiliates, such as the ALPHA SPIDER/BlackCat ransomware group [<a href="#a5" rel="noopener">5</a>], to leverage these services for initial compromise.&nbsp;</span></span><span data-contrast="auto" xml:lang="EN-GB" lang="EN-GB"><span></span></span></p>
<p><a href="https://www.eclecticiq.com/hubfs/_blogs/corporate-blog/2024/Lunar%20Spider/image001.png" rel="noopener"><img src="https://blog.eclecticiq.com/hs-fs/hubfs/_blogs/corporate-blog/2024/Lunar%20Spider/image001.png?width=693&amp;height=394&amp;name=image001.png" width="693" height="394" loading="lazy" alt="image001" style="height: auto; max-width: 100%; width: 693px;" srcset="https://blog.eclecticiq.com/hs-fs/hubfs/_blogs/corporate-blog/2024/Lunar%20Spider/image001.png?width=347&amp;height=197&amp;name=image001.png 347w, https://blog.eclecticiq.com/hs-fs/hubfs/_blogs/corporate-blog/2024/Lunar%20Spider/image001.png?width=693&amp;height=394&amp;name=image001.png 693w, https://blog.eclecticiq.com/hs-fs/hubfs/_blogs/corporate-blog/2024/Lunar%20Spider/image001.png?width=1040&amp;height=591&amp;name=image001.png 1040w, https://blog.eclecticiq.com/hs-fs/hubfs/_blogs/corporate-blog/2024/Lunar%20Spider/image001.png?width=1386&amp;height=788&amp;name=image001.png 1386w, https://blog.eclecticiq.com/hs-fs/hubfs/_blogs/corporate-blog/2024/Lunar%20Spider/image001.png?width=1733&amp;height=985&amp;name=image001.png 1733w, https://blog.eclecticiq.com/hs-fs/hubfs/_blogs/corporate-blog/2024/Lunar%20Spider/image001.png?width=2079&amp;height=1182&amp;name=image001.png 2079w" sizes="(max-width: 693px) 100vw, 693px"></a></p>

<p><span data-contrast="auto" xml:lang="EN-GB" lang="EN-GB"><span>On May 30, 2024, the FBI and international partners executed Operation Endgame </span></span><span><span data-contrast="auto" xml:lang="EN-US" lang="EN-US">[6]</span></span><span data-contrast="auto" xml:lang="EN-GB" lang="EN-GB"><span>, dismantling the command-and-control infrastructures of at least four malware variants, including </span><span>IcedID</span><span> (</span><span>BokBot</span><span>), </span><span>Smokeloader</span><span> </span></span><span><span data-contrast="auto" xml:lang="EN-US" lang="EN-US">[<a data-contrast="auto" xml:lang="EN-US" lang="EN-US">[<a href="#a8" rel="noopener">8</a>]</span></span><span data-contrast="auto" xml:lang="EN-GB" lang="EN-GB"><span>, and Bumblebee </span></span><span><span data-contrast="auto" xml:lang="EN-US" lang="EN-US">[<a href="#a9" rel="noopener">9</a>]</span></span><span data-contrast="auto" xml:lang="EN-GB" lang="EN-GB"><span>. </span><span>EclecticIQ</span><span> analysts assess with high confidence that LUNAR SPIDER</span><span> </span></span><span data-contrast="auto" xml:lang="EN-US" lang="EN-US"><span>resumed operations following law enforcement actions that disrupted their infrastructure</span></span><span data-contrast="auto" xml:lang="EN-GB" lang="EN-GB"><span>.</span></span><span data-contrast="auto" <h3><span data-ccp-parastyle="heading 1">Conti Leak Revealed the Connection Between </span><span data-ccp-parastyle="heading 1">LUNAR SPIDER</span><span data-ccp-parastyle="heading 1"> and </span><span data-ccp-parastyle="heading 1">WIZARD SPIDER </span><span data-ccp-parastyle="heading 1">members</span></h3>
<p>EclecticIQ analysts assess with high confidence that, based on leaked Conti ransomware group communications that was published in 2022, LUNAR SPIDER has established significant connections within the cybercrime ecosystem [<a href="#a10" rel="noopener">10</a>]. They have very likely provided initial access to ransomware operators such as WIZARD SPIDER [<a href="#a11" rel="noopener">11</a>], the Russia-based group behind the TrickBot [<a href="#a12" rel="noopener">12</a>] malware and the Conti Ransomware-as-a-Service (RaaS) [<a href="#a13" rel="noopener">13</a>]. This collaboration between LUNAR SPIDER and WIZARD SPIDER has facilitated ransomware campaigns by sharing tools and infrastructures like IcedID and other services for evading EDR/AV detection. &nbsp;<br><br>The LUNAR SPIDER group was previously led by Vyacheslav Igorevich Penchukov [<a href="#a14" rel="noopener">14</a>], also known by several aliases including Tank, Zeus, Zevs, Father, and TopBro. Penchukov was a key figure in LUNAR SPIDER’s operations before his arrest in Switzerland in September 2022. Figure 2 shows the leaked conversation between Russian speaking threat actors angelo and manuel, very likely the developers inside the Conti Ransomware as a service (RaaS). Translated conversation revealed that LUNAR SPIDER leader Zeus (Penchukov) was their partner.&nbsp;<br><br>Despite his extradition to the United States and sentencing to 18 years in prison in 2024, LUNAR SPIDER continues to operate, adapting to leadership changes and law enforcement actions with resilience. &nbsp;</p>

<p><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559739&quot;:160,&quot;335559740&quot;:279}">Analysts assess with high confidence that LUNAR SPIDER maintains affiliations with other ransomware groups, including Nemty [<a href="#a15" rel="noopener">15</a>] (aka: TRAVELING SPIDER) and TA2101 (aka: TWISTED SPIDER) [<a href="#a16" rel="noopener">16</a>], which have leveraged LUNAR SPIDER's malware IcedID to gain initial access to victim environments. These collaborations further emphasize LUNAR SPIDER's central role as an initial access broker in the cybercrime ecosystem.&nbsp;<br>&nbsp;</span></p>

<h3><span data-contrast="auto" xml:lang="EN-GB" lang="EN-GB"><span>LUNAR SPIDER Threat Actor Switched from IcedID to Brute Ratel C4 Malware &nbsp;<br></span></span></h3>
<p><span data-contrast="auto" xml:lang="EN-GB" lang="EN-GB"><span>EclecticIQ analysts assess with high confidence that LUNAR SPIDER has shifted tactics, moving away from their previous use of IcedID (BokBot) to now leveraging Latrodectus and Brute Ratel C4 malware.&nbsp;<br><br>Analysts have uncovered that the threat actor group LUNAR SPIDER is behind over 200 malicious infrastructures (figure 4) associated with both the IcedID and Latrodectus malware families. While these malware operations were previously considered separate, they share significant overlaps in their underlying infrastructure. For instance, both use SSL certificates with nearly identical issuer details like "AU," "Some-State," and "Internet Widgits Pty Ltd." Additionally, LUNAR SPIDER consistently employs the same service providers, such as SHOCK-1 (ASN 395092), across both campaigns. This consistent use of shared providers and similar infrastructure highlights how LUNAR SPIDER is efficiently coordinating its malicious activities across different malware families.&nbsp;<br></span></span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559739&quot;:160,&quot;335559740&quot;:279}">&nbsp;</span></p>

<h3><span>Tracking Latrodectus Infrastructures &nbsp;<br></span></h3>
<p><span>Analysts utilized the EclecticIQ Threat Intelligence Platform (TIP), Intelligence Center, to extract malicious infrastructures that were linked to Latrodectus. <h3>IcedID Malware Enables ALPHV Ransomware Attack, Revealing Shared Infrastructure with LUNAR SPIDER&nbsp;</h3>
<p>In a campaign observed in October 2023, threat actors linked to ALPHV (also known as BlackCat) executed a Ransomware attack by using IcedID malware as the initial compromise vector. [<a href="#a17" rel="noopener">17</a>] The operation began with a spam campaign delivering a version of IcedID through a malicious ZIP file containing a Visual Basic Script (VBS). Upon execution, the IcedID loader installed itself, and the attackers used Impacket’s wmiexec [<a href="#a18" rel="noopener">18</a>] and RDP for lateral movement, deploying ScreenConnect across systems. The campaign further escalated with the deployment of Cobalt Strike beacons for command-and-control (C2) purposes and the use of the CSharp Streamer RAT [<a href="#a19" rel="noopener">19</a>] to exfiltrate credentials and sensitive data via tools like Rclone [<a href="#a20" rel="noopener">20</a>]. Eight days after the initial breach, ALPHV ransomware was deployed across all domain-joined Windows systems, leading to successful data encryption and a ransom note being left behind.&nbsp;</p>
<p><a href="https://www.eclecticiq.com/hubfs/_blogs/corporate-blog/2024/Lunar%20Spider/image007.png" rel="noopener"><img src="https://blog.eclecticiq.com/hs-fs/hubfs/_blogs/corporate-blog/2024/Lunar%20Spider/image007.png?width=717&amp;height=392&amp;name=image007.png" width="717" height="392" loading="lazy" alt="image007" style="height: auto; max-width: 100%; width: 717px; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto; display: block;" srcset="https://blog.eclecticiq.com/hs-fs/hubfs/_blogs/corporate-blog/2024/Lunar%20Spider/image007.png?width=359&amp;height=196&amp;name=image007.png 359w, https://blog.eclecticiq.com/hs-fs/hubfs/_blogs/corporate-blog/2024/Lunar%20Spider/image007.png?width=717&amp;height=392&amp;name=image007.png 717w, https://blog.eclecticiq.com/hs-fs/hubfs/_blogs/corporate-blog/2024/Lunar%20Spider/image007.png?width=1076&amp;height=588&amp;name=image007.png 1076w, https://blog.eclecticiq.com/hs-fs/hubfs/_blogs/corporate-blog/2024/Lunar%20Spider/image007.png?width=1434&amp;height=784&amp;name=image007.png 1434w, https://blog.eclecticiq.com/hs-fs/hubfs/_blogs/corporate-blog/2024/Lunar%20Spider/image007.png?width=1793&amp;height=980&amp;name=image007.png 1793w, https://blog.eclecticiq.com/hs-fs/hubfs/_blogs/corporate-blog/2024/Lunar%20Spider/image007.png?width=2151&amp;height=1176&amp;name=image007.png 2151w" sizes="(max-width: 717px) 100vw, 717px"></a></p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559739&quot;:0,&quot;335559740&quot;:259}"><span style="font-size: 14px;"><span data-contrast="auto" xml:lang="EN-GB" lang="EN-GB">Figure 7 – Possible infrastructure sharing between LUNAR SPIDER <br>and ALPHV/BlackCat Ransomware</span></span></span>.</p>
<p style="text-align: left;"><span data-contrast="auto" style="font-size: 0.875rem; background-color: transparent;">EclecticIQ analysts have uncovered evidence suggesting a very likely connection between LUNAR SPIDER and ALPHV/BlackCat ransomware affiliates. The domain peronikilinfer[.]com, which serves as a command-and-control (C2) server for Latrodectus malware in September 2024 - developed and managed by LUNAR SPIDER - was hosted on the IP address 173[.]255[.]204.[]62. In October 2023, ALPHV/BlackCat used another domain, jkbarmossen[.]com, also hosted on the same IP address and functioning as a C2 server for IcedID, another malware family developed and managed by LUNAR SPIDER. This overlapping use of the same infrastructures and malware usage emphasizes that both IcedID and Latrodectus are central to LUNAR SPIDER's operations. The shared infrastructure indicates that LUNAR SPIDER's malware is enabling ALPHV/BlackCat's ransomware activities, highlighting a collaborative relationship between these groups.&nbsp;<br><br>The reuse of infrastructure and overlapping command-and-control assets, evidenced by passive DNS records, reinforces the theory of coordination between LUNAR SPIDER and ALPHV/BlackCat. LUNAR SPIDER likely facilitated initial access through IcedID, which was then leveraged by ALPHV/BlackCat operators to deploy ransomware and exfiltrate sensitive data. These connections, supported by passive DNS evidence, highlight the operational synergy between the two groups, further bolstering the assessment of shared tactics, techniques, and infrastructure.&nbsp;</span></p>
<h3 style="text-align: left;"><span data-contrast="auto" style="font-size: 0.875rem; background-color: transparent;"><span style="font-size: 17px;">Latrodectus Malware Targets Financial Services via SEO Poisoning to Deliver Brute Ratel C4&nbsp;</span></span></h3>
<p style="text-align: left;"><span data-contrast="auto" style="font-size: 0.875rem; background-color: transparent;">EclecticIQ analysts observed a Latrodectus downloader variant in a SEO poisoning <span data-contrast="auto" style="font-size: 0.875rem; background-color: transparent;">malvertising</span> campaign against financial services to download and execute the Brute Ratel C4 malware. After execution of Brute Ratel C4, the malware communicates through the command-and-control server very likely owned by LUNAR SPIDER members and give them remote access to victim devices.&nbsp;<br><br>Figure 8 illustrates the attack flow of the malvertising campaign, which leveraged an SEO poisoning technique to deliver its payload. SEO poisoning involves manipulating search engine rankings to display malicious links prominently, tricking users into clicking them. In this case, victims searching for tax-related content on the Bing browser were redirected to download a malicious, obfuscated JavaScript file named Document-16-32-50.js.&nbsp;</span></p>

<p aria-level="1" style="text-align: left;"><span>Upon execution, the JavaScript file retrieved a Windows Installer (MSI) from a remote server, which installed the Brute Ratel malware. The MSI file, downloaded from 45[.]14[.]244[.]124/dsa.msi, executed via the rundll32.exe process, disguising the malicious DLL (vierm_soft_x64.dll) as a legitimate NVIDIA file.&nbsp;<br><br><span style="font-weight: bold;">Persistence Mechanism and Command &amp; Control (C2):&nbsp;</span><br>To establish persistence, the malware created a registry key entry under:&nbsp;<br><br>HKEY_CURRENT_USER\Software\Microsoft\Windows\CurrentVersion\Run&nbsp;<br><br>This enabled it to remain active even after system reboots.
