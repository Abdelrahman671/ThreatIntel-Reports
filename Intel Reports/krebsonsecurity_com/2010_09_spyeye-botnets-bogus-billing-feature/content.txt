					<h1 class="entry-title">SpyEye Botnet&#8217;s Bogus Billing Feature</h1>
				</div>
							
			
			</header><!-- .entry-header -->

	<div id="primary" class="site-content">
		<div id="content" role="main">

			
					<article id="post-4063" class="post-4063 post type-post status-publish format-standard hentry category-other category-web-fraud-2-0 tag-billinghammer tag-clickbank tag-digital-river tag-esellerate tag-fastspring tag-setsystems tag-spyeye">
				<header class="entry-header">
					<div class="clear"></div>
	<div class="btm-wrap">
	<div class="below-title-meta">
		<div class="adt">
			
			
				
				<span class="date updated">September 17, 2010</span>
					
        </div>
		<div class="adt-comment">
		<span><a class="link-comments" href="https://krebsonsecurity.com/2010/09/spyeye-botnets-bogus-billing-feature/#comments">11 Comments</a></span> 
        </div>    <div class="clear"></div>	   
    </div><!-- below title meta end -->
	</div>
		
						</header><!-- .entry-header -->
		
		
							<div class="entry-content">
												<p>Miscreants who control large groupings of hacked PCs or &#8220;botnets&#8221; are always looking for ways to better monetize their crime machines, and competition among rival bot developers is leading to devious innovations. The <strong>SpyEye</strong> botnet kit, for example, now not only allows botnet owners to automate the extraction of credit card and other financial data from infected systems, but it also can be configured to use those credentials to generate bogus sales at online stores set up by the botmaster.</p>
<p><a class="lightbox" href="https://krebsonsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2010/09/esellerate.png"><img decoding="async" class="alignright size-medium wp-image-5112" title="esellerate" src="https://krebsonsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2010/09/esellerate-300x223.png" alt="The &quot;billing&quot; section from SpyEye admin page" width="300" height="223" srcset="https://krebsonsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2010/09/esellerate-300x223.png 300w, https://krebsonsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2010/09/esellerate.png 743w" sizes="(max-width: 300px) 100vw, 300px" /></a>As I noted in <a href="http://krebsonsecurity.com/2010/04/spyeye-vs-zeus-rivalry/" target="_blank">a post in April</a>, SpyEye is a software package that promises to make running a botnet a point-and-click exercise. A unique component of SpyEye is a feature called &#8220;billinghammer,&#8221; which automates the purchase of worthless or copycat software using credit card data stolen from victims of the botnet.</p>
<p>The SpyEye author explained this feature in detail on several hacking forums where his kit is sold, even including a video that walks customers through the process of setting it up. Basically, the scam works like this: The botmaster acquires some freeware utility or legitimate program, renames it, claims it as his own and places it up for sale at one of several pre-selected software sales and distribution platforms, including <a href="http://www.clickbank.com/" target="_blank">ClickBank</a>, <a href="http://www.fastspring.com/" target="_blank">FastSpring</a>, <a href="http://www.esellerate.net/" target="_blank">eSellerate</a>, <a href="http://www.setsystems.com/" target="_blank">SetSystems</a>, or <a href="http://www.shareit.com/" target="_blank">Shareit</a>.  The botmaster then logs in to his SpyEye control panel (picture above), feeds it a list of credit card numbers and corresponding cardholder data, after which SpyEye opens an Internet Explorer Window and &#8212; at user-defined intervals &#8212; starts auto-filling the proper fields at the botmaster&#8217;s online store and making purchases.</p>
<p><span id="more-4063"></span></p>
<p><a class="lightbox" href="https://krebsonsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2010/09/spyeeyegrabber.jpg"><img decoding="async" loading="lazy" class="alignleft size-medium wp-image-5122" title="spyeeyegrabber" src="https://krebsonsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2010/09/spyeeyegrabber-295x300.jpg" alt="" width="295" height="300" srcset="https://krebsonsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2010/09/spyeeyegrabber-295x300.jpg 295w, https://krebsonsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2010/09/spyeeyegrabber.jpg 746w" sizes="(max-width: 295px) 100vw, 295px" /></a>The billinghammer module also is set up to evade anti-fraud controls at the online software stores, by funneling each transaction through a SpyEye-infected system whose Internet address traces back to a  geographic location that approximates the cardholder&#8217;s street addresss.</p>
<p>In the video that shows how to use this portion of the bot kit, it appears that SpyEye customers have the option either to make sales at their own stores, or to use some that are apparently set up by the author of the bot kit himself.</p>
<p>In an e-mail to KrebsOnSecurity.com, FastSpring&#8217;s chief customer service officer Ken White said: &#8220;We understand what this system tries to do, and how the bad guys attempt to use it to convert stolen cards into cash.  We haven&#8217;t yet been exploited successfully and believe we have a good system in place to prevent it.&#8221;</p>
<p>All other software sales and distribution systems coded into the SpyEye bot kit are entities operated by <strong>Digital River</strong>, which did not respond to repeated requests for comment. It&#8217;s not clear how many &#8212; if any &#8212; SpyEye customers are using the billinghammer plug-in. But assuming that there are some scammers out there abusing these services through SpyEye, it seems that it would be a great way to catch botmasters in the act. After all, the check or wire transfer for any bogus software sales has to be sent somewhere.</p>

				<p>Nice article that skims the surface of how hackers monetize the hack and try to circumvent anti-fraud tools of the various companies.  </p>
<p>For the readers, a tool like this can be used to run traffic and sign ups through affiliate networks to cost per action (CPA) advertising campaigns.  In the past, given that the networks get a % of the CPA price for the traffic, networks did little to monitor this.  Now a number of the networks, realizing in order to stay active in the industry and win the big clients are hiring in experts/firms to address fraud and abuse of the tracking systems. (full disclosure- My firm Online Intelligence is one such group)</p>
<p>Though affiliate networks do not have access to the credit cards being used to make purchases, they do have data to see a pattern of fraud.  Even though a package like this rotates through various IP&#8217;s, the time and date of the clicks/leads, the user agent of the computer behind the click and the duration on site are all patterns that indicate a automated/bot is responsible for the leads.</p>
<p>Also if the merchant/advertiser runs the input credit cards and notifies the affiliate network of declinations within 48-72 hours rather than retrying the cards over and over in the hopes the sale will go through, the affialite accounts involved in these schemes can be identified and stopped.  </p>
<p>Here is the progression, Hacker steals cards and creates bot network, hacker works with friend or becomes an affiliate marketer himself, hacker runs tracffic through affilaite account to offer using stolen cards.   Hacker makes between $2-$50 per bogus sign up.</p>
<p>If the affiliate network and the end merchant work together to review the data collected  during the clickthru order path, these fraudsters will be ferreted out.</p>
<p>For those readers attending the DOD cyber crime conference, I will be doing a presentation that walks the audience through the full monetization of the hack form data to cash and how to detect and track the money makers.</p>
