    <h1 class="page-headline">Life on a crooked RedLine: Analyzing the infamous infostealer’s backend</h1>    <p class="sub-title">Following the takedown of RedLine Stealer by international authorities, ESET researchers are publicly releasing their research into the infostealer’s backend modules</p>
    
    <div class="article-authors d-flex flex-wrap"><div class="article-author d-flex"><a href="/en/our-experts/alexandre-cote-cyr/" title="Alexandre Côté Cyr"><picture><source srcset="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/tn/-x45/wls/2022/03/alexandre-cote-cyr.jpg" media="(max-width: 768px)" /><img class="author-image me-3"  src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/tn/-x45/wls/2022/03/alexandre-cote-cyr.jpg" alt="Alexandre Côté Cyr"  /></picture></a><div class="author-text"><p><a href="/en/our-experts/alexandre-cote-cyr/" title="Alexandre Côté Cyr"><b>Alexandre Côté Cyr</b></a></p></div></div></div>

                    <div class="article-body">
    <p>On October 28<sup>th</sup>, 2024, the Dutch National police, alongside the FBI, Eurojust, and several other law enforcement organizations, performed a <a href="https://www.eurojust.europa.eu/news/malware-targeting-millions-people-taken-down-international-coalition" target="_blank" rel="noopener">takedown </a>of the infamous RedLine Stealer malware-as-a-service (MaaS) operation, and its clone called META Stealer. This global effort, named <a href="https://operation-magnus.com/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Operation Magnus</a>, resulted in the takedown of three servers in the Netherlands, the seizure of two domains, two people being taken into custody in Belgium, and the unsealing of charges against one of the alleged perpetrators in the United States.</p>
<p>Back in April 2023, ESET participated in a partial <a href="https://x.com/ESETresearch/status/1647916167770537986" target="_blank" rel="noopener">disruption operation</a> of the RedLine malware, which consisted of the removal of several GitHub repositories used as dead-drop resolvers for the malware&rsquo;s control panel. Around that time, we investigated previously undocumented backend modules of this malware family in collaboration with fellow researchers at <a href="https://flare.io/">Flare</a>. These modules don&rsquo;t interact directly with the malware, but rather handle authentication and provide functionality for the control panel.</p>
<p>Since RedLine has now been taken down, we are revealing our findings from 2023 publicly, along with some more recent discoveries that were made based on source code and samples shared with us by the Dutch National Police.</p>
<p>Note that even with the takedown, RedLine may continue working to some degree for a time: panels that were already running would still be able to receive data, even if unable to build new samples or reconnect after logging out. Moreover, there are also old, cracked copies of the malware that might still work. Because of these factors, we are describing the RedLine operation as if it is an ongoing activity.</p>
<blockquote>
<div><strong>Key points of the blogpost:</strong></div>
<ul>
<li>In 2023, ESET researchers, in collaboration with law enforcement, collected multiple modules used to run the infrastructure behind RedLine Stealer.</li>
<li>We analyzed these previously undocumented modules to provide insight into the internal workings of this malware-as-a-service empire.</li>
<li>We were able to identify over 1,000 unique IP addresses used to host RedLine control panels.</li>
<li>The 2023 versions of RedLine Stealer we investigated in detail used the Windows Communication Framework for communication between the components, while the latest version from 2024 uses a REST API.</li>
<li>Based on our analysis of the source code and backend samples, we have determined that Redline Stealer and META Stealer share the same creator.</li>
</ul>
</blockquote>
<p>RedLine Stealer is information stealing malware first discovered in 2020 by <a href="https://www.proofpoint.com/us/blog/threat-insight/new-redline-stealer-distributed-using-coronavirus-themed-email-campaign" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Proofpoint</a>. Rather than being centrally operated, RedLine operates on a MaaS model in which anyone can buy a turnkey infostealer solution from various online forums and Telegram channels. Clients, called affiliates, can opt for a monthly subscription, or a lifetime license; in exchange for their money, they get a control panel that generates malware samples and acts as a C&amp;C server for them. The generated samples can collect a large variety of information, including local cryptocurrency wallets; cookies, saved credentials, and saved credit card details from browsers; and saved data from Steam, Discord, Telegram, and various desktop VPN applications.</p>
<p>Using a ready-made solution makes it easier for the affiliates to integrate RedLine Stealer into larger campaigns. Some notable examples include posing as <a href="https://www.darkreading.com/cyberattacks-data-breaches/attackers-hide-redline-stealer-behind-chatgpt-google-bard-facebook-ads" target="_blank" rel="noopener">free downloads</a> of ChatGPT in 2023, and <a href="https://www.bleepingcomputer.com/news/security/fake-cheat-lures-gamers-into-spreading-infostealer-malware/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">masquerading</a> as video game cheats in the first half of 2024.</p>
<blockquote>
<div><strong>Note on terminology used</strong></div>
<div>&nbsp;</div>
<div>Because of its MaaS model, any comprehensive discussion of RedLine will involve multiple different components and layers of network infrastructure. To limit any possible confusion, we will use the following terms consistently throughout the text:</div>
<ul>
<li><strong>RedLine malware</strong>: The RedLine Stealer malware or a sample thereof.</li>
<li><strong>RedLine panel</strong>: GUI control panel used to manage infostealing campaigns.</li>
<li><strong>RedLine backend</strong>: Collection of modules that provide authentication and functionality for the RedLine panel.</li>
<li><strong>RedLine</strong>: The whole malware operation. This includes the RedLine malware, the RedLine panel, and the RedLine backend modules.</li>
<li><strong>Backend server</strong>: A server on which the RedLine backend runs.</li>
<li><strong>Victim</strong>: Entity targeted with the RedLine malware.</li>
<li><strong>Operator</strong>: The individual or team that develops RedLine, sells licenses, and operates the licensing and associated backend infrastructure.</li>
<li><strong>Affiliate</strong>: Entity that operates infostealing campaigns via an instance of the RedLine panel. They usually have a license bought from the operator, but may also use a cracked version of the panel.</li>
</ul>
</blockquote>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>In this blogpost we document modules running on RedLine&rsquo;s backend servers to provide a greater understanding of the inner workings of this MaaS empire. We also provide some information on the RedLine panel. Figure 1 contains a simplified overview of the whole RedLine operation.</p>
<p>Ordinarily, known samples of RedLine panel distributed to affiliates are heavily packed and virtualized after the first layer of obfuscation is applied. But as we were looking through our telemetry for activity related to RedLine Stealer and its panel, we came across an old version of the RedLine panel that was only obfuscated with <a href="https://www.eziriz.com/dotnet_reactor.htm" target="_blank" rel="noopener">.NET Reactor</a>, making it much easier to analyze.</p>

<h2>RedLine&rsquo;s toolset</h2>
<p>All the components of RedLine, from the malware itself to the backend authentication server, are written in C# with the .NET framework. The versions we analyzed used the <a href="https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/dotnet/framework/wcf/whats-wcf" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Windows Communication Foundation</a> (WCF) framework to communicate with each other. This framework allows one to define an <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/API" target="_blank" rel="noopener">API</a> using <a href="https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/dotnet/framework/wcf/feature-details/contracts" target="_blank" rel="noopener">contracts</a>, which are statements applied to classes and interfaces to specify how objects and actions are translated between their representation in the code and the network communications. Thus, programs that interact using this framework must share definitions of these data structures or classes. These shared models proved useful in our analysis of the obfuscated components.</p>
<p>Note that the latest 2024 RedLine version uses a REST API instead of WCF to communicate with the backend.</p>
<h3>RedLine panel</h3>
<p>This control panel is what affiliates can buy on forums and Telegram channels. Licenses sell for US$150 per month or US$900 for a lifetime license. In exchange for the money, the affiliates get a GUI through which they manage their campaigns, with features to configure what information to collect, create malware samples, view and manage collected information, and integrate with a Telegram bot to sell stolen information.</p>
<p>The 2023 versions of the panel we investigated were heavily obfuscated using <a href="http://dnguard.net/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">DNGuard</a>, a well-known .NET obfuscator, and <a href="https://www.boxedapp.com/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">BoxedApp</a>, a commercial packer and virtualization library. Luckily for us, RedLine used the WCF framework so the panel had to share some classes and interfaces with other components. By analyzing these shared elements from the malware and backend components, which are less protected, we were able to understand a lot of the panel&rsquo;s functionality despite the protections.</p>
<p>All RedLine panels from 2023 were signed with certificates issued to <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">AMCERT,LLC</span> by Sectigo, which have since been revoked after we reported them. <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">AMCERT,LLC</span> corresponds to a company registered in Armenia that doesn&rsquo;t seem to have an online presence &ndash; quite unusual for a purported software developer.</p>

<p>In order to use a RedLine panel, affiliates must first authenticate. Figure 2 shows the login prompt.</p>

<p>The RedLine panels we analyzed use GitHub repositories, like the one in Figure 3, as dead-drop resolvers for their authentication servers. <h3>RedLine backend</h3>
<p>The RedLine backend we analyzed in 2023 consists of two modules. The first one, named <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">RedLine.Nodes.DbController</span>, manages affiliate and advertisement data. Despite its name, this module doesn&rsquo;t use a traditional database, but rather stores records as <a href="https://protobuf.dev/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Protobuf</a>-encoded objects in individual files, with a specific subdirectory for each type of data. The second module, named <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">RedLine.Nodes.LoadBalancer</span>, functions as the server and provides most of the functionality used by the RedLine panel.</p>
<p>We also found a module called <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">RedLine.MainServer</span>, which is probably the ancestor of <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">RedLine.Nodes.DbController</span> and <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">RedLine.Nodes.LoadBalancer</span>. In later versions, it was split off as a separate module that handles user and advertisement data.</p>
<p>In the 2024 version of the backend, <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">DbController</span> and <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">LoadBalancer</span> have been replaced by a single module named <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">Nodes.Api</span>.</p>

<p>Since it was first documented in 2020, RedLine Stealer has been rewritten to use the WCF framework, and later a REST API, for network communication. As seen in Figure 11, the internal name for these rewritten versions appears to be <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">RedLine.Reburn</span>.</p>
<figure class="image"><img title="Figure 11. Code to obtain the path to the RedLine.Reburn solution" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2024/11-2024/redline/figure-11.png" alt="Figure 11. Code to obtain the path to the RedLine.Reburn solution" width="" height="" />

<p>To create samples of the RedLine Stealer malware, the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">OnCreateLastBuild</span> handler shown in Figure 10 uses the <a name="_Hlk130209963"></a><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">CreateBuild</span> method from the custom <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">VSBuilder</span> class. When a <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">CreateBuild</span> request is received, the cleartext password for the affiliate&rsquo;s account is logged to the console. This shows a casual disregard for well-known security practices.</p>
<p>If in the request no port is specified for the C&amp;C server, the builder defaults to port <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">6677</span>. When building a sample, the code in Figure 12 is used to derive a token from the campaign&rsquo;s Build ID and the affiliate account&rsquo;s Build Key. We believe this corresponds to the value of the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">ns1.Authorization</span> header used in communication between the samples and panel. This header seems to be used by the panel to filter out connections from samples not created by the current account.</p>
<figure class="image"><img title="Figure 12. Code used to derive token values for RedLine Stealer samples" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2024/11-2024/redline/figure-12.png" alt="Figure 12. Code used to derive token values for RedLine Stealer samples" width="" height="" />

<p>The <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">VSBuilder.CreateBuild</span> method uses a local C# solution to create samples. Some of the source files are read into memory to replace specific values before being written back to disk. While we were unable to collect the project&rsquo;s files, the decompiler output for this method along with that of a RedLine Stealer sample provide enough context to understand that the modified values are:</p>
<ul>
<li>C&amp;C address (encrypted),</li>
<li>Build ID (encrypted),</li>
<li>fake error message (encrypted),</li>
<li>decryption key,</li>
<li>flag to indicate whether to send stolen information in parts or all at once,</li>
<li>authentication token, and</li>
<li>assembly name.</li>
</ul>
<p>Once these values are replaced, Visual Studio is invoked to build the project.</p>
<p>If the affiliate has selected the corresponding option, and has a lifetime license, the compiled executable is obfuscated with the commercially available <a href="https://www.babelfor.net/products/babel-obfuscator/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Babel Obfuscator</a>. In the 2024 version, obfuscation is implemented for all users and is done via <a href="https://www.eziriz.com/dotnet_reactor.htm" target="_blank" rel="noopener">.NET Reactor</a> instead.</p>
<p>Finally, a self-signed certificate is generated with the code from Figure 13, and used to sign the executable. The fields of its Distinguished Name (DN) are filled with random dictionary words. The certificates generated this way use the hardcoded password <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">123321</span> and have a validity period extending from seven days before their generation to 10 years after.</p>
<figure class="image"><img title="Figure 13. <p>The <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">LoadBalancer</span> module also provided an <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">OnCreateClipper</span> handler. As the name suggests, this was used to generate clipboard hijacking malware; in this case, it was used to hijack cryptocurrency transactions by replacing wallet addresses in the clipboard with those of attacker-controlled wallets. This functionality has been removed in the latest versions of the Redline backend.</p>
<p>Interestingly, the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">Builder</span> class also contained dead code to generate a malware sample from a stub executable. We believe this was a leftover from the method used to generate previous versions of RedLine Stealer. The latest version of the Redline backend does not contain the code anymore.</p>

<h4>RedLine.MainServer</h4>
<p>The <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">RedLine.MainServer</span> module combines some of the functionality of the two previous modules with a GUI that allows the admin to easily manage affiliate accounts and advertisement data. Despite this added functionality, multiple factors indicate that this version of <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">MainServer</span> is probably an ancestor of <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">LoadBalancer</span> and <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">DbController</span> rather than a successor:</p>
<ul>
<li><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">MainServer</span> samples are compiled with an older version of the .NET framework (4.6.2 vs 4.8).</li>
<li>The assembly&rsquo;s copyright year is 2020 instead of 2021.</li>
<li>Some <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">MainServer</span> samples do not contain functionality for advertisements.</li>
<li>The main WCF service&rsquo;s contract (<span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">MainPanelService</span>) does not include the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">IsAlive</span> handler that is present in the same class as <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">LoadBalancer</span>. This handler is used by all the most recent RedLine panels we have analyzed.</li>
</ul>

<p>Unlike the 2023 versions of the backend we originally analyzed, lifetime licenses no longer appear in the code of the new module. Since licenses of this type were still being sold, we believe they were likely handled by simply setting their expiration to a date far in the future. Functionality related to code signing has also been completely removed.</p>
<p>Samples created by the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">Nodes.Api</span> module are obfuscated using <a href="https://www.eziriz.com/dotnet_reactor.htm" target="_blank" rel="noopener">.NET Reactor</a> if it is present at the hardcoded path <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">C:\Program Files (x86)\Eziriz\.NET Reactor\dotNET_Reactor.exe</span>. This corresponds to its default installation path.</p>
<p>Beyond the aforementioned changes, features that were present in older versions behave much as they did before. However, a couple of interesting functionalities have been added.</p>
<h4>Affiliate management</h4>
<p>Three endpoints used to manage affiliate data are exposed. These appear to be meant for use only by the operator, but this is handled in two very different ways. The <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">/secure-api/updateDb</span> endpoint causes the server to reload user data from disk. Figure 18 shows the update method invoked by the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">MainServer</span> module whenever a new entry is created or modified. The operation is only performed by the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">Nodes.Api</span> module if the request comes from a loopback address, which prevents Panel users from using it.</p>
<figure class="image"><img title="Figure 18. Method invoked by the MainServer module" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2024/11-2024/redline/figure-18.png" alt="Figure 18. Method invoked by the MainServer module" width="" height="" />

As seen in Figure 19, they can only be invoked if the request contains an <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">sko3s</span> header set to a seemingly random hardcoded value.</p>

<p>The other interesting feature is the ability to back up affiliate data, functionality that was missing in the older versions of the code. Since this data is stored in a series of files on disk, the backup is a ZIP archive of the directory that contains the files. This archive is then sent via Telegram using a hardcoded Chat ID shown in Figure 20.</p>

<h3>Links with META Stealer</h3>
<p>META Stealer is the other infostealer disrupted alongside RedLine Stealer. According to an <a href="https://www.kelacyber.com/blog/information-stealers-a-new-landscape/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">article by Kela,</a> META Stealer was first announced on cybercrime forums in March 2022. The author claimed that it used the same code as RedLine Stealer and provided the same functionality and panel. META Stealer hasn&rsquo;t been investigated as thoroughly as RedLine Stealer, but our research indicates that the claims it initially made are accurate. Based on the source code, the two infostealers are most probably made by the same person. While there are some differences, most of the code is the same with instances of the string <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">RedLine</span> replaced by <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">Meta</span>. As can be seen in Figure 21, the code that is commented out to be inactive in RedLine is present in META.</p>
<figure class="image"><img title="Figure 21. RedLine Stealer (left), and META Stealer (right) source code comparison" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2024/11-2024/redline/figure-21.png" alt="Figure 21. RedLine Stealer (left), and META Stealer (right) source code comparison" width="" height="" />
<figcaption><em>Figure 21. RedLine Stealer (left), and META Stealer (right) source code comparison</em></figcaption>
</figure>
<p>As we already mentioned, another piece of evidence pointing towards RedLine and META having the same operators is seen in the code of the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">Nodes.Api</span> module, which handles requests sent to both RedLine and META servers, as shown in Figure 17.</p>
<p>We also found two samples of the META panel signed with a certificate that was also used to sign samples of the RedLine panel. This panel used the same dead-drop resolver schemes, going as far as using the same AES and RSA keys, only with a different GitHub repository. Additionally, as Figure 22 shows, a comparison of the panels used by META and RedLine reveals only minor cosmetic differences.</p>
<figure class="image"><img title="Figure 22. Panel login prompts for RedLine (top) and META (below)" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2024/11-2024/redline/figure-22.png" alt="Figure 22. Panel login prompts for RedLine (top) and META (below)" width="" height="" />
<figcaption><em>Figure 22. Panel login prompts for RedLine (top) and META (below)</em></figcaption>
</figure>
<p>META Stealer uses the same combination of DNGuard and BoxedApp to protect its panel from analysis. However, META&rsquo;s authentication process could not be completed when run against our instance of the RedLine backend from 2023, so it seems to have been modified from that of RedLine.</p>

Figure 23 shows the geographical distribution of these hosted panels. Russia, Germany, and the Netherlands each account for about 20% of the total, while Finland and the United States each represent around 10%. Even though this data only comes from samples targeting our customers, we believe it paints a fairly accurate picture with regards to the overall distribution of the malware.</p>

<h3>Dead-drop resolvers</h3>
<p>As we already mentioned, the 2023 versions of the RedLine panel used GitHub repositories as dead-drop resolvers for its authentication servers. These repositories all contained a file with an encrypted list of server addresses. The file was encrypted using a custom module simply named <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">RSA</span>. In one version of this module, shown in Figure 25, the list was encrypted using AES-CBC with a hardcoded key and IV, and saved to a file named <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">nodesUpdate.config</span>.</p>
<figure class="image"><img title="Figure 25. EncryptHosts function with hardcoded AES key and IV" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2024/11-2024/redline/figure-25.png" alt="Figure 25. EncryptHosts function with hardcoded AES key and IV" width="" height="" />
<figcaption><em>Figure 25. </em><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">EncryptHosts</span><em> function with hardcoded AES key and IV</em></figcaption>
</figure>
<p>More recent versions, which were in use at least until mid 2024, employ RSA encryption instead (see Figure 26), with the output written to a file named <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">nodes.config</span>. In this case, the key is read from a file rather than being hardcoded in the executable. However, the class used to perform RSA encryption is also present in the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">LoadBalancer</span> module, with hardcoded default values for the public and private keys. Note the use of &ldquo;nodes&rdquo; again to refer to the backend servers.</p>
<figure class="image"><img title="Figure 26. RSA encryption and decryption functions with hardcoded default keys" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2024/11-2024/redline/figure-26.png" alt="Figure 26. RSA encryption and decryption functions with hardcoded default keys" width="" height="" />
<figcaption><em>Figure 26. RSA encryption and decryption functions with hardcoded default keys</em></figcaption>
</figure>
<p>We have observed GitHub repositories that contain at least one such encrypted file. Along with the keys shown above, we were able to extract an RSA private key from a sample of the panel. This allowed us to obtain lists of authentication servers, which we shared with law enforcement agencies.</p>
<h2>Conclusion</h2>
<p>Before Operation Magnus, RedLine was among the most widespread of infostealer malware with a very large number of affiliates using its control panel. However, the malware-as-a-service enterprise seems to be orchestrated by only a handful of people, some of whom have been identified by law enforcement.</p>
<p>META Stealer, RedLine Stealer&rsquo;s clone and most probably created by the same threat actors, made its entry into the field in 2022. It does not appear to be a successor to RedLine, since the development of both families has continued in parallel. It was taken down alongside RedLine Stealer.</p>
<p>Our in-depth analysis of the behind-the-scenes parts of RedLine &ndash; its control panel and backend &ndash; hopefully provides a more holistic understanding of this threat.</p>
