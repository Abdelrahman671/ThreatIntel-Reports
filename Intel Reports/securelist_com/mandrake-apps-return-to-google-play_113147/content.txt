													<h2 id="introduction">Introduction</h2>
<p>In May 2020, Bitdefender released a <a href="https://www.bitdefender.com/blog/labs/mandrake-owning-android-devices-since-2016/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">white paper</a> containing a detailed analysis of Mandrake, a sophisticated Android cyber-espionage platform, which had been active in the wild for at least four years.</p>
<p>In April 2024, we discovered a suspicious sample that appeared to be a new version of Mandrake. Ensuing analysis revealed as many as five Mandrake applications, which had been available on Google Play from 2022 to 2024 with more than 32,000 installs in total, while staying undetected by any other vendor. The new samples included new layers of obfuscation and evasion techniques, such as moving malicious functionality to obfuscated native libraries, using certificate pinning for C2 communications, and performing a wide array of tests to check if Mandrake was running on a rooted device or in an emulated environment.</p>
<p>Our findings, in a nutshell, were as follows.</p>
<ul>
<li>After a two-year break, the Mandrake Android spyware returned to Google Play and lay low for two years.</li>
<li>The threat actors have moved the core malicious functionality to native libraries obfuscated with <a href="https://github.com/obfuscator-llvm/obfuscator/wiki" target="_blank" rel="noopener">OLLVM</a>.</li>
<li>Communication with command-and-control servers (C2) uses certificate pinning to prevent capture of SSL traffic.</li>
<li>Mandrake is equipped with a diverse arsenal of sandbox evasion and anti-analysis techniques.</li>
</ul>
<p>Kaspersky products detect this threat as 
			<span id="crayon-6719ae8860965350029347" class="crayon-syntax crayon-syntax-inline  crayon-theme-classic crayon-theme-classic-inline crayon-font-monaco" style="font-size: 12px !important; line-height: 15px !important;font-size: 12px !important;"><span class="crayon-pre crayon-code" style="font-size: 12px !important; line-height: 15px !important;font-size: 12px !important; -moz-tab-size:4; -o-tab-size:4; -webkit-tab-size:4; tab-size:4;"><span class="crayon-i">HEUR</span><span class="crayon-sy">:</span><span class="crayon-i">Trojan</span>-<span class="crayon-i">Spy</span><span class="crayon-sy">.</span><span class="crayon-i">AndroidOS</span><span class="crayon-sy">.</span><span class="crayon-i">Mandrake</span><span class="crayon-sy">.</span>*</span></span>.</p>
<h2 id="technical-details">Technical details</h2>
<h3 id="background">Background</h3>
<p>The original Mandrake campaign with its two major infection waves, in 2016–2017 and 2018–2020, was analyzed by Bitdefender in May 2020. After the Bitdefender report was published, we <a href="https://x.com/sh1shk0va/status/1261260464681553924" target="_blank" rel="noopener">discovered</a> one more sample associated with the campaign, which was still available on Google Play.</p>
<div id="attachment_113150" style="width: 1034px" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><a href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2024/07/22140014/Mandrake_01.png" class="magnificImage"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" aria-describedby="caption-attachment-113150" class="size-large wp-image-113150" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2024/07/22140014/Mandrake_01-1024x802.png" alt="The Mandrake application from the previous campaign on Google Play" width="1024" height="802" srcset="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2024/07/22140014/Mandrake_01-1024x802.png 1024w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2024/07/22140014/Mandrake_01-300x235.png 300w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2024/07/22140014/Mandrake_01-768x601.png 768w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2024/07/22140014/Mandrake_01-447x350.png 447w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2024/07/22140014/Mandrake_01-740x580.png 740w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2024/07/22140014/Mandrake_01-358x280.png 358w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2024/07/22140014/Mandrake_01-800x627.png 800w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2024/07/22140014/Mandrake_01.png 1425w" sizes="(max-width: 1024px) 100vw, 1024px" /></a><p id="caption-attachment-113150" class="wp-caption-text">The Mandrake application from the previous campaign on Google Play</p></div>
<p>In April 2024, we found a suspicious sample that turned out to be a new version of Mandrake. The main distinguishing feature of the new Mandrake variant was layers of obfuscation designed to bypass Google Play checks and hamper analysis. We discovered five applications containing Mandrake, with more than 32,000 total downloads. All these were published on Google Play in 2022 and remained available for at least a year. The newest app was last updated on March 15, 2024 and removed from Google Play later that month. As at July 2024, none of the apps had been detected as malware by any vendor, according to VirusTotal.</p>

<h3 id="infection-chain">Infection chain</h3>
<p>Like the previous versions of Mandrake described by Bitdefender, applications in the latest campaign work in stages: dropper, loader and core. Unlike the previous campaign where the malicious logic of the first stage (<strong>dropper</strong>) was found in the application DEX file, the new versions hide all the first-stage malicious activity inside the native library 
			<span id="crayon-6719ae8860970222219555" class="crayon-syntax crayon-syntax-inline  crayon-theme-classic crayon-theme-classic-inline crayon-font-monaco" style="font-size: 12px !important; line-height: 15px !important;font-size: 12px !important;"><span class="crayon-pre crayon-code" style="font-size: 12px !important; line-height: 15px !important;font-size: 12px !important; -moz-tab-size:4; -o-tab-size:4; -webkit-tab-size:4; tab-size:4;"><span class="crayon-i">libopencv_dnn</span><span class="crayon-sy">.</span><span class="crayon-i">so</span></span></span>, which is harder to analyze and detect than DEX files. This library exports functions to decrypt the next stage (loader) from the 
			<span id="crayon-6719ae8860971133084276" class="crayon-syntax crayon-syntax-inline  crayon-theme-classic crayon-theme-classic-inline crayon-font-monaco" style="font-size: 12px !important; line-height: 15px !important;font-size: 12px !important;"><span class="crayon-pre crayon-code" style="font-size: 12px !important; line-height: 15px !important;font-size: 12px !important; -moz-tab-size:4; -o-tab-size:4; -webkit-tab-size:4; tab-size:4;"><span class="crayon-i">assets</span>/<span class="crayon-i">raw</span></span></span> folder.</p>
<div id="attachment_113157" style="width: 195px" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><a href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2024/07/22140500/Mandrake_08.png" class="magnificImage"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" aria-describedby="caption-attachment-113157" class="size-full wp-image-113157" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2024/07/22140500/Mandrake_08.png" alt="Contents of the main APK file" width="185" height="292" srcset="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2024/07/22140500/Mandrake_08.png 185w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2024/07/22140500/Mandrake_08-177x280.png 177w" sizes="(max-width: 185px) 100vw, 185px" /></a><p id="caption-attachment-113157" class="wp-caption-text">Contents of the main APK file</p></div>
<p>Interestingly, the sample 
			<span id="crayon-6719ae8860972758483283" class="crayon-syntax crayon-syntax-inline  crayon-theme-classic crayon-theme-classic-inline crayon-font-monaco" style="font-size: 12px !important; line-height: 15px !important;font-size: 12px !important;"><span class="crayon-pre crayon-code" style="font-size: 12px !important; line-height: 15px !important;font-size: 12px !important; -moz-tab-size:4; -o-tab-size:4; -webkit-tab-size:4; tab-size:4;"><span class="crayon-i">com</span><span class="crayon-sy">.</span><span class="crayon-i">shrp</span><span class="crayon-sy">.</span><span class="crayon-i">sght</span></span></span> has only two stages, where the loader and core capabilities are combined into one APK file, which the dropper decrypts from its assets.</p>
<p>While in the past Mandrake campaigns we saw different branches (&#8220;oxide&#8221;, &#8220;briar&#8221;, &#8220;ricinus&#8221;, &#8220;darkmatter&#8221;), the current campaign is related to the &#8220;ricinus&#8221; branch. The second- and third-stage files are named &#8220;ricinus_airfs_3.4.0.9.apk&#8221;, &#8220;ricinus_dropper_core_airfs_3.4.1.9.apk&#8221;, &#8220;ricinus_amber_3.3.8.2.apk&#8221; and so on.</p>
<p>When the application starts, it loads the native library:</p>
<div id="attachment_113158" style="width: 468px" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><a href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2024/07/22140528/Mandrake_09.png" class="magnificImage"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" aria-describedby="caption-attachment-113158" class="size-full wp-image-113158" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2024/07/22140528/Mandrake_09.png" alt="Loading the native library" width="458" height="90" srcset="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2024/07/22140528/Mandrake_09.png 458w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2024/07/22140528/Mandrake_09-300x59.png 300w" sizes="(max-width: 458px) 100vw, 458px" /></a><p id="caption-attachment-113158" class="wp-caption-text">Loading the native library</p></div>
<p>To make detection harder, the first-stage native library is heavily obfuscated with the <a href="https://github.com/obfuscator-llvm/obfuscator/wiki" target="_blank" rel="noopener">OLLVM obfuscator</a>. Its main goal is to decrypt and load the second stage, named &#8220;<strong>loader</strong>&#8220;. After unpacking, decrypting and loading into memory the second-stage DEX file, the code calls the method 
			<span id="crayon-6719ae8860974616966145" class="crayon-syntax crayon-syntax-inline  crayon-theme-classic crayon-theme-classic-inline crayon-font-monaco" style="font-size: 12px !important; line-height: 15px !important;font-size: 12px !important;"><span class="crayon-pre crayon-code" style="font-size: 12px !important; line-height: 15px !important;font-size: 12px !important; -moz-tab-size:4; -o-tab-size:4; -webkit-tab-size:4; tab-size:4;"><span class="crayon-i">dex_load</span></span></span> and executes the second stage. In this method, the second-stage native library path is added to the class loader, and the second-stage main activity and service start. The application then shows a notification that asks for permission to draw overlays.</p>
<p>When the main service starts, the second-stage native library 
			<span id="crayon-6719ae8860975678823915" class="crayon-syntax crayon-syntax-inline  crayon-theme-classic crayon-theme-classic-inline crayon-font-monaco" style="font-size: 12px !important; line-height: 15px !important;font-size: 12px !important;"><span class="crayon-pre crayon-code" style="font-size: 12px !important; line-height: 15px !important;font-size: 12px !important; -moz-tab-size:4; -o-tab-size:4; -webkit-tab-size:4; tab-size:4;"><span class="crayon-i">libopencv_java3</span><span class="crayon-sy">.</span><span class="crayon-i">so</span></span></span> is loaded, and the certificate for C2 communications, which is placed in the second-stage assets folder, is decrypted. The treat actors used an IP address for C2 communications, and if the connection could not be established, the malware tried to connect to more domains. After successfully connecting, the app sends information about the device, including the installed applications, mobile network, IP address and unique device ID, to the C2. If the threat actors find their target relevant on the strength of that data, they respond with a command to download and run the &#8220;core&#8221; component of Mandrake. The app then downloads, decrypts and executes the third stage (<strong>core</strong>), which contains the main malware functionality.</p>

<p>When Mandrake receives a 
			<span id="crayon-6719ae8860976518453046" class="crayon-syntax crayon-syntax-inline  crayon-theme-classic crayon-theme-classic-inline crayon-font-monaco" style="font-size: 12px !important; line-height: 15px !important;font-size: 12px !important;"><span class="crayon-pre crayon-code" style="font-size: 12px !important; line-height: 15px !important;font-size: 12px !important; -moz-tab-size:4; -o-tab-size:4; -webkit-tab-size:4; tab-size:4;"><span class="crayon-i">start_v</span></span></span> command, the service starts and loads the specified URL in an application-owned webview with a custom JavaScript interface, which the application uses to manipulate the web page it loads.</p>
<p>While the page is loading, the application establishes a websocket connection and starts taking screenshots of the page at regular intervals, while encoding them to base64 strings and sending these to the C2 server. The attackers can use additional commands to adjust the frame rate and quality. The threat actors call this &#8220;vnc_stream&#8221;.  At the same time, the C2 server can send back control commands that make application execute actions, such as swipe to a given coordinate, change the webview size and resolution, switch between the desktop and mobile page display modes, enable or disable JavaScript execution, change the User Agent, import or export cookies, go back and forward, refresh the loaded page, zoom the loaded page and so on.</p>
<p>When Mandrake receives a 
			<span id="crayon-6719ae8860978482344549" class="crayon-syntax crayon-syntax-inline  crayon-theme-classic crayon-theme-classic-inline crayon-font-monaco" style="font-size: 12px !important; line-height: 15px !important;font-size: 12px !important;"><span class="crayon-pre crayon-code" style="font-size: 12px !important; line-height: 15px !important;font-size: 12px !important; -moz-tab-size:4; -o-tab-size:4; -webkit-tab-size:4; tab-size:4;"><span class="crayon-i">start_i</span></span></span> command, it loads a URL in a webview, but instead of initiating a &#8220;VNC&#8221; stream, the C2 server starts recording the screen and saving the record to a file. The recording process is similar to the &#8220;VNC&#8221; scenario, but screenshots are saved to a video file. Also in this mode, the application waits until the user enters their credentials on the web page and then collects cookies from the webview.</p>
<p>The 
			<span id="crayon-6719ae8860979253000354" class="crayon-syntax crayon-syntax-inline  crayon-theme-classic crayon-theme-classic-inline crayon-font-monaco" style="font-size: 12px !important; line-height: 15px !important;font-size: 12px !important;"><span class="crayon-pre crayon-code" style="font-size: 12px !important; line-height: 15px !important;font-size: 12px !important; -moz-tab-size:4; -o-tab-size:4; -webkit-tab-size:4; tab-size:4;"><span class="crayon-i">start_a</span></span></span> command allows running automated actions in the context of the current page, such as swipe, click, etc. If this is the case, Mandrake downloads automation scenarios from the URL specified in the command options. In this mode, the screen is also recorded.</p>
<p>Screen recordings can be uploaded to the C2 with the 
			<span id="crayon-6719ae886097b133828103" class="crayon-syntax crayon-syntax-inline  crayon-theme-classic crayon-theme-classic-inline crayon-font-monaco" style="font-size: 12px !important; line-height: 15px !important;font-size: 12px !important;"><span class="crayon-pre crayon-code" style="font-size: 12px !important; line-height: 15px !important;font-size: 12px !important; -moz-tab-size:4; -o-tab-size:4; -webkit-tab-size:4; tab-size:4;"><span class="crayon-i">upload_i</span></span></span> or 
			<span id="crayon-6719ae886097c440613645" class="crayon-syntax crayon-syntax-inline  crayon-theme-classic crayon-theme-classic-inline crayon-font-monaco" style="font-size: 12px !important; line-height: 15px !important;font-size: 12px !important;"><span class="crayon-pre crayon-code" style="font-size: 12px !important; line-height: 15px !important;font-size: 12px !important; -moz-tab-size:4; -o-tab-size:4; -webkit-tab-size:4; tab-size:4;"><span class="crayon-i">upload_d</span></span></span> commands.</p>
<p>The main goals of Mandrake are to steal the user&#8217;s credentials, and download and execute next-stage malicious applications.</p>
<h3 id="data-decryption-methods">Data decryption methods</h3>
<p>Data encryption and decryption logic is similar across different Mandrake stages. In this section, we will describe the second-stage data decryption methods.</p>
<p>The second-stage native library 
			<span id="crayon-6719ae886097d616120068" class="crayon-syntax crayon-syntax-inline  crayon-theme-classic crayon-theme-classic-inline crayon-font-monaco" style="font-size: 12px !important; line-height: 15px !important;font-size: 12px !important;"><span class="crayon-pre crayon-code" style="font-size: 12px !important; line-height: 15px !important;font-size: 12px !important; -moz-tab-size:4; -o-tab-size:4; -webkit-tab-size:4; tab-size:4;"><span class="crayon-i">libopencv_java3</span><span class="crayon-sy">.</span><span class="crayon-i">so</span></span></span> contains AES-encrypted C2 domains, and keys for configuration data and payload decryption. Encrypted strings are mixed with plain text strings.</p>
<p>To get the length of the string, Mandrake XORs the first three bytes of the encrypted array, then uses the first two bytes of the array as keys for custom XOR encoding.</p>
<div id="attachment_113159" style="width: 457px" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><a href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2024/07/22140621/Mandrake_10.png" class="magnificImage"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" aria-describedby="caption-attachment-113159" class="size-full wp-image-113159" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2024/07/22140621/Mandrake_10.png" alt="Strings decryption algorithm" width="447" height="463" srcset="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2024/07/22140621/Mandrake_10.png 447w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2024/07/22140621/Mandrake_10-290x300.png 290w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2024/07/22140621/Mandrake_10-338x350.png 338w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2024/07/22140621/Mandrake_10-270x280.png 270w" sizes="(max-width: 447px) 100vw, 447px" /></a><p id="caption-attachment-113159" class="wp-caption-text">Strings decryption algorithm</p></div>
<p>The key and IV for decrypting AES-encrypted data are encoded in the same way, with part of the data additionally XORed with constants.</p>
<div id="attachment_113160" style="width: 753px" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><a href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2024/07/22142349/Mandrake_11.png" class="magnificImage"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" aria-describedby="caption-attachment-113160" class="size-full wp-image-113160" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2024/07/22142349/Mandrake_11.png" alt="AES key decryption" width="743" height="400" srcset="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2024/07/22142349/Mandrake_11.png 743w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2024/07/22142349/Mandrake_11-300x162.png 300w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2024/07/22142349/Mandrake_11-650x350.png 650w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2024/07/22142349/Mandrake_11-740x398.png 740w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2024/07/22142349/Mandrake_11-520x280.png 520w" sizes="(max-width: 743px) 100vw, 743px" /></a><p id="caption-attachment-113160" class="wp-caption-text">AES key decryption</p></div>
<p>Mandrake uses the OpenSSL library for AES decryption, albeit in quite a strange way. The encrypted file is divided into 16-byte blocks, each of these decrypted with AES-CFB128.</p>
<p>The encrypted certificate for C2 communication is located in the 
			<span id="crayon-6719ae886097f415303708" class="crayon-syntax crayon-syntax-inline  crayon-theme-classic crayon-theme-classic-inline crayon-font-monaco" style="font-size: 12px !important; line-height: 15px !important;font-size: 12px !important;"><span class="crayon-pre crayon-code" style="font-size: 12px !important; line-height: 15px !important;font-size: 12px !important; -moz-tab-size:4; -o-tab-size:4; -webkit-tab-size:4; tab-size:4;"><span class="crayon-i">assets</span>/<span class="crayon-i">raw</span></span></span> folder of the second stage as a file named 
			<span id="crayon-6719ae8860980609170282" class="crayon-syntax crayon-syntax-inline  crayon-theme-classic crayon-theme-classic-inline crayon-font-monaco" style="font-size: 12px !important; line-height: 15px !important;font-size: 12px !important;"><span class="crayon-pre crayon-code" style="font-size: 12px !important; line-height: 15px !important;font-size: 12px !important; -moz-tab-size:4; -o-tab-size:4; -webkit-tab-size:4; tab-size:4;"><span class="crayon-i">cart</span><span class="crayon-sy">.</span><span class="crayon-i">raw</span></span></span>, which is decrypted using the same algorithm.</p>
<h3 id="installing-next-stage-applications">Installing next-stage applications</h3>
<p>When Mandrake gets an 
			<span id="crayon-6719ae8860981583789368" class="crayon-syntax crayon-syntax-inline  crayon-theme-classic crayon-theme-classic-inline crayon-font-monaco" style="font-size: 12px !important; line-height: 15px !important;font-size: 12px !important;"><span class="crayon-pre crayon-code" style="font-size: 12px !important; line-height: 15px !important;font-size: 12px !important; -moz-tab-size:4; -o-tab-size:4; -webkit-tab-size:4; tab-size:4;"><span class="crayon-i">apk</span></span></span> command from the C2, it downloads a new separate APK file with an additional module and shows the user a notification that looks like something they would receive from Google Play. The user clicking the notification initiates the installation process.</p>
<p>Android 13 introduced the &#8220;Restricted Settings&#8221; feature, which prohibits sideloaded applications from directly requesting dangerous permissions. To bypass this feature, Mandrake processes the installation with a <a href="https://developer.android.com/reference/android/content/pm/PackageInstaller.Session" target="_blank" rel="noopener">&#8220;session-based&#8221; package installer</a>.</p>
<div id="attachment_113161" style="width: 642px" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><a href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2024/07/22142433/Mandrake_12.png" class="magnificImage"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" aria-describedby="caption-attachment-113161" class="size-full wp-image-113161" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2024/07/22142433/Mandrake_12.png" alt="Installing additional applications" width="632" height="337" srcset="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2024/07/22142433/Mandrake_12.png 632w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2024/07/22142433/Mandrake_12-300x160.png 300w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2024/07/22142433/Mandrake_12-525x280.png 525w" sizes="(max-width: 632px) 100vw, 632px" /></a><p id="caption-attachment-113161" class="wp-caption-text">Installing additional applications</p></div>
<h3 id="sandbox-evasion-techniques-and-environment-checks">Sandbox evasion techniques and environment checks</h3>
<p>While the main goal of Mandrake remains unchanged from past campaigns, the code complexity and quantity of the emulation checks have significantly increased in recent versions to prevent the code from being executed in environments operated by malware analysts. However, we were able to bypass these restrictions and discovered the changes described below.</p>
<p>The versions of the malware discovered earlier contained only a basic emulation check routine.</p>
<div id="attachment_113162" style="width: 704px" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><a href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2024/07/22142502/Mandrake_13.png" class="magnificImage"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" aria-describedby="caption-attachment-113162" class="size-full wp-image-113162" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2024/07/22142502/Mandrake_13.png" alt="Emulator checks in an older Mandrake version" width="694" height="105" srcset="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2024/07/22142502/Mandrake_13.png 694w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2024/07/22142502/Mandrake_13-300x45.png 300w" sizes="(max-width: 694px) 100vw, 694px" /></a><p id="caption-attachment-113162" class="wp-caption-text">Emulator checks in an older Mandrake version</p></div>
<p>In the new version, we discovered more checks.</p>
<p>To start with, the threat actors added Frida detection. When the application starts, it loads the first-stage native library 
			<span id="crayon-6719ae8860982198216643" class="crayon-syntax crayon-syntax-inline  crayon-theme-classic crayon-theme-classic-inline crayon-font-monaco" style="font-size: 12px !important; line-height: 15px !important;font-size: 12px !important;"><span class="crayon-pre crayon-code" style="font-size: 12px !important; line-height: 15px !important;font-size: 12px !important; -moz-tab-size:4; -o-tab-size:4; -webkit-tab-size:4; tab-size:4;"><span class="crayon-i">libopencv_dnn</span><span class="crayon-sy">.</span><span class="crayon-i">so</span></span></span>. The 
			<span id="crayon-6719ae8860984451637652" class="crayon-syntax crayon-syntax-inline  crayon-theme-classic crayon-theme-classic-inline crayon-font-monaco" style="font-size: 12px !important; line-height: 15px !important;font-size: 12px !important;"><span class="crayon-pre crayon-code" style="font-size: 12px !important; line-height: 15px !important;font-size: 12px !important; -moz-tab-size:4; -o-tab-size:4; -webkit-tab-size:4; tab-size:4;"><span class="crayon-i">init_array</span></span></span> section of this library contains the Frida detector function call. The threat actors used the <a href="https://github.com/darvincisec/DetectFrida" target="_blank" rel="noopener">DetectFrida method</a>. First, it computes the CRC of all libraries, then it starts a Frida detect thread. Every five seconds, it checks that libraries in memory have not been changed. Additionally, it checks for Frida presence by looking for specific thread and pipe names used by Frida. So, when an analyst tries to use Frida against the application, execution is terminated. Even if you use a custom build of Frida and try to hook a function in the native library, the app detects the code change and terminates.</p>
<p>Next, after collecting device information to make a request for the next stage, the application checks the environment to find out if the device is rooted and if there are analyst tools installed. Unlike some other threat actors who seek to take advantage of root access, Mandrake developers consider a rooted device dangerous, as average users, their targets, do not typically root their phones. First, Mandrake tries to find a su binary, a SuperUser.apk, Busybox or Xposed framework, and Magisk and Saurik Substrate files. Then it checks if the system partition is mounted as read-only. Next, it checks if development settings and ADB are enabled. And finally, it checks for the presence of a Google account and Google Play application on the device.</p>
<h3 id="c2-communication">C2 communication</h3>
<p>All C2 communications are maintained via the native part of the applications, using an OpenSSL static compiled library.</p>
<p>To prevent network traffic sniffing, Mandrake uses an encrypted certificate, decrypted from the 
			<span id="crayon-6719ae8860985528935615" class="crayon-syntax crayon-syntax-inline  crayon-theme-classic crayon-theme-classic-inline crayon-font-monaco" style="font-size: 12px !important; line-height: 15px !important;font-size: 12px !important;"><span class="crayon-pre crayon-code" style="font-size: 12px !important; line-height: 15px !important;font-size: 12px !important; -moz-tab-size:4; -o-tab-size:4; -webkit-tab-size:4; tab-size:4;"><span class="crayon-i">assets</span>/<span class="crayon-i">raw</span></span></span> folder, to secure C2 communications. The client needs to be verified by this certificate, so an attempt to capture SSL traffic results in a handshake failure and a breakdown in communications. Still, any packets sent to the C2 are saved locally for additional AES encryption, so we are able to look at message content. Mandrake uses a custom JSON-like serialization format, the same as in previous campaigns.</p>

id="crayon-6719ae8860987047347288-36"><span class="crayon-sy">}</span></div></div></td>
					</tr>
				</table>
			</div>
		</div><p>
Mandrake uses opcodes from 1000 to 1058. The same opcode can represent different actions depending on whether it is used for a request or a response. See below for examples of this.</p>
<ul>
<li>Request opcode <strong>1000</strong>: send device information;</li>
<li>Request opcode <strong>1003</strong>: send list of installed applications;</li>
<li>Request opcode <strong>1010</strong>: send information about the component;</li>
<li>Response opcode <strong>1002</strong>: set contact rate (client-server communication);</li>
<li>Response opcode <strong>1010</strong>: install next-stage APK;</li>
<li>Response opcode <strong>1011</strong>: abort next-stage install;</li>
<li>Response opcode <strong>1022</strong>: request user to allow app to run in background;</li>
<li>Response opcode <strong>1023</strong>: abort request to allow app to run in background;</li>
<li>Response opcode <strong>1027</strong>: change application icon to default or Wi-Fi service icon.</li>
</ul>
<h2 id="attribution">Attribution</h2>
<p>Considering the similarities between the current campaign and the previous one, and the fact that the C2 domains are registered in Russia, we assume with high confidence that the threat actor is the same as stated in the <a href="https://www.bitdefender.com/blog/labs/mandrake-owning-android-devices-since-2016/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Bitdefender&#8217;s report</a>.</p>
<h2 id="victims">Victims</h2>
<p>The malicious applications on Google Play were available in a wide range of countries. Most of the downloads were from Canada, Germany, Italy, Mexico, Spain, Peru and the UK.</p>
<h2 id="conclusions">Conclusions</h2>
<p>The Mandrake spyware is evolving dynamically, improving its methods of concealment, sandbox evasion and bypassing new defense mechanisms. After the applications of the first campaign stayed undetected for four years, the current campaign lurked in the shadows for two years, while still available for download on Google Play. This highlights the threat actors&#8217; formidable skills, and also that stricter controls for applications before being published in the markets only translate into more sophisticated, harder-to-detect threats sneaking into official app marketplaces.</p>
