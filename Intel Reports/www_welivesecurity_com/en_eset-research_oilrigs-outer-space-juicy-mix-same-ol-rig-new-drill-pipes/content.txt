    <h1 class="page-headline">OilRig’s Outer Space and Juicy Mix: Same ol’ rig, new drill pipes</h1>    <p class="sub-title">ESET researchers document OilRig’s Outer Space and Juicy Mix campaigns, targeting Israeli organizations in 2021 and 2022</p>
    
    <div class="article-authors d-flex flex-wrap"><div class="article-author d-flex"><a href="/en/our-experts/zuzana-hromcova/" title="Zuzana Hromcová"><picture><source srcset="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/tn/-x45/wls/2023/07/zuzana-hromcova-2.jpeg" media="(max-width: 768px)" /><img class="author-image me-3"  src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/tn/-x45/wls/2023/07/zuzana-hromcova-2.jpeg" alt="Zuzana Hromcová"  /></picture></a><div class="author-text"><p><a href="/en/our-experts/zuzana-hromcova/" title="Zuzana Hromcová"><b>Zuzana Hromcová</b></a></p></div></div><div class="article-author d-flex"><a href="/en/our-experts/adam-burgher/" title="Adam Burgher"><picture><source srcset="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/tn/-x45/wls/2021/06/Adam-Burgher.jpg" media="(max-width: 768px)" /><img class="author-image me-3"  src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/tn/-x45/wls/2021/06/Adam-Burgher.jpg" alt="Adam Burgher"  /></picture></a><div class="author-text"><p><a href="/en/our-experts/adam-burgher/" title="Adam Burgher"><b>Adam Burgher</b></a></p></div></div></div>

        <picture><source srcset="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/tn/-x266/wls/2023/2023-09/eset-threat-research-oilrig-outer-space-juicy-mix.jpeg" media="(max-width: 768px)" /><source srcset="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/tn/-x425/wls/2023/2023-09/eset-threat-research-oilrig-outer-space-juicy-mix.jpeg" media="(max-width: 1120px)" /><img class="hero-image"  src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/tn/-x700/wls/2023/2023-09/eset-threat-research-oilrig-outer-space-juicy-mix.jpeg" alt="OilRig’s Outer Space and Juicy Mix: Same ol’ rig, new drill pipes"  /></picture>    </div>

                    <div class="article-body">
    <p>ESET researchers have analyzed two campaigns by the OilRig APT group: Outer Space (2021), and Juicy Mix (2022). Both of these cyberespionage campaigns targeted Israeli organizations exclusively, which is in line with the group&rsquo;s focus on the Middle East, and used the same playbook: OilRig first compromised a legitimate website to use as a C&amp;C server and then used VBS droppers to deliver a C#/.NET backdoor to its victims, while also deploying a variety of post-compromise tools mostly used for data exfiltration on the target systems.</p>
<p>In their Outer Space campaign, OilRig used a simple, previously undocumented C#/.NET backdoor we named Solar, along with a new downloader, SampleCheck5000 (or SC5k), that uses the Microsoft Office Exchange Web Services API for C&amp;C communication. For the Juicy Mix campaign, the threat actors improved on Solar to create the Mango backdoor, which possesses additional capabilities and obfuscation methods. In addition to detecting the malicious toolset, we also notified the Israeli CERT about the compromised websites.</p>
<blockquote>
<p><em><strong>Key points of this blogpost:</strong></em></p>
<ul>
<li class="Bullets1CxSpFirst"><em>ESET observed two OilRig campaigns which occurred throughout 2021 (Outer Space) and 2022 (Juicy Mix).</em></li>
<li class="Bullets1CxSpMiddle"><em>The operators exclusively targeted Israeli organizations and compromised legitimate Israeli websites for use in their C&amp;C communications.</em></li>
<li class="Bullets1CxSpMiddle"><em>They used a new, previously undocumented C#/.NET first-stage backdoor in each campaign: Solar in Outer Space, then its successor Mango in Juicy Mix.</em></li>
<li class="Bullets1CxSpMiddle"><em>Both backdoors were deployed by VBS droppers, presumably spread via spearphishing emails.</em></li>
<li class="Bullets1CxSpLast"><em>A variety of post-compromise tools were deployed in both campaigns, notably the SC5k downloader that uses Microsoft Office Exchange Web Services API for C&amp;C communication, and several tools to steal browser data and credentials from Windows Credential Manager.</em></li>
</ul>
</blockquote>
<p>OilRig, also known as APT34, Lyceum, or Siamesekitten, is a cyberespionage group that has been active since at least 2014 and <a href="https://attack.mitre.org/groups/G0049/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">is commonly believed</a> to be based in Iran. The group targets Middle Eastern governments and a variety of business verticals, including chemical, energy, financial, and telecommunications. OilRig carried out the DNSpionage campaign in <a href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/2018/11/dnspionage-campaign-targets-middle-east.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener">2018</a> and <a href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/2019/04/dnspionage-brings-out-karkoff.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener">2019</a>, which targeted victims in Lebanon and the United Arab Emirates. In 2019 and 2020, OilRig continued attacks with the <a href="https://www.mandiant.com/resources/hard-pass-declining-apt34-invite-to-join-their-professional-network" target="_blank" rel="noopener">HardPass</a> campaign, which used LinkedIn to target Middle Eastern victims in the energy and government sectors. In 2021, OilRig updated its <a href="https://vblocalhost.com/uploads/VB2021-Kayal-etal.pdf">DanBot</a> backdoor and began deploying the <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20230129145433/https:/www.prevailion.com/latest-targets-of-cyber-group-lyceum/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Shark, Milan</a>, and Marlin backdoors, mentioned in the <a href="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/en/papers/threat-reports/eset_threat_report_t32021.pdf" target="_blank" rel="noopener">T3 2021 issue</a> of the ESET Threat Report.</p>
<p>In this blogpost, we provide technical analysis of the Solar and Mango backdoors, of the VBS dropper used to deliver Mango, and of the post-compromise tools deployed in each campaign.</p>
<h2><a name="_Toc143163831"></a>Attribution</h2>
<p>The initial link that allowed us to connect the Outer Space campaign to OilRig is the use of the same custom Chrome data dumper (tracked by ESET researchers under the name MKG) as in the <a href="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/en/papers/threat-reports/eset_threat_report_t32021.pdf" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Out to Sea campaign</a>. We observed the Solar backdoor deploy the very same sample of MKG as in Out to Sea on the target&rsquo;s system, along with two other variants.</p>
<p>Besides the overlap in tools and targeting, we also saw multiple similarities between the Solar backdoor and the backdoors used in Out to Sea, mostly related to upload and download: both Solar and Shark, another OilRig backdoor, use URIs with simple upload and download schemes to communicate with the C&amp;C server, with a &ldquo;d&rdquo; for download and a &ldquo;u&rdquo; for upload; additionally, the downloader SC5k uses uploads and downloads subdirectories just like other OilRig backdoors, namely ALMA, Shark, DanBot, and Milan. These findings serve as a further confirmation that the culprit behind Outer Space is indeed OilRig.</p>
<p>As for the Juicy Mix campaign&rsquo;s ties to OilRig, besides targeting Israeli organizations &ndash; which is typical for this espionage group &ndash; there are code similarities between Mango, the backdoor used in this campaign, and Solar. Moreover, both backdoors were deployed by VBS droppers with the same string obfuscation technique. The choice of post-compromise tools employed in Juicy Mix also mirrors previous OilRig campaigns.</p>
<h2><a name="_Toc143163832"></a>Outer Space campaign overview</h2>
<p>Named for the use of an astronomy-based naming scheme in its function names and tasks, Outer Space is an OilRig campaign from 2021. In this campaign, the group compromised an Israeli human resources site and subsequently used it as a C&amp;C server for its previously undocumented C#/.NET backdoor, Solar. Solar is a simple backdoor with basic functionality such as reading and writing from disk, and gathering information.</p>
<p>Through Solar, the group then deployed a new downloader SC5k, which uses the Office Exchange Web Services API to download additional tools for execution, as shown in <!-- [if supportFields]><span

<h2><a name="_Toc143163833"></a>Juicy Mix campaign overview</h2>
<p>In 2022 OilRig launched another campaign targeting Israeli organizations, this time with an updated toolset. We named the campaign Juicy Mix for the use of a new OilRig backdoor, Mango (based on its internal assembly name, and its filename, <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">Mango.exe</span>). In this campaign, the threat actors compromised a legitimate Israeli job portal website for use in C&amp;C communications. The group&rsquo;s malicious tools were then deployed against a healthcare organization, also based in Israel.</p>
<p>The Mango first-stage backdoor is a successor to Solar, also written in C#/.NET, with notable changes that include exfiltration capabilities, use of native APIs, and added detection evasion code.</p>
<p>Along with Mango, we also detected two previously undocumented browser-data dumpers used to steal cookies, browsing history, and credentials from the Chrome and Edge browsers, and a Windows Credential Manager stealer, all of which we attribute to OilRig. These tools were all used against the same target as Mango, as well as at other compromised Israeli organizations throughout 2021 and 2022. <!-- [if supportFields]><span

<h2><span style="mso-bookmark: _Toc143163834;">Technical analysis</span></h2>
<p>In this section, we provide a technical analysis of the Solar and Mango backdoors and the SC5k downloader, as well as other tools that were deployed to the targeted systems in these campaigns.</p>
<h3><a name="_Toc143163835"></a>VBS droppers</h3>
<p>To establish a foothold on the target&rsquo;s system, Visual Basic Script (VBS) droppers were used in both campaigns, which were very likely spread by spearphishing emails. Our analysis below focuses on the VBS script used to drop Mango (SHA-1: <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">3699B67BF4E381847BF98528F8CE2B966231F01A</span>); note that Solar&rsquo;s dropper is very similar.</p>
<p>The dropper&rsquo;s purpose is to deliver the embedded Mango backdoor, schedule a task for persistence, and register the compromise with the C&amp;C server. The embedded backdoor is stored as a series of base64 substrings, which are concatenated and base64 decoded. As shown in <!-- [if supportFields]><span
lang=EN-US><span style='mso-element:field-begin'></span><span
style='mso-spacerun:yes'> </span>REF _Ref125477632 \h <span style='mso-element:
field-separator'></span></span><![endif]-->Figure <span style="mso-no-proof: yes;">3</span><!-- [if gte mso 9]><xml>
</xml><![endif]--><!-- [if supportFields]><span
style='mso-element:field-end'></span></span><![endif]-->, the script also uses a simple string deobfuscation technique, where strings are assembled using arithmetic operations and the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">Chr</span> function.</p>
<figure class="image"><img style="width: 50%; margin: 0 auto; display: block;" title="" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2023/2023-8/figure-03-mango-string-obfuscation.png" alt="Figure_03_Mango_string_obfuscation" />
<figcaption><em>Figure 3. String deobfuscation technique used by OilRig&rsquo;s VBS dropper for Mango</em></figcaption>
</figure>
<p>On top of that, Mango&rsquo;s VBS dropper adds another type of string obfuscation and code to set up persistence and register with the C&amp;C server. As shown in <!-- [if supportFields]><span
lang=EN-US><span style='mso-element:field-begin'></span><span
style='mso-spacerun:yes'> </span>REF _Ref125479004 \h<span
style='mso-spacerun:yes'>  </span>\* MERGEFORMAT <span style='mso-element:field-separator'></span></span><![endif]-->Figure <span style="mso-no-proof: yes;">4</span><!-- [if gte mso 9]><xml>
</xml><![endif]--><!-- [if supportFields]><span
style='mso-element:field-end'></span></span><![endif]-->, to deobfuscate some strings, the script replaces any characters in the set <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">#*+-_)(}{@$%^&amp;</span> with <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">0</span>, then divides the string into three-digit numbers that are then converted into ASCII characters using the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">Chr</span><code> </code>function. For example, the string <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">116110101109117+99111$68+77{79$68}46-50108109120115}77</span> translates to <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">Msxml2.DOMDocument</span>.</p>
<figure class="image"><img style="width: 50%; margin: 0 auto; display: block;" title="" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2023/2023-8/figure-03-mango-string-obfuscation-1.png" alt="Figure_03_Mango_string_obfuscation" width="" height="" />
<figcaption><em>Figure 4. String obfuscation function used by Mango&rsquo;s VBS dropper</em></figcaption>
</figure>
<p>Once the backdoor is embedded on the system, the dropper moves on to create a scheduled task that executes Mango (or Solar, in the other version) every 14 minutes. Finally, the script sends a base64-encoded name of the compromised computer via a POST request to register the backdoor with its C&amp;C server.</p>
<h3><a name="_Toc143163836"></a>Solar backdoor</h3>
<p>Solar is the backdoor used in OilRig&rsquo;s Outer Space campaign. Possessing basic functionalities, this backdoor can be used to, among other things, download and execute files, and automatically exfiltrate staged files.</p>
<p>We chose the name Solar based on the filename used by OilRig, <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">Solar.exe</span>. It is a fitting name since the backdoor uses an astronomy naming scheme for its function names and tasks used throughout the binary (<span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">Mercury</span>, <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">Venus</span>, <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">Mars</span>, <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">Earth</span>, and <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">Jupiter</span>).</p>
<p>Solar begins execution by performing the steps shown in <!-- [if supportFields]><span
style='mso-element:field-begin'></span><span
style='mso-spacerun:yes'> </span>REF _Ref98146919 \h <span
style='mso-spacerun:yes'> </span>\* MERGEFORMAT <span style='mso-element:field-separator'></span></span><![endif]-->Figure <span style="mso-no-proof: yes;">5</span><!-- [if gte mso 9]><xml>
</xml><![endif]--><!-- [if supportFields]><span
style='mso-element:field-end'></span></span><![endif]-->.</p>
<figure class="image"><img title="" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2023/2023-8/figure-03-mango-string-obfuscation-1-2.png" alt="Figure_03_Mango_string_obfuscation" width="" height="" />
<figcaption><em>Figure 5. Initial execution flow of Solar</em></figcaption>
</figure>
<p>The backdoor creates two tasks, <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">Earth</span><code> </code>and <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">Venus</span>, that run in memory. There is no stop function for either of the two tasks, so they will run indefinitely. <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">Earth</span><code> </code>is scheduled to run every 30 seconds and <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">Venus</span><code> </code>is set to run every 40 seconds.</p>
<p><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">Earth</span> is the primary task, responsible for the bulk of Solar&rsquo;s functions. It communicates with the C&amp;C server using the function <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">MercuryToSun</span>, which sends basic system and malware version information to the C&amp;C server and then handles the server&rsquo;s response. <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">Earth</span> sends the following info to the C&amp;C server:</p>
<ul>
<li class="Bullets1CxSpFirst">The string <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">(@) &lt;system hostname&gt;</span>; the whole string is encrypted.</li>
<li class="Bullets1CxSpMiddle">The string <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">1.0.0.0</span>, encrypted (possibly a version number).</li>
<li class="Bullets1CxSpLast">The string <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">30000</span>, encrypted (possibly the scheduled runtime of <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">Earth</span><code> </code>in milliseconds).</li>
</ul>
<p>Encryption and decryption are implemented in functions named <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">JupiterE</span><code> </code>and <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">JupiterD</span>, respectively. Both of them call a function named <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">JupiterX</span>, which implements an XOR loop as shown in <!-- [if supportFields]><span
lang=EN-US><span style='mso-element:field-begin'></span><span
style='mso-spacerun:yes'> </span>REF _Ref98146962 \h <span style='mso-element:
field-separator'></span></span><![endif]-->Figure <span style="mso-no-proof: yes;">6</span><!-- [if gte mso 9]><xml>
</xml><![endif]--><!-- [if supportFields]><span
style='mso-element:field-end'></span></span><![endif]-->.</p>
<figure class="image"><img title="" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2023/2023-8/figure-03-mango-string-obfuscation.jpeg" alt="Figure_03_Mango_string_obfuscation" width="" height="" />
<figcaption><em>Figure 6. The </em><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">for </span><em>loop in JupiterX that is used to encrypt and decrypt data</em></figcaption>
</figure>
<p>The key is derived from a hardcoded global string variable, <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">6sEj7*0B7#7</span>, and a <a href="https://csrc.nist.gov/glossary/term/nonce" target="_blank" rel="noopener">nonce</a>: in this case, a random hex string from 2&ndash;24 characters long. Following the XOR encryption, standard base64 encoding is applied.</p>
<p>An Israeli human resources company&rsquo;s web server, which OilRig compromised at some point before deploying Solar, was used as the C&amp;C server:</p>
<p><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">http://organization.co[.]il/project/templates/office/template.aspx?rt=d&amp;sun=&lt;encrypted_MachineGuid&gt;&amp;rn=&lt;encryption_nonce&gt;</span></p>
<p>Prior to being appended to the URI, the encryption nonce is encrypted, and the value of the initial query string, <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">rt</span>, is set to <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">d</span> here, likely for &ldquo;download&rdquo;.</p>
<p>The last step of the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">MercuryToSun</span><code> </code>function is to process a response from the C&amp;C server. It does so by retrieving a substring of the response, which is found between the characters <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">QQ@</span> and <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">@kk</span>. This response is a string of instructions separated by asterisks (<span class="Code1">*</span>) that is processed into an array. <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">Earth</span><code> </code>then carries out the backdoor commands, which include downloading additional payloads from the server, listing files on the victim&rsquo;s system, and running specific executables.</p>
<p>Command output is then gzip compressed using the function <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">Neptune</span><code> </code>and encrypted with the same encryption key and a new nonce. Then the results are uploaded to the C&amp;C server, thus:</p>
<p><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">http://organization.co[.]il/project/templates/office/template.aspx?rt=u&amp;sun=&lt;MachineGuid&gt;&amp;rn=&lt;new_nonce&gt;</span></p>
<p><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">MachineGuid</span> and the new nonce are encrypted with the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">JupiterE</span><code> </code>function, and here the value of <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">rt</span> is set to <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">u</span>, likely for &ldquo;upload&rdquo;.</p>
<p><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">Venus</span>, the other scheduled task, is used for automated data exfiltration. This small task copies the content of files from a directory (also named <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">Venus</span>) to the C&amp;C server. These files are likely dropped here by some other, as yet unidentified, OilRig tool. After uploading a file, the task deletes it from disk.</p>
<h3><a name="_Toc143163837"></a>Mango backdoor</h3>
<p>For its Juicy Mix campaign, OilRig switched from the Solar backdoor to Mango. It has a similar workflow to Solar and overlapping capabilities, but there are nevertheless several notable changes:</p>
<ul>
<li class="Bullets1CxSpFirst">Use of TLS for C&amp;C communications.</li>
<li class="Bullets1CxSpMiddle">Use of native APIs, rather than .NET APIs, to execute files and shell commands.</li>
<li class="Bullets1CxSpMiddle"><!--[endif]-->Although not actively used, detection evasion code was introduced.</li>
<li class="Bullets1CxSpMiddle">Support for automated exfiltration (<span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">Venus</span><code> </code>in Solar) has been removed; instead, Mango supports an additional backdoor command for exfiltrating selected files.</li>
<li class="Bullets1CxSpLast">Support for log mode has been removed, and symbol names have been obfuscated.</li>
</ul>
<p><span lang="EN-US" style="mso-no-proof: yes;">Contrary to Solar&rsquo;s astronomy-themed naming scheme, Mango obfuscates its symbol names, as can be seen in </span><!-- [if supportFields]><span lang=EN-US style='mso-no-proof:

<figure class="image"><img title="" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2023/2023-8/figure-03-mango-string-obfuscation-1-2-3-4.png" alt="Figure_03_Mango_string_obfuscation" width="" height="" />

<p>Similar to Solar, the Mango backdoor starts by creating an in-memory task, scheduled to run indefinitely every 32 seconds. This task communicates with the C&amp;C server and executes backdoor commands, similar to Solar&rsquo;s <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">Earth</span><code> </code>task. While Solar also creates <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">Venus</span>, a task for automated exfiltration, this functionality has been replaced in Mango by a new backdoor command.</p>
<p><span lang="EN-US" style="mso-no-proof: yes;">In the main task, Mango first generates a victim identifier, </span><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">&lt;victimID&gt;</span><span lang="EN-US" style="mso-no-proof: yes;">, to be used in C&amp;C communications. The ID is computed as an MD5 hash of </span><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">&lt;machine name&gt;&lt;username&gt;</span><span lang="EN-US" style="mso-no-proof: yes;">, formatted as a hexadecimal string.</span></p>
<p>To request a backdoor command, Mango then sends the string <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace; white-space: nowrap;">d@&lt;victimID&gt;@&lt;machine name&gt;|&lt;username&gt;</span> to the C&amp;C server <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">http://www.darush.co[.]il/ads.asp</span> &ndash; a legitimate Israeli job portal, likely compromised by OilRig before this campaign. We notified the Israeli national CERT organization about the compromise.</p>
<p>The request body is constructed as follows:</p>
<ul>
<li class="Bullets1CxSpFirst">The data to be transmitted is XOR encrypted using the encryption key <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">Q&amp;4g</span>, then base64 encoded.</li>
<li class="Bullets1CxSpMiddle">A pseudorandom string of 3&ndash;14 characters is generated from this alphabet (as it appears in the code): <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">i8p3aEeKQbN4klFMHmcC2dU9f6gORGIhDBLS0jP5Tn7o1AVJ</span>.</li>
<li class="Bullets1CxSpLast">The encrypted data is inserted in a pseudorandom position within the generated string, enclosed between <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;"> [@</span> and <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">@]</span> delimiters.</li>
</ul>
<p>To communicate with its C&amp;C server, Mango uses the TLS (Transport Layer Security) protocol, which is used to provide an additional layer of encryption<span style="mso-no-proof: yes;">.</span></p>

The command itself is either <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">NCNT</span><code> </code>(in which case no action is taken), or a string of several parameters delimited by<code> </code><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">@</span>, as detailed in <!-- [if supportFields]><span

<h3><span style="mso-bookmark: _Toc143163838;">Post-compromise tools</span></h3>
<p>In this section, we review a selection of post-compromise tools used in OilRig&rsquo;s Outer Space and Juicy Mix campaigns, aimed at downloading and executing additional payloads, and stealing data from the compromised systems.</p>
<h4>SampleCheck5000 (SC5k) downloader</h4>
<p>SampleCheck5000 (or SC5k) is a downloader used to download and execute additional OilRig tools, notable for using the Microsoft Office Exchange Web Services API for C&amp;C communication: the attackers create draft messages in this email account and hide the backdoor commands in there. Subsequently, the downloader logs into the same account, and parses the drafts to retrieve commands and payloads to execute.</p>
<p>SC5k uses predefined values &ndash; Microsoft Exchange URL, email address, and password &ndash; to log into the remote Exchange server, but it also supports the option to override these values using a configuration file in the current working directory named <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">setting.key</span>. We chose the name SampleCheck5000 based on one of the email addresses that the tool used in the Outer Space campaign.</p>
<p>Once SC5k logs into the remote Exchange server, it retrieves all the emails in the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">Drafts</span><code> </code>directory, sorts them by most recent, keeping only the drafts that have attachments. It then iterates over every draft message with an attachment, looking for JSON attachments that contain <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">"data"</span> in the body. It extracts the value from the key <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">data</span> in the JSON file, base64 decodes and decrypts the value, and calls <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">cmd.exe</span> to execute the resulting command line string. SC5k then saves the output of the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">cmd.exe</span><code> </code>execution to a local variable.</p>

<h4>Browser-data dumpers</h4>
<p>It is characteristic of OilRig operators to use browser-data dumpers in their post-compromise activities. We discovered two new browser-data stealers among the post-compromise tools deployed in the Juicy Mix campaign alongside the Mango backdoor. They dump the stolen browser data in the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">%TEMP%</span> directory into files named <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">Cupdate</span><code> </code>and <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">Eupdate</span><code> </code>(hence our names for them: CDumper and EDumper).</p>
<p>Both tools are C#/.NET browser-data stealers, collecting cookies, browsing history, and credentials from the Chrome (CDumper) and Edge (EDumper) browsers. We focus our analysis on CDumper, since both stealers are practically identical, save for some constants.</p>
<p>When executed, CDumper creates a list of users with Google Chrome installed. On execution, the stealer connects to the Chrome SQLite <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">Cookies</span>, <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">History</span><code> </code>and <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">Login Data</span> databases under <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">%APPDATA%\Local\Google\Chrome\User Data</span>, and collects browser data including visited URLs and saved logins, using SQL queries.</p>
<p>The cookie values are then decrypted, and all collected information is added to a log file named <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">C:\Users\&lt;user&gt;\AppData\Local\Temp\Cupdate</span>, in cleartext. This functionality is implemented in CDumper functions named <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">CookieGrab</span><code> </code>(see <!-- [if supportFields]><span

Note that there is no exfiltration mechanism implemented in CDumper, but Mango can exfiltrate selected files via a backdoor command.</p>

<h4>Windows Credential Manager stealer</h4>
<p>Besides browser-data dumping tools, OilRig also used a Windows Credential Manager stealer in the Juicy Mix campaign. This tool steals credentials from Windows Credential Manager, and similar to CDumper and EDumper, stores them in the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">%TEMP%</span> directory &ndash; this time into a file named <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">IUpdate</span><code> </code>(hence the name IDumper). Unlike CDumper and EDumper, IDumper is implemented as a PowerShell script.</p>
<p>As with the browser dumper tools, it is not uncommon for OilRig to collect credentials from the Windows Credential Manager. Previously, OilRig&rsquo;s operators were observed using VALUEVAULT, a <a href="https://www.softpedia.com/get/Security/Password-Managers-Generators/Windows-Vault-Password-Dumper.shtml" target="_blank" rel="noopener">publicly available</a><span class="MsoHyperlink">,</span> Go-compiled credential-theft tool (see the <a href="https://www.mandiant.com/resources/blog/hard-pass-declining-apt34-invite-to-join-their-professional-network" target="_blank" rel="noopener">2019 HardPass campaign</a> and a <a href="https://www.intezer.com/blog/malware-analysis/new-iranian-campaign-tailored-to-us-companies-uses-updated-toolset/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">2020 campaign</a>), for the same purpose.</p>
<h2><a name="_Toc143163839"></a>Conclusion</h2>
<p>OilRig continues to innovate and create new implants with backdoor-like capabilities while finding new ways to execute commands on remote systems. The group improved upon its C#/.NET Solar backdoor from the Outer Space campaign to create a new backdoor named Mango for the Juicy Mix campaign. The group deploys a set of custom post-compromise tools that are used to collect credentials, cookies, and browsing history from major browsers and from the Windows Credential Manager. Despite these innovations, OilRig also continues to rely on established ways to obtain user data.</p>
