					<h1 class="entry-title">Canadian Police Raid &#8216;Orcus RAT&#8217; Author</h1>
				</div>
							
			
			</header><!-- .entry-header -->

	<div id="primary" class="site-content">

					<article id="post-47142" class="post-47142 post type-post status-publish format-standard hentry category-breadcrumbs category-neer-do-well-news tag-john-revesz tag-orcus-rat tag-rcmp">

	<div class="btm-wrap">
	<div class="below-title-meta">
		<div class="adt">
			
			
				
				<span class="date updated">April 2, 2019</span>
					
        </div>
		<div class="adt-comment">
		<span><a class="link-comments" href="https://krebsonsecurity.com/2019/04/canadian-police-raid-orcus-rat-author/#comments">59 Comments</a></span> 
        </div>    <div class="clear"></div>

							<div class="entry-content">
												<p>Canadian police last week raided the residence of a Toronto software developer behind “<strong>Orcus RAT</strong>,” a product that’s been marketed on underground forums and used in countless malware attacks since its creation in 2015. Its author maintains Orcus is a legitimate <strong>R</strong>emote <strong>A</strong>dministration <strong>T</strong>ool that is merely being abused, but security experts say it includes multiple features more typically seen in malware known as a <strong>R</strong>emote <strong>A</strong>ccess <strong>T</strong>rojan.</p>
<div id="attachment_47154" style="width: 605px" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><a class="lightbox" href="https://krebsonsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2019/04/orcusad.jpg"><img aria-describedby="caption-attachment-47154" decoding="async" class="wp-image-47154" src="https://krebsonsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2019/04/orcusad.jpg" alt="" width="595" height="243" /></a><p id="caption-attachment-47154" class="wp-caption-text">An advertisement for Orcus RAT.</p></div>
<p>As <a href="https://krebsonsecurity.com/2016/07/canadian-man-is-author-of-popular-orcus-rat/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">first detailed by KrebsOnSecurity in July 2016</a>, Orcus is the brainchild of <a href="https://www.linkedin.com/in/john-revesz-b3437b2/?authType=NAME_SEARCH&amp;authToken=JxFD&amp;locale=en_US&amp;srchid=24600671469033281031&amp;srchindex=3&amp;srchtotal=4&amp;trk=vsrp_people_res_name&amp;trkInfo=VSRPsearchId%3A24600671469033281031%2CVSRPtargetId%3A6884248%2CVSRPcmpt%3Aprimary%2CVSRPnm%3Atrue%2CauthType%3ANAME_SEARCH" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><strong>John “Armada” Revesz</strong></a>, a Toronto resident who until recently maintained and sold the RAT under the company name <strong>Orcus Technologies</strong>.</p>
<p>In an “official press release” <a href="https://pastebin.com/JgZpxwpf" target="_blank" rel="noopener">posted to pastebin.com</a> on Mar. 31, 2019, Revesz said his company recently was the subject of an international search warrant executed jointly by the <strong>Royal Canadian Mounted Police</strong> (RCMP) and the <strong>Canadian Radio-television and Telecommunications Commission</strong> (CRTC).</p>
<p>“In this process authorities seized numerous backup hard drives [containing] a large portion of Orcus Technologies business, and practices,” Revesz wrote. “Data inclusive on these drives include but are not limited to: User information inclusive of user names, real names, financial transactions, and further. The arrests and searches expand to an international investigation at this point, including countries as America, Germany, Australia, Canada and potentially more.”</p>
<p>Reached via email, Revesz declined to say whether he was arrested in connection with <a href="https://krebsonsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2019/04/orcussearch.jpg" target="_blank" rel="noopener">the search warrant</a>, a copy of which he shared with KrebsOnSecurity. In response to an inquiry from this office, the RCMP stopped short of naming names, but said “we can confirm that our National Division Cybercrime Investigative Team did execute a search warrant at a Toronto location last week.”</p>
<p>The RCMP said the raid was part of an international coordinated effort with the Federal Bureau of Investigation and the Australian Federal Police, as part of “a series of ongoing, parallel investigations into Remote Access Trojan (RAT) technology. This type of malicious software (malware) enables remote access to Canadian computers, without their users’ consent and can lead to the subsequent installation of other malware and theft of personal information.”</p>
<p>“The CRTC executed a warrant under Canada’s Anti-Spam Legislation (CASL) and the RCMP National Division executed a search warrant under the Criminal Code respectively,” reads <a href="https://www.canada.ca/en/radio-television-telecommunications/news/2019/03/crtc-and-rcmp-national-division-execute-warrants-in-malware-investigation.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener">a statement</a> published last week by the Canadian government. “Tips from international private cyber security firms triggered the investigation.”</p>
<p>Revesz maintains his software was designed for legitimate use only and for system administrators seeking more powerful, full-featured ways to remotely manage multiple PCs around the globe. He’s also said he’s not responsible for how licensed customers use his products, and that he actively kills software licenses for customers found to be using it for online fraud.</p>
<p>Yet the <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20170602061727/https://orcustechnologies.com/features.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener">list of features and plugins</a> advertised for this RAT includes functionality that goes significantly beyond what one might see in a traditional remote administration tool, such as DDoS-for-hire capabilities, and the ability to disable the light indicator on webcams so as not to alert the target that the RAT is active.</p>
<p>“It can also implement a watchdog that restarts the server component or even trigger a Blue Screen of Death (BSOD) if the someone tries to kill its process,” wrote researchers at security firm Fortinet in <a href="https://www.fortinet.com/blog/threat-research/a-peculiar-case-of-orcus-rat-targeting-bitcoin-investors.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener">a Dec. 2017 analysis of the RAT</a>. “This makes it harder for targets to remove it from their systems. These are, of course, on top of the obviously ominous features such as password retrieval and key logging that are normally seen in Remote Access Trojans.”</p>
<p>As KrebsOnSecurity <a href="https://krebsonsecurity.com/2016/07/canadian-man-is-author-of-popular-orcus-rat/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">noted in 2016</a>, in conjunction with his RAT Revesz also sold and marketed a bulletproof “dynamic DNS service” that promised not to keep any records of customer activity.<span id="more-47142"></span></p>
<p>Revesz appears to have <a href="https://www.facebook.com/photo.php?fbid=10160525561715403&amp;set=pb.753335402.-2207520000.1554220457.&amp;type=3&amp;theater" target="_blank" rel="noopener">a flair for the dramatic</a>, and has periodically emailed this author over the years. Sometimes, the missives were taunting, or vaguely ominous and threatening. Like the time he reached out to say he was hiring a private investigator to find and track me. Still other unbidden communications from Revesz were friendly, even helpful with timely news tips.</p>
<p>According to Revesz himself, he is no stranger to the Canadian legal system. In June 2018, Revesz shared court documents indicating he has been involved in multiple physical assault charges since 2007, including &#8220;7 domestic disputes between partners as well as incidents with his parents.&#8221;</p>
<p>“I am not your A-typical computer geek, Brian,” he wrote in a 2018 email. “I tend to have a violent nature, and have both Martial arts and Military training. So, I suppose it is really good that I took your article with a grain of salt instead of actually really getting upset.”</p>
<p><a class="lightbox" href="https://krebsonsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2019/04/armada-abuse.jpg"><img decoding="async" loading="lazy" class="aligncenter wp-image-47149 " src="https://krebsonsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2019/04/armada-abuse.jpg" alt="" width="599" height="254" /></a></p>
<p>The sale and marketing of remote administration tools is not illegal in the United States, and indeed there are plenty of such tools sold by legitimate companies to help computer experts remotely administer computers.</p>
<p>However, these tools tend to be viewed by prosecutors as malware and spyware when their proprietors advertise them as hacking devices and provide customer support aimed at helping buyers deploy the RATs stealthily and evade detection by anti-malware programs.</p>
<p>Last year, a 21-year-old Kentucky man <a href="https://krebsonsecurity.com/2018/07/luminositylink-rat-author-pleads-guilty/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">pleaded guilty</a> to authoring and distributing a popular hacking tool called “<strong>LuminosityLink</strong>,” which experts say was used by thousands of customers to gain access to tens of thousands of computers across 78 countries worldwide.</p>
<p>Also in 2018, 27-year-old Arkansas resident <strong>Taylor Huddleston</strong> was <a href="https://krebsonsecurity.com/2018/02/bot-roundup-avalanche-kronos-nanocore/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">sentenced to three years in jail</a> for making and selling the “<strong>NanoCore RAT</strong>,” which was being used to spy on webcams and steal passwords from systems running the software.</p>
<p>In many previous law enforcement investigations targeting RAT developers and sellers, investigators also have targeted customers of these products. In 2014, the U.S. Justice Department announced a series of actions against more than 100 people accused of purchasing and using “<strong>Blackshades</strong>,” a cheap and powerful RAT that the U.S. government said was used to infect more than a half million computers worldwide.</p>
<p>Earlier this year, Revesz <a href="https://twitter.com/OrcusTech" target="_blank" rel="noopener">posted on Twitter</a> that he was making the source code for Orcus RAT publicly available, and focusing his attention on developing <a href="https://github.com/MazeAdmin/Maze" target="_blank" rel="noopener">a new and improved RAT product</a>.</p>
<p>Meanwhile on Hackforums[.]net — the forum where Orcus was principally advertised and sold — members and customers expressed concern that authorities would soon be visiting Orcus RAT customers, posts that were deleted almost as quickly by the Hackforums administrator.</p>
<p>As if in acknowledgement of that concern, in the Pastebin press release published this week Revesz warned people away from using Orcus RAT, and added some choice advice for others who would follow his path.</p>
<p><span class="gmail-il">“Orcus</span> is no longer to be considered safe or secure solution to Remote Administrative needs,&#8221; he wrote, pointing to <a href="https://i.imgur.com/UdfIj0l.jpg" target="_blank" rel="noopener">a screenshot</a> of a court order he says came from one of the police investigators, which requires him to abstain from accessing Hackforums or Orcus-related sites. &#8220;Please move away from this software without delay. It has been a pleasure getting to know everyone in my time online, and I hope you all can take my words as a life lesson. Stay safe, don’t do stupid shit.”</p>

			<section class="comment-content comment">
				<p>Good day,</p>
<p>My name is Patricia Valladao and I am the manager of media Relations for the CRTC.  I would like to thank you for your recent article and provide you some clarification on our role (CRTC) and the requirements under CASL</p>
<p>Investigation into RAT and search warrant</p>
<p>In February 2018, the Canadian Radio-television and Telecommunications Commission (CRTC) initiated an investigation into a Remote Access Trojan (RAT).</p>
<p>By the fall of 2018, and having gathered sufficient evidence on various third parties connected to the RAT, the CRTC solicited, and obtained, the involvement of the Royal Canadian Mounted Police (RCMP), the Australian Federal Police (AFP) and the Federal Bureau of Investigation (FBI). </p>
<p>On March 27, 2019, the CRTC and the Royal Canadian Mounted Police (RCMP) National Division each executed a warrant at a residence located in the Greater Toronto Area. The CRTC and the RCMP are both part of an international coordinated effort with the AFP and the FBI.</p>
<p>The Canadian Radio-television and Telecommunications Commission (CRTC)</p>
<p>The CRTC is an administrative tribunal that regulates and supervises broadcasting and telecommunications in the public interest. It are dedicated to ensuring that Canadians have access to a world-class communication system that promotes innovation and enriches their lives.</p>
<p>The Electronic Commerce Enforcement Division (ECE)</p>
<p>The ECE is responsible for reducing the harmful effects of spam, malware and related threats. Its goal is to help create a safer and more secure online marketplace, and to that end, it enforces Canada’s Anti-Spam Legislation (CASL). </p>
<p>The law targets those who send commercial electronic messages without the recipient’s consent or install programs on computers or networks without express consent. This includes malware, spyware and viruses in computer programs, in spam messages, or downloaded through infected Web links.</p>
<p>The ECE has primary enforcement responsibility, including powers to investigate and take action against violators, and set administrative monetary penalties.</p>
<p>It also works to promote compliance among organisations and individuals, ensuring that businesses have the information they need to compete in the global marketplace.</p>
<p>Finally, it is responsible for administering the Spam Reporting Centre (SRC).</p>
<p>You can find a snapshot of recent enforcement activities here.</p>
<p>CASL Sections 6-8</p>
<p>CASL prohibits, in the context of a commercial activity, and without a person’s consent:</p>
<p>•	[Section 6] The sending of commercial electronic messages, including spam and phishing<br />
•	[Section 7] The alteration of transmission data, including botnet activity<br />
•	[Section 8] The installation of computer programs on computers or networks, including malware and spyware</p>
<p>CASL Section 9</p>
<p>In addition, [Section 9] of CASL also prohibits an individual or organization from aiding, inducing, procuring or causing to be procured the doing of any of the above acts. This allows us to target intermediaries who, through their actions or through inaction, facilitate the commission of CASL violations. </p>
<p>For instance, the ECE recently took action against Datablocks Inc. and Sunlight Media Network Inc for having violated CASL section 9 by disseminating online ads that caused malicious computer programs to be downloaded onto the computers of unsuspecting victims. </p>
<p>It also released an Information Bulletin on Section 9, which describes the types of activities likely to contravene Section 9, and identifies measures that individuals and organizations may take to ensure compliance with the Act.</p>
<p>Powers under CASL</p>
<p>Under CASL, and for the purposes of verifying compliance or determining whether any of sections 6 to 9 were contravened, the CRTC may </p>
<p>•	[Section 17] compel individuals and organizations to provide any information in their possession or control;</p>
<p>•	[Section 19] ask a justice of the peace to issue a warrant authorizing entry into a place of residence;</p>
<p>In the event that the CRTC believes, on reasonable grounds, that an individual or organization has contravened one of more of section 6 to 9, it may :</p>
<p>•	[Section 21] Issue a Notice of Violation. According to [Section 20], the maximum penalty for a violation is $1,000,000 in the case of an individual, and $10,000,000 in the case of an organization.</p>
<p>•	[Section 26] Issue a Restraining Order directing an individual or organization to cease contravening the provision(s) to which the contravention relates.</p>
<p>•	[Section 41] Obtain an Injunction from a court of competent jurisdiction in the event an individual or organization is about to do or is likely to do anything that constitutes or is directed toward the contravention of any of sections 6 to 9.</p>
