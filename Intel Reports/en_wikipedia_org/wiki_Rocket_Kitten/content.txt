						<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>

					<div id="contentSub"><div id="mw-content-subtitle"></div></div>
					
					
					<div id="mw-content-text" class="mw-body-content"><div class="mw-content-ltr mw-parser-output" lang="en" dir="ltr"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Hacker group from Iran</div>
<p><b>Rocket Kitten</b> or the <b>Rocket Kitten Group</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Hacker_group" title="Hacker group">hacker group</a> thought to be linked to the <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iranian</a> government.<sup id="cite_ref-CP_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CP-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The threat actor group has targeted organizations and individuals in the Middle East, particularly Israel, Saudi Arabia, Iran as well as the United States and Europe.
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Origins">Origins</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rocket_Kitten&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origins"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Cybersecurity firm <a href="/wiki/FireEye" class="mw-redirect" title="FireEye">FireEye</a> first identified the group as <b>Ajax Security Team</b>,<sup id="cite_ref-FT_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FT-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> writing that the group appears to have been formed in 2010 by the hacker personas "Cair3x" and "HUrr!c4nE!". By 2012, the threat actor group turned their focus to Iran's political opponents.<sup id="cite_ref-FireEye_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FireEye-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Their targeted attack campaigns, dubbed "Rocket Kitten", have been known since mid-2014.<sup id="cite_ref-Reuters_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reuters-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> By 2013 or 2014, Rocket Kitten had shifted its focus to malware-based cyberespionage.<sup id="cite_ref-FireEye_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FireEye-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Security firm <a href="/wiki/Check_Point" title="Check Point">Check Point</a> describes Rocket Kitten as an "attacker group of Iranian origin."<sup id="cite_ref-CP_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CP-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Rocket Kitten's code uses <a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">Persian language</a> references. The group's targets are involved in defense, diplomacy, international affairs, security, policy research, human rights, and journalism. According to Check Point, the group has targeted Iranian dissidents, the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Saud" title="House of Saud">Saudi royal family</a>, Israeli nuclear scientists and <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a> officials. Security researchers found that they carried out a "common pattern of spearphishing campaigns reflecting the interests and activities of the Iranian security apparatus."<sup id="cite_ref-Reuters_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reuters-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Other researchers determined that Rocket Kitten's attacks bore a similarity to those attributed to Iran's <a href="/wiki/Islamic_Revolutionary_Guard_Corps" title="Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps">Revolutionary Guards</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Reuters_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reuters-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Intelligence officials from the Middle East and Europe linked Rocket Kitten to the Iranian military establishment.<sup id="cite_ref-FT_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FT-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Rocket Kitten favours a <a href="/wiki/Remote_Access_Trojan" class="mw-redirect" title="Remote Access Trojan">Remote Access Trojan</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-SC_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SC-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and by 2015, researchers found it was using customised malware.<sup id="cite_ref-FT_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FT-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="History">History</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rocket_Kitten&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Operation_Saffron_Rose">Operation Saffron Rose</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rocket_Kitten&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Operation Saffron Rose"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Cybersecurity firm FireEye released a report in 2013 finding that Rocket Kitten had conducted several cyberespionage operations against United States <a href="/wiki/Defense_industrial_base" title="Defense industrial base">defense industrial base</a> companies. The report also detailed the targeting of Iranian citizens who use anti-censorship tools to bypass Iran's Internet filters.<sup id="cite_ref-FireEye_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FireEye-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Operation_Woolen-Goldfish">Operation Woolen-Goldfish</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rocket_Kitten&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Operation Woolen-Goldfish"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Trend_Micro" title="Trend Micro">Trend Micro</a> identified the Operation Woolen-Goldfish campaign in a March 2015 paper. The campaign included improved spearphishing content.<sup id="cite_ref-CP_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CP-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Oyun">Oyun</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rocket_Kitten&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Oyun"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>In November 2015, security errors by Rocket Kitten allowed the firm Check Point to gain password-less root access to "Oyun", the hackers' back-end database. They discovered an application that  was able to generate personalized phishing pages and contained a list of over 1,842 individual targets.<sup id="cite_ref-FT_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FT-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Infosec_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Infosec-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Among Rocket Kitten's spearphishing targets from June 2014 to June 2015, 18% were from Saudi Arabia, 17% were from the United States, 16% were from Iran, 8% were from the Netherlands, and 5% were from Israel.<sup id="cite_ref-FT_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FT-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Analysts used credentials to access <a href="/wiki/Keystroke_logging" title="Keystroke logging">key logs</a> of the group's victims and found that Rocket Kitten had apparently tested their malware on their own workstations and failed to erase the logs from the data files.<sup id="cite_ref-Infosec_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Infosec-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Check Point identified an individual named Yaser Balaghi, going by Wool3n.H4t, as a ringleader of the operation.<sup id="cite_ref-SC_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SC-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Telegram_hack">Telegram hack</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rocket_Kitten&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Telegram hack"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>In August 2016, researchers identified Rocket Kitten as being behind a hack of <a href="/wiki/Telegram_(software)" title="Telegram (software)">Telegram</a>, a cloud-based instant messaging service. The hackers exploited Telegram's reliance on SMS verification, comprising over a dozen accounts and stealing the user IDs and telephone numbers of 15 million Iranians who use the software. Opposition organizations and reformist political activists were among the victims.<sup id="cite_ref-Reuters_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reuters-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
