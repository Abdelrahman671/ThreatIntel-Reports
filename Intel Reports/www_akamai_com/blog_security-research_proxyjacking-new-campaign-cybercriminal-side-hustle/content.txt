<div data-cmp-is="text" class="cmp-text" data-cmp-data-layer="{&#34;text-4220a6f6e9&#34;:{&#34;@type&#34;:&#34;akamai/components/text&#34;,&#34;repo:modifyDate&#34;:&#34;2023-06-28T20:21:57Z&#34;,&#34;xdm:text&#34;:&#34;&lt;ul>\r\n&lt;li>&lt;p>The Akamai Security Intelligence Response Team (SIRT) has discovered a proxyjacking campaign that is targeting vulnerable SSH servers, then launching Docker services that share the victim&#39;s bandwidth for money.&lt;/p>\r\n&lt;/li>\r\n&lt;li>&lt;p>This campaign uses a compromised web server to distribute necessary dependencies,&amp;nbsp; actively searches for and removes competing instances, and employs obfuscation techniques to evade detection.&lt;/p>\r\n&lt;/li>\r\n&lt;li>&lt;p>Proxyjacking has become the newest way for cybercriminals to make money from compromised devices in both a corporate ecosystem as well as the consumer ecosystem.     <ul>
<li><p>The Akamai Security Intelligence Response Team (SIRT) has discovered a proxyjacking campaign that is targeting vulnerable SSH servers, then launching Docker services that share the victim's bandwidth for money.</p>
</li>
<li><p>This campaign uses a compromised web server to distribute necessary dependencies,  actively searches for and removes competing instances, and employs obfuscation techniques to evade detection.</p>
</li>
<li><p>Proxyjacking has become the newest way for cybercriminals to make money from compromised devices in both a corporate ecosystem as well as the consumer ecosystem. It is a stealthier alternative to cryptojacking and has serious implications that can increase the headaches that proxied Layer 7 attacks already serve.</p>
</li>
</ul>

      <h2 class="cmp-title__text">Introduction<br />
</h2>
    
</div>

<div class="text aem-GridColumn aem-GridColumn--default--12">
<div data-cmp-is="text" class="cmp-text" data-cmp-data-layer="{&#34;text-4d53ede0ad&#34;:{&#34;@type&#34;:&#34;akamai/components/text&#34;,&#34;repo:modifyDate&#34;:&#34;2023-06-29T15:06:41Z&#34;,&#34;xdm:text&#34;:&#34;&lt;p>In the ever-evolving world of cyberthreats, attackers continually seek innovative strategies to maximize their gains while minimizing their efforts. The latest example of this was discovered within one of Akamai SIRT’s&amp;nbsp;globally distributed honeypots in early June: proxyjacking for profit.&lt;/p>\r\n&lt;p>Although the concept of proxyjacking is not new, the ability to easily monetize it as affiliates of mainstream companies is. Providing a simple path to financial gain makes this vector a threat to both the corporate world and the&amp;nbsp; average consumer alike, heightening the need for awareness and, hopefully, mitigation. The emergent threat landscape is marked by actors such as Meris and Anonymous Sudan, and when that landscape is paired with the notable rise in Layer 7 attacks propagated through proxy networks, there is substantial monetary value in the accumulation of a large quantity of usable proxies.&amp;nbsp; &amp;nbsp;&lt;/p>\r\n&lt;p>In this post, we will dive into a new proxyjacking campaign uncovered by our security researchers on the SIRT. This is an active campaign in which the attacker leverages SSH for remote access, running malicious scripts that stealthily enlist victim servers into a peer-to-peer (P2P) proxy network, such as Peer2Proxy or Honeygain.&amp;nbsp;&lt;/p>\r\n&lt;p>This allows for the attacker to monetize an unsuspecting victim’s extra bandwidth, with only a fraction of the resource load that would be required for cryptomining, with less chance of discovery.&amp;nbsp;&lt;/p>\r\n&lt;p>We will describe the technical details of the campaign, as well as explore the threat profile of proxyjacking for profit. We will also make suggestions for mitigations. It is our hope to bring this new use case of an older technique to the forefront.&lt;/p>\r\n&#34;}}">
    <p>In the ever-evolving world of cyberthreats, attackers continually seek innovative strategies to maximize their gains while minimizing their efforts. The latest example of this was discovered within one of Akamai SIRT’s globally distributed honeypots in early June: proxyjacking for profit.</p>
<p>Although the concept of proxyjacking is not new, the ability to easily monetize it as affiliates of mainstream companies is. Providing a simple path to financial gain makes this vector a threat to both the corporate world and the  average consumer alike, heightening the need for awareness and, hopefully, mitigation. The emergent threat landscape is marked by actors such as Meris and Anonymous Sudan, and when that landscape is paired with the notable rise in Layer 7 attacks propagated through proxy networks, there is substantial monetary value in the accumulation of a large quantity of usable proxies.   </p>
<p>In this post, we will dive into a new proxyjacking campaign uncovered by our security researchers on the SIRT. This is an active campaign in which the attacker leverages SSH for remote access, running malicious scripts that stealthily enlist victim servers into a peer-to-peer (P2P) proxy network, such as Peer2Proxy or Honeygain. </p>
<p>This allows for the attacker to monetize an unsuspecting victim’s extra bandwidth, with only a fraction of the resource load that would be required for cryptomining, with less chance of discovery. </p>
<p>We will describe the technical details of the campaign, as well as explore the threat profile of proxyjacking for profit. We will also make suggestions for mitigations. It is our hope to bring this new use case of an older technique to the forefront.</p>

      <h2 class="cmp-title__text">What is proxyjacking?<br />
</h2>
    
</div>

    


</div>
<div class="text aem-GridColumn aem-GridColumn--default--12">
<div data-cmp-is="text" class="cmp-text" data-cmp-data-layer="{&#34;text-b9487ca66a&#34;:{&#34;@type&#34;:&#34;akamai/components/text&#34;,&#34;repo:modifyDate&#34;:&#34;2023-06-28T20:25:55Z&#34;,&#34;xdm:text&#34;:&#34;&lt;p>Cryptojacking has become fairly well-known around the world: Victim resources are stolen to contribute to a mining pool in exchange for money that is given to the attacker. A lesser-known technique that has been on the rise recently is called proxyjacking.&amp;nbsp;&lt;/p>\r\n&lt;p>With proxyjacking, the attacker doesn&#39;t just steal resources but also leverages the victim&#39;s unused bandwidth. The victim&#39;s system is covertly used to run various services as a P2P proxy node that the attackers have recently started to monetize through organizations such as Peer2Profit or Honeygain. These companies provide the average user the opportunity to get paid for their extra bandwidth, an attractive, and legitimate, prospect to many people and entities.&amp;nbsp;&lt;/p>\r\n&lt;p>However, these sites often do not ask questions about how the new proxy node was sourced, which is where the illegitimacy begins. &lt;b>Although the act of proxyjacking has been around for some time now, it has only recently begun to be used strictly for profit, which is what we have observed in this campaign&lt;/b>.&lt;/p>\r\n&#34;}}">
    <p>Cryptojacking has become fairly well-known around the world: Victim resources are stolen to contribute to a mining pool in exchange for money that is given to the attacker. A lesser-known technique that has been on the rise recently is called proxyjacking. </p>
<p>With proxyjacking, the attacker doesn't just steal resources but also leverages the victim's unused bandwidth. The victim's system is covertly used to run various services as a P2P proxy node that the attackers have recently started to monetize through organizations such as Peer2Profit or Honeygain. These companies provide the average user the opportunity to get paid for their extra bandwidth, an attractive, and legitimate, prospect to many people and entities. </p>
<p>However, these sites often do not ask questions about how the new proxy node was sourced, which is where the illegitimacy begins. <b>Although the act of proxyjacking has been around for some time now, it has only recently begun to be used strictly for profit, which is what we have observed in this campaign</b>.</p>

      <h2 class="cmp-title__text">What is the value of a proxy to a cybercriminal?<br />
</h2>
    
</div>

    


</div>
<div class="text text__pb-20 aem-GridColumn aem-GridColumn--default--12">
<div data-cmp-is="text" class="cmp-text" data-cmp-data-layer="{&#34;text-33ca7c8d3c&#34;:{&#34;@type&#34;:&#34;akamai/components/text&#34;,&#34;repo:modifyDate&#34;:&#34;2023-06-28T20:26:58Z&#34;,&#34;xdm:text&#34;:&#34;&lt;p>&lt;b>The value of proxies for cybercriminals is inherent in their ability to obfuscate their tracks, making it immensely challenging to trace illicit activities back to their origins&lt;/b>. This obfuscation is achieved by routing malicious traffic through a multitude of peer nodes before reaching its final destination. Figure 1 illustrates this, showing how individuals openly exchange access to proxies, allowing for a diversified path for their traffic.&amp;nbsp;&lt;/p>\r\n&lt;p>This particular list was found as a result of online posts tied to the main IP that this investigation centered on, and contained over 16,500 other open proxies along with it. With the rise of proxyjacking, it is clear that not all of these nodes are participating&amp;nbsp; as willing peers in this network.This list is also by no means exhaustive, as there were many other posts found as well.&lt;/p>\r\n&#34;}}">
    <p><b>The value of proxies for cybercriminals is inherent in their ability to obfuscate their tracks, making it immensely challenging to trace illicit activities back to their origins</b>. This obfuscation is achieved by routing malicious traffic through a multitude of peer nodes before reaching its final destination. Figure 1 illustrates this, showing how individuals openly exchange access to proxies, allowing for a diversified path for their traffic. </p>
<p>This particular list was found as a result of online posts tied to the main IP that this investigation centered on, and contained over 16,500 other open proxies along with it. With the rise of proxyjacking, it is clear that not all of these nodes are participating  as willing peers in this network.This list is also by no means exhaustive, as there were many other posts found as well.</p>

    <p>In the campaign discussed in this blog post, our focus isn't on classically “open” proxies, as companies like Peer2Profit and Honeygain claim to only share their proxies with theoretically vetted partners. <b>The incentives offered by these companies to aid in contributing to proxy networks, however, not only attract legitimate installations for passive income, but also those trying to exploit the system at other people’s expense</b>.</p>
<p>Likewise, the actual services they provide are sure to attract legitimate business and threat actors alike. This case, therefore, serves as a stark reminder of the potential security implications of these seemingly beneficial services.</p>

<div class="text text__pt-none text__pb-20 aem-GridColumn aem-GridColumn--default--12">
<div data-cmp-is="text" class="cmp-text" data-cmp-data-layer="{&#34;text-32a0b16e1a&#34;:{&#34;@type&#34;:&#34;akamai/components/text&#34;,&#34;repo:modifyDate&#34;:&#34;2023-06-28T21:02:23Z&#34;,&#34;xdm:text&#34;:&#34;&lt;p>This particular campaign first came to light on June 8, 2023.     <p>This particular campaign first came to light on June 8, 2023. Our team noticed an attacker establishing multiple SSH connections to one of our Cowrie honeypots (Figure 2), an asset managed by the SIRT. <div data-cmp-is="text" class="cmp-text" data-cmp-data-layer="{&#34;text-9afe0f0064&#34;:{&#34;@type&#34;:&#34;akamai/components/text&#34;,&#34;repo:modifyDate&#34;:&#34;2023-06-28T22:56:34Z&#34;,&#34;xdm:text&#34;:&#34;&lt;p>The emergence of proxyjacking for profit follows many other forms of criminal moneymaking schemes via compromised devices. The most obvious comparison is cryptojacking, but the strategy of taking a legitimate service and abusing its profitability through criminal means goes back even further than this.&amp;nbsp;&lt;/p>\r\n&lt;p>Another prime example is the fact that spam’s origins largely &lt;a href=\&#34;https://darknetdiaries.com/episode/78/\&#34; target=\&#34;_blank\&#34; rel=\&#34;nofollow noreferrer\&#34;>started with affiliate marketing&lt;/a>, a very common practice even today.     <p>The emergence of proxyjacking for profit follows many other forms of criminal moneymaking schemes via compromised devices. The most obvious comparison is cryptojacking, but the strategy of taking a legitimate service and abusing its profitability through criminal means goes back even further than this. </p>
<p>Another prime example is the fact that spam’s origins largely <a href="https://darknetdiaries.com/episode/78/" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noreferrer">started with affiliate marketing</a>, a very common practice even today. Some companies would pay you by the click as long as you drove customers to their site, with many ignoring the fact that people were using spam to do it. As long as these incentives exist, and companies are willing to ignore the ethics of the sourcing, criminal industries will be built around exploiting these practices. </p>
<p>There is another fact about the accumulation of proxies that make it particularly relevant and concerning: Proxyjacking essentially addresses the only significant downside to cryptojacking — the detection via high CPU usage. By requiring minimal CPU, and instead relying on unused internet bandwidth, <b>proxyjacking can avoid some of the means of detection that were previously used for cryptojacking</b>. </p>

<div data-cmp-is="text" class="cmp-text" data-cmp-data-layer="{&#34;text-fbb8b3ea8c&#34;:{&#34;@type&#34;:&#34;akamai/components/text&#34;,&#34;repo:modifyDate&#34;:&#34;2023-06-28T23:10:17Z&#34;,&#34;xdm:text&#34;:&#34;&lt;p>Less CPU usage means that there is an even higher emphasis on IDS/IPS solutions to mitigate proxyjacking from a corporate perspective. The everyday user should implement strong security fundamentals, such as using complicated &lt;a href=\&#34;/content/akamai/en/blog/security/passwords---extinction-event-looming-.html\&#34; target=\&#34;_self\&#34;>passwords&lt;/a> and storing them in a password manager, applying &lt;a href=\&#34;/content/akamai/en/blog/security-research/akamai-perspective-patch-tuesday-june-2023.html\&#34; target=\&#34;_self\&#34;>patches&lt;/a> to applications, and enabling multi-factor authentication (&lt;a href=\&#34;/content/akamai/en/glossary/what-is-multifactor-authentication.html\&#34; target=\&#34;_self\&#34;>MFA&lt;/a>) whenever possible. Users with deeper knowledge of computer security can additionally remain vigilant by paying attention to the &lt;a href=\&#34;/content/akamai/en/solutions/cloud-computing/containers.html\&#34; target=\&#34;_self\&#34;>containers&lt;/a> currently running, monitoring network traffic for anomalies, and even running vulnerability scans on a regular basis.&lt;/p>\r\n&lt;p>In this particular campaign, we saw the use of SSH to gain access to a server and install a Docker container, but past campaigns have exploited web vulnerabilities as well. If you check your local running Docker services and find any unwanted resource sharing on your system, you should investigate the intrusion, determine how the script was uploaded and run, and perform a thorough cleanup.&amp;nbsp;&lt;/p>\r\n&lt;p>Be sure to check for any other signs of intrusion or compromise. Change all passwords, ensure all software is up-to-date, and consider implementing a stricter security policy. Additionally, you should consider engaging &lt;a href=\&#34;/content/akamai/en/global-services.html\&#34; target=\&#34;_self\&#34;>professional incident response services&lt;/a> if the affected system is sensitive or contains valuable data.&lt;/p>\r\n&lt;p>In addition to the sneaky nature of these attacks, open proxies serve as a crucial tool in the cybercriminal&#39;s arsenal, with criminal organizations such as Meris and Anonymous Sudan demonstrating how devastating and pain-inducing proxied attacks can be. With a continued rise of Layer 7 attacks, this problem will only continue to increase, and the reliance on these proxy networking companies to properly manage their partners is a very poor defense mechanism and weak assurance.&lt;/p>\r\n&#34;}}">
    <p>Less CPU usage means that there is an even higher emphasis on IDS/IPS solutions to mitigate proxyjacking from a corporate perspective. The everyday user should implement strong security fundamentals, such as using complicated <a href="/blog/security/passwords---extinction-event-looming-" target="_self">passwords</a> and storing them in a password manager, applying <a href="/blog/security-research/akamai-perspective-patch-tuesday-june-2023" target="_self">patches</a> to applications, and enabling multi-factor authentication (<a href="/glossary/what-is-multifactor-authentication" target="_self">MFA</a>) whenever possible. Users with deeper knowledge of computer security can additionally remain vigilant by paying attention to the <a href="/solutions/cloud-computing/containers" target="_self">containers</a> currently running, monitoring network traffic for anomalies, and even running vulnerability scans on a regular basis.</p>
<p>In this particular campaign, we saw the use of SSH to gain access to a server and install a Docker container, but past campaigns have exploited web vulnerabilities as well. If you check your local running Docker services and find any unwanted resource sharing on your system, you should investigate the intrusion, determine how the script was uploaded and run, and perform a thorough cleanup. </p>
<p>Be sure to check for any other signs of intrusion or compromise. Change all passwords, ensure all software is up-to-date, and consider implementing a stricter security policy. Additionally, you should consider engaging <a href="/global-services" target="_self">professional incident response services</a> if the affected system is sensitive or contains valuable data.</p>
<p>In addition to the sneaky nature of these attacks, open proxies serve as a crucial tool in the cybercriminal's arsenal, with criminal organizations such as Meris and Anonymous Sudan demonstrating how devastating and pain-inducing proxied attacks can be. With a continued rise of Layer 7 attacks, this problem will only continue to increase, and the reliance on these proxy networking companies to properly manage their partners is a very poor defense mechanism and weak assurance.</p>
