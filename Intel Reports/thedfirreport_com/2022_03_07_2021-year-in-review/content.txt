			<h1 class="entry-title">2021 Year In Review</h1>
		<div class="entry-meta">
			<span class="posted-on"><a href="https://thedfirreport.com/2022/03/07/2021-year-in-review/" rel="bookmark"><time class="entry-date published" datetime="2022-03-07T02:30:33+00:00">March 7, 2022</time></a></span>		</div><!-- .entry-meta -->

		
	</header><!-- .entry-header -->

	<div class="entry-content">
		


<p>As we come to the end of the first quarter of 2022, we want to take some time to look back over our cases from 2021, in aggregate, and look at some of the top tactics, techniques and procedures (TTP&#8217;s) we observed. In total, we reported on 20 incidents in 2021, the vast majority were initial access broker malware (Trickbot, IcedID, BazarLoader, etc.), which often lead to full domain compromise and ransomware.</p>
<p>This report will contain details from all of our public reports over 2021, this is not comprehensive of overall threat actor activity, as there is always inherit sampling and collection bias. However, reviewing these common activities can help a defender prioritize their time and budget, to protect against some of the most common threat actor behaviors.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Shout out to our analysts who put this report together!</h3>



<p>Report lead <a href="https://twitter.com/Kostastsale" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">@kostastsale</a></p>
<p>Contributing analysts <a href="https://twitter.com/IcsNick" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">@ICSNick</a>, <a href="https://twitter.com/yatinwad" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">@yatinwad</a>, <a href="https://twitter.com/_pete_0" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">@_pete_0</a> and 1 unnamed contributor</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Services</h3>



<p>We offer multiple services including a <a href="https://thedfirreport.com/services/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">Threat Feed</a> service which tracks Command and Control frameworks such as Cobalt Strike, Metasploit, Empire, PoshC2, etc. More information on this service and others can be found <a href="https://thedfirreport.com/services/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">here</a>.</p>
<p>We also have artifacts available from our cases such as pcaps, memory captures, files, event logs including Sysmon, Kape packages, and more, under our <a href="https://www.patreon.com/thedfirreport" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">Security Researcher and Organization</a> services.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Intrusion statistics aligned to the MITRE ATT&amp;CK framework</h2>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Initial Access</h3>



<p>Over the last year, we have witnessed numerous intrusions where malware variants such as Trickbot, Hancitor, Bazar, and IcedID have been utilized as an entry point for ransomware attacks. In the majority of the intrusions, the malware was distributed in the form of non-targeted phishing, such as mass malware spam campaigns.</p>
<p>Initial Access Sources 2021.</p>



<figure class="wp-block-image"><a href="https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/03/2021-01.png"><img fetchpriority="high" decoding="async" class="size-full wp-image-5720 aligncenter" src="https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/03/2021-01.png" alt="" width="1144" height="773" srcset="https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/03/2021-01.png 1144w, https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/03/2021-01-300x203.png 300w, https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/03/2021-01-1024x692.png 1024w, https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/03/2021-01-768x519.png 768w" sizes="(max-width: 1144px) 100vw, 1144px" /></a></figure>



<p>Our reports tend to focus on instances where phishing is the initial vector, as was the case in 16 of 20 reports. Although we still experience cases of threat actors compromising vulnerable web-facing applications, those cases do not always turn into a large-scale intrusion.. Our report on <a href="https://thedfirreport.com/2021/11/15/exchange-exploit-leads-to-domain-wide-ransomware/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">Exchange Exploit Leads to Domain Wide Ransomware</a>, was the only case where the threat actors actively sought to deploy ransomware after getting access through a vulnerable application. The other two cases (<a href="https://thedfirreport.com/2021/01/18/all-that-for-a-coinminer/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">All that for a Coinminer?</a> and <a href="https://thedfirreport.com/2021/06/03/weblogic-rce-leads-to-xmrig/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">WebLogic RCE Leads to XMRig</a>) are a good representation of less impactful malicious activity, where the threat actors installed cryptominers. However, we believe that these intrusions can signal vulnerabilities that need immediate patching and remediation before being exploited by a threat actor seeking larger scale objectives.</p>
<p>Phishing was the main initial access vector for our cases in 2021. The malware vectors we saw in 2021 were:</p>



<ol class="wp-block-list">
<li><a href="https://thedfirreport.com/category/trickbot/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">TrickBot</a></li>
<li><a href="https://thedfirreport.com/category/bazar/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">Bazar</a></li>
<li><a href="https://thedfirreport.com/category/icedid/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">IcedID</a></li>
<li><a href="https://thedfirreport.com/category/hancitor/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">Hancitor</a></li>
</ol>



<p>The below graphic displays the tools/methods used by threat actors after getting initial access via the initial access malware listed above.</p>

<h3>Privilege Escalation/Credential Access</h3>



<p>With respect to credential access, multiple techniques were utilized by the threat actors. Some of the notable ones are: Dumping of LSASS using Task Manager and Procdump, creation of a copy of NTDS.dit using ntdsutil.exe and extraction of SAM, SECURITY and SYSTEM hives.</p>
<p>Depending on the level of access, threat actors are looking to escalate privileges on the beachhead host to leverage high integrity Beacon sessions. Having high integrity access allows them to access credentials from the host using various methods. Below, we highlight the privilege escalation and credential access methods observed across our 2021 reports.</p>

<h3>Defense Evasion</h3>



<p>When it comes to defense evasion, we noticed that process injection techniques were very common among threat actors. This allows them to establish additional Beacons on the already compromised hosts, to avoid detection.</p>
<p>In five separate cases, we encountered threat actors disabling security tools using various methods. One of the most notable cases was the <a href="https://thedfirreport.com/2021/10/18/icedid-to-xinglocker-ransomware-in-24-hours/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">IcedID to XingLocker Ransomware in 24 hours</a> case. In that case, the attackers used multiple batch files to disable well-known AV and EDR agents on the host. The batch scripts came from the first revision of <a href="https://gist.github.com/anadr/7465a9fde63d41341136949f14c21105/revisions" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">Revisions · quick-disable-windows-defender.bat · GitHub</a>, which was used by the ransomware operators without making any changes.</p>

<h3>Discovery</h3>



<p>Once access is established, threat actors then need to enumerate the victim environment. Common initial discovery tools include Windows built-in utilities (net.exe, nltest.exe, systeminfo, ipconfig, whoami, etc) and the <a href="https://www.joeware.net/freetools/tools/adfind/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">AdFind</a> tool. In a few cases, adversaries attempted to get a listing of open ports/running services on remote hosts by performing port scans using tools such as Advanced IP Scanner and KPortScan 3.0.</p>
<p>The first thing we observe from hands-on keyboard operators is usually additional discovery activity. We see threat actors concentrate on searching for the Domain Controllers and general environmental information.</p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Lateral Movement</h3>



<p>Lateral movement is a vital component of threat actor TTPs. Once they get the lay of the land through the discovery methods we outlined above, we repeatedly see them move laterally across the network. Domain Controllers, file shares and similarly high-value servers are primary targets.</p>
<p>The number one post-exploitation framework of choice, Cobalt Strike, allows threat actors to leverage different techniques for the purpose of lateral movement.</p>
<p>Other common choices for threat attackers include Remote Desktop connections, remote WMI execution of transferred binaries, and the Sysinternals tool PsExec.</p>

<h3>Command and Control</h3>



<p>One common theme seen across the majority of the intrusions has been the reliance on Cobalt Strike for performing post-exploitation activities. In almost all cases, the initial access brokers such as Trickbot and Bazar, dropped multiple Cobalt Strike beacons across the victim environment.</p>
<p>For an in-depth breakdown of some of the network operations of Cobalt Strike see our <a href="https://thedfirreport.com/2022/01/24/cobalt-strike-a-defenders-guide-part-2/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">recent report on the topic</a>.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Exfiltration</h3>



<p>While exfiltration of data was not a common sight in our data set, we observed exfil in 6 of the 20 cases. In the cases where data exfiltration was observed, the threat actors used tools such as RClone, FileZilla, or WinSCP to transfer the data to their controlled servers. In many other cases, threat actors downloaded sensitive data via Cobalt Strike Beacons.</p>
<p>One notable case for the year was the <a href="https://thedfirreport.com/2021/12/13/diavol-ransomware/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">Diavol Ransomware</a>. Ransomware operators used <a href="http://ufile.io" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">ufile.io</a> to upload the LSASS dump file they extracted from one of the domain controllers.</p>



<figure class="wp-block-image"><a href="https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/03/2021-20.png"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-5741" src="https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/03/2021-20.png" alt="" width="812" height="595" srcset="https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/03/2021-20.png 812w, https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/03/2021-20-300x220.png 300w, https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/03/2021-20-768x563.png 768w" sizes="auto, (max-width: 812px) 100vw, 812px" /></a></figure>



<p>To wrap up this chapter, we&#8217;d like to provide an overview of the tools that we&#8217;ve seen attackers employ this year. We included the tools in their respective phases of attack after seeing them in action. We used the MITRE ATT&amp;CK framework to show how these tools work at various phases of an attack.</p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Indicators of Attack/Behavior-based information focusing on the human element</h2>



<p>In our cases, we frequently observe hands-on keyboard activity by the threat actor during the intrusion. This provides a unique insight into the human side of the attack &#8211; how they conduct operations, respond to challenges, and how they use the tooling to achieve effects. This provides additional detection opportunities &#8211; in addition to Indicators of Compromise (IoC) and Indicators of Activity (IoA).</p>
<p>It is impressive to see that, in some instances, threat actors have adapted their tools, techniques, and procedures (TTPs) to evade detection. In other cases, we&#8217;ve observed operator mistakes, errors of judgement, and operational security (OPSEC) failures. We have also witnessed some of the challenges they encounter during intrusions.</p>
<p>Looking into the early Cyber Kill Chain steps of the intrusions, we see that some tasks are automated. However, once an attacker is within a target&#8217;s operating environment, many activities often require hands-on keyboard intervention by the operator in order to continue with their objectives. During this period, all the attacker&#8217;s hands-on keyboard activities can bring risk of detection or bring them one step closer to their objectives. Some of the typical hands-on keyboard activities include:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Discovering security audit tools</li>
<li>Overcoming security tools by disabling them all together</li>
<li>Encountering lateral movement barriers and seeking alternative approaches, etc.</li>
</ul>



<p>We looked into our most popular and unusual activities related to attackers&#8217; activities, which we will discuss below, from attackers&#8217; tooling configurations, to usage, and conduct during intrusions.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Cobalt Strike</h3>



<p>A common trend observed in our cases is the use of Cobalt Strike which is usually configured with a standard malleable C2 profile. A malleable C2 profile specifies a number of parameters, such as user agent string, spawn to process, jitter etc. Most of the default profiles are well known and can be detected by host and network monitoring rules.</p>
<p>Cobalt Strike continues to be the top post-exploitation tool favored by most threat actor groups, with the most popular malleable c2 profile observed this year being &#8216;jquery-3.3.1.min.js&#8217; with the relevant Beacon spawning under Rundll32.exe. The chart below illustrates the most popular spawn-as executable types and some rare ones, such as calc.exe that we saw in 2021.</p>



<figure class="wp-block-image"><a href="https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/03/2021-22.png"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-5742" src="https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/03/2021-22.png" alt="" width="569" height="327" srcset="https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/03/2021-22.png 569w, https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/03/2021-22-300x172.png 300w" sizes="auto, (max-width: 569px) 100vw, 569px" /></a></figure>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">BYOT (Bring Your Own Tools)</h3>



<p>We are astonished by the number of tools brought into an intrusion. Along with third-party tooling, living-off-the-land techniques are routinely observed – especially during the discovery phase. Bringing tools into an environment introduces several risks for the operator and opportunities for the defender from a detection point of view. Some of those risks include detection and blocking by AV, software incompatibilities, software restriction policies, etc. As discussed in the sections above, the most popular tool observed this year is AdFind. AdFind is the usual suspect in almost every intrusion we report. In 2020, we published a whole article covering <a href="https://thedfirreport.com/2020/05/08/adfind-recon/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">this tool</a>.</p>

<p>We have observed several instances where the threat actor&#8217;s infrastructure becomes exposed during the intrusion. One example includes the operator&#8217;s source machine during a remote desktop session &#8211; <a href="https://thedfirreport.com/2021/11/29/continuing-the-bazar-ransomware-story/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">CONTInuing the Bazar Ransomware</a>.</p>

<h2>Final Advice for Defenders</h2>



<p>From a defender&#8217;s perspective, each of the above points provides various detection opportunities. These could include recognizing outlier behavior, such as binaries executing from non-standard locations, or detecting outlier activity, such as a high frequency of crashes within a short period of each other.</p>
<p>Reducing your attack surface and regular patching can have some big wins and avoid some common scenarios we have seen in 2021, such as initial compromises through Log4j and ProxyShell/ProxyLogon exploits. Disabling macros and forcing scriptable files to open in notepad will also provide a high level of return on investment.</p>
<p>We would like to highlight a few guides that CISA has released that can assist defenders and organizations in getting their attack surface under control.</p>
<p><a href="https://www.cisa.gov/publication/stuff-off-search" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">Stuff off Search</a></p>
<p>By using various online search platforms it is possible to get an understanding of what services, assets and devices are exposed on the internet. Reducing this footprint limits the adversaries&#8217; potential entries into the organization&#8217;s network.</p>
<p><a href="https://www.cisa.gov/known-exploited-vulnerabilities" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">Known Exploited Vulnerabilities</a> &amp; <a href="https://www.cisa.gov/uscert/ncas/alerts/aa21-209a" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">Top Routinely Exploited Vulnerabilities in 2021</a></p>
<p>Using these resources along with <a href="https://www.cisa.gov/publication/stuff-off-search" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">&#8220;S.O.S&#8221;</a> defenders and organizations can identify the vulnerabilities associated with their exposed assets. By prioritizing assets that have known vulnerabilities which are actively exploited, the organization can remediate risk quicker and focus on things that provide a high return on investment.</p>
<p>CISA, ASD/ACSC, Mandiant, Microsoft and the UK NCSC have plenty of information and guides regarding protecting against ransomware and general best practices for logging, network architecture and tips for everyday users.</p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Outlook</h2>



<p>There is no magic bullet to make ransomware disappear, and we anticipate that ransomware-based attacks will continue while incentives remain. Across the globe, multiple countries like USA, Australia and Netherlands have announced task forces to focus on the ransomware threat.</p>
<p>Australia specifically announced their &#8220;Ransomware Action Plan&#8221; and planned to achieve part of it using offensive cyber capabilities [1]. The Netherlands has also announced similar efforts [2]. Ransomware actors may become risk-averse and select their targets more carefully to avoid being attacked by such operations. We hope to see more ransomware groups brought down in the following year, as well, as a consequence of such operations.</p>
