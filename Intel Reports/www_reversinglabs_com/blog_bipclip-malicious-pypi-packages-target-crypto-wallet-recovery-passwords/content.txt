<h1><span id="hs_cos_wrapper_name" class="hs_cos_wrapper hs_cos_wrapper_meta_field hs_cos_wrapper_type_text" style="" data-hs-cos-general-type="meta_field" data-hs-cos-type="text">BIPClip: Malicious PyPI packages target crypto wallet recovery passwords</span></h1>
<div id="hs_cos_wrapper_module_1577203992502740" class="hs_cos_wrapper hs_cos_wrapper_widget hs_cos_wrapper_type_module" style="" data-hs-cos-general-type="widget" data-hs-cos-type="module">
<h2 class="post-header__secondary-head blog__post-secondary-h2">RL has discovered a campaign using PyPI packages posing as open-source libraries to steal BIP39 mnemonic phrases, which are used for wallet recovery.</h2>

<div class="blog__post-content html-content">
<span id="hs_cos_wrapper_post_body" class="hs_cos_wrapper hs_cos_wrapper_meta_field hs_cos_wrapper_type_rich_text" style="" data-hs-cos-general-type="meta_field" data-hs-cos-type="rich_text"><p><img src="https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/Malicious-PyPI-packages-target-crypto-wallet-recovery-passwords.webp?width=1400&amp;height=732&amp;name=Malicious-PyPI-packages-target-crypto-wallet-recovery-passwords.webp" width="1400" height="732" loading="lazy" alt="Malicious PyPI packages target crypto wallet recovery passwords" style="height: auto; <p>ReversingLabs has identified a new, malicious campaign consisting of seven different open source packages with 19 different versions on the Python Package Index (PyPI), with the oldest package dating back to December, 2022. The campaign's goal: to steal mnemonic phrases used to recover lost or destroyed crypto wallets. &nbsp;<br><br>This is just the latest software supply chain campaign to target crypto assets — a list that includes the compromise of Voice over IP (VoIP) vendor 3CX. It confirms that cryptocurrency continues to be one of the most popular targets for supply chain threat actors.</p>
<p>This campaign, which my team is&nbsp;calling “BIPClip,” also underscores the steps that supply chain threat actors are taking to disguise their malicious wares, including the use of malicious file dependencies and various types of “name squatting” to throw security teams off their scent.</p>
<!--more-->
<h3>Discussion</h3>
<p>Here's what the RL research team knows about the malicious campaign, which is distributed through seven newly discovered malicious PyPI packages designed to work in concert to steal crypto wallet recovery phrases, all while minimizing the risk of detection.&nbsp;</p>
<h4>Crypto in the crosshairs</h4>
<p>The targets of this latest campaign were developers working on projects related to generating and securing cryptocurrency wallets. In particular, the attackers sought to fool developers looking to implement the <a href="https://github.com/bitcoin/bips/blob/master/bip-0039.mediawiki#user-content-Abstract" rel="noopener" target="_blank">Bitcoin Improvement Proposal 39, or BIP39</a>, a list of 2,048 easy-to-remember words that are used to generate a binary seed that creates deterministic BitCoin wallets (or "HD Wallets").The idea behind BIP39 is that a mnemonic code or sentence is easier for wallet owners to recall compared with raw binary or hexadecimal representations of a wallet seed, offering “computer-generated randomness with a human-readable transcription.”&nbsp;<br><br>Infrastructure and assets related to cryptocurrency creation, storage and transactions are a frequent target of supply chain attacks. That includes everything from the December 2023 <a href="https://checkmarx.com/blog/npm-account-takeover-results-in-crypto-supply-chain-attack/" rel="noopener" target="_blank">compromise of the open source Ledger Connect Kit</a>, resulting in the redirection of crypto transactions; to the publication of <a href="https://blog.sonatype.com/pypi-package-secretslib-drops-fileless-linux-malware-to-mine-monero" rel="noopener" target="_blank">Python libraries that covertly run cryptominers</a>; to posting <a href="/blog/more-malicious-npm-packages-found-in-wake-of-jumpcloud-supply-chain-hack" rel="noopener">malicious npm packages related to cryptocurrency applications and platforms</a>.&nbsp;<br><br>The interest in cryptocurrency applications and exchanges is easy to explain — a 21st century version of Willie Sutton's famous adage about robbing banks because “that’s where the money is.” In the case of cryptocurrency, nation-state actors affiliated with the Democratic Republic of North Korea (DPRK) are reported to have <a href="https://www.wsj.com/podcasts/the-journal/how-north-koreas-hacker-army-stole-3-billion-in-crypto/83d1b31d-3b93-41f2-9a5c-0916d43c3669" rel="noopener" target="_blank">stolen as much as $3 billion in cryptocurrency in the past five years</a>, accounting for as much as 5% of the country’s GDP.</p>
<h3>A malicious pair</h3>
<p>In the latest campaign, ReversingLabs initially discovered two PyPI packages that work together to exfiltrate sensitive data used to protect cryptocurrency wallets: <span style="font-style: italic;">mnemonic_to_address </span>and <span style="font-style: italic;">bip39_mnemonic_decrypt</span>.&nbsp;<br><br>The <span style="font-style: italic;">bip39_mnemonic_decrypt</span> package first turned up in a scan by our RL Spectra Assure platform due to a combination of "red flags"— suspicious characteristics in the package. Those included the presence of Base64 decoding as well as network communications, with <span style="font-style: italic;">bip39_mnemonic_decrypt</span> importing the <a href="https://requests.readthedocs.io/en/latest/" rel="noopener" target="_blank">requests</a> package, a common library typically used for network communication within the Python ecosystem.&nbsp;<br><br>After more investigation, the RL research team came to the conclusion that the campaign involved two packages, with the second package, <span style="font-style: italic;">mnemonic_to_address</span>, serving as a "clean" package with the malicious <span style="font-style: italic;">bip39_mnemonic_decrypt</span> listed as a dependency.</p>
<h4>Mnemonic_to_address: accomplice to a crime</h4>
<p>The first package the RL team discovered, <span style="font-style: italic;">mnemonic_to_address</span>, does not contain any malicious functionality. Rather, it faithfully implements the functionality advertised in the package description, namely: creating a seed from the user’s secret&nbsp;mnemonic seed phrase. The package does this by forwarding the BIP39 data to functions imported from another legitimate project: <a href="https://github.com/ethereum/eth-account" rel="noopener" target="_blank" style="font-style: italic;">eth-account</a>, which is maintained by <a href="https://github.com/ethereum" rel="noopener" target="_blank">Ethereum</a>.&nbsp;</p>
<a href="https://blog.reversinglabs.com/hubfs/Blog/blog-crypto-wallet-recovery-passwords-figure-01.webp" class="lightbox-image"><img src="https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/blog-crypto-wallet-recovery-passwords-figure-01.webp?width=1400&amp;name=blog-crypto-wallet-recovery-passwords-figure-01.webp" alt="Code example from eth-account documentation" width="1400" style="width: 1400px;" srcset="https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/blog-crypto-wallet-recovery-passwords-figure-01.webp?width=700&amp;name=blog-crypto-wallet-recovery-passwords-figure-01.webp 700w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/blog-crypto-wallet-recovery-passwords-figure-01.webp?width=1400&amp;name=blog-crypto-wallet-recovery-passwords-figure-01.webp 1400w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/blog-crypto-wallet-recovery-passwords-figure-01.webp?width=2100&amp;name=blog-crypto-wallet-recovery-passwords-figure-01.webp 2100w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/blog-crypto-wallet-recovery-passwords-figure-01.webp?width=2800&amp;name=blog-crypto-wallet-recovery-passwords-figure-01.webp 2800w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/blog-crypto-wallet-recovery-passwords-figure-01.webp?width=3500&amp;name=blog-crypto-wallet-recovery-passwords-figure-01.webp 3500w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/blog-crypto-wallet-recovery-passwords-figure-01.webp?width=4200&amp;name=blog-crypto-wallet-recovery-passwords-figure-01.webp 4200w" sizes="(max-width: 1400px) 100vw, 1400px"></a>
<p style="font-weight: bold; font-size: 16px; text-align: center;">Figure 1: Code example from eth-account documentation for generating an account from a mnemonic</p>
<p>The <span style="font-style: italic;">mnemonic_to_address </span>package basically serves as a wrapper and makes function calls as described in <span style="font-style: italic;">eth-account</span> project’s <a href="https://eth-account.readthedocs.io/en/stable/eth_account.html#eth_account.account.Account.from_mnemonic" rel="noopener" target="_blank">documentation</a> (Figure 1). But there’s one subtle difference: <span style="font-style: italic;">mnemonic_to_address</span> calls a function not present in the <span style="font-style: italic;">eth-account</span> package named <span style="font-style: italic;">decrypt_jsBIP39</span>. Where does that function come from? Well, it is imported from the <span style="font-style: italic;">bip39_mnemonic_decrypt</span> module, with code from the <span style="font-style: italic;">mnemonic_to_address</span> package passing the user's mnemonic passphrase to it as the function argument.&nbsp;</p>

<h4>Bip39_mnemonic_decrypt: subtly malicious</h4>
<p>The <em>bip39_mnemonic_decrypt</em> package is the second package from this campaign.&nbsp;It is declared as the dependency of the <em>mnemonic_to_address</em> package. It was in this package that ReversingLabs discovered clearly malicious functionality.&nbsp;<br><br>As with the <em>mnemonic_to_address</em> package, <em>bip39_mnemonic_decrypt</em> was published by <em>james_pycode</em>, a throwaway PyPI maintainer account that was created on the same day as the packages were published — a behavior that the RL research team&nbsp;often find associated with malicious campaigns distributed through open source package repositories.&nbsp;<br><br>As you can see from the maintainer’s account (Figure 3), minimal effort was made to bolster the reputation or credibility of the <em>james_pycode</em> account prior to — or after publishing the malicious PyPI packages. That’s not always the case. Sophisticated supply chain attackers that leverage open source repositories often invest time and resources to mimic official pages.&nbsp;<br><br></p>
<a href="https://blog.reversinglabs.com/hubfs/Blog/blog-crypto-wallet-recovery-passwords-figure-03.webp" class="lightbox-image"><img src="https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/blog-crypto-wallet-recovery-passwords-figure-03.webp?width=1400&amp;name=blog-crypto-wallet-recovery-passwords-figure-03.webp" alt="Throwaway account used to publish the malicious packages" width="1400" style="width: 1400px;" srcset="https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/blog-crypto-wallet-recovery-passwords-figure-03.webp?width=700&amp;name=blog-crypto-wallet-recovery-passwords-figure-03.webp 700w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/blog-crypto-wallet-recovery-passwords-figure-03.webp?width=1400&amp;name=blog-crypto-wallet-recovery-passwords-figure-03.webp 1400w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/blog-crypto-wallet-recovery-passwords-figure-03.webp?width=2100&amp;name=blog-crypto-wallet-recovery-passwords-figure-03.webp 2100w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/blog-crypto-wallet-recovery-passwords-figure-03.webp?width=2800&amp;name=blog-crypto-wallet-recovery-passwords-figure-03.webp 2800w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/blog-crypto-wallet-recovery-passwords-figure-03.webp?width=3500&amp;name=blog-crypto-wallet-recovery-passwords-figure-03.webp 3500w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/blog-crypto-wallet-recovery-passwords-figure-03.webp?width=4200&amp;name=blog-crypto-wallet-recovery-passwords-figure-03.webp 4200w" sizes="(max-width: 1400px) 100vw, 1400px"></a>
<p style="font-weight: bold; font-size: 16px; text-align: center;">Figure 3: Throwaway account used to publish the malicious packages</p>
<p>But that doesn’t mean the malicious actor behind this campaign didn’t make an effort to hide their malicious wares. Just the opposite — this campaign took a number of steps to avoid detection.&nbsp;<br><br>The first, as noted above, was the use of a malicious file dependency to facilitate the supply chain attack. The advantage of this approach is obvious: a developer who decides to use the <em>mnemonic_to_address</em> package and audits the code would conclude that the file was not malicious and worked “as advertised.” However, that audit might not extend to a security assessment of the <em>mnemonic_to_address</em> package’s many dependencies.&nbsp;<br><br>Even if they did opt to look at the package’s dependencies, the name of the imported module and invoked function are carefully chosen to mimic legitimate functions and not raise suspicion, since implementations of the BIP39 standard include many cryptographic operations.&nbsp;</p>
<a href="https://blog.reversinglabs.com/hubfs/Blog/blog-crypto-wallet-recovery-passwords-figure-04.webp" class="lightbox-image"><img src="https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/blog-crypto-wallet-recovery-passwords-figure-04.webp?width=1400&amp;name=blog-crypto-wallet-recovery-passwords-figure-04.webp" alt="Malicious function from bip39_mnemonic_decrypt package" width="1400" style="width: 1400px;" srcset="https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/blog-crypto-wallet-recovery-passwords-figure-04.webp?width=700&amp;name=blog-crypto-wallet-recovery-passwords-figure-04.webp 700w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/blog-crypto-wallet-recovery-passwords-figure-04.webp?width=1400&amp;name=blog-crypto-wallet-recovery-passwords-figure-04.webp 1400w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/blog-crypto-wallet-recovery-passwords-figure-04.webp?width=2100&amp;name=blog-crypto-wallet-recovery-passwords-figure-04.webp 2100w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/blog-crypto-wallet-recovery-passwords-figure-04.webp?width=2800&amp;name=blog-crypto-wallet-recovery-passwords-figure-04.webp 2800w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/blog-crypto-wallet-recovery-passwords-figure-04.webp?width=3500&amp;name=blog-crypto-wallet-recovery-passwords-figure-04.webp 3500w, https://www.reversinglabs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog/blog-crypto-wallet-recovery-passwords-figure-04.webp?width=4200&amp;name=blog-crypto-wallet-recovery-passwords-figure-04.webp 4200w" sizes="(max-width: 1400px) 100vw, 1400px"></a>
<p style="font-weight: bold; font-size: 16px; text-align: center;">Figure 4: Malicious function from bip39_mnemonic_decrypt package designed to exfiltrate the data received as function argument</p>
<p>Specifically, the malicious function, <em>decrypt_jsBIP39</em>, is hidden in the <em>bip39_mnemonic_decrypt</em> package at the very end of the <em>__init__.py</em> file, coming after several, non-malicious functions that are not actually used in the code base. A developer looking for red flags would have to be careful to examine <em>_init_.py</em> and scroll to the end of the file to discover the malicious function.&nbsp;<br><br>On first look, <em>decrypt_jsBIP39</em> is a pretty straightforward function. First, it decodes the Base64 encoded URL of the data exfiltration server. Then it invokes another function with a cleverly chosen name: <em>cli_keccak256</em>. That name is no accident: <em>keccak256</em> is a cryptographic hash function commonly used to compute the hashes of Ethereum addresses, transaction IDs, and other important values in the Ethereum ecosystem.</p>
<p>Malicious code found in this package encodes the provided mnemonic passphrase using Base64 and then sends it to the exfiltration server using a HTTP POST request. The malicious code further disguises the passphrase by placing it in the “license” data field. For security tools or operators monitoring network traffic, this encoded text sequence might be interpreted as a legitimate software license value and overlooked.<br><br></p>
<h3>Evidence of an broader campaign</h3>
<p>After analyzing the initial “malicious pair,” RL researchers discovered three additional packages on PyPI in the first week of March that also appear to be a part of this campaign.</p>
<h4>Another malicious pair</h4>
<p>The first two, <em>public-address-generator</em> and <em>erc20-scanner</em><span style="font-weight: normal;">,</span>&nbsp;were also published from a throwaway PyPI account on March 1st. Peeking under the hood: they appear to work the same way as the <em>mnemonic_to_address </em>and <em>bip39_mnemonic_decrypt </em>pair described above. Malicious functionality identical to that found in the bip39_mnemonic_decrypt package is implemented in the <em>erc20-scanner</em> package. The <em>public-address-generator</em> package serves the same role as the <em>mnemonic_to_address </em>package, acting as a lure for the targets.&nbsp;<br><br>Links to the BIPClip campaign are evident. In addition to shared code and functionality, the newer packages use the same command and control (C2) server to exfiltrate stolen mnemonics.</p>
<h4>Hashdecrypts: venomous code</h4>
<p>The third package, <em>hashdecrypts</em>, also appears connected to the BIPClip campaign, but revealed more information about it.&nbsp;<br><br>The <em>hashdecrypts</em> package was published on March 1st by a PyPI user account, <em>luislindao</em>, that was first registered in August 2019.&nbsp; It contains almost identical malicious code to the <em>bip39_mnemonic_decrypt</em> and <em>erc20-scanner</em> packages, but adds another level of redirection.&nbsp;</p>

<h4>HashSnake’s long tail</h4>
<p>A look at the commit history for the package reveals that the campaign began more than a year ago, with the first commit to the HashSnake github repository on February 5th, 2023. It also revealed that the repository previously imported a different package, <em>hashdecrypt </em>(Editor's note: no trailing "s"), that was first published on December 4, 2022. All three published versions of that package contained the same malicious functionality and fetched the same command and control (C2) server address from the same GitHub repository.&nbsp;<br><br></p>

<p>Looking at the commit history of the <em>backendapi/settings</em> file reveals the C2 infrastructure used throughout the history. Each commit modifies the address of the true C2 server, with the first commit dating all the way back to December 4th, 2022 — the same day that the first version of the <em>hashdecrypt</em> package was published.<br><br></p>
<h4>Modesty and stealth</h4>
<p>The threat actors behind this campaign combined a variety of known and well-documented methods to achieve their goals while avoiding detection. First, they made their packages less suspicious by putting their malicious functionality into dependent packages and not into the packages that were directly distributed to their targets. That basic evasion demands more of would-be target organizations. Targets inspecting open source packages wouldn’t find anything malicious in the primary package, but might not bother to investigate the (many) file dependencies it contains. Practically, few development organizations have the resources or time to dig that deeply into the open source code they rely on.&nbsp;<br><br>Furthermore, the content of each of the discovered packages was carefully crafted to make it look less suspicious. The distributed packages <em>public-address-generator</em> and <em>mnemonic_to_address</em> implement their functionality as advertised. Code in both packages was written to look like it is truly dealing with cryptographic operations expected from the package that deals with services related to crypto assets.&nbsp;<br><br>The threat actors behind this campaign weren’t greedy, either. They focused only on what they wanted to get, making no effort to leverage their access to achieve full control over a compromised system or move laterally within the compromised development organization. Instead, they were laser focused on compromising crypto wallets and stealing the crypto currencies they contained. That absence of a broader agenda and ambitions made it less likely this campaign would trip up security and monitoring tools deployed within compromised organizations.<br><br></p>
<h4>Impact</h4>
<p>Based on our research, the impact of the campaign was limited. The initial malicious package RL&nbsp;discovered, <em>bip39_mnemonic_decrypt</em>, was only available for download for around two weeks: from February 4th until February 19th before it was detected and removed from PyPI. During that time, it was downloaded almost 300 times.<br><br>The additional packages RL&nbsp;discovered in early March, <em>public-address-generator</em>, <em>erc20-scanner</em>, and <em>hashdecrypts</em>: were all taken down shortly after appearing. Only the <em>hashdecrypt</em> package, which was discovered based on the reference in the GitHub repository, appears to have been available for longer, with versions of that package existing as far back as December 2022.&nbsp;<br><br>Not surprisingly, the number of downloads of each of these packages was limited. There were 997 downloads of the <em>public-address-generator</em> package, 341 of the <em>erc20-scanner</em> package, and 224 of the <em>hashdecrypts</em> package. Our assessment of the overall reach of this campaign, therefore, is that it was limited. The newly discovered PyPI packages were quickly removed from the package manager and likely did not cause much damage.</p>
<p>The story is a bit different in the case of <em>hashdecrypt</em>, and the security impact may be greater. <h3>Conclusion</h3>
<p>The BIPClip campaign is more proof that developers need to be vigilant about software supply chain security threats which lurk in open source package repositories.&nbsp;<br><br>Threat actors like those behind the BIPClip campaign clearly understand that harried developers and development organizations aren't inclined to dig too deeply into the packages they are downloading and incorporating into their applications. Simple measures on the part of supply chain threat actors, such as clever naming and delivering malicious code via code dependencies, are enough to evade detection.&nbsp;<br><br>For development organizations, the time to raise the bar on software supply chain security is now. Software hygiene assessments need to be performed on a regular basis. These should include security assessments of third party tools used in the development process, as well as regular vetting of software release artifacts before they are shipped to ensure that software artifacts ship without malicious implants.&nbsp;<br><br>The BIPClip campaign also provides more evidence (if any was needed) that crypto assets are one of the most popular targets of cybercriminal groups and other threat actors (like North Korean APTs). These groups are well resourced and capable of uncovering subtle and ingenious ways to get their hands on the contents of crypto wallets and exchanges. As an individual, that means you need to keep an open eye on your crypto wallets as well as sensitive information like private keys and mnemonic phrases that can be abused by threat actors to gain control of your crypto assets. As a developer working on cryptocurrency applications or crypto-adjacent apps and services, it means presuming that your applications and code will be targeted by sophisticated cybercriminal actors eyeing supply chain compromises, and setting your security bar appropriately.&nbsp;<br><br></p>
