		<div class="q-single__post--toc"><div class="toc"><h4>Table of Contents</h4><ul><li><a href='#technical-analysis'>Technical Analysis:</a></li><li><a href='#indicators-of-compromise-iocs'>Indicators of Compromise (IOCs):</a></li><li><a href='#agent-tesla-ttp-map'>Agent Tesla TTP Map:</a></li><li><a href='#mitigation-or-additional-important-safety-measures'>Mitigation or Additional Important Safety Measures</a></li></ul></div></div>
		<div class="q-single__post--content">
			
<p>For the last few years, the Qualys Research Team has been observing an infamous &#8220;Malware-as-a-service&#8221; RAT (Remote Access Trojan) called Agent Tesla.</p>



<p>It first appeared in 2014, and since then many variants have been deployed. This malware uses multiple techniques for evading detection as well as making analysis quite difficult. Agent Tesla mainly gets delivered through phishing emails and has capabilities such as keylogging, screen capture, form-grabbing, credential stealing, and more. It will also exfiltrate credentials from multiple software programs like Google Chrome, Mozilla Firefox, and Microsoft Outlook – making its potential impact truly catastrophic.</p>



<p>The malware itself goes through multiple layers of unpacking before deploying its final payload, which is very similar behavior to what’s found in families like Formbook. Agent Tesla is dotnet compiled malware and uses a <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steganography">steganography</a> technique. We have observed a sudden increase in the use of this technique.</p>



<p>This blog reviews Agent Tesla malware’s updated functionality as well as its ongoing evolution.</p>



<h2 id="technical-analysis" class="wp-block-heading">Technical Analysis:</h2>



<p>Agent Tesla performs two-level unpacking to get its final payload delivered, as shown in this flow chart diagram.</p>



<figure class="wp-block-image size-full"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" width="853" height="480" data-attachment-id="29292" data-permalink="https://blog.qualys.com/vulnerabilities-threat-research/2022/02/02/catching-the-rat-called-agent-tesla/attachment/flowchart-diagram" data-orig-file="https://blog.qualys.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/02/Flowchart-Diagram.gif" data-orig-size="853,480" data-comments-opened="1" data-image-meta="{&quot;aperture&quot;:&quot;0&quot;,&quot;credit&quot;:&quot;&quot;,&quot;camera&quot;:&quot;&quot;,&quot;caption&quot;:&quot;&quot;,&quot;created_timestamp&quot;:&quot;0&quot;,&quot;copyright&quot;:&quot;&quot;,&quot;focal_length&quot;:&quot;0&quot;,&quot;iso&quot;:&quot;0&quot;,&quot;shutter_speed&quot;:&quot;0&quot;,&quot;title&quot;:&quot;&quot;,&quot;orientation&quot;:&quot;0&quot;}" data-image-title="Flowchart-Diagram" data-image-description="" data-image-caption="" data-medium-file="https://blog.qualys.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/02/Flowchart-Diagram-300x169.gif" data-large-file="https://blog.qualys.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/02/Flowchart-Diagram.gif" src="https://blog.qualys.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/02/Flowchart-Diagram.gif" alt="" class="wp-image-29292"/></figure>

<p>In the malware sample, the method names and strings have been heavily obfuscated, as shown in fig. 1.</p>

<p>This module is called &#8220;representative&#8221;, which is a dotnet compiled dll module. After de-obfuscation, the main payload loads this first stage dll module in memory.</p>



<p>Agent Tesla uses a steganography technique as shown in fig. 3, where an image contains an embedded PE file. This resource image is used by the first stage dll module to extract the second stage dll module.</p>

<p>Initial decryption logic is the same as is used for the second stage dll module extraction… but with a different key. After initial decryption routines, further decryption is carried out where data is decrypted with a 16 bytes XOR key. This key is present at the start of the previously decrypted buffer. After this decryption, the malware delivers the final payload (fig. 9).</p>

<p>It also steals information such as IMAP Password, POP3 Password, HTTP Password, and SMTP Password. For this, it checks Microsoft Outlook registry entries as shown below (fig. 20).</p>

<h2 id="mitigation-or-additional-important-safety-measures" class="wp-block-heading">Mitigation or Additional Important Safety Measures</h2>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Keep software updated</h3>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Always keep your security software (antivirus, firewall, etc.) up to date to protect your computer from new variants of malware.</li>



<li>Regularly patch and update applications, software, and operating systems to address any exploitable software vulnerabilities.</li>



<li>Do not download cracked/pirated software as they risk backdoor entry for malware into your computer.</li>



<li>Avoid downloading software from untrusted P2P or torrent sites. In most cases, they are malicious software.</li>
</ul>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Beware of emails</h3>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Don’t open attachments and links from unsolicited emails. Delete suspicious looking emails you receive from unknown sources, especially if they contain links or attachments. Cybercriminals use ‘Social Engineering’ techniques to lure users into opening attachments or clicking on links that lead to infected websites.</li>
</ul>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Disable macros for Microsoft Office</h3>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Don’t enable macros in document attachments received via emails. A lot of malware infections rely on your actin to turn ON macros.</li>



<li>Consider installing Microsoft Office Viewers. These viewer applications let you see what documents look like without even opening them in Word or Excel. More importantly, the viewer software doesn’t support macros at all, so this reduces the risk of enabling macros unintentionally.</li>
</ul>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Having minimum required privileges</h3>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Don’t assign Administrator privileges to users. Most importantly, don’t stay logged in as an administrator unless it is strictly necessary. Also, avoid browsing, opening documents or other regular work activities while logged in as an administrator.</li>
</ul>
