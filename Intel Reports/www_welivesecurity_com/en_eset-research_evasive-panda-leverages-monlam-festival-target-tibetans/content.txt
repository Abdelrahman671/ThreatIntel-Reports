    <h1 class="page-headline">Evasive Panda leverages Monlam Festival to target Tibetans</h1>    <p class="sub-title">ESET researchers uncover strategic web compromise and supply-chain attacks targeting Tibetans</p>
    
    <div class="article-authors d-flex flex-wrap"><div class="article-author d-flex"><a href="/en/our-experts/anh-ho/" title="Anh Ho"><picture><source srcset="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/tn/-x45/wls/anh-ho-1.jpeg" media="(max-width: 768px)" /><img class="author-image me-3"  src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/tn/-x45/wls/anh-ho-1.jpeg" alt="Anh Ho"  /></picture></a><div class="author-text"><p><a href="/en/our-experts/anh-ho/" title="Anh Ho"><b>Anh Ho</b></a></p></div></div><div class="article-author d-flex"><a href="/en/our-experts/facundo-munoz/" title="Facundo Muñoz"><picture><source srcset="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/tn/-x45/wls/2021/02/facundo-munoz.jpeg" media="(max-width: 768px)" /><img class="author-image me-3"  src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/tn/-x45/wls/2021/02/facundo-munoz.jpeg" alt="Facundo Muñoz"  /></picture></a><div class="author-text"><p><a href="/en/our-experts/facundo-munoz/" title="Facundo Muñoz"><b>Facundo Muñoz</b></a></p></div></div><div class="article-author d-flex"><a href="/en/our-experts/marc-etienne-m-leveille/" title="Marc-Etienne M.Léveillé"><picture><source srcset="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/tn/-x45/wls/2013/06/Marc-Etienne-M.Leveille.png" media="(max-width: 768px)" /><img class="author-image me-3"  src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/tn/-x45/wls/2013/06/Marc-Etienne-M.Leveille.png" alt="Marc-Etienne M.Léveillé"  /></picture></a><div class="author-text"><p><a href="/en/our-experts/marc-etienne-m-leveille/" title="Marc-Etienne M.Léveillé"><b>Marc-Etienne M.Léveillé</b></a></p></div></div></div>

                    <div class="article-body">
    <p>ESET researchers discovered a cyberespionage campaign that, since at least September 2023, has been victimizing Tibetans through a targeted watering hole (also known as a strategic web compromise), and a supply-chain compromise to deliver trojanized installers of Tibetan language translation software. The attackers aimed to deploy malicious downloaders for Windows and macOS to compromise website visitors with MgBot and a backdoor that, to the best of our knowledge, has not been publicly documented yet; we have named it Nightdoor.</p>
<div>
<blockquote><strong>Key points in this blogpost:</strong>
<ul>
<li>We discovered a cyberespionage campaign that leverages the Monlam Festival &ndash; a religious gathering &ndash; to target Tibetans in several countries and territories.</li>
<li>The attackers compromised the website of the organizer of the annual festival, which takes place in India, and added malicious code to create a watering-hole attack targeting users connecting from specific networks.</li>
<li>We also discovered that a software developer&rsquo;s supply chain was compromised and trojanized installers for Windows and macOS were served to users.</li>
<li>The attackers fielded a number of malicious downloaders and full-featured backdoors for the operation, including a publicly undocumented backdoor for Windows that we have named Nightdoor.</li>
<li>We attribute this campaign with high confidence to the China-aligned Evasive Panda APT group.</li>
</ul>
</blockquote>
</div>
<h2>Evasive Panda profile</h2>
<p><a href="https://vb2020.vblocalhost.com/uploads/VB2020-43.pdf" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Evasive Panda</a> (also known as <a href="https://www.secureworks.com/research/threat-profiles/bronze-highland" target="_blank" rel="noopener">BRONZE HIGHLAND</a> and <a href="https://symantec-enterprise-blogs.security.com/blogs/threat-intelligence/apt-attacks-telecoms-africa-mgbot" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Daggerfly</a>) is a Chinese-speaking APT group, <a href="https://www.virusbulletin.com/virusbulletin/2014/02/needle-haystack" target="_blank" rel="noopener">active since at least 2012</a>. ESET Research has observed the group conducting cyberespionage against individuals in mainland China, Hong Kong, Macao, and Nigeria. Government entities were targeted in Southeast and East Asia, specifically China, Macao, Myanmar, The Philippines, Taiwan, and Vietnam. Other organizations in China and Hong Kong were also targeted. According to public reports, the group has also targeted unknown entities in Hong Kong, India, and Malaysia.</p>
<p>The group uses its own custom malware framework with a modular architecture that allows its backdoor, known as MgBot, to receive modules to spy on its victims and enhance its capabilities. Since 2020 we have also observed that Evasive Panda has capabilities to deliver its backdoors via adversary-in-the-middle attacks <a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/2023/04/26/evasive-panda-apt-group-malware-updates-popular-chinese-software/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">hijacking updates of legitimate software</a>.</p>
<h2>Campaign overview</h2>
<p>In January 2024, we discovered a cyberespionage operation in which attackers compromised at least three websites to carry out watering-hole attacks as well as a supply-chain compromise of a Tibetan software company.</p>
<p>The compromised website abused as a watering hole belongs to Kagyu International Monlam Trust, an organization based in India that promotes Tibetan Buddhism internationally. The attackers placed a script in the website that verifies the IP address of the potential victim and if it is within one of the targeted ranges of addresses, shows a fake error page to entice the user to download a &ldquo;fix&rdquo; named <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">certificate</span> (with a .exe extension if the visitor is using Windows or <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">.pkg</span> if macOS). This file is a malicious downloader that deploys the next stage in the compromise chain.</p>
<p>Based on the IP address ranges the code checks for, we discovered that the attackers targeted users in India, Taiwan, Hong Kong, Australia, and the United States; the attack might have aimed to capitalize on international interest in the Kagyu Monlam Festival (Figure 1) that is held annually in January in the city of Bodhgaya, India.</p>

<h3>Breaking the hash</h3>
<p>The condition for payload delivery requires getting the correct hash from the server at <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">update.devicebug[.]com</span>, so the 74 hashes are the key to the attacker&rsquo;s victim selection mechanism. However, since the hash is computed on the server side, it posed a challenge for us to know what data is used to compute it.</p>
<p>We experimented with different IP addresses and system configurations and narrowed down the input for the MD5 algorithm to a formula of the first three octets of the user&rsquo;s IP address. In other words, by inputting IP addresses sharing the same network prefix, for example <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">192.168.0.1</span> and <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">192.168.0.50</span>, will receive the same MD5 hash from the C&amp;C server.</p>
<p>However, an unknown combination of characters, or a <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salt_(cryptography)">salt</a>,</span> is included with the string of first three IP octets before hashing to prevent the hashes from being trivially brute-forced. Therefore, we needed to brute-force the salt to secure the input formula and only then generate hashes using the entire range of IPv4 addresses to find the matching 74 hashes.</p>
<p>Sometimes the stars do align, and we figured out that the salt was <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">1qaz0okm!@#</span>. With all pieces of the MD5 input formula (for example, <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">192.168.1.1qaz0okm!@#</span>), we brute-forced the 74 hashes with ease and generated a list of targets. See the <a href="#Appendix">Appendix</a> for a complete list.</p>
<p>As shown in Figure 9, the majority of targeted IP address ranges are in India, followed by Taiwan, Australia, the United States, and Hong Kong. Note that most of the <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tibetan_diaspora" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Tibetan diaspora</a>&nbsp;lives in India.</p>

Figure 14 shows the script responsible for installing and launching the malicious Launch Agent.</p>

<p>The malicious Mach-O, <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">Monlam-bodyig_Keyboard_2017</span> in Figure 13 is signed, but not notarized, using a developer certificate (not a <a href="https://developer.apple.com/help/account/reference/certificate-types" target="_blank" rel="noopener">certificate type</a><a name="_Hlt160018372"></a><a name="_Hlt160018371"></a> usually used for distribution) with the name and team identifier <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">ya ni yang</span> (<span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">2289F6V4BN</span>). The timestamp in the signature shows that it was signed January 7<sup>th</sup>, 2024. This date is also used in the modified timestamp of the malicious files in the metadata of the ZIP archive. The certificate was issued only three days before. The full certificate is available in the <a href="#IoCs">IoCs</a> section. Our team reached out to Apple on January 25<sup>th</sup> and the certificate was revoked the same day.</p>
<p>This first-stage malware downloads a JSON file that contains the URL to the next stage. The architecture (ARM or Intel), macOS version, and hardware UUID (an identifier unique to each Mac) are reported in the User-Agent HTTP request header. The same URL as the Windows version is used to retrieve that configuration: <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">https://www.monlamit[.]com/sites/default/files/softwares/updateFiles/Monlam_Grand_Tibetan_Dictionary_2018/UpdateInfo.dat</span>. However, the macOS version will look at the data under the mac key of the JSON object instead of the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">win</span> key.</p>
<p>The object under the mac key should contain the following:</p>
<ul>
<li><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">url</span>: The URL to the next stage.</li>
<li><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">md5</span>: MD5 sum of the payload.</li>
<li><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">vernow</span>: A list of hardware UUIDs. If present, the payload will only be installed on Macs that have one of the listed hardware UUIDs. This check is skipped if the list is empty or missing.</li>
<li><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">version</span>: A numerical value that must be higher than the previously downloaded second stage &ldquo;version&rdquo;. The payload is not downloaded otherwise. The value of the currently running version is kept in the application <a href="https://developer.apple.com/documentation/foundation/nsuserdefaults?language=objc%20%22NSUserDefaults%20documentation" target="_blank" rel="noopener">user defaults</a><a name="_Hlt160018610"></a><a name="_Hlt160018609"></a>.</li>
</ul>
<p>After the malware downloads the file from the specified URL using curl, the file is hashed using MD5 and compared to the hexadecimal digest under the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">md5</span> key. If it matches, its extended attributes are removed (to clear the com.apple.quarantine attribute), the file is moved to <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">$HOME/Library/SafariBrowser/Safari.app/Contents/MacOS/SafariBrower</span>, and is launched using <a href="https://developer.apple.com/library/archive/documentation/System/Conceptual/ManPages_iPhoneOS/man3/execvp.3.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">execvp</span></a> with the argument run.</p>
<p>Unlike the Windows version, we could not find any of the later stages of the macOS variant. One JSON configuration contained an MD5 hash (<span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">3C5739C25A9B85E82E0969EE94062F40</span>), but the URL field was empty.</p>
<h2 id="Nightdoor">Nightdoor</h2>
<p>The backdoor that we have named Nightdoor (and is named NetMM by the malware authors according to PDB paths) is a late addition to Evasive Panda&rsquo;s toolset. Our earliest knowledge of Nightdoor goes back to 2020, when Evasive Panda deployed it onto a machine of a high-profile target in Vietnam. The backdoor communicates with its C&amp;C server via UDP or the Google Drive API. The Nightdoor implant from this campaign used the latter. It encrypts a Google API <a href="https://developers.google.com/identity/protocols/oauth2" target="_blank" rel="noopener">OAuth 2.0</a> token within the data section and uses the token to access the attacker&rsquo;s Google Drive. We have requested that the Google account associated with this token be taken down.</p>
<p>First, Nightdoor creates a folder in Google Drive containing the victim&rsquo;s MAC address, which also acts as a victim ID. This folder will contain all the messages between the implant and the C&amp;C server. Each message between Nightdoor and the C&amp;C server is structured as a file and separated into filename and file data, as depicted in Figure 15.</p>
<figure><img title="Figure 15. <p>Each filename contains eight main attributes, which is demonstrated in the example below.</p>
<p>Example:</p>
<p><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">1_2_0C64C2BAEF534C8E9058797BCD783DE5_168_0_1_4116_0_00-00-00-00-00-00</span></p>
<ul>
<li><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">1_2</span>: magic value.</li>
<li><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">0C64C2BAEF534C8E9058797BCD783DE5</span>: header of <a href="https://www.nongnu.org/lwip/2_0_x/group__pbuf.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener">pbuf</a> data structure.</li>
<li><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">168</span>: size of the message object or file size in bytes.</li>
<li><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">0</span>: filename, which is always the default of 0 (null).</li>
<li><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">1</span>: command type, hardcoded to 1 or 0 depending on the sample.</li>
<li><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">4116</span>: command ID.</li>
<li><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">0</span>: quality of service (QoS).</li>
<li><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">00-00-00-00-00-00</span>: meant to be MAC address of the destination but always defaults to <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">00-00-00-00-00-00</span>.</li>
</ul>

In previous versions, each command ID was assigned to a handler function one by one, as shown in Figure 18. <h2>Conclusion</h2>
<p>We have analyzed a campaign by the China-aligned APT Evasive Panda that targeted Tibetans in several countries and territories. We believe that the attackers capitalized, at the time, on the upcoming Monlam festival in January and February of 2024 to compromise users when they visited the festival&rsquo;s website-turned-watering-hole. In addition, the attackers compromised the supply chain of a software developer of Tibetan language translation apps.</p>
<p>The attackers fielded several downloaders, droppers, and backdoors, including MgBot &ndash; which is used exclusively by Evasive Panda &ndash; and Nightdoor: the latest major addition to the group&rsquo;s toolkit and which has been used to target several networks in East Asia.</p>
