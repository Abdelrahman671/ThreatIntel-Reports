          <span class="f6 mt0" dir="ltr"><a href="https://citizenlab.ca/category/research/" class="breadcrumbs link"><a href="https://citizenlab.ca/category/research/" class="breadcrumbs link">Research</a><span class="fa-solid fa-chevron-right mh2" aria-hidden="true"></span></a><a href="https://citizenlab.ca/category/research/targeted-threats/" class="breadcrumbs link">Targeted Threats</a></span>
            <h1 itemprop="headline" rel="bookmark" class="ma0 mt5 lh-title">
            	<!-- Title -->
	<span class="db f2 f1-ns black lh-solid">The Million Dollar Dissident</span>
	<!-- Subtitle -->
	<span class="db f4 f2-ns mid-gray mt2 lh-title oswald-regular"> NSO Group&#8217;s iPhone Zero-Days used against a UAE Human Rights Defender</span>
	            </h1>
            <div dir="ltr" class="mt2">
              
            <div class="f5 mr4 b dark-gray dib">By 
            <a href="https://citizenlab.ca/author/bmarczak/" title="Posts by Bill Marczak" class="author url fn" rel="author">Bill Marczak</a> and <a href="https://citizenlab.ca/author/jsrailton/" title="Posts by John Scott-Railton" class="author url fn" rel="author">John Scott-Railton</a></div>

                          <div>

                   <a class="f6 link br1 ba ph3 pv2 mb2 mt2 dib black hover-bg-lightest-blue hover-black" href="https://tspace.library.utoronto.ca/bitstream/1807/96976/1/Report%2378--Million-Dollar-Dissident.pdf" target="" title="Download Download this report">Download this report</a>

                <section itemprop="articleBody" class="article-body mb4 mt4 pt2 bt b--light-gray">
                  <!DOCTYPE html PUBLIC "-//W3C//DTD HTML 4.0 Transitional//EN" "http://www.w3.org/TR/REC-html40/loose.dtd">
<html><body><p><span style="font-weight: 400;">This report was written with the assistance of the research team at Lookout Security.</span></p>
<p><b>Update (Sept 1, 2016)</b>: Today Apple&nbsp;<a href="https://support.apple.com/en-us/HT201222" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener">released security updates</a>&nbsp;for Desktop Safari and Mac OS X. &nbsp;These updates patch the Trident vulnerabilities that identified in this report for desktop users. The Trident vulnerabilities used by NSO could have been weaponized against users of non iOS devices, including OSX.&nbsp;<strong>We encourage all Apple users to install the update as soon as possible</strong>.&nbsp;Citizen Lab is not releasing samples of the attack at this time to protect the integrity of still-ongoing investigations.</p>
<p><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">This report describes how a government targeted an internationally recognized human rights defender, Ahmed Mansoor, with the Trident, a chain of zero-day exploits designed to infect his iPhone with sophisticated commercial spyware.</span></i></p>
<h1 class=" lh-solid mb3"><b style="">1. Executive Summary</b></h1>
<p class="mt0"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Ahmed Mansoor is an internationally recognized human rights defender, based in the United Arab Emirates (UAE), and recipient of the </span><a href="https://www.amnesty.org/en/latest/news/2015/10/ahmed-mansoor-selected-as-the-2015-laureate-martin-ennals-award-for-human-rights-defenders/" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Martin Ennals Award</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> (sometimes referred to as a &ldquo;</span><a href="http://www.lemonde.fr/asie-pacifique/article/2008/11/24/le-combat-de-mutabar-tadjibaeva-survivante-des-geoles-de-tachkent_1122352_3216.html" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Nobel Prize for human rights</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">&rdquo;). &nbsp;On August 10 and 11, 2016, Mansoor received SMS text messages on his iPhone promising &ldquo;new secrets&rdquo; about detainees tortured in UAE jails if he clicked on an included link. Instead of clicking, Mansoor sent the messages to Citizen Lab researchers. &nbsp;We recognized the links as belonging to an exploit infrastructure connected to NSO Group, an Israel-based &ldquo;cyber war&rdquo; company that sells </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">Pegasus</span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;">, a government-exclusive &ldquo;lawful intercept&rdquo; spyware product. &nbsp;NSO Group is reportedly owned by an American venture capital firm, Francisco Partners Management.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">The ensuing investigation, a collaboration between researchers from Citizen Lab and from Lookout Security, determined that the links led to a chain of </span><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zero-day_(computing)" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">zero-day exploits</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> (&ldquo;zero-days&rdquo;) that would have remotely </span><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IOS_jailbreaking?" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">jailbroken</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> Mansoor&rsquo;s stock iPhone 6 and installed sophisticated spyware. &nbsp;We are calling this exploit chain</span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">&nbsp;Trident</span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;">. &nbsp;Once infected, Mansoor&rsquo;s phone would have become a digital spy in his pocket, capable of employing his iPhone&rsquo;s camera and microphone to snoop on activity in the vicinity of the device, recording his WhatsApp and Viber calls, logging messages sent in mobile chat apps, and tracking his movements. &nbsp;</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">We are not aware of any previous instance of an iPhone remote jailbreak used in the wild as part of a targeted attack campaign, making this a rare find.</span></p>
<div class="about-author pa3 br1 f5 lh-copy mv1 bg-washed-yellow ba b--mid-gray" style="">
<p><b>The Trident Exploit Chain:</b></p>
<ul style="margin: 0px;">
<li style="font-weight: 400;" class="mt2"><a href="https://www.cve.org/CVERecord?id=CVE-2016-4657" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><b>CVE-2016-4657</b></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">: Visiting a maliciously crafted website may lead to arbitrary code execution</span></li>
<li style="font-weight: 400;" class="mt2"><a href="https://www.cve.org/CVERecord?id=CVE-2016-4655" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><b>CVE-2016-4655</b></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">: An application may be able to disclose kernel memory</span></li>
<li style="font-weight: 400;" class="mt2"><a href="https://www.cve.org/CVERecord?id=CVE-2016-4656" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><b>CVE-2016-4656</b></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">: An application may be able to execute arbitrary code with kernel privileges</span></li>
</ul>
</div>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Once we confirmed the presence of what appeared to be iOS zero-days, Citizen Lab and Lookout quickly initiated a responsible disclosure process by notifying Apple and sharing our findings. Apple responded promptly, and notified us that they would be addressing the vulnerabilities. We are releasing this report to coincide with the availability of the iOS 9.3.5 patch, which blocks the Trident exploit chain by closing the vulnerabilities that NSO Group appears to have exploited and sold to remotely compromise iPhones.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Recent Citizen Lab research has shown that many state-sponsored spyware campaigns against civil society groups and human rights defenders use &ldquo;</span><a href="https://targetedthreats.net/" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">just</span></a> <a href="https://citizenlab.ca/2016/08/group5-syria/" class="pointer"><span style="font-weight: 400;">enough</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">&rdquo; technical sophistication, coupled with carefully planned deception. This case demonstrates that not all threats follow this pattern. &nbsp;The iPhone has a well-deserved reputation for security. &nbsp;As the iPhone platform is tightly controlled by Apple, technically sophisticated exploits are often required to enable the remote installation and operation of iPhone monitoring tools. These exploits are rare and expensive. Firms that specialize in acquiring zero-days often pay handsomely for iPhone exploits. &nbsp;One such firm, Zerodium, acquired an exploit chain similar to the Trident for </span><a href="https://www.zerodium.com/ios9.html" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">one million dollars</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> in November 2015.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">The high cost of iPhone zero-days, the apparent use of NSO Group&rsquo;s government-exclusive Pegasus product, and&nbsp;</span><a href="https://citizenlab.ca/2012/10/backdoors-are-forever-hacking-team-and-the-targeting-of-dissent/" class="pointer"><span style="font-weight: 400;">prior known targeting of Mansoor</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> by the UAE government provide indicators that point to the UAE government as the likely operator behind the targeting. </span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Remarkably, this case marks the </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">third </span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;">commercial &ldquo;lawful intercept&rdquo; spyware suite employed in attempts to compromise Mansoor. &nbsp;In 2011, he was targeted with FinFisher&rsquo;s FinSpy spyware, and in 2012 he was targeted with Hacking Team&rsquo;s Remote Control System. &nbsp;Both Hacking Team and FinFisher have been the object of several years of revelations highlighting the misuse of spyware to compromise civil society groups, journalists, and human rights workers.</span></p>

<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">The attack on Mansoor is further evidence that &ldquo;lawful intercept&rdquo; spyware has significant abuse potential, and that some governments cannot resist the temptation to use such tools against political opponents, journalists, and human rights defenders. Our findings also highlight the continuing lack of effective human rights policies and due diligence at spyware companies, and the continuing lack of incentives to address abuses of &ldquo;lawful intercept&rdquo; spyware.</span></p>

<h1 class=" lh-solid mb3"><b style="">2. Ahmed Mansoor Targeted With iPhone Zero-Day</b></h1>
<p class="mt0"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Ahmed Mansoor is an </span><a href="https://www.amnesty.org/en/latest/news/2015/10/ahmed-mansoor-selected-as-the-2015-laureate-martin-ennals-award-for-human-rights-defenders/" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">internationally recognized</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> human rights defender, blogger, and&nbsp;member of Human Rights Watch&rsquo;s </span><a href="https://www.hrw.org/news/2011/04/09/uae-government-detains-human-rights-defender" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">advisory committee</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">. &nbsp;Mansoor, who is based in the UAE, was jailed for eight&nbsp;months in 2011 along with four other activists for </span><a href="https://www.hrw.org/news/2011/09/22/uae-free-activists-elections" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">supporting a pro-democracy petition</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">. &nbsp;After he was released, Mansoor&rsquo;s </span><a href="http://www.martinennalsaward.org/index.php?option=com_content&amp;view=article&amp;id=254%3Aahmed-mansoor-martin-ennals-award-2015-final-nominee&amp;catid=44%3Apress&amp;Itemid=152&amp;lang=en" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">passport was confiscated</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">, his </span><a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2016/05/30/technology/governments-turn-to-commercial-spyware-to-intimidate-dissidents.html" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">car was stolen</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">, and $140,000 disappeared from his bank account. &nbsp;Mansoor is banned from traveling overseas, and his work continues to attract significant harassment and punishment</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">On the morning of August 10, 2016, Mansoor received an SMS text message that appeared suspicious. The next day he received a second, similar text. The messages promised &ldquo;new secrets&rdquo; about detainees tortured in UAE prisons, and contained a hyperlink to an unfamiliar website. The messages arrived on Mansoor&rsquo;s stock iPhone 6 running iOS 9.3.3.</span></p>

</a><span style="font-weight: 400;">Mansoor quickly forwarded the messages to Citizen Lab researchers for investigation. &nbsp;He has good reason to be concerned about unsolicited messages: every year since 2011, Mansoor has been targeted with spyware attacks, including with FinFisher spyware in 2011 and </span><a href="https://citizenlab.ca/2012/10/backdoors-are-forever-hacking-team-and-the-targeting-of-dissent/" class="pointer"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Hacking Team</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> spyware in </span><a href="https://citizenlab.ca/2012/10/backdoors-are-forever-hacking-team-and-the-targeting-of-dissent/" class="pointer"><span style="font-weight: 400;">2012</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> (see </span><b>Section 8: Ahmed Mansoor and Previous UAE Attacks</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">).</span></p>

<p style="text-align: left;">&nbsp;<a href="https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/webpc-passthru.php?src=https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2016/08/image13.jpg&amp;nocache=1" class="pointer"><br>
</a><span style="font-weight: 400;">When Mansoor&rsquo;s messages reached us, we recognized the links: the domain name </span><b>webadv.co</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> belongs to a network of domains that we believe to be part of an exploit infrastructure provided by the spyware company NSO Group (see </span><b>Section 6: Linking NSO Group Products to the Attack on Mansoor</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">). &nbsp;We had first come across the NSO Group infrastructure during the course of our earlier research into </span><a href="https://citizenlab.ca/2016/05/stealth-falcon/" class="pointer"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Stealth Falcon</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">, a UAE-based threat actor (see </span><b>Section 5: Tracking a Mobile Attack Infrastructure</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">). &nbsp;</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">When we first found the infrastructure and connected it to NSO Group, we hypothesized that operators of the NSO Group spyware would target a user by sending them an infection link containing one of the exploit infrastructure domain names. &nbsp;Though we had previously found several public occurrences of links involving these domains on Twitter (see </span><b>Section 7: Evidence of Other Targets</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">), none of the links we found seemed to be </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">active</span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;"> (i.e., none produced an infection when we tested them). &nbsp;In other exploit infrastructures with which we are familiar (e.g., </span><a href="https://wikileaks.org/hackingteam/emails/emailid/121783" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Hacking Team&rsquo;s exploit infrastructure</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">), we had noted that operators prefer to deactivate such links after a single click, or after a short period of time, perhaps in order to prevent the disclosure of the exploit to security researchers.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">We accessed the link Mansoor provided us on our own stock factory-reset iPhone 5 (Mansoor had an iPhone 6) with iOS 9.3.3 (the same version as Mansoor). &nbsp;When we clicked the link, we saw that it was indeed active, and watched as unknown software was remotely implanted on our phone. &nbsp;This suggested that the link contained a zero-day iPhone remote jailbreak: a chain of heretofore unknown exploits used to remotely circumvent iPhone security measures. &nbsp;To verify our observations, we shared our findings with Lookout Security. &nbsp;Both research teams determined that Mansoor was targeted with a zero-day iPhone remote jailbreak. The chain of exploits, which we are calling the Trident, included the following (see </span><b>Section 4: The Trident iOS Exploit Chain and Payload</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> for more details):</span></p>
<ul>
<li style="font-weight: 400;" class="mt2"><a href="https://www.cve.org/CVERecord?id=CVE-2016-4657" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><b>CVE-2016-4657</b></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">: An exploit for WebKit, which allows execution of the initial shellcode</span></li>
<li style="font-weight: 400;" class="mt2"><a href="https://www.cve.org/CVERecord?id=CVE-2016-4655" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><b>CVE-2016-4655</b></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">: A Kernel Address Space Layout Randomization (KASLR) bypass exploit to find the base address of the kernel</span></li>
<li style="font-weight: 400;" class="mt2"><a href="https://www.cve.org/CVERecord?id=CVE-2016-4656" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><b>CVE-2016-4656</b></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">: 32 and 64 bit iOS kernel exploits that allow execution of code in the kernel, used to jailbreak the phone and allow software installation</span></li>
</ul>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">The implant installed by the Trident exploit chain would have turned Mansoor&rsquo;s iPhone into a digital spy in his pocket. The spyware, which appears to be NSO&rsquo;s Pegasus spyware solution, was capable of employing his iPhone&rsquo;s camera and microphone to eavesdrop on activity in the vicinity of the device, recording his WhatsApp and Viber calls, logging messages sent in mobile chat apps, and tracking his movements.</span></p>

<h1 class=" lh-solid mb3"><b style="">3. NSO Group and the Pegasus Solution</b></h1>
<p class="mt0"><span style="font-weight: 400;">The attack on Mansoor appears to have used Pegasus, a remote monitoring solution sold by </span><a href="http://www.haaretz.com/israel-news/business/1.574805" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">NSO Group Technologies Ltd</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> (see </span><b>Section 6: Linking NSO Group Products to the Attack on Mansoor</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">). &nbsp;NSO Group, based in Herzelia, Israel (CR# 514395409), develops and sells mobile phone surveillance software to governments around the world. &nbsp;The company describes itself as a &ldquo;leader&rdquo; in &ldquo;</span><a href="https://www.documentcloud.org/documents/815991-1276-nso-group-brochure-pegasus.html" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">mobile and cellular Cyber Warfare</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">,&rdquo; and has been operating for more than six years since its founding in 2010.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">NSO Group appears to be owned by a private equity firm with headquarters in San Francisco: </span><a href="http://www.franciscopartners.com/" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Francisco Partners Management LLC</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">, which reportedly acquired it in 2014 after </span><a href="http://www.haaretz.com/israel-news/business/.premium-1.580721" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">approval from the Israeli Defense Ministry</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">. &nbsp;However, as of November 2015, Francisco Partners was </span><a href="http://www.reuters.com/article/us-nsogroup-m-a-idUSKCN0SR2JF20151103" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">reportedly exploring</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> selling NSO Group, with a stated valuation of up to $1 billion. Interestingly, Francisco Partners previously </span><a href="http://www.franciscopartners.com/investments/blue-coat" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">invested in Blue Coat</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">, a company selling network filtering and monitoring solutions, whose technology has been used by repressive regimes according to </span><a href="https://citizenlab.ca/2013/07/planet-blue-coat-redux/" class="pointer"><span style="font-weight: 400;">previous Citizen Lab research</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">.</span></p>

<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">NSO Group has largely avoided the kind of high profile media attention that companies like Hacking Team and FinFisher have sometimes courted. The company maintains no website, there is little concrete information about NSO Group&rsquo;s Pegasus product available online, and we know of no prior technical analysis of NSO Group&rsquo;s products or infrastructure.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Some previous media reports have linked </span><a href="http://foreignpolicy.com/2016/01/22/the-espionage-econom" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">NSO Group</span></a> <a href="http://impresa.prensa.com/panorama/Virzi-ligado-compra-equipo-Pegasus_0_4267073341.html" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">and Pegasus</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> to a scandal involving potential illegal eavesdropping in Panama, and possible </span><a href="https://privacyinternational.org/sites/default/files/global_surveillance.pdf" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">sales to Mexico</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">. &nbsp;Other reports have suggested that NSO Group&rsquo;s activities have </span><a href="http://blogs.wsj.com/digits/2014/08/01/can-this-israeli-startup-hack-your-phone/" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">aroused concern within the United States intelligence community</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Two of NSO Group&rsquo;s three co-founders, Shalev Hulio and Omri Lavie, are also co-founders of mobile security company Kaymera, which promises a &ldquo;Multi Layered Cyber Defense Approach&rdquo; to clients. &nbsp;On Kaymera&rsquo;s website, the company reprints a Bloomberg article pointing out that they &ldquo;</span><a href="https://www.kaymera.com/httpwww-bloomberg-comnews2014-09-29israeli-entrepreneurs-play-both-sides-of-the-cyber-wars-html/" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">play both sides of the cyber wars</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">.&rdquo; &nbsp;The article also quotes NSO Group&rsquo;s CEO, who suggests that they entered the defense business when potential clients saw the capabilities of NSO Group&rsquo;s tools.</span></p>

<h2 class=" lh-solid mb3"><b style="">3.1. Pegasus Documents in Hacking Team Leak</b></h2>
<p class="mt0"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Much of the publicly available information about Pegasus seems to be rumor, conjecture, or </span><a href="http://blogs.wsj.com/digits/2014/08/01/can-this-israeli-startup-hack-your-phone/" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">unverifiable claims made to media</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> about capabilities. However, when we examined the </span><a href="https://wikileaks.org/hackingteam/emails/" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Hacking Team emails</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> leaked online after a 2015 breach, we found several instances of Hacking Team clients or resellers sharing what appeared to be NSO Group&rsquo;s product documentation and sales pitches.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">For instance, in December 2014, a reseller of surveillance technologies to the Mexican government forwarded a PDF document containing </span><a href="https://wikileaks.org/hackingteam/emails/emailid/5391" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">detailed technical specifications of NSO Group&rsquo;s Pegasus system to Hacking Team</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">. &nbsp;According to the document&rsquo;s metadata, it appears to have been created in December 2013 by Guy Molho, who is </span><a href="https://www.linkedin.com/in/guymolho" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">listed on LinkedIn</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> as the Director of Product Management at NSO Group.</span></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h2 class=" lh-solid mb3"><b style="">3.2. Device Infection</b></h2>
<p class="mt0"><span style="font-weight: 400;">According to the purported 2013 NSO Group Pegasus documentation found in the Hacking Team materials, NSO Group offers two remote installation vectors for spyware onto a target&rsquo;s device: a zero-click vector, and a one-click vector. &nbsp;The one-click vector involves sending the target a normal SMS text message with a link to a malicious website. &nbsp;The malicious website contains an exploit for the web browser on the target&rsquo;s device, and any other required exploits to implant the spyware. In the attack against Mansoor, the Trident exploit chain was used.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">To use NSO Group&rsquo;s zero-click vector, an operator instead sends the same link via a special type of SMS message, like a </span><a href="https://www.silentservices.de/security-advisory-samsung-leaves-its-android-smartphones-with-wap-push-feature-open-to-attacks-one-sms-to-rule-them-all/" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">WAP Push Service Loading (SL) message</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">. &nbsp;A WAP Push SL message causes a phone to automatically open a link in a web browser instance, eliminating the need for a user to click on the link to become infected. Many newer models of phones have started ignoring or restricting WAP Push messages. &nbsp;Mobile network providers may also decide to block these messages.</span></p>
<figure class="center mw-100 ba b--light-gray" style="width:690px;"><div class="tc pa2 bg-white"><a href="https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/webpc-passthru.php?src=https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2016/08/image16.png&amp;nocache=1" class="pointer"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="wp-image-28071" src="https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/webpc-passthru.php?src=https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2016/08/image16.png&amp;nocache=1" alt="image16" width="690" height="274" title="The Million Dollar Dissident: NSO Group&#039;s iPhone Zero-Days used against a UAE Human Rights Defender 6" srcset="https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/webpc-passthru.php?src=https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2016/08/image16.png&amp;nocache=1 899w, https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/webpc-passthru.php?src=https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2016/08/image16-300x119.png&amp;nocache=1 300w, https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/webpc-passthru.php?src=https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2016/08/image16-768x305.png&amp;nocache=1 768w, https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/webpc-passthru.php?src=https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2016/08/image16-180x71.png&amp;nocache=1 180w, https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/webpc-passthru.php?src=https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2016/08/image16-605x240.png&amp;nocache=1 605w, https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/webpc-passthru.php?src=https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2016/08/image16-297x118.png&amp;nocache=1 297w" sizes="(max-width: 690px) 100vw, 690px" /></a></div><figcaption class="f5-ns f6 black-70 pa2 bg-light-gray">Figure 6: Diagram from purported NSO Group Pegasus documentation showing the sequence through which the spyware (&ldquo;Agent&rdquo;) is installed on a target&rsquo;s mobile device. &nbsp;Source: Hacking Team Emails.</figcaption></figure>
<p><a href="https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/webpc-passthru.php?src=https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2016/08/image16.png&amp;nocache=1" class="pointer"><br>
</a><span style="font-weight: 400;">The documentation refers to a malicious website employed in installation of the spyware (&ldquo;Agent&rdquo;) as an </span><b>Anonymizer</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">, which communicates with a </span><b>Pegasus Installation Server</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> located on the operator&rsquo;s premises. &nbsp;When a target visits a malicious link from their device, the Anonymizer forwards the request to the Pegasus Installation Server, which examines the target device&rsquo;s </span><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/User_agent" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">User-Agent</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> header to determine if Pegasus has an exploit chain, such as the Trident, that supports the device. </span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">If the device is supported, the Pegasus Installation Server returns the appropriate exploit to the target device through the Anonymizer and attempts an infection. &nbsp;If infection fails for any reason, the target&rsquo;s web browser will redirect to a legitimate website specified by the Pegasus operator, in order to avoid arousing the target&rsquo;s suspicion.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">In the operation targeting Mansoor, the one-click vector was used, with anonymizer </span><b>sms.webadv.co </b><span style="font-weight: 400;">(see </span><b>Section 4: The Trident iOS Exploit Chain and Payload </b><span style="font-weight: 400;">for more details).</span></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h2 class=" lh-solid mb3"><b style="">3.3. Data Collection</b></h2>
<p class="mt0"><span style="font-weight: 400;">According to the purported NSO Group documentation, once successfully implanted on a phone using an exploit chain like the Trident, Pegasus can actively record or passively gather a variety of different data about the device. &nbsp;By giving full access to the phone&rsquo;s files, messages, microphone and video camera, the operator is able to turn the device into a silent digital spy in the target&rsquo;s pocket.</span></p>
<figure class="center mw-100 ba b--light-gray" style="width:506px;"><div class="tc pa2 bg-white"><a href="https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/webpc-passthru.php?src=https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2016/08/image10.png&amp;nocache=1" class="pointer"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="wp-image-28072 size-full" src="https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/webpc-passthru.php?src=https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2016/08/image10.png&amp;nocache=1" alt="image10" width="506" height="564" title="The Million Dollar Dissident: NSO Group&#039;s iPhone Zero-Days used against a UAE Human Rights Defender 7" srcset="https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/webpc-passthru.php?src=https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2016/08/image10.png&amp;nocache=1 506w, https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/webpc-passthru.php?src=https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2016/08/image10-269x300.png&amp;nocache=1 269w, https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/webpc-passthru.php?src=https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2016/08/image10-180x201.png&amp;nocache=1 180w, https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/webpc-passthru.php?src=https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2016/08/image10-305x340.png&amp;nocache=1 305w, https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/webpc-passthru.php?src=https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2016/08/image10-179x199.png&amp;nocache=1 179w" sizes="(max-width: 506px) 100vw, 506px" /></a></div><figcaption class="f5-ns f6 black-70 pa2 bg-light-gray">Figure 7: Diagram from purported NSO Group Pegasus documentation showing the range of information gathered from a device infected with Pegasus. &nbsp;Source: Hacking Team Emails.</figcaption></figure>
<p style="text-align: left;">In the spyware used in targeting Mansoor, we confirmed many elements of this functionality, and observed indications that the collection of the following types of data was supported, among others (see <b>Section 4.2: The Payload</b> for more details):</p>
<ul>
<li style="font-weight: 400;" class="mt2"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Calls made by phone, WhatsApp and Viber,</span></li>
<li style="font-weight: 400;" class="mt2"><span style="font-weight: 400;">SMS messages, as well as messages and other data from popular apps like Gmail, WhatsApp, Skype, Facebook, KakaoTalk, Telegram, and others,</span></li>
<li style="font-weight: 400;" class="mt2"><span style="font-weight: 400;">A wide range of personal data, such as calendar data and contact lists, as well as passwords, including Wi-Fi passwords.</span></li>
</ul>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h2 class=" lh-solid mb3"><b style="">3.4. Exfiltration</b></h2>
<p class="mt0"><span style="font-weight: 400;">According to the purported NSO Group documentation, an infected device transmits collected information back to a </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">Pegasus Data Server</span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;"> at the operator&rsquo;s premises, via the PATN (Pegasus Anonymizing Transmission Network). &nbsp;The PATN appears to be a proxy chain system similar to </span><a href="https://citizenlab.ca/2014/02/mapping-hacking-teams-untraceable-spyware/" class="pointer"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Hacking Team&rsquo;s anonymizers</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> and </span><a href="https://citizenlab.ca/2015/10/mapping-finfishers-continuing-proliferation/" class="pointer"><span style="font-weight: 400;">FinFisher&rsquo;s relays</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">. The chain is intended to obfuscate the identity of the government client associated with a particular operation. &nbsp;Once the collected information arrives on the Pegasus Data Server, an operator may visualize the information on a Pegasus Working Station.</span></p>
<figure class="center mw-100 ba b--light-gray" style="width:690px;"><div class="tc pa2 bg-white"><a href="https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/webpc-passthru.php?src=https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2016/08/image07.png&amp;nocache=1" class="pointer"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="wp-image-28073" src="https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/webpc-passthru.php?src=https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2016/08/image07-1024x573.png&amp;nocache=1" alt="image07" width="690" height="386" title="The Million Dollar Dissident: NSO Group&#039;s iPhone Zero-Days used against a UAE Human Rights Defender 8" srcset="https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/webpc-passthru.php?src=https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2016/08/image07-1024x573.png&amp;nocache=1 1024w, https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/webpc-passthru.php?src=https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2016/08/image07-300x168.png&amp;nocache=1 300w, https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/webpc-passthru.php?src=https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2016/08/image07-768x430.png&amp;nocache=1 768w, https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/webpc-passthru.php?src=https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2016/08/image07-180x101.png&amp;nocache=1 180w, https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/webpc-passthru.php?src=https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2016/08/image07-297x166.png&amp;nocache=1 297w, https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/webpc-passthru.php?src=https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2016/08/image07.png&amp;nocache=1 1304w" sizes="(max-width: 690px) 100vw, 690px" /></a></div><figcaption class="f5-ns f6 black-70 pa2 bg-light-gray">Figure 8: A purported screenshot of NSO Group&rsquo;s Pegasus Working Station software, which visualizes location data collected from infected devices (as of March 2012). &nbsp;Source: Hacking Team Emails.</figcaption></figure>

</a><span style="font-weight: 400;">The implant in the attack targeting Mansoor communicated with two PATN nodes: </span><b>aalaan.tv</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> and </span><b>manoraonline.net</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">. &nbsp;The first of these, aalaan.tv, appears to be a lookalike domain for the legitimate </span><a href="http://www.alaan.tv/" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">alaan.tv</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">, a Gulf-based satellite television channel (see </span><b>Section 5.2</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> for more details on lookalike domains observed in apparent NSO Group infrastructure).</span></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h2 class=" lh-solid mb3"><b style="">3.5. Prioritizing Stealth</b></h2>
<p class="mt0"><span style="font-weight: 400;">One interesting design decision of NSO Group&rsquo;s Pegasus system, according to the purported NSO Group documentation, is that it emphasizes stealth above almost all else. &nbsp;As the documentation states:</span></p>
<blockquote><p><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">In general, we understand that it is more important that the source will not be exposed and the target will suspect nothing than keeping the agent alive and working.</span></i></p></blockquote>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Certain Pegasus features are only enabled when the device is idle and the screen is off, such as &ldquo;environmental sound recording&rdquo; (hot mic) and &ldquo;photo taking.&rdquo; &nbsp;The documentation also states that the spyware implements a &ldquo;self-destruct mechanism,&rdquo; which may be activated automatically &ldquo;in cases where a great probability of exposing the agent exists.&rdquo; &nbsp;However, the documentation claims that sometimes Pegasus removal can result in an infected device rebooting immediately after removal.</span></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h1 class=" lh-solid mb3"><b style="">4. The Trident iOS Exploit Chain and Payload</b></h1>
<p class="mt0"><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">In this section, we describe our technical analysis of the attack on Mansoor, including the Trident iOS Exploit chain and payload. Given the accelerated timeframe of this case, we are publishing the results of a </span></i><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">preliminary</span></i><i><span style="font-weight: 400;"> analysis.</span></i></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Recall that the investigation that led to the discovery of the Trident exploit chain began when UAE human rights activist Ahmed Mansoor forwarded to Citizen Lab two suspicious links that he received via SMS on his iPhone (</span><b>Section 2</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">). &nbsp;Suspecting the links to be iPhone spyware associated with NSO Group (</span><b>Section 6</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">), we accessed them from our own stock factory-reset iPhone 5 running iOS 9.3.3. &nbsp;Mansoor&rsquo;s device is an iPhone 6, running iOS 9.3.3; we did not have an iPhone 6 available for testing. &nbsp;Although the latest iOS version when Mansoor received the links was 9.3.4, this version had been released only </span><a href="https://support.apple.com/en-us/HT207026" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">one week beforehand</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">We accessed the links by opening Safari on our iPhone, and manually transcribing the links from the screenshots that Mansoor sent. &nbsp;After about ten seconds of navigating to the URL, which displayed a blank page, the Safari window closed, and we observed no further visual activity on the iPhone&rsquo;s screen. &nbsp;Meanwhile, we saw that the phone was served what appeared to be a Safari exploit, followed by intermediate files (</span><b>final111</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">), and a final payload (</span><b>test111.tar</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">). The first two payloads form the Trident exploit chain, and test111.tar is the payload.</span></p>

</a><span style="font-weight: 400;">Suspecting what we had observed to be the work of a zero-day iPhone remote jailbreak, we shared the exploit and payloads with colleagues at Lookout Security, initiated a responsible disclosure process with Apple, and sent Apple the exploit and payloads.</span></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h2 class=" lh-solid mb3"><b style="">4.1. The Trident Exploit Chain</b></h2>
<p class="mt0"><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">This section provides a high-level overview of the Trident exploit chain used in the attack against Mansoor. &nbsp;For further details, see <a href="https://info.lookout.com/rs/051-ESQ-475/images/lookout-pegasus-technical-analysis.pdf" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Lookout&rsquo;s report</a>.</span></i></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">When a user opens the links sent to Mansoor on an iPhone, a stage1 containing obfuscated JavaScript is downloaded. The JavaScript downloads (via XMLHttpRequest) stage2 binaries for either 32-bit (iPhone 5 and earlier) or 64-bit (iPhone 5s and later), depending on the type of device. &nbsp;The stage1 employs a previously undocumented memory corruption vulnerability in WebKit to execute this code within the context of the Safari browser (CVE-2016-4657).</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">The stage2 exploits a function that returns a kernel memory address, from which the base address of the kernel can be mapped (CVE-2016-4655). &nbsp;The stage2 then employs a memory corruption vulnerability in the kernel (CVE-2016-4656). &nbsp;This last vulnerability is employed to disable code signing enforcement, allowing the running of unsigned binaries. &nbsp;The stage2 downloads and installs the stage3, which is the spyware payload.</span></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h2 class=" lh-solid mb3"><b style="">4.2. The Payload</b></h2>
<p class="mt0"><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">This section provides a high-level overview of the functionality of the spyware payload. &nbsp;For more details, see <a href="https://info.lookout.com/rs/051-ESQ-475/images/lookout-pegasus-technical-analysis.pdf" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Lookout&rsquo;s report</a>.</span></i></p>
<h3 class=" lh-solid mb3"><b style="">4.2.1. Persistence</b></h3>
<p class="mt0"><span style="font-weight: 400;">The Trident is re-run locally on the phone at each boot, using the JavaScriptCore binary. &nbsp;To facilitate persistence, the spyware disables Apple&rsquo;s automatic updates, and detects and removes other jailbreaks.</span></p>
<h3 class=" lh-solid mb3"><b style="">4.2.2. Recording</b></h3>
<p class="mt0"><span style="font-weight: 400;">The attack payload includes a renamed copy of </span><a href="http://www.cydiasubstrate.com/" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Cydia Substrate</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">, a third-party app developer framework, which it uses to help facilitate recording of messages and phone calls from targeted apps. &nbsp;To record WhatsApp and Viber calls, the spyware injects WhatsApp and Viber using the Cydia Substrate, hooks various call status methods, and sends system-wide notifications when call events occur; the spyware listens for these notifications and starts or stops recording as appropriate. &nbsp;It appears that the payload can spy on apps including: iMessage, Gmail, Viber, Facebook, WhatsApp, Telegram, Skype, Line, KakaoTalk, WeChat, Surespot, Imo.im, Mail.Ru, Tango, VK, and Odnoklassniki.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">The spyware also exfiltrates calendar and contact data, as well as passwords saved in the phone&rsquo;s keychain, including Wi-Fi passwords and networks.</span></p>

<p class="mt0"><span style="font-weight: 400;">The attack payload beacons back to command and control (C2) servers delivered in stage2 of the Trident, via HTTPS. &nbsp;One of the binaries in the stage2 of the link sent to Mansoor contained the following string:</span></p>

<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">A similar obfuscation appears to be used for exchange of information over SMS between an infected phone and the C2 Server. &nbsp;In case the spyware&rsquo;s C2 servers are disabled or unreachable, an operator may deliver updated C2 servers to an infection using this type of SMS, similar to FinFisher&rsquo;s &ldquo;</span><a href="https://citizenlab.ca/2012/08/the-smartphone-who-loved-me-finfisher-goes-mobile/" class="pointer"><span style="font-weight: 400;">emergency configuration update</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">&rdquo; functionality.</span></p>

<h1 class=" lh-solid mb3"><b style="">5. Tracking a Mobile Attack Infrastructure</b></h1>
<p class="mt0"><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">This section explains how we first identified what appeared to be a mobile attack infrastructure while tracking Stealth Falcon. &nbsp;We then outline some basic observations about the infrastructure, including themes in the domain names used by the attackers. &nbsp;We link the infrastructure we found to NSO Group in </span></i><b><i>Section 6</i></b><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">.</span></i></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h2 class=" lh-solid mb3"><b style="">5.1. </b><b style=""><i>Stealth Falcon</i></b><b style=""> Leads Us to a Mobile Attack Infrastructure</b></h2>
<p class="mt0"><span style="font-weight: 400;">A year or so before Ahmed Mansoor received his suspicious SMS messages, we were tracking </span><a href="https://citizenlab.ca/2016/05/stealth-falcon/" class="pointer"><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">Stealth Falcon</span></i></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">, a threat actor targeting individuals critical of the UAE government at home and abroad, several of whom were later arrested. For full details on Stealth Falcon, read our </span><a href="https://citizenlab.ca/2016/05/stealth-falcon/" class="pointer"><span style="font-weight: 400;">May 2016 report</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">In the course of our investigation, we traced Stealth Falcon&rsquo;s spyware to dozens of different command and control (C2) domains. One server that matched our C2 fingerprint for Stealth Falcon&rsquo;s custom spyware, </span><b>icloudcacher.com</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">, was connected to the email address </span><b><a href="/cdn-cgi/l/email-protection" class="__cf_email__" data-cfemail="85f5ebb4e2b6f5c5f6ece2e4ecebf1abeaf7e2">[email&#160;protected]</a></b><span style="font-weight: 400;">, according to data in its DNS SOA record. &nbsp;The same email address appeared in WHOIS records for the following three domains:</span></p>
<div class="about-author pa3 br1 f5 lh-copy mv1 bg-washed-yellow ba b--mid-gray" style="">
<pre style="margin: 0px; white-space: pre-wrap;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">asrarrarabiya.com</span>
<span style="font-weight: 400;">asrararabiya.co</span>
<span style="font-weight: 400;">asrararablya.com</span>
</pre>
</div>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">These domains did not match our Stealth Falcon fingerprint. As we examined the domains, however, we found that the index page on these domains contained an iframe pointing to the website asrararabiya.com (</span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">Asrar Arabiya</span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;">, or &ldquo;Arabian Secrets&rdquo; in English), which appears to be a </span><a href="http://asrararabiya.com/%D9%85%D9%86-%D9%86%D8%AD%D9%86/" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">benign website</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> that takes a critical view of the Arab World&rsquo;s &ldquo;dictatorships.&rdquo; &nbsp;The index page also contained a nearly invisible iframe pointing to an odd looking site, </span><b>smser.net</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">.</span></p>

<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">We devised a number of fingerprints for various behaviors of </span><b>smser.net</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">, checked </span><a href="https://www.shodan.io/" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Shodan</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> and </span><a href="https://censys.io/" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Censys</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">, and conducted our own scanning with </span><a href="https://zmap.io/" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">zmap</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> to identify related servers. &nbsp;We found 237 live IP addresses, and extracted their domain names from the SSL certificates returned by the each server. &nbsp;The SSL certificates we found included </span><b>*.webadv.co</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">, </span><b>manoraonline.net</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">, and </span><b>aalaan.tv</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">, the three domains in the spyware attack sent to Mansoor.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">We linked these IPs and domain names to what appears to be NSO Group exploit infrastructure.</span></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h2 class=" lh-solid mb3"><b style="">5.2. Coding the Domain Names</b></h2>
<p class="mt0"><span style="font-weight: 400;">We coded the domain names we found, and identified several common themes, perhaps indicating the type of bait content that targets would receive. &nbsp;Interestingly, the most common theme among the domains we identified was &ldquo;News Media,&rdquo; perhaps indicating the use of fake news articles to trick targets into clicking on spyware links. &nbsp;An example of one such attack in action is the targeting of Mexican journalist Rafael Cabrera (</span><b>Section 7.1</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">).</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">We also noted the prevalence of themes we had seen in other spearphishing attacks, e.g., online accounts, document sharing, shipment tracking, corporate account portals. Another common theme was ISPs, perhaps because a target may trust an SMS appearing to come from an ISP or Telco they subscribe to.</span></p>

<h1 class=" lh-solid mb3"><b style="">6. Linking NSO Group Products to the Attack on Mansoor</b></h1>
<p class="mt0"><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">In this section, we explain why we believe the attack on Ahmed Mansoor incorporated the use of NSO Group&rsquo;s Pegasus product. &nbsp;We explain how we connected the domain name in the link that Ahmed Mansoor received, </span></i><b><i>sms.webadv.co</i></b><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">, to a network of domain names that we had mapped out while working on the </span></i><a href="https://citizenlab.ca/2016/05/stealth-falcon/" class="pointer"><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">May 2016 Stealth Falcon report</span></i></a><i><span style="font-weight: 400;"> (</span></i><b><i>Section 5</i></b><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">). We also highlight links to the UAE.</span></i></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h2 class=" lh-solid mb3"><b style="">6.1. Spyware Points to NSO Group&rsquo;s Pegasus Solution</b></h2>
<p class="mt0"><span style="font-weight: 400;">The final payload that we identified, test111.tar, contained several files, including </span><b>libaudio.dylib</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">, which appeared to be the base library for call recording, </span><b>libimo.dylib</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">, which appeared to be the library for recording chat messages from apps, and two libraries for WhatsApp and Viber call recording: </span><b>libvbcalls.dylib</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">, and </span><b>libwacalls.dylib</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">. &nbsp;In each file, we found several hundred strings containing the text &ldquo;_kPegasusProtocol,&rdquo; the name of NSO Group&rsquo;s solution.</span></p>

<h2 class=" lh-solid mb3"><b style="">6.2. Historical Scanning Data Connects Mansoor Attack to NSO Group-linked Infrastructure</b></h2>
<p class="mt0"><span style="font-weight: 400;">The links sent to Mansoor used the domain </span><b>sms.webadv.co</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">. The network of 237 live IP addresses we mapped (</span><b>Section 5</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">) included </span><b>52.8.153.44</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">, to which </span><b>sms.webadv.co</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> resolves, and which returns an SSL certificate for </span><b>*.webadv.co</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">. The 237 IPs also included </span><b>52.8.52.166</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> (</span><b>aalaan.tv</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">) and </span><b>162.209.103.68</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> (</span><b>manoraonline.net</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">), which were the two C2 servers in the spyware used in targeting Mansoor.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">However, the 237 IPs and related domain names that we mapped did not provide insight into the identity of the threat actor. The IP addresses all appeared to be associated with cloud VPS providers, which gave no clue as to the identities of the operators, and the WHOIS information was mostly private. We did note that several domain names had WHOIS registrants based in Israel (e.g., </span><b>thainews.asia</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">, </span><b>kenyasms.org</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">).</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">We examined </span><a href="https://scans.io/study/sonar.http" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">historical scanning data</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> to see whether we could attribute the 237 IPs to a threat actor. &nbsp;We noted that at least 19 of these IPs had previously returned a different distinctive Google redirect in response to a &ldquo;GET /&rdquo;.</span></p>

<h2 class=" lh-solid mb3"><b style="">6.3. Additional UAE Infrastructure</b></h2>
<p class="mt0"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Recall that our first window into this infrastructure came from our Stealth Falcon research, when we identified the </span><b>smser.net</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> domain, fingerprinted it, and traced it to 237 live IP addresses that shared the same characteristics (</span><b>Section 5.1</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">).</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Using </span><a href="https://www.passivetotal.org/" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">PassiveTotal</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">, we were able to further trace </span><b>smser.net</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> to seven other domains,&nbsp;indicating Stealth Falcon targeting that appeared to use&nbsp;NSO Group&rsquo;s Pegasus solution in Qatar (</span><b>ooredoodeals.com</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">), UAE (</span><b>alawaeltech.com</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">, which </span><a href="https://sites.google.com/a/alawaeltech.com/alawael/home" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">may be a fake mobile phone company based in the Emirate of Ajman</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">), and Bahrain (</span><b>bahrainsms.co</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">). &nbsp;Based on our </span><a href="https://citizenlab.ca/2016/05/stealth-falcon/" class="pointer"><span style="font-weight: 400;">previously published research</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">, we believe there is strong circumstantial evidence to support the conclusion that the operator of Stealth Falcon is connected to an entity within the UAE Government.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">We also identified five .ae TLDs that all shared the same registrant name (&ldquo;Gerald Binord&rdquo;), which may have been used to target people in the UAE. &nbsp;We further identified another group of domains including </span><b>damanhealth.online</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> (&ldquo;Daman Health&rdquo; is a </span><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Daman_(health_insurance_company)" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">UAE-based health insurer</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">) and </span><b>uaenews.online</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">, which also included a domain </span><b>turkeynewsupdates.com</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">, suggesting an operator that is targeting both UAE and Turkey targets.</span></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h1 class=" lh-solid mb3"><b style="">7. Evidence of Other Targets</b></h1>
<p class="mt0"><span style="font-weight: 400;">In two cases, Mexico and Kenya, we found evidence of other targets who may have been targeted with&nbsp;NSO Group&rsquo;s Pegasus, based on messages they sent or received containing links that involve domain names we traced to what appears to be a mobile attack infrastructure associated with NSO Group&rsquo;s Pegasus&nbsp;(see </span><b>Section 5: Tracking a Mobile Attack Infrastructure</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">).</span></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>

<p class="mt0"><span style="font-weight: 400;">In the case of Mexico, one target appears to be the journalist Rafael Cabrera, who recently </span><a href="http://www.icfj.org/news/investigative-journalists-uncover-corruption-latin-america" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">reported</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> on the </span><a href="http://aristeguinoticias.com/0911/mexico/la-casa-blanca-de-enrique-pena-nieto/" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">Casa Blanca </span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;">controversy</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">, a reported conflict of interest involving the President and First Lady of Mexico. &nbsp;On August 30, 2015 the journalist Cabrera </span><a href="https://twitter.com/raflescabrera/status/638057388180803584" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">tweeted</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> that he had received suspicious messages purporting to come from Mexican television station </span><a href="http://www.unotv.com/noticias/" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">UNO TV</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">. &nbsp;His tweet included screencaptures of the messages, which said that Mexico&rsquo;s Presidency was considering defamation claims and imprisonment of reporters related to the Casa Blanca report that Cabrera had worked on.</span></p>

<h2 class=" lh-solid mb3"><b style="">7.2. Kenya: A Tweet Discussing the Opposition</b></h2>
<p class="mt0"><span style="font-weight: 400;">In the case of Kenya, we found a past tweet containing a link to the NSO Group exploit infrastructure from June 3, 2015. &nbsp;The </span><a href="https://twitter.com/kachuats/status/606125321021190144" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">tweet</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">, sent by a &ldquo;</span><a href="https://www.linkedin.com/in/kachu-wa-sisungo-a808b832" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Senior Research Officer</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">&rdquo; in the Office of the Senate Minority Leader, references </span><a href="https://twitter.com/wetangulamoses" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Moses Wetangula</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">, who is the current Minority Leader of Kenya&rsquo;s Senate.</span></p>

<h1 class=" lh-solid mb3"><b style="">8. Ahmed Mansoor and Previous UAE Attacks</b></h1>
<p class="mt0"><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">In this section, we provide an overview of previous attacks we have documented against Ahmed Mansoor, and other UAE dissidents. &nbsp;The technical sophistication of previous attacks we observed pales in comparison to the present attack.</span></i></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Ahmed Mansoor has been a frequent target of past electronic attacks. &nbsp;In March 2011, he was targeted with FinFisher spyware disguised as a PDF of a pro-democracy petition he had previously signed. &nbsp;The spyware arrived in the form of an executable file inside a .rar file attached to an email. &nbsp;Mansoor noticed that the file was an EXE file rather than a PDF, and did not open it. &nbsp;Mansoor and four other activists (the &ldquo;UAE Five&rdquo;) </span><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UAE_Five" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">were imprisoned in April 2011</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">, and charged with insulting the leaders of the UAE. &nbsp;Mansoor and the others were pardoned in November of the same year.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">In July of 2012, Ahmed Mansoor&rsquo;s laptop </span><a href="https://citizenlab.ca/2012/10/backdoors-are-forever-hacking-team-and-the-targeting-of-dissent/" class="pointer"><span style="font-weight: 400;">was infected with Hacking Team spyware</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> delivered via a booby-trapped Microsoft Word document exploiting an old Microsoft Office vulnerability, CVE 2010-3333. &nbsp;The spyware sent information from his computer to a UAE intelligence agency, apparently operating under the auspices of the office of Sheikh Tahnoon bin Zayed al-Nahyan, a son of the founder of the UAE, and now the UAE Deputy National Security Advisor. &nbsp;Attackers broke into Mansoor&rsquo;s email account shortly after the infection. &nbsp;We assisted Mansoor in recovering from the attack. &nbsp;Another UAE-based human rights activist, and a UAE-based journalist were also targeted in the same operation.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">In early 2013, Mansoor was sent a link to a website that attempted to install spyware on his computer by exploiting a public Java vulnerability for which no patch had yet been issued. &nbsp;He realized the link was suspicious and did not click on it. &nbsp;Throughout 2013 and 2014, Mansoor was unsuccessfully targeted several times with spyware, </span><a href="https://www.usenix.org/system/files/conference/usenixsecurity14/sec14-paper-marczak.pdf" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">mostly XTremeRAT, SpyNet RAT, and njRAT</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> delivered as executable files in attachments or through Google Drive links. &nbsp;In 2014, Mansoor&rsquo;s </span><a href="https://twitter.com/AmnestyUAE/status/528909930783592449" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Twitter account was hacked</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">In a campaign stretching from 2012 until 2016, UAE dissidents at home and abroad were targeted by </span><a href="https://citizenlab.ca/2016/05/stealth-falcon/" class="pointer"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Stealth Falcon</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">, an attacker likely linked to a UAE government agency. &nbsp;Stealth Falcon sent out links involving a fake URL shortener that employed Javascript to profile targets&rsquo; computers, checked which antivirus programs they had installed, and attempted to deanonymize them if they were using Tor. &nbsp;Stealth Falcon also sent out Microsoft Word documents containing custom spyware that was installed if a user enabled macros. &nbsp;Targets included five dissidents who were later arrested or convicted in absentia, as well as Rori Donaghy, a UK-based journalist who had been publishing articles about leaked emails involving members of the UAE government.</span></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h1 class=" lh-solid mb3"><b style="">9. Conclusion</b></h1>
<p class="mt0"><span style="font-weight: 400;">In this report, we identify a highly technically sophisticated attack involving a zero-day iPhone remote jailbreak &mdash; Trident &mdash; which installs spyware on a phone whose user clicks just once on a malicious link. &nbsp;We connected the attack to NSO Group&rsquo;s Pegasus spyware suite, sold exclusively to government agencies by Israel-based NSO Group. &nbsp;We made the connection based on our previous work tracing a group of servers that appeared to be part of an infrastructure for attacking mobile phones. &nbsp;Long before Ahmed Mansoor had forwarded us any suspicious links he received, we had mapped out a set of 237 servers (</span><b>Section 5</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">), and linked this set to NSO Group (</span><b>Section 6</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">). &nbsp;When Mansoor sent us screencaptures of the SMS messages containing the links, we immediately matched the links&rsquo; domain name to our list of suspected servers associated with NSO Group&rsquo;s Pegasus.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">We visited the links Mansoor sent us from a colleague&rsquo;s factory-reset stock iPhone, and managed to capture the exploits&nbsp;and payload, as the phone was infected. &nbsp;We shared these artifacts with Lookout to gain more insight into the technical capabilities of the exploits and spyware, and with Apple as part of a responsible disclosure process. &nbsp;Apple has been highly responsive, and has worked very quickly to develop and issue a patch in the form of iOS 9.3.5, approximately 10 days after our initial report to them. &nbsp;Once an iPhone is updated to this most recent version, it will be immediately protected against the Trident exploit chain used in this attack. &nbsp;While we assume that NSO Group and others will continue to develop replacements for the Trident, we hope that our experience encourages other researchers to promptly and responsibly disclose such vulnerabilities to Apple and to other vendors.</span></p>
<div class="about-author pa3 br1 f5 lh-copy mv1 bg-washed-yellow ba b--mid-gray" style="">
<p><b>What Can You Do?</b></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">All iPhone owners should <a href="https://support.apple.com/en-us/HT204204" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener">update to the latest version of iOS (9.3.5) immediately</a>. If you&rsquo;re unsure what version you&rsquo;re running, you can </span><a href="https://support.apple.com/en-us/HT201685" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">check it yourself</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> by tapping </span><b>Settings</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> &gt; </span><b>General</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> &gt; </span><b>About</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> &gt; </span><b>Version</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Citizen Lab </span><a href="http://apple.com/support" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">agrees with Apple</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> that users should avoid opening or downloading items from messages and websites unless they are certain that they come from a legitimate, trusted source. If you uncertain about the source, you </span><a href="https://www.johnscottrailton.com/jsrs-digital-security-low-hanging-fruit/" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">should not click the link or open the file</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">. If you believe you have been the victim of a targeted attack, should consider sharing it with a trusted expert. If you suspect you have been the target of this attack, please contact the Citizen Lab at </span><b><a href="/cdn-cgi/l/email-protection" class="__cf_email__" data-cfemail="c7aea9a1a887a4aeb3aebda2a9aba6a5e9a8b5a0">[email&#160;protected]</a></b><span style="font-weight: 400;">.</span></p>
</div>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Zero-day exploits are expensive and rare, especially one-click remote jailbreak exploits for iPhones, like the Trident. &nbsp;Such exploits can fetch </span><a href="http://www.pcworld.com/article/3106168/security/a-new-500000-ios-bug-bounty-beats-apples-offer.html" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">hundreds of thousands</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> or even a </span><a href="https://www.zerodium.com/ios9.html" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">million dollars</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">. &nbsp;While Citizen Lab research has shown that many state-sponsored spyware campaigns against civil society groups and human rights defenders use &ldquo;</span><a href="https://targetedthreats.net/" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">just</span></a> <a href="https://citizenlab.ca/2016/08/group5-syria/" class="pointer"><span style="font-weight: 400;">enough</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">&rdquo; technical sophistication, coupled with carefully planned deception, the attack on Mansoor demonstrates that not all threats follow this pattern.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">This is the third time Mansoor has been targeted with &ldquo;lawful intercept&rdquo; spyware; Mansoor was targeted in 2011 with spyware from FinFisher (based in Germany and the UK), in 2012 with spyware from Hacking Team (based in Italy), and now in 2016 with what appears to be spyware from NSO Group (based in Israel and reportedly owned by a&nbsp;US firm). &nbsp;That the companies whose spyware was used to target Mansoor are all owned and operated from democracies speaks volumes about the lack of accountability and effective regulation in the cross-border commercial spyware trade.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">While these spyware tools are developed in democracies, they continue to be sold to countries with notorious records of abusive targeting of human rights defenders. Such sales occur despite the existence of applicable export controls. &nbsp;For example, Israel&rsquo;s export regime </span><a href="http://www.hfn.co.il/client-update-decided-%E2%80%93-facilitate-cyber-export-controls-much-possible" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span style="font-weight: 400;">incorporates the dual-use technology controls of the Wassenaar Arrangement</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">, including those related to &ldquo;intrusion software.&rdquo; As such, NSO Group would presumably be required to obtain a license to export its products to the UAE. &nbsp;If NSO Group did submit a license application, the human rights abuses perpetrated by the UAE, including the misuse of &ldquo;lawful intercept&rdquo; capabilities, must not have outweighed authorities&rsquo; other motivations to approve the export. </span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Clearly, additional legal and regulatory scrutiny of the the &ldquo;lawful intercept&rdquo; market, and of NSO Group&rsquo;s activities in relation to the attacks we have described, is essential. &nbsp;Citizen Lab and others have repeatedly demonstrated that advanced &ldquo;lawful intercept&rdquo; spyware enables some governments and agencies, especially those operating without strong oversight, to target and harass journalists, activists and human rights workers. If spyware companies are unwilling to recognize the role that their products play in undermining human rights, or address these urgent concerns, they will continue to strengthen the case for further intervention by governments and other stakeholders. </span></p>
<p><strong>Note</strong>:&nbsp;<span class="s1">We are not releasing the malicious files at this time to protect the integrity of ongoing investigations.</span></p>
