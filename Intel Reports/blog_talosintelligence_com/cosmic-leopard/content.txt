    <h1 class="text-center">Operation Celestial Force employs mobile and desktop malware to target Indian entities</h1>

    <div class="text-center m-1">
        <div class="post-author">
            <span>By </span>
                <a href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/author/cisco/">Cisco Talos</a>
        </div>

        <br/>
        <time class="post-datetime" datetime="June 13, 2024 06:00">
            Thursday, June 13, 2024 06:00
        </time>

        <div class="m-3">
                            <a href="/category/apt/" class="category primary-category">
                                APT
                            </a>
                                                    <a href="/category/malware/" class="category primary-category">
                                malware
                            </a>
                                </div>
    </div>

    <section class="post-content-wrapper mt-5">
        <div class="post-content">
            <p><em>By </em><a href="https://twitter.com/Gi7w0rm" rel="noreferrer noopener"><em><u>Gi7w0rm</u></em></a><em>,</em> <a href="https://twitter.com/asheermalhotra" rel="noreferrer noopener"><em><u>Asheer Malhotra</u></em></a><em> and </em><a href="https://twitter.com/_vventura" rel="noreferrer noopener"><em><u>Vitor Ventura</u></em></a><em>.</em>&nbsp;</p><ul><li>Cisco Talos is disclosing a new malware campaign called “Operation Celestial Force” running since at least 2018. It is still active today, employing the use of GravityRAT, an Android-based malware, along with a Windows-based malware loader we track as “HeavyLift.”&nbsp;&nbsp;</li><li>All GravityRAT and HeavyLift infections are administered by a standalone tool we are calling “GravityAdmin,” which carries out malicious activities on an infected device. Analysis of the panel binaries reveals that they are meant to administer and run multiple campaigns at the same time, all of which are codenamed and have their own admin panels.&nbsp;&nbsp;</li><li>Talos attributes this operation with high confidence to a Pakistani nexus of threat actors we’re calling “Cosmic Leopard,” focused on espionage and surveillance of their targets.&nbsp; This multiyear operation continuously targeted Indian entities and individuals likely belonging to defense, government and related technology spaces. Talos initially <a href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/gravityrat-two-year-evolution-of-apt/" rel="noreferrer noopener"><u>disclosed</u></a> the use of the Windows-based GravityRAT malware by suspected Pakistani threat actors in 2018 — also used to target Indian entities.&nbsp;&nbsp;</li><li>While this operation has been active for at least the past six years, Talos has observed a general uptick in the threat landscape in recent years, with respect to the use of mobile malware for espionage to target high-value targets, including the use of <a href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/what-is-commercial-spyware/" rel="noreferrer noopener"><u>commercial spyware</u></a>.&nbsp;</li></ul><h1 id="operation-celestial-force-a-multi-campaign-multi-component-infections-operation">Operation Celestial Force: A multi-campaign, multi-component infections operation&nbsp;</h1><p>Talos assesses with high confidence that this series of campaigns we’re clustering under the umbrella of “Operation Celestial Force” is conducted by a nexus of Pakistani threat actors. The tactics, techniques, tooling and victimology of Cosmic Leopard contain some overlaps with those of <a href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/transparent-tribe-targets-education/" rel="noreferrer noopener"><u>Transparent Tribe</u></a>, another suspected Pakistani APT group, which has a history of targeting high-value individuals from the Indian subcontinent. However, we do not have enough technical evidence to link both the threat actors together for now, therefore we track this cluster of activity under the “Cosmic Leopard” tag.&nbsp;</p><p>Operation Celestial Force has been active since at least 2018 and continues to operate today — increasingly utilizing an expanding and evolving malware suite — indicating that the operation has likely seen a high degree of success targeting users in the Indian subcontinent. Cosmic Leopard initially began the operation with the creation and deployment of the <a href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/gravityrat-two-year-evolution-of-apt/" rel="noreferrer noopener"><u>Windows<s> </s>based GravityRAT</u></a> malware family distributed via malicious documents (maldocs). Cosmic Leopard then created <a href="https://securelist.com/gravityrat-the-spy-returns/99097/" rel="noreferrer noopener"><u>Android-based versions of GravityRAT</u></a> to widen their net of infections to begin targeting mobile devices around 2019. During the same year, Cosmic Leopard also expanded their arsenal to use the HeavyLift malware family as a malware loader. HeavyLift is primarily wrapped in malicious installers sent to targets tricked into running the into running the malware via social engineering techniques.&nbsp;</p><p>Some campaigns from this multi-year operation have been <a href="https://securelist.com/gravityrat-the-spy-returns/99097/" rel="noreferrer noopener"><u>disclosed</u></a> and loosely attributed to <a href="https://about.fb.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/06/Meta-Quarterly-Adversarial-Threat-Report-Q1-2023.pdf" rel="noreferrer noopener"><u>Pakistani threat actors</u></a> in previous <a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/2023/06/15/android-gravityrat-goes-after-whatsapp-backups/" rel="noreferrer noopener"><u>reporting.</u></a> However, there has been little evidence to tie all of them together until now. Each campaign in the operation has been codenamed by the threat actor and managed/administered using custom-built panel binaries we call “GravityAdmin.”&nbsp;</p><p>Adversaries like Cosmic Leopard may use low-sophistication techniques such as social engineering and spear phishing, but will aggressively target potential victims with various TTPs. Therefore, organizations must remain vigilant against such motivated adversaries conducting targeted attacks by educating users on proper cyber hygiene and implementing defense-in-depth models to protect against such attacks across various attack surfaces.</p><h2 id="initiating-contact-and-infecting-targets">Initiating contact and infecting targets&nbsp;</h2><p>This campaign primarily utilizes two infection vectors — spear phishing and social engineering. Spear phishing consists of messages sent to targets with pertinent language and maldocs that contain malware such as GravityRAT.&nbsp;</p><p>The other infection vector, gaining popularity in this operation, and now a staple tactic of the Cosmic Leopard’s operations consists of contacting targets over <a href="https://about.fb.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/06/Meta-Quarterly-Adversarial-Threat-Report-Q1-2023.pdf" rel="noreferrer noopener"><u>social media channels</u></a>, establishing trust with them and eventually sending them a malicious link to download either the Windows- or Android-based GravityRAT or the Windows-based loader, HeavyLift.&nbsp;</p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card kg-card-hascaption"><img src="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/2024/06/data-src-image-03c01dfd-af16-4070-bb35-1cbe72253ffd.png" class="kg-image" alt="" loading="lazy" width="936" pre-wrap;">&nbsp;Malicious drop site delivering HeavyLift.&nbsp;</span></figcaption></figure><h2 id="operation-celestial-force%E2%80%99s-malware-and-its-management-interfaces">Operation Celestial Force’s malware and its management interfaces&nbsp;</h2><p>Talos’ analysis reveals the use of multiple components, including Android- and Windows-based malware, and administrative binaries supporting multiple campaign panels used by Operation Celestial Force.&nbsp;</p><ul><li><strong>GravityRAT</strong>: GravityRAT, a closed-source malware family, first disclosed by Talos in 2018, is a Windows- and Android-based RAT used to target Indian entities.&nbsp;&nbsp;</li><li><strong>HeavyLift</strong>: A previously unknown Electron-based malware loader family distributed via malicious installers targeting the Windows operating system.&nbsp;&nbsp;</li><li><strong>GravityAdmin</strong>: A tool to administer infected systems (panel binary), used by operators since at least 2021, by connecting to GravityRAT’s and HeavyLift’s C2 servers. GravityAdmin consists of multiple inbuilt User Interfaces (UIs) that correspond to specific, codenamed, campaigns being operated by malicious operators.&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;</li></ul><p>Operation Celestial Force’s infection chains are:&nbsp;&nbsp;</p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card"><img src="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/2024/06/data-src-image-ee13eb90-a7a2-44b1-a495-91ddbe033d05.jpeg" class="kg-image" alt="" loading="lazy" width="1432" height="1076" srcset="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/size/w600/2024/06/data-src-image-ee13eb90-a7a2-44b1-a495-91ddbe033d05.jpeg 600w, https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/size/w1000/2024/06/data-src-image-ee13eb90-a7a2-44b1-a495-91ddbe033d05.jpeg 1000w, https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/2024/06/data-src-image-ee13eb90-a7a2-44b1-a495-91ddbe033d05.jpeg 1432w" sizes="(min-width: 720px) 720px"></figure><h1 id="gravityadmin-panel-binaries-administering-the-campaigns">GravityAdmin: Panel binaries administering the campaigns&nbsp;</h1><p>The Panel binaries we analyzed consist of multiple versions with the earliest compiled in August 2021. The panel binary asks for a user ID, password and campaign ID (from a drop-down menu) from the operator when it runs.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card kg-card-hascaption"><img src="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/2024/06/data-src-image-422e9a66-58fc-49ee-aee2-d3ae5e18d27c.png" class="kg-image" alt="" loading="lazy" width="510" height="936"><figcaption><span style="white-space: pre-wrap;">&nbsp;Login screen for GravityAdmin titled “Bits Before Bullets.” </span></figcaption></figure><p>When the operator clicks the login button, the executable will check if it is connected to the internet by sending a ping request to www[.]google[.]com. Then, the user ID and password are authenticated with an authentication server which sends back:&nbsp;</p><ul><li>A code to direct the panel binary to open the panel UI for the specified panel.&nbsp;</li><li>Also sends a value back via the HTTP “Authorization” Header. This value acts as an authentication token when communicating with campaign-specific[ C2 servers to load data such as a list of infected machines, etc.&nbsp;</li></ul><p>A typical Panel screen will list the machines infected as part of the specific campaign. It also has buttons to trigger various malicious actions against one or more infected systems.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card"><img src="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/2024/06/data-src-image-1a11425b-1908-4119-ba7f-b2b2d205b288.png" class="kg-image" alt="" loading="lazy" width="936" height="488" srcset="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/size/w600/2024/06/data-src-image-1a11425b-1908-4119-ba7f-b2b2d205b288.png 600w, https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/2024/06/data-src-image-1a11425b-1908-4119-ba7f-b2b2d205b288.png 936w" sizes="(min-width: 720px) 720px"></figure><p>Different panels have different capabilities, however, some core capabilities are common across all campaigns.&nbsp;</p><p>The various campaigns configured in the Panel binaries are code named as:&nbsp;</p><ul><li>"SIERRA"&nbsp;</li><li>"QUEBEC"&nbsp;</li><li>"ZULU"&nbsp;</li><li>"DROPPER"&nbsp;</li><li>"WORDDROPPER"&nbsp;</li><li>"COMICUM"&nbsp;</li><li>"ROCKAMORE"&nbsp;</li><li>"FOXTROT"&nbsp;</li><li>"CLOUDINFINITY"&nbsp;</li><li>"RECOVERBIN"&nbsp;</li><li>"CVSCOUT"&nbsp;</li><li>"WEBBUCKET"&nbsp;</li><li>"CRAFTWITHME"&nbsp;</li><li>"SEXYBER"&nbsp;</li><li>"CHATICO"&nbsp;</li></ul><p>Each of the codenamed campaigns from the Panel binaries consists of its own infection mechanisms. For example, “FOXTROT,” “CLOUDINFINITY” and “<a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/2023/06/15/android-gravityrat-goes-after-whatsapp-backups/" rel="noreferrer noopener"><u>CHATICO</u></a>” are names given to all Android-based GravityRAT infections whereas “CRAFTWITHME,” “SEXYBER” and “CVSCOUT” are named for attacks deploying HeavyLift. Our analysis correlates the campaigns listed above with the Operating Systems being targeted with respective malware families.&nbsp;</p>

<h3 id="gravityrat-a-multi-platform-remote-access-trojan">GravityRAT: A multi-platform remote access trojan</h3><p><a href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/gravityrat-two-year-evolution-of-apt/" rel="noreferrer noopener"><u>GravityRAT</u></a> is a Windows-based remote access trojan first disclosed by Talos in 2018. GravityRAT was later <a href="https://securelist.com/gravityrat-the-spy-returns/99097/" rel="noreferrer noopener"><u>ported to the Android</u></a> operating system to target mobile devices around 2019. Since 2019, we’ve observed a continuous addition of a multitude of capabilities in GravityRAT and its associated infrastructure. So far, we have observed the use of GravityRAT exclusively by suspected Pakistani threat actors to target entities and individuals in India. There is currently no publicly available evidence to suggest that GravityRAT is a commodity/open-source malware, suggesting its potential use by multiple, disparate threat actors.&nbsp;</p><p>Our analysis of the entire ecosystem of Operation Celestial Force revealed that GravityRAT’s use in this campaign likely began as early as 2016 and continues to this day.&nbsp;</p><p>The latest variants of GravityRAT are distributed through malicious websites, some registered and set up as late as early January 2024, pretending to distribute legitimate Android applications. href="https://cyble.com/blog/gravity-rat-malware-returns-as-a-chat-application/" rel="noreferrer noopener"><u>Cyble</u></a> and consist of the following capabilities:&nbsp;</p><ol><li>Send preliminary information about the device to the C2. This information includes IMEI, phone number, network country ISO code, network operator name, SIM country ISO code, SIM operator name, SIM serial number, device model, brand, product and manufacturer, addresses surrounding the obtained longitude and latitude of the device and the current build information, including release, host, etc.&nbsp;</li><li>Read SMS data and content and upload to the C2.&nbsp;</li><li>Read specific file formats and upload them to the C2.&nbsp;</li><li>Read call logs and upload them to the C2.&nbsp;</li><li>Obtain IMEI information including associated email ID and send it to C2.&nbsp;</li><li>Delete all contacts, call logs and files related to the malware.&nbsp;</li></ol><h1 id="heavylift-electron-based-malware-loader">HeavyLift: Electron-based malware loader</h1><p>Some of the campaigns in this operation use Electron-based malware loaders we’re calling “HeavyLift,” which consist of JavaScript code communicating and controlled by C2 servers. These are the same C2 servers that interact with GravityAdmin, the panel tool used by the operators to govern infected systems. HeavyLift is essentially a stage one malware component that downloads and installs other malicious implants whenever available on the C2 server. HeavyLift bears some similarities with <a href="https://securelist.com/gravityrat-the-spy-returns/99097/" rel="noreferrer noopener"><u>GravityRAT’s Electron versions</u></a> disclosed previously by Kaspersky in 2020.&nbsp;</p><p>A HeavyLift infection begins with an executable masquerading as an installer for a legitimate application. The installer installs a dummy application but also installs and sets up a malicious <a href="https://www.electronjs.org/" rel="noreferrer noopener"><u>Electron</u></a>-based desktop application. This malicious application is, in fact, HeavyLift and consists of JavaScript code that carries out malicious operations on the infected system.&nbsp;</p><p>On execution, HeavyLift will check if it is running on a macOS or Windows system. If it is running on macOS, and not running as root, it will execute with admin privileges using the command:&nbsp;</p>
<!--kg-card-begin: html-->
<pre>
 /usr/bin/osascript -e 'do shell script "bash -c " _process_path " with administrator privileges'  
</pre>
<!--kg-card-end: html-->
<p>If it is running as root, it will set the default HTTP User-Agent to “M_9C9353252222ABD88B123CE5A78B70F6”, then get system info using the commands:&nbsp;</p>
<!--kg-card-begin: html-->
<pre>
system_profiler SPHardwareDataType | grep 'Model Name' 

system_profiler SPHardwareDataType | grep 'SMC' 

system_profiler SPHardwareDataType | grep 'Model Identifier' 

system_profiler SPHardwareDataType | grep 'ROM' 

system_profiler SPHardwareDataType | grep 'Serial Number'  
</pre>
<!--kg-card-end: html-->
<p>For a Windows-based system, the HTTP User-Agent is set to “W_9C9353252222ABD88B123CE5A78B70F6”. The malware will then obtain preliminary system information such as:&nbsp;</p><ul><li>Processor ID&nbsp;</li><li>MAC address&nbsp;</li><li>Installed anti-virus product name&nbsp;</li><li>Username&nbsp;</li><li>Domain name&nbsp;</li><li>Platform information&nbsp;</li><li>Process, OS architecture&nbsp;</li><li>Agent (hardcoded value)&nbsp;</li><li>OS release number&nbsp;</li></ul><p>All this preliminary information is sent to the hardcoded C2 server URL to register the infection with the C2.&nbsp;</p><p>HeavyLift will then reach out to the C2 server to poll for any new payloads to execute on the infected system. A payload received from the C2 will be dropped to a directory in the “AppData” directory and persisted on the system.&nbsp;</p><p>On macOS, the payload is a ZIP file that is extracted, and the resulting binary persists using crontab via the command:&nbsp;</p>
<!--kg-card-begin: html-->
<pre>
crontab -l 2>/dev/null; echo ' */2 * * * * “_filepath_” _arguments_ ‘ | crontab - 
</pre>
<!--kg-card-end: html-->
<p>For Windows, the payload received is an EXE file that persists on the system via a scheduled task. The malware will create an XML file for the scheduled tasks with the payload path, arguments and working directory and then use the XML to set up the schedtask:&nbsp;</p>
<!--kg-card-begin: html-->
<pre>
SCHTASKS /Create /XML "_xmlpath_" /TN "_taskname_" /F 
</pre>
<!--kg-card-end: html-->
<p>The malware will then open the accompanying HTML file via web view to appear legitimate.&nbsp;</p><p>&nbsp;In some cases, the malware will also perform anti-analysis checks to see if it’s running in a virtual environment. &nbsp;</p><p>It checks for the presence of specific keywords before closing if there is a match:&nbsp;</p><ul><li>Innotek GmbH&nbsp;</li><li>VirtualBox&nbsp;</li><li>VMware&nbsp;</li><li>Microsoft Corporation&nbsp;</li><li>HITACHI</li></ul><p>These keywords are checked against model information, SMC, ROM and serial numbers on macOS and Windows against manufacturer information, such as product, vendor, processor and more.&nbsp;</p><h1 id="coverage">Coverage&nbsp;</h1><p>Ways our customers can detect and block this threat are listed below.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card"><img src="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/2024/06/data-src-image-9abbe7f5-a746-4330-920e-99f9813d9937.jpeg" class="kg-image" alt="" loading="lazy" width="1600" height="323" srcset="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/size/w600/2024/06/data-src-image-9abbe7f5-a746-4330-920e-99f9813d9937.jpeg 600w, https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/size/w1000/2024/06/data-src-image-9abbe7f5-a746-4330-920e-99f9813d9937.jpeg 1000w, https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/2024/06/data-src-image-9abbe7f5-a746-4330-920e-99f9813d9937.jpeg 1600w" sizes="(min-width: 720px) 720px"></figure><p>&nbsp;<a href="https://www.cisco.com/c/en/us/products/security/amp-for-endpoints/index.html?ref=cisco-talos-blog" rel="noreferrer noopener">Cisco Secure Endpoint</a> (formerly AMP for Endpoints) is ideally suited to prevent the execution of the malware detailed in this post.
