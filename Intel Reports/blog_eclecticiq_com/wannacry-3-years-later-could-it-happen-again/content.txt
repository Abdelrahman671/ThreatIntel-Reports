        <article class="flex-grow lg:mr-24 c-copy">
            <span id="hs_cos_wrapper_post_body" class="hs_cos_wrapper hs_cos_wrapper_meta_field hs_cos_wrapper_type_rich_text" style="" data-hs-cos-general-type="meta_field" data-hs-cos-type="rich_text"><p style="text-align: justify;"><img src="https://blog.eclecticiq.com/hs-fs/hubfs/EIQ_wannecry_blogimage.png?width=1500&amp;name=EIQ_wannecry_blogimage.png" alt="WannaCry 3 Years Later, Could it Happen Again?" width="1500" style="width: 1500px;" srcset="https://blog.eclecticiq.com/hs-fs/hubfs/EIQ_wannecry_blogimage.png?width=750&amp;name=EIQ_wannecry_blogimage.png 750w, https://blog.eclecticiq.com/hs-fs/hubfs/EIQ_wannecry_blogimage.png?width=1500&amp;name=EIQ_wannecry_blogimage.png 1500w, https://blog.eclecticiq.com/hs-fs/hubfs/EIQ_wannecry_blogimage.png?width=2250&amp;name=EIQ_wannecry_blogimage.png 2250w, https://blog.eclecticiq.com/hs-fs/hubfs/EIQ_wannecry_blogimage.png?width=3000&amp;name=EIQ_wannecry_blogimage.png 3000w, https://blog.eclecticiq.com/hs-fs/hubfs/EIQ_wannecry_blogimage.png?width=3750&amp;name=EIQ_wannecry_blogimage.png 3750w, https://blog.eclecticiq.com/hs-fs/hubfs/EIQ_wannecry_blogimage.png?width=4500&amp;name=EIQ_wannecry_blogimage.png 4500w" sizes="(max-width: 1500px) 100vw, 1500px"></p>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><em>By Ippolito Forni, Threat Intelligence Analyst</em></p>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><span data-contrast="auto">On</span><span data-contrast="auto"> May 12,&nbsp; 2017,  computers at multiple UK’s National Health Services (NHS) facilities were displaying an ominous message informing the user that the data on the device had been encrypted and asking for a payment to receive the key to decrypt the  data. </span></p>
<!--more--><span data-contrast="auto" style="text-align: justify;">More than 70,000 devices, including MRI scanners, blood-storage refrigerators</span><span data-contrast="none" style="text-align: justify;">, </span><span data-contrast="auto" style="text-align: justify;">and other medical equipment across  multiple  facilities  in  the United Kingdom were displaying the same message. Most  medical  services were shut down  except for the  emergency services.</span><span data-contrast="auto" style="text-align: justify;"> </span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;134233117&quot;:true,&quot;134233118&quot;:true,&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559740&quot;:240}" style="text-align: justify;">&nbsp;</span>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><span data-contrast="auto">Had  the  target been only the NHS, it would have already been a </span><span data-contrast="none">cyber-attack </span><span data-contrast="auto">of  </span><span data-contrast="none">remarkable  </span><span data-contrast="auto">size, but as the day continued,  there were multiple  reports of the same type of messages appearing in networks in more than 150 countries  world-wide and  it became clear this was a cyber-attack of a scale never seen before.</span><span data-contrast="auto"> </span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;134233117&quot;:true,&quot;134233118&quot;:true,&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559740&quot;:240}">&nbsp;</span></p>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><span data-contrast="auto">The malware deployed was a ransomware named WannaCry. The  self-propagating capability  of this malware was due to its worm features: WannaCry did not required  any command from its operators or interaction from  victims  to keep spreading. It would infect vulnerable machines using the EternalBlue </span><span data-contrast="auto"> exploit.  </span><a href="https://www.wired.com/story/eternalblue-leaked-nsa-spy-tool-hacked-world/" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank"><span data-contrast="auto">EternalBlue</span></a><span data-contrast="auto">  was  </span><span data-contrast="none">allegedly</span><span data-contrast="none">&nbsp;</span><span data-contrast="auto">developed as a cyber  </span><span data-contrast="none">tool </span><span data-contrast="auto"> by the  </span><span data-contrast="none">U.S.  </span><span data-contrast="auto">National Security Agency and was later stolen and  leaked by the mysterious</span><span data-contrast="none">  self-described  “</span><span data-contrast="auto">Shadow</span><span data-contrast="none"> </span><span data-contrast="auto">Broker</span><span data-contrast="none">s” </span><span data-contrast="auto"> group in an act of defiance</span><span data-contrast="none">, or information  warfare</span><span data-contrast="none">, </span><span data-contrast="auto">against the </span><span data-contrast="none">U.S. intelligence community. The  EternalBlue</span><span data-contrast="none">, a  </span><i><span data-contrast="none">de facto </span></i><span data-contrast="auto">cyber weapon</span><span data-contrast="none">,</span><span data-contrast="auto">  exploited  the now notorious  CVE-2017-0144 vulnerability  </span><span data-contrast="none">in </span><span data-contrast="auto"> SMBv1 server</span><span data-contrast="none">  present </span><span data-contrast="auto">in multiple versions of Windows and </span><span data-contrast="none">millions  of  enterprise machines</span><span data-contrast="auto">.</span><span data-contrast="auto"> </span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;134233117&quot;:true,&quot;134233118&quot;:true,&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559740&quot;:240}">&nbsp;</span></p>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><span data-contrast="auto">While Microsoft had released patches  for the  CVE-2017</span><span data-contrast="auto">-</span><span data-contrast="auto">0144 </span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;134233117&quot;:true,&quot;134233118&quot;:true,&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559740&quot;:240}"> vulnerability </span><span data-contrast="none">two</span><span data-contrast="auto">  months  </span><span data-contrast="none">earlier</span><span data-contrast="auto">,  countless numbers of  </span><span data-contrast="none">Windows  </span><span data-contrast="auto">machines had still not applied  the patches when the WannaCry  </span><span data-contrast="none">events  emerged</span><span data-contrast="auto">. Since the exploit did not require human interaction to work, the ransomware developers managed to create a ransomware-worm capable of infecting machines and</span><span data-contrast="auto">&nbsp;</span><span data-contrast="auto">scan</span><span data-contrast="none">ning</span><span data-contrast="auto">  local  </span><span data-contrast="none">and remote  </span><span data-contrast="auto">network</span><span data-contrast="none">s  </span><span data-contrast="auto">for further vulnerable targets. This allowed the ransomware to spread at an extremely fast pace.</span><span data-contrast="none">  </span><span data-contrast="none">Networks were at risk  even when TCP port 139 was closed to external internet traffic, </span><span data-contrast="none"> </span><span data-contrast="auto">the port used by SMB</span><span data-contrast="none">, because any infection vector on internal networks could cause the worm to spread.</span><span data-contrast="none"> </span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;134233117&quot;:true,&quot;134233118&quot;:true,&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559740&quot;:240}">&nbsp;</span></p>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><span data-contrast="auto">The attack was</span><span data-contrast="none">&nbsp;</span><span data-contrast="none">almost certainly  </span><span data-contrast="auto">not targeted</span><span data-contrast="none">.  </span><span data-contrast="none">I</span><span data-contrast="auto">t appears</span><span data-contrast="auto">&nbsp;</span><span data-contrast="auto">the ransomware creators  </span><span data-contrast="none">intended  </span><span data-contrast="auto">to infect as many devices as possible, no matter what data was  </span><span data-contrast="none">stored  </span><span data-contrast="auto">on those devices and no matter who owned them. The ransom requested was the equivalent in Bitcoin of $300 which would become $600 if the victim did not pay within 7 days.</span><span data-contrast="auto"> </span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;134233117&quot;:true,&quot;134233118&quot;:true,&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559740&quot;:240}">&nbsp;</span></p>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><strong><span data-contrast="none">Hidden Kill Switch Prevents Further Escalation</span></strong><span data-contrast="auto"> </span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;134233117&quot;:true,&quot;134233118&quot;:true,&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559740&quot;:240}">&nbsp;</span></p>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><span data-contrast="auto">Later that day,</span><span data-contrast="auto">&nbsp;</span><span data-contrast="auto">security researcher  Marcus Hutchins  discovered that</span><span data-contrast="auto">&nbsp;</span><span data-contrast="auto">WannaCry always attempted to connect to a specific domain before encrypting the data</span><span data-contrast="none">.</span><span data-contrast="none">&nbsp;</span><span data-contrast="none">I</span><span data-contrast="auto">f  the domain </span><span data-contrast="auto">was</span><span data-contrast="auto"> not found, the malware would proceed with data </span><span data-contrast="auto">encryption on the victim’s machine. Marcus therefore proceeded registering the domain and redirected the connection attempts to a sinkhole  he controlled. </span></p>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><span data-contrast="none">All </span><span data-contrast="auto"> new</span><span data-contrast="none">&nbsp;</span><span data-contrast="auto">WannaCry  </span><span data-contrast="none">infections  </span><span data-contrast="auto">would connect  to the registered domain effectively </span><span data-contrast="none">stopping</span><span data-contrast="none">  </span><span data-contrast="auto">the ransomware from encrypting the data. Marcus had found and enabled the kill-switch.  </span><span data-contrast="auto">Ma</span><span data-contrast="auto">lware  developers sometimes hid</span><span data-contrast="none">e  kill-switches  in the</span><span data-contrast="auto">ir code  giv</span><span data-contrast="none">ing</span><span data-contrast="auto"> t hem access  to  an emergency “shut off”  </span><span data-contrast="none">function</span><span data-contrast="none">&nbsp;</span><span data-contrast="auto">should they require</span><span data-contrast="auto">&nbsp;to&nbsp;</span><span data-contrast="auto">suddenly&nbsp;</span><span data-contrast="auto">disable</span><span data-contrast="auto">&nbsp;the malware.</span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;134233117&quot;:true,&quot;134233118&quot;:true,&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559740&quot;:240}">&nbsp;</span></p>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><span data-contrast="auto"> </span><span data-contrast="auto">The activation of the kill-switch effectively defanged new WannaCry infections as the most damaging part, the data encryption, was not occurring any longer.  From that moment on,  most of the activities  of  </span><span data-contrast="none">security staff around the  </span><span data-contrast="none">world&nbsp;</span><span data-contrast="auto">focused on the  clean up  of the already  infected devices </span><span data-contrast="auto">and patching of vulnerable Windows operating systems to prevent the vulnerability  CVE-2017-0144  from being exploited  again  by  any  malware  leveraging  </span><span data-contrast="auto">EternalBlue</span><span data-contrast="auto">.</span><span data-contrast="auto"> </span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;134233117&quot;:true,&quot;134233118&quot;:true,&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559740&quot;:240}">&nbsp;</span></p>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><strong><span data-contrast="none">WannaCry&nbsp;</span></strong><strong><span data-contrast="none">Monetizaion</span></strong><strong><span data-contrast="none">&nbsp;Effort</span></strong><span data-contrast="auto"> </span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;134233117&quot;:true,&quot;134233118&quot;:true,&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559740&quot;:240}">&nbsp;</span></p>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><span data-contrast="auto">The threat actor behind  WannaCry  did not manage to capitalize efficiently on this  cyber-</span><span data-contrast="auto">attack</span><span data-contrast="auto">,</span><span data-contrast="none">&nbsp;</span><span data-contrast="none">if</span><span data-contrast="none">  m</span><span data-contrast="auto">onetization was their primary goal. By  mid-June 2017, </span><span data-contrast="auto">the</span><span data-contrast="none">&nbsp;</span><span data-contrast="none">operators</span><span data-contrast="auto">  managed to rack up little more than US  $100,000 which  </span><span data-contrast="none">is very little money  </span><span data-contrast="auto">by 2020  ransomware landscape standards,  particularly considering the  massive number of machines  compromised</span><span data-contrast="auto">&nbsp;which, according to Europol</span><span data-contrast="auto">,</span><span data-contrast="auto">&nbsp;was <a href="https://www.reuters.com/article/us-cyber-attack-europol/cyber-attack-hits-200000-in-at-least-150-countries-europol-idUSKCN18A0FX" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">more than 200,000 in at least 150 countries</a></span><span data-contrast="auto">. The reason behind this  relatively  </span><span data-contrast="auto">little monetization is due to the spray-and-pray approach used by the WannaCry  operators. Unlike today’s ransomware operations  </span><span data-contrast="none">that choose and  profile  their  </span><span data-contrast="auto">targets</span><span data-contrast="auto">&nbsp;</span><span data-contrast="auto">extensively, the  </span><span data-contrast="none">actors</span><span data-contrast="none">&nbsp;</span><span data-contrast="auto">behind WannaCry,  </span><span data-contrast="none">very likely</span><span data-contrast="auto">, were</span><span data-contrast="auto">&nbsp;</span><span data-contrast="auto">trying to  infect as many machines as possible in the hope that a higher numbers of infected machines  would lead to a higher </span><span data-contrast="auto">payout. </span></p>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><span data-contrast="none">Curiously</span><span data-contrast="none">,</span><span data-contrast="none">  the  three</span><span data-contrast="auto">  Bitcoin addresses hardcoded into the ransomware&nbsp; are still receiving payments today</span><span data-contrast="auto">&nbsp;in the</span><span data-contrast="auto">&nbsp;same</span><span data-contrast="auto"> US dollar amounts equivalents </span><span data-contrast="auto">requested by&nbsp;</span><span data-contrast="auto">the operator behind WannaCry 3 years ago</span><span data-contrast="auto">,&nbsp;</span><span data-contrast="auto">$300</span><span data-contrast="auto"> and $600</span><span data-contrast="auto">. </span><span data-contrast="auto">This&nbsp;</span><span data-contrast="none">strongly indicates</span><span data-contrast="none">&nbsp;that&nbsp;</span><span data-contrast="auto">WannaCry is still</span><span data-contrast="auto">&nbsp;</span><span data-contrast="auto">infecting machines</span><span data-contrast="auto">&nbsp;and people are still paying the ransom</span><span data-contrast="auto">. </span><span data-contrast="auto"> </span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;134233117&quot;:true,&quot;134233118&quot;:true,&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559740&quot;:240}">&nbsp;</span></p>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><span data-contrast="none">Analyst</span><span data-contrast="none">s</span><span data-contrast="none"> also  highlight</span><span data-contrast="none">&nbsp;</span><span data-contrast="auto">that the last time those  </span><span data-contrast="none">three </span><span data-contrast="auto"> Bitcoin wallets were emptied was on the 3</span><span data-contrast="auto">rd </span><span data-contrast="auto"> of August 2017</span><span data-contrast="none">. T</span><span data-contrast="auto">hey have been  left idle ever since even though they contain around $</span><span data-contrast="auto">20</span><span data-contrast="auto">,000 in Bitcoin&nbsp;</span><span data-contrast="auto">and they, occasional</span><span data-contrast="auto">ly</span><span data-contrast="auto">, keep receiving&nbsp;</span><span data-contrast="auto">new&nbsp;</span><span data-contrast="auto">ransom payments</span><span data-contrast="auto">.</span><span data-contrast="auto"> </span><span data-contrast="auto">$20,000 is a decent amount of money that any APT would like to retrieve to finance further operations. It is not clear why the APT behind WannaCry decided to leave those Bitcoins, but a possible explanation is that those Bitcoin are likely tainted and monitored and any transaction occurring from those wallet would likely trigger alerts&nbsp;</span><span data-contrast="auto">from all the Blockchain Analysis companies</span><span data-contrast="auto">&nbsp;monitoring them</span><span data-contrast="auto">. In addition to that, should the funds reach any cryptocurrency exchange&nbsp;</span><span data-contrast="auto">for cryptocurrency conversion purposes,&nbsp;</span><span data-contrast="auto">they would likely be identified as WannaCry ransom funds and immediately frozen.</span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;134233117&quot;:true,&quot;134233118&quot;:true,&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559740&quot;:240}">&nbsp;</span></p>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><strong><span data-contrast="none">Infections Like WannaCry are P</span></strong><strong><span data-contrast="none">os</span></strong><strong><span data-contrast="none">sible in 2020</span></strong><span data-contrast="auto"> </span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;134233117&quot;:true,&quot;134233118&quot;:true,&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559740&quot;:240}">&nbsp;</span></p>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><span data-contrast="auto">Given the impact that WannaCry had in such a short time frame, the question </span><span data-contrast="auto">is</span><span data-contrast="none">:&nbsp;</span><span data-contrast="none">c</span><span data-contrast="auto">ould  this happen again?  The answer is a definite  “yes”  for another </span><span data-contrast="auto">wormable</span><span data-contrast="auto">  </span><span data-contrast="auto">malware, while for</span><span data-contrast="auto">&nbsp;</span><span data-contrast="auto">another  </span><span data-contrast="auto">wormable </span><span data-contrast="auto"> ransomware, just like WannaCry, the answer is  “maybe not”.</span><span data-contrast="auto"> </span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;134233117&quot;:true,&quot;134233118&quot;:true,&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559740&quot;:240}">&nbsp;</span></p>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><span data-contrast="auto">Worms, malware  families  capable of self-propagating without operator or victim’s interaction, are nothing new. They rely on exploits that provide remote code execution by leveraging severe vulnerabilities.  </span><span data-contrast="none">At the time of reporting, analysts believe with moderate confidence that&nbsp;</span><span data-contrast="none">8</span><span data-contrast="none">&nbsp;vulnerabilities affecting Windows devices could be paired with a worm variant</span><span data-contrast="none">:</span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;134233117&quot;:true,&quot;134233118&quot;:true,&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559740&quot;:240}">&nbsp;</span></p>

<li data-leveltext="" data-font="Symbol" data-listid="2" aria-setsize="-1" data-aria-posinset="5" data-aria-level="1"><span data-contrast="none">BlueKeep</span><span data-contrast="none"> V CVE-2019-1226</span><span data-contrast="auto"> </span><span data-contrast="auto"> </span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;134233117&quot;:true,&quot;134233118&quot;:true,&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559685&quot;:360,&quot;335559731&quot;:0,&quot;335559740&quot;:240}">&nbsp;</span></li>

<p style="text-align: justify;"><span data-contrast="auto">Al</span><span data-contrast="auto">l  </span><span data-contrast="auto">BlueKeep</span><span data-contrast="auto">  vulnerabilities are being actively exploited in the wild</span><span data-contrast="none">, yet  no threat actor seems to have  weaponized  them into a worm.  Analysts believe that the self-propagating features make worms very noisy and difficult to control. It is unlikely they will be used in targeted </span><span data-contrast="none">attacks</span><span data-contrast="none">,&nbsp;</span><span data-contrast="none">but</span><span data-contrast="none">&nbsp;are&nbsp;</span><span data-contrast="none">befitting</span><span data-contrast="none">&nbsp;if chaos/diversion is an intended goal.  This is probably the reason why the developers of WannaCry created a kill-switch in the first place</span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;134233117&quot;:true,&quot;134233118&quot;:true,&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559740&quot;:240}">&nbsp;</span></p>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><strong><span data-contrast="none">New Ransomware Modi Operandi and TTPs</span></strong><span data-contrast="auto"> </span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;134233117&quot;:true,&quot;134233118&quot;:true,&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559740&quot;:240}">&nbsp;</span></p>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><span data-contrast="auto">APTs have very defined goals  such as  data exfiltration and/or  monetization from a tailored attack. A worm does not fulfill these use-case</span><span data-contrast="none">s</span><span data-contrast="auto">.  Particularly in  the ransomware arena,  </span><span data-contrast="none">EclecticIQ analysts </span><span data-contrast="auto">have seen major developments in the last few&nbsp;</span><span data-contrast="none">years.&nbsp;</span><span data-contrast="none">Most r</span><span data-contrast="none">ansomware</span><span data-contrast="auto">&nbsp;attacks are now</span><span data-contrast="auto">&nbsp;</span><span data-contrast="auto">targeted </span><span data-contrast="none">and APT groups extensively profile organizations to:</span><span data-contrast="none"> </span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;134233117&quot;:true,&quot;134233118&quot;:true,&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559740&quot;:240}">&nbsp;</span></p>
<ol style="text-align: justify;">
<li data-leveltext="%1." data-font="Calibri" data-listid="3" aria-setsize="-1" data-aria-posinset="1" data-aria-level="1"><span data-contrast="none">Understand the victim</span><span data-contrast="none">’</span><span data-contrast="none">s capability&nbsp;</span><span data-contrast="none">and</span><span data-contrast="none">&nbsp;willingnes</span><span data-contrast="none">s to</span><span data-contrast="none">&nbsp;pay the ransom.</span><span data-contrast="none"> </span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;134233117&quot;:true,&quot;134233118&quot;:true,&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559685&quot;:360,&quot;335559731&quot;:0,&quot;335559740&quot;:240}">&nbsp;</span></li>
<li data-leveltext="%1." data-font="Calibri" data-listid="3" aria-setsize="-1" data-aria-posinset="1" data-aria-level="1"><span data-contrast="none">Dentify data that should be encrypted.</span><span data-contrast="none"> </span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;134233117&quot;:true,&quot;134233118&quot;:true,&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559685&quot;:360,&quot;335559731&quot;:0,&quot;335559740&quot;:240}">&nbsp;</span></li>
<li data-leveltext="%1." data-font="Calibri" data-listid="3" aria-setsize="-1" data-aria-posinset="1" data-aria-level="1"><span data-contrast="none">Target data for  exfiltration  prior  to  encryption</span><span data-contrast="none">.</span><span data-contrast="none"> </span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;134233117&quot;:true,&quot;134233118&quot;:true,&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559685&quot;:360,&quot;335559731&quot;:0,&quot;335559740&quot;:240}">&nbsp;</span></li>
</ol>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><span data-contrast="auto">These types of attacks are leading to  a single,  <a href="https://www.bleepingcomputer.com/news/security/ragnarlocker-ransomware-hits-edp-energy-giant-asks-for-10m/" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">one-time  ransom payment in the  hundreds of thousand/million-dollar  range</a></span><span data-contrast="auto">, a figure multiple times over what the threat actor behind WannaCry managed to collect in 3 years, targeting hundreds of thousands of machines.</span><span data-contrast="auto"> </span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;134233117&quot;:true,&quot;134233118&quot;:true,&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559740&quot;:240}">&nbsp;</span></p>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><span data-contrast="none">It is&nbsp;</span><span data-contrast="none">plausible</span><span data-contrast="none">&nbsp;that an APT&nbsp;</span><span data-contrast="none">might&nbsp;</span><span data-contrast="none">leverage a worm, or even a ransomware worm like WannaCry, as diversion.  An APT could use a</span><span data-contrast="none">&nbsp;</span><span data-contrast="none">disruptive&nbsp;</span><span data-contrast="none">ransomware-</span><span data-contrast="none">worm  not to monetize, but to create  disarray  and use that as  smokescreen to divert  incident responders and  security </span><span data-contrast="none">researcher</span><span data-contrast="none">s’</span><span data-contrast="none">&nbsp;</span><span data-contrast="none">from the</span><span data-contrast="auto"> real  objective and activities of  </span><span data-contrast="none">an  </span><span data-contrast="auto">APT&nbsp; group.</span><span data-contrast="auto"> </span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;134233117&quot;:true,&quot;134233118&quot;:true,&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559740&quot;:240}">&nbsp;</span></p>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><span data-contrast="none">Wormable</span><span data-contrast="none">  exploits remain possible </span><span data-contrast="none">as </span><span data-contrast="auto">long as </span><span data-contrast="auto"> there are systems with Remote </span><span data-contrast="auto">Code  Execution  vulnerabilities.  According to </span><span data-contrast="auto">an </span><span data-contrast="auto"> </span><a href="https://blog.erratasec.com/2019/05/almost-one-million-vulnerable-to.html#.XruE_hMzbUo" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank" id="__hsNewLink"><span data-contrast="auto">Erratasec</span><span data-contrast="auto"> report </span></a><span data-contrast="auto">form last year, there are approximately 1 million vulnerable RDP devices facing the internet. This does not </span><span data-contrast="none">include</span><span data-contrast="none">&nbsp;</span><span data-contrast="auto">vulnerable RDP devices in internal networks. With an attack surface this wide, a worm would find fertile ground for speedy propagation.</span><span data-contrast="auto"> </span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;134233117&quot;:true,&quot;134233118&quot;:true,&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559740&quot;:240}">&nbsp;</span></p>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><span data-contrast="auto">Patching is the primary response to prevent, not just worms, but any </span><span data-contrast="none">cyber-attack</span><span data-contrast="auto"> exploiting one of these vulnerabilities. Patching is the  responsibility of every organization to defend  from  </span><span data-contrast="none">cyber-attacks  </span><span data-contrast="auto">but also to prevent their compromised infrastructure from being used  as a bridgehead to attack other organizations. If there is one lesson that needs to be learnt from the WannaCry outbreak of 3 years ago, it is the importance of keeping your operating systems and applications patched and up to date.c</span><span data-contrast="none"> Enterprises concerned with protecting information assets should  think carefully about patch prioritization.&nbsp; </span></p>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><span data-contrast="none">Investment  in information security  and threat intelligence can help identify trending vulnerabilities like  the  </span><span data-contrast="none">EternalBlue</span><span data-contrast="none">-associated CVE-2017-0144  for&nbsp; high priority patching&nbsp; before catastrophe occurs.</span><span data-contrast="none"> </span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;134233117&quot;:true,&quot;134233118&quot;:true,&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559740&quot;:240}">&nbsp;</span></p>
