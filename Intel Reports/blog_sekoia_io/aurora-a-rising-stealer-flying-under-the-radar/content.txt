<h1 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-summary">Summary</h1>



<p>In July 2022, Sekoia.io discovered a new Golang botnet advertised by its alleged developer as Aurora botnet since April 2022. Since we published an analysis of the malware and the profile of the threat actor advertising Aurora on underground forums for our clients, the botnet’s activity slowed down.</p>



<p>Since September 2022, Aurora malware is advertised as an infostealer and several traffers teams announced they added it to their malware toolset. Furthermore, Sekoia.io observed an increase in the number of Aurora samples distributed in the wild, as well as C2 servers.</p>



<p>As the Aurora malware is widespread, not well detected, or publicly documented either, Sekoia.io analysed Aurora in depth and share the results of our investigation in this article.</p>



<span id="more-3124"></span>



<h1 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-context">Context</h1>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-the-evolution-from-botnet-to-stealer">The evolution from botnet to stealer</h2>



<p>First advertised on Russian-speaking underground forums in April 2022, Aurora is a multi-purpose botnet with stealing, downloading and remote access capabilities. The botnet was sold as a Malware-as-a-Service (MaaS) by a threat actor going by the handle <em>Cheshire</em>.<br><br>In July 2022, we identified around 50 samples, the majority of which belonging to the&nbsp; “Cheshire” and “Zelizzard” botnets, and less than a dozen C2 servers associated with Aurora botnets. In late July, the Aurora servers were no longer active, and no more recent Aurora samples were submitted on an online public repository. At the time, Sekoia.io assessed that the activity of Aurora botnets was near at standstill.</p>



<p>Additionally, the presumed developer stopped publishing about Aurora botnet on Dark Web forums and on its Telegram channel at the beginning of June 2022. Another publication on BHF forum in late July 2022 suggested that <em>Cheshire</em> developers shifted to developing malware on demand. Based on these observations, we assess it is possible that the Aurora Botnet MaaS development is now abandoned.</p>



<p>In late August 2022, Aurora was advertised as a stealer instead of a botnet on Telegram and underground forums.</p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-a-popular-stealer-in-the-traffers-landscape">A popular stealer in the traffers landscape</h2>



<p>Based on the Dark Web <a href="https://blog.sekoia.io/a-war-on-multiple-fronts-the-turbulent-cybercrime-landscape/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">cybercrime forums</a>, Sekoia.io identified 9 traffers teams that announced they added Aurora in their <a href="https://blog.sekoia.io/overview-of-the-russian-speaking-infostealer-ecosystem-the-logs/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">infostealer</a> arsenal. Most of them created their team after the advertisement of Aurora as a stealer, and are still very active.</p>

<h1 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-technical-analysis">Technical Analysis</h1>



<p>As previously introduced, Aurora is a Golang information stealer. Following is an overview of the Aurora stealer capabilities: data collection, exfiltration to its C2 server and load of the next-stage payload.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-data-collection">Data collection</h2>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-fingerprint">Fingerprint</h3>



<p>Aurora mainly uses the <a href="https://github.com/lxn/win" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">lxn/win</a> library to interact with the Windows API, this library relies on Windows Management Instrumentation Command (WMIC).&nbsp;</p>



<p>To fingerprint the host, Aurora executes three commands on the infected host:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li><code>wmic os get Caption</code></li>



<li><code>wmic path win32_VideoController get name</code></li>



<li><code>wmic cpu get name</code></li>
</ul>



<figure class="wp-block-image"><img decoding="async" src="data:image/svg+xml,%3Csvg%20xmlns='http://www.w3.org/2000/svg'%20viewBox='0%200%200%200'%3E%3C/svg%3E" alt="Aurora commands executed on the infected host in SEKOIA.IO XDR" data-lazy-src="https://lh3.googleusercontent.com/0dmJZthrgC2L0EnZMoOFmq2Mi-1xzvoqQr1BSS8lmUcRJ1-2tsoaxtoS110LAp1DBVGfTIas-YfHG89waP8f1ebYpZ-EQChTZ9zdjQCxzdIn6lFklA4Yx1cutsDyy9Yq-9mu2WqdBHGoxpd-cLnmxqEPFOKoMNJA1gynPByjoG_VuNrncQ_6Rmt8WqHG"/><noscript><img decoding="async" src="https://lh3.googleusercontent.com/0dmJZthrgC2L0EnZMoOFmq2Mi-1xzvoqQr1BSS8lmUcRJ1-2tsoaxtoS110LAp1DBVGfTIas-YfHG89waP8f1ebYpZ-EQChTZ9zdjQCxzdIn6lFklA4Yx1cutsDyy9Yq-9mu2WqdBHGoxpd-cLnmxqEPFOKoMNJA1gynPByjoG_VuNrncQ_6Rmt8WqHG" alt="Aurora commands executed on the infected host in SEKOIA.IO XDR"/></noscript></figure>



<p class="has-text-align-center"><em>Figure 3. Aurora commands executed on the infected host in Sekoia.io XDR</em></p>



<p>Like previously analysed stealers, Aurora also takes one screenshot of the infected host.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-data-from-browsers-extensions-and-applications">Data from browsers, extensions and applications</h3>



<p>To collect information, Aurora targets multiple web browsers, as well as browser extensions including those managing cryptocurrency wallets and applications such as Telegram.</p>



<p>Targeted extensions are listed in the sample, applications, web browsers are written in the sample (see Annex 2). The malware uses the function walk of the built-in module <a href="https://pkg.go.dev/path/filepath#Walk" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">path</a> to loop over files and directories until it matches a filename or directory name of one of the targeted applications or extensions.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-file-grabber">File grabber</h3>



<p>The grabber configuration is simple, the stealer gathers a list of directories to search for files of interest.</p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-command-and-control-communications">Command and Control communications</h2>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-canal-format-and-structure">Canal, format and structure</h3>



<p>The malware communicates using TCP connection on ports 8081 and 9865 &#8211; 8081 being the most widespread open port. Exfiltrated data are in JSON format.&nbsp;</p>



<p>&nbsp;All messages abide by the same structure, each keys are described below:&nbsp;</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Browser: name of the browser where data was collected (ex: Mozilla, Chromium, <em>etc</em>.);</li>



<li>Cache: content of the stolen file encoded in base64;</li>



<li>FileName: name of the stolen file (<em>e.g. </em>cookies.sqlite, Login Data);</li>



<li>GRB: likely the grabber configuration. Of note, Sekoia.io only observed the value &#8220;null&#8221;;</li>



<li>Info: host fingerprint information, including:
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Name: a random name defined by threat actor;</li>



<li>BuildID: name of the build, the value often matches a threat actor’s Telegram account;</li>



<li>OS: Windows version;</li>



<li>HWID: hardware ID;</li>



<li>GPU: graphical card information;</li>



<li>CPU: CPU name and vendor;</li>



<li>RAM: amount of memory;</li>



<li>Location: execution path of Aurora sample;</li>



<li>Screen: size of the screen of the infected host;</li>



<li>IP: expecting the IP address of the infected host but the value is always an empty string.</li>
</ul>
</li>



<li>MasterKey: encryption key used to read the data of the stolen file, for instance some browsers store the saved password encrypted;</li>



<li>Path: always empty string;</li>



<li>Type: type of the exfiltrated data (Browser-Mozilla, Screenshot, <em>etc.</em>).</li>
</ul>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-next-stage-loading">Next-stage loading</h2>



<p>Aurora’s promoter claims the stealer has a file grabber and a loader capabilities. During the investigation, only the loader capabilities were observed (see Annex 1).</p>



<p>Aurora loader is straightforward, it downloads a remote payload using <em>net_http_Get</em> from the built-in library <a href="https://pkg.go.dev/net/http" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">net/http</a>, then the file is written on the disk in the temporary directory with a random name. The stealer executes the next stage using the following PowerShell command:</p>



<p><code>powershell.exe start-process “C:\Users\Admin\AppData\Local\Temp\oH7P8GCPXQ.exe”&nbsp;</code></p>

<h1 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-conclusion">Conclusion</h1>



<p>Aurora is another <strong>infostealer targeting data from browsers, cryptocurrency wallets, local systems, and acting as a loader</strong>. Sold at a high price on market places, <strong>collected data</strong> is of particular interest to cybercriminals, allowing them to <strong>carry out follow-up lucrative campaigns</strong>, including <strong>Big Game Hunting operations</strong>.</p>



<p>As multiple threat actors, including traffers teams, <strong>added the malware to their arsenal</strong>, Aurora Stealer is becoming a prominent threat. As observed by Sekoia.io, cybercriminal threat actors <strong>widely distribute it using multiple infection chains</strong> including phishing websites masquerading legitimate ones, YouTube videos and fake “free software catalogue” websites.<br><br>To provide our customers with actionable intelligence, Sekoia.io analysts will continue to monitor emerging and prevalent infostealers, including Aurora.</p>
