More often than not, the primary motive for APT attacks in the Asia-Pacific (APAC) region is not financial gain, but rather espionage.<br /><br />Group-IB continuously explores and analyzes the methods, tools, and tactics used by some of the world’s most prominent APT groups, <a href="https://blog.group-ib.com/apt41-world-tour-2021" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener" style="font-weight: 500;">such as APT41</a>, but how can large-scale companies and organizations protect themselves when a new APT group emerges, or, if an already existing APT group begins to utilize a completely new toolkit. Enter Dark Pink.<br /><br /><span style="font-weight: 500;">Dark Pink</span> is the name given by Group-IB to a <span style="font-weight: 500;">new wave of APT attacks</span> that has struck the APAC region. At the present time, Group-IB cannot attribute the campaign to any known threat actor, making it highly likely that Dark Pink is an entirely new APT group. Bearing this in mind, we will refer to Dark Pink as an APT group throughout the entirety of this text. The name Dark Pink was coined by forming a hybrid of some of the email addresses used by the threat actors during data exfiltration. The APT group has also been termed <a href="https://mp.weixin.qq.com/s/G3gUjg9WC96NW4cRPww6gw" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">Saaiwc Group</a> by Chinese cybersecurity researchers.<br /><br />There is evidence to suggest that Dark Pink began operations as early as mid-2021, although the group’s activity surged in mid-to-late 2022. To date, <a href="https://www.group-ib.com/products/threat-intelligence/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener" style="font-weight: 500;">Group-IB’s sector-leading Threat Intelligence</a> uncovered <span style="font-weight: 500;">seven confirmed attacks</span> by Dark Pink. The bulk of the attacks were carried out against countries in the APAC region, although the threat actors spread their wings and targeted one European governmental ministry. The confirmed victims include <span style="font-weight: 500;">two military bodies</span> in the <span style="font-weight: 500;">Philippines </span><span style="font-weight: 300;">and</span><span style="font-weight: 400;"> </span><span style="font-weight: 500;">Malaysia</span>, government agencies in <span style="font-weight: 500;">Cambodia, Indonesia</span> and <span style="font-weight: 500;">Bosnia and Herzegovina</span>, and a <span style="font-weight: 500;">religious organization in Vietnam</span>. Group-IB also became aware of an unsuccessful attack on a European state development agency based in Vietnam. In line with Group-IB’s zero tolerance policy to cybercrime, <span style="font-weight: 500;">confirmed and potential victims of Dark Pink were issued proactive notifications</span>, and we note that the list of companies breached by this particular APT group is likely to be longer.<br /><br />Group-IB’s early research into Dark Pink has revealed that these threat actors are leveraging a new set of tactics, techniques, and procedures rarely utilized by previously known APT groups. They leverage a custom toolkit, featuring <span style="font-weight: 500;">TelePowerBot, KamiKakaBot</span>, and <span style="font-weight: 500;">Cucky </span><span style="font-weight: 300;">and</span><span style="font-weight: 500;"> Ctealer</span> information stealers (all names dubbed by Group-IB) with the aim of stealing confidential documentation held on the networks of government and military organizations. Of particular note is Dark Pink’s ability to <span style="font-weight: 500;">infect even the USB devices</span> attached to compromised computers, and also its ability to gain access to messengers on infected machines. Furthermore, Dark Pink threat actors utilize two core techniques: <a href="https://attack.mitre.org/techniques/T1574/002/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">DLL Side-Loading</a> and <span style="font-weight: 500;">executing malicious content triggered by a file type association</span> (<a href="https://attack.mitre.org/techniques/T1546/001/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">Event Triggered Execution: Change Default File Association</a>). The latter of these tactics is one rarely seen utilized in the wild by threat actors.<br /><br />At the time of writing, Dark Pink is still active. Given the fact that many of the attacks identified by Group-IB researchers took place in the <span style="font-weight: 500;">final months of 2022</span>, Group-IB researchers are still in the process of identifying the full scope of the APT attack, and efforts to uncover the origin of this APT group are in process. However, we believe that this preliminary research, which will be of great interest to CISO, heads of cybersecurity teams, SOC analysts and incident response specialists, will go a long way to raising awareness of the new TTPs utilized by this threat actor and help organizations to take the relevant steps to protect themselves from a potentially devastating APT attack. </div></div> </div></div></div></div><div id="rec535317701" class="r t-rec t-rec_pt_30 t-rec_pb_0" style="padding-top:30px;padding-bottom:0px;
