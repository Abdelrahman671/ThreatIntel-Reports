<h1 class="blog-banner__heading">
      <span>Coronavirus Cyber Threat Landscape Update</span>

  </h1>

    </div>
  </div>

  <div class="blog-content">

        <div class="blog-content__metadata blog-content__metadata-author">
      <span class="blog-content__date">  <time datetime="2020-03-18T15:55:58Z">March 18, 2020</time>

</span>
        <span class="blog-content__author">
                Axel F, Sam Scholten
  
        </span>
    </div>
    <div class="node-full__body blog-content__body">  <h1 style="margin-bottom: 8px;"><strong><span style="font-size:15pt"><span style="line-height:normal"><span style="font-family:Arial,sans-serif"><span style="color:#595959"><span class="Heading1Char" style="font-family:Arial,sans-serif"><span style="color:#262626"><span style="font-size:11.0pt"><span style="font-family:&quot;Calibri&quot;,sans-serif">Overview</span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></strong></h1>

<p><span style="font-size:15pt"><span style="line-height:normal"><span style="font-family:Arial,sans-serif"><span style="color:#595959"><span style="font-size:11.0pt"><span style="font-family:&quot;Calibri&quot;,sans-serif"><span style="color:black">Proofpoint researchers have tracked attackers leveraging the coronavirus pandemic since January 29. This blog serves as an update of the overall threat landscape and includes selected examples to highlight what we are seeing.</span></span></span></span></span></span></span></p>

<p><span style="font-size:15pt"><span style="line-height:normal"><span style="font-family:Arial,sans-serif"><span style="color:#595959"><span style="font-size:11.0pt"><span style="font-family:&quot;Calibri&quot;,sans-serif"><span style="color:black">Currently, attackers are using coronavirus themes for nearly all types of attacks, including (but not limited to) <a href="https://www.proofpoint.com/us/products/email-fraud-defense">business email compromise</a> (BEC), credential phishing, malware, and spam email campaigns. </span></span></span></span></span></span></span></p>

<p><span style="font-size:15pt"><span style="line-height:normal"><span style="font-family:Arial,sans-serif"><span style="color:#595959"><span style="font-size:11.0pt"><span style="font-family:&quot;Calibri&quot;,sans-serif"><span style="color:black">The targeting of these attacks has ranged from extremely broad to narrowly focused and campaign volumes have fluctuated between small and large. Attribution includes both well-known and unknown threat actors. Some of the well-known threat actors include TA505&nbsp;and TA542.</span></span></span></span></span></span></span></p>

<p><span style="font-size:15pt"><span style="line-height:normal"><span style="font-family:Arial,sans-serif"><span style="color:#595959"><span style="font-size:11.0pt"><span style="font-family:&quot;Calibri&quot;,sans-serif"><span style="color:black">Weve observed attacks around the world, most notably in Italy, the Czech Republic, Japan, United States, Canada, Australia, and Turkey. In addition to English, attackers have used Italian, Czech, Japanese, Spanish, and French languages within their messages.</span></span></span></span></span></span></span></p>

<p><span style="font-size:15pt"><span style="line-height:normal"><span style="font-family:Arial,sans-serif"><span style="color:#595959"><span style="font-size:11.0pt"><span style="font-family:&quot;Calibri&quot;,sans-serif"><span style="color:black">While all industries have been targeted, weve seen specific targeting of <a href="https://www.proofpoint.com/us/solutions/healthcare-information-security">healthcare</a>, education, manufacturing, media, advertising, and hospitality organizations in certain campaigns.</span></span></span></span></span></span></span></p>

<p><span style="font-size:15pt"><span style="line-height:normal"><span style="font-family:Arial,sans-serif"><span style="color:#595959"><span style="font-size:11.0pt"><span style="font-family:&quot;Calibri&quot;,sans-serif"><span style="color:black">Attackers are actively abusing the names and logos of many companies and organizations within these campaigns in an attempt to manipulate recipients. Of particular note is the spoofing and brand abuse of national and international health organizations around the world, including the World Health Organization (WHO), the United States Centers for Disease Control (CDC), and Canadian and Australian national health organizations.&nbsp; </span></span></span></span></span></span></span></p>

<p><span style="font-size:15pt"><span style="line-height:normal"><span style="font-family:Arial,sans-serif"><span style="color:#595959"><span style="font-size:11.0pt"><span style="font-family:&quot;Calibri&quot;,sans-serif"><span style="color:black">Threat actors have launched coronavirus campaigns to spread remote access Trojans (RATs),&nbsp; keyloggers, information stealers, and bankers.&nbsp; We are also seeing credential phishing campaigns with this theme.&nbsp; For example, we observed attempts to harvest credentials&nbsp;for Facebook, DocuSign, Microsoft Outlook Web Access (OWA), Microsoft OneDrive, and universities around the world.&nbsp;</span></span></span></span></span></span></span></p>

<p><span style="font-size:15pt"><span style="line-height:normal"><span style="font-family:Arial,sans-serif"><span style="color:#595959"><span style="font-size:11.0pt"><span style="font-family:&quot;Calibri&quot;,sans-serif"><span style="color:black">We expect attackers will continue to leverage coronavirus themes in their attacks for some time to come. Proofpoint researchers will continue to track closely and provide updates on our blog and through our </span></span></span><span style="font-size:11.0pt"><span style="font-family:&quot;Calibri&quot;,sans-serif"><a href="https://twitter.com/threatinsight" style="color:#276d5b; text-decoration:underline">Threat Insight Twitter handle</a></span></span><span style="font-size:11.0pt"><span style="font-family:&quot;Calibri&quot;,sans-serif"><span style="color:black">.</span></span></span></span></span></span></span></p>

<p><span style="font-size:15pt"><span style="line-height:normal"><span style="font-family:Arial,sans-serif"><span style="color:#595959"><span style="font-size:11.0pt"><span style="font-family:&quot;Calibri&quot;,sans-serif"><span style="color:black">Below are several notable examples of what weve seen.</span></span></span></span></span></span></span></p>

<h2><u><strong><span style="font-size:15pt"><span style="line-height:normal"><span style="font-family:Arial,sans-serif"><span style="color:#595959"><span style="font-size:11.0pt"><span style="font-family:&quot;Calibri&quot;,sans-serif"><span style="color:black">BEC Attempt: People tested positive downtown so I cant go out right now</span></span></span></span></span></span></span></strong></u></h2>

<p style="margin-bottom:8px"><span style="font-size:15pt"><span style="line-height:107%"><span style="font-family:Arial,sans-serif"><span style="color:#595959"><b><span style="font-size:11.0pt"><span style="line-height:107%"><span style="font-family:&quot;Calibri&quot;,sans-serif"><span style="color:black">Key Points: </span></span></span></span></b><span style="font-size:11.0pt"><span style="line-height:107%"><span style="font-family:&quot;Calibri&quot;,sans-serif"><span style="color:black">BEC attackers use the claim of positive coronavirus tests in their area to start email conversation.</span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></p>

<p><img alt="Coronavirus BEC Email Example" data-entity-type="file" data-entity-uuid="e9ac217a-417e-4c1e-9ac2-698cbf0c7df2" height="383" src="/sites/default/files/inline-images/image-20200318091010-1.png" width="1023" loading="lazy"></p>

<p style="margin-bottom:8px"><span style="font-size:15pt"><span style="line-height:107%"><span style="font-family:Arial,sans-serif"><span style="color:#595959"><b><span style="font-size:11.0pt"><span style="line-height:107%"><span style="font-family:&quot;Calibri&quot;,sans-serif"><span style="color:black">BEC Attempt Summary:</span></span></span></span></b></span></span></span></span></p>

<p><span style="font-size:15pt"><span style="line-height:normal"><span style="font-family:Arial,sans-serif"><span style="color:#595959"><span style="font-size:11.0pt"><span style="font-family:&quot;Calibri&quot;,sans-serif"><span style="color:black">This BEC email attempts to capitalize on current events and the global shift towards quarantine to prevent further spreading of coronavirus. BEC actors often try to convey a sense of urgency or immediate need. In this attack, the urgency is present in the subject line used here: "Urgent Reply needed about corona virus"[SIC].&nbsp; </span></span></span></span></span></span></span><span style="font-size:15pt"><span style="line-height:normal"><span style="font-family:Arial,sans-serif"><span style="color:#595959">&nbsp;</span></span></span></span></p>

<p><span style="font-size:15pt"><span style="line-height:normal"><span style="font-family:Arial,sans-serif"><span style="color:#595959"><span style="font-size:11.0pt"><span style="font-family:&quot;Calibri&quot;,sans-serif"><span style="color:black">BEC attacks are often delivered in stages. The first email sent is typically innocuous, meaning that they do not contain the attacker's end goal. The attackers craft plausible scenarios in hopes the recipient will reply. Once theyre on the hook, the attacker will send their true ask. (I need you to buy gift cards, wire transfer funds, etc.)</span></span></span></span></span></span></span><span style="font-size:15pt"><span style="line-height:normal"><span style="font-family:Arial,sans-serif"><span style="color:#595959">&nbsp;</span></span></span></span></p>

<p><span style="font-size:15pt"><span style="line-height:normal"><span style="font-family:Arial,sans-serif"><span style="color:#595959"><span style="font-size:11.0pt"><span style="font-family:&quot;Calibri&quot;,sans-serif"><span style="color:black">These coronavirus-themed BEC attacks often come with spoofed display names, which are likely real people known to the recipient. In the body of this message, the actor attempts to eliminate the possibility of voice-verification, in hopes of ensuring a higher success rate, by saying their phone is "faulty at the moment."&nbsp; </span></span></span></span></span></span></span></p>

<h2 style="margin-top: 31px;"><u><strong><span style="font-size:15pt"><span style="line-height:normal"><span style="page-break-after:avoid"><span style="font-family:Arial,sans-serif"><span style="color:#595959"><span style="font-size:11.0pt"><span style="font-family:&quot;Calibri&quot;,sans-serif"><span style="color:black">Credential Phish: Microsoft Office </span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></strong></u></h2>

<p style="margin-bottom:8px"><span style="font-size:15pt"><span style="line-height:107%"><span style="font-family:Arial,sans-serif"><span style="color:#595959"><b><span style="font-size:11.0pt"><span style="line-height:107%"><span style="font-family:&quot;Calibri&quot;,sans-serif"><span style="color:black">Key Points: </span></span></span></span></b><span style="font-size:11.0pt"><span style="line-height:107%"><span style="font-family:&quot;Calibri&quot;,sans-serif"><span style="color:black">Attackers use credible, customized, fake internal emails for credential phishing attacks targeting the healthcare industry.</span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></p>

<p><img alt="Microsoft Office Coronavirus Phishing Email Example" data-entity-type="file" data-entity-uuid="c4e1964b-916d-49ec-b144-8ae63b8c2858" height="1340" src="/sites/default/files/inline-images/image-20200318091010-2.png" width="1428" loading="lazy"></p>

<p style="margin-bottom:8px"><span style="font-size:15pt"><span style="line-height:107%"><span style="font-family:Arial,sans-serif"><span style="color:#595959"><b><span style="font-size:11.0pt"><span style="line-height:107%"><span style="font-family:&quot;Calibri&quot;,sans-serif"><span style="color:black">Microsoft Office Credential Phish Summary:</span></span></span></span></b></span></span></span></span></p>

<p><span style="font-size:15pt"><span style="line-height:normal"><span style="font-family:Arial,sans-serif"><span style="color:#595959"><span style="font-size:11.0pt"><span style="font-family:&quot;Calibri&quot;,sans-serif"><span style="color:black">These specific Coronavirus cyber attacks targeted companies in the healthcare industry. Our researchers found that the emails were highly customized to each target to increase their credibility. They claimed to be from the targeted company's actual president and used the targeted company name and presidents name multiple times in the email. </span></span></span></span></span></span></span></p>

<p><span style="font-size:15pt"><span style="line-height:normal"><span style="font-family:Arial,sans-serif"><span style="color:#595959"><span style="font-size:11.0pt"><span style="font-family:&quot;Calibri&quot;,sans-serif"><span style="color:black">The messages conveyed information about halting all travel to China and contained an attached Word document with a link inside of it. If a user clicked the link, they would be brought to a spoofed Microsoft Office branded credential phishing site that asks for email login and password. </span></span></span></span></span></span></span></p>

<p style="margin-bottom:8px">&nbsp;</p>

<h2 style="margin-bottom: 8px;"><u><strong><span style="font-size:15pt"><span style="line-height:107%"><span style="font-family:Arial,sans-serif"><span style="color:#595959"><span style="font-size:11.0pt"><span style="line-height:107%"><span style="font-family:&quot;Calibri&quot;,sans-serif"><span style="color:black">Credential Phish: Outlook Web Access (OWA)</span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></strong></u></h2>

<p style="margin-bottom:8px"><span style="font-size:15pt"><span style="line-height:107%"><span style="font-family:Arial,sans-serif"><span style="color:#595959"><b><span style="font-size:11.0pt"><span style="line-height:107%"><span style="font-family:&quot;Calibri&quot;,sans-serif"><span style="color:black">Key Points: </span></span></span></span></b><span style="font-size:11.0pt"><span style="line-height:107%"><span style="font-family:&quot;Calibri&quot;,sans-serif"><span style="color:black">Attackers use an employee survey about coronavirus to target Outlook Web Access credentials.</span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></p>

<h2><u><span style="font-size:15pt"><span style="line-height:normal"><span style="font-family:Arial,sans-serif"><span style="color:#595959"><b><span style="font-size:11.0pt"><span style="font-family:&quot;Calibri&quot;,sans-serif"><span style="color:black">Spam: Coronavirus Testing Kit</span></span></span></b></span></span></span></span></u></h2>

<p><img alt="Fake Coronavirus Testing Kit Spam Email Example" data-entity-type="file" data-entity-uuid="061b96e9-08b5-487c-95f7-36b63295a520" height="600" src="/sites/default/files/inline-images/image-20200318091010-13.png" width="678" loading="lazy"></p>

<p style="margin-bottom:8px"><span style="font-size:15pt"><span style="line-height:107%"><span style="font-family:Arial,sans-serif"><span style="color:#595959"><b><span style="font-size:11.0pt"><span style="line-height:107%"><span style="font-family:&quot;Calibri&quot;,sans-serif"><span style="color:black">Coronavirus Testing Kit Spam Summary:</span></span></span></span></b></span></span></span></span></p>

<p><span style="font-size:15pt"><span style="line-height:normal"><span style="font-family:Arial,sans-serif"><span style="color:#595959"><span style="font-size:11.0pt"><span style="font-family:&quot;Calibri&quot;,sans-serif"><span style="color:black">In early March, Proofpoint researchers observed a small spam campaign targeting media and advertising companies in the United States. The email did not try to get the recipient to open the malicious URL, but instead asked the recipients to spread a website URL to their audience. Specifically, it asked them to place the URL and a short message on top of their most recent YouTube video description. The sender offered a payment of $100 in exchange for this.</span></span></span></span></span></span></span></p>

<p><span style="font-size:15pt"><span style="line-height:normal"><span style="font-family:Arial,sans-serif"><span style="color:#595959"><span style="font-size:11.0pt"><span style="font-family:&quot;Calibri&quot;,sans-serif"><span style="color:black">The website, coronavirusmedicalkit[.]com, offered to sell free COVID-19 testing kits. However, at the end of the ordering process a $10 fee was added (likely for shipping). As Better Business Bureau </span></span></span><span style="font-size:11.0pt"><span style="font-family:&quot;Calibri&quot;,sans-serif"><a href="https://www.bbb.org/article/news-releases/21482-scam-alert-preparing-for-coronavirus-that-face-mask-could-be-a-con" style="color:#276d5b; text-decoration:underline"><span style="color:black"><span style="text-decoration:none"><span style="text-underline:none">put it</span></span></span></a></span></span><span style="font-size:11.0pt"><span style="font-family:&quot;Calibri&quot;,sans-serif"><span style="color:black">, a lot of times these phony sellers take victims money and never deliver anything at allThese sites use tricks like limited time deals to entice you into ordering more. This campaign is interesting because we do not often see direct outreach like this via email.</span></span></span></span></span></span></span></p>
