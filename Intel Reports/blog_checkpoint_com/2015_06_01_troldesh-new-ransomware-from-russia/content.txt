                                                <h2><strong>Overview</strong></h2>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>“Troldesh”, aka Encoder.858 or Shade, is a Trojan and a crypto-ransomware variant created in Russia and spread all over the world.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>Troldesh is based on so-called encryptors that encrypt all of the user’s personal data and extort money to decrypt the files. Troldesh encrypts a user’s files with an “.xtbl” extension. Troldesh is spread initially via e-mail spam.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>A distinctive feature of the Troldesh attack is direct communication with the victim. While the most Ransom-Trojan attackers try to hide themselves and avoid any direct contact, Troldesh’s creators provide their victims with an e-mail address. The attackers use this email correspondence to demand a ransom and dictate a payment method.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>In this report you’ll learn about the infection procedure, the primary symptoms, and you will find out how I ended up getting a discount from the hackers.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h2><strong>The Infection Process</strong></h2>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>As mentioned previously, Troldesh is a Trojan which encrypts all the user’s data and demands a ransom in exchange for decryption.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>In my research, I used a malicious sample with this hash downloaded from VirusTotal: <em>a8b27aa4fe7df15a677f9ab9b62764d557525059a9da5f4196f1f15049e2b433</em></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>After execution, Troldesh encrypts all of the user’s data and displays this message:</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>Additionally, it renames the encrypted files using this format: <em>[random characters]=.xbtl</em> For example, this is a screenshot of my machine’s “Pictures” folder with the encrypted files:</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><a href="http://blog.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/06/td1.jpg"><img decoding="async" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-11489" src="https://blog.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/06/td1.jpg" alt="td1" width="710" height="471" srcset="https://blog.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/06/td1.jpg 710w, https://blog.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/06/td1-300x199.jpg 300w" sizes="(max-width: 710px) 100vw, 710px" /></a></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>Approximately 20 <em>txt</em> files were placed on my desktop. In other cases, a <em>txt</em> file was placed in each folder that had an encrypted file.</p>
<p>Each <em>txt</em> file has the filename in the format <em>README[number].txt</em> and looks like this:</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><a href="http://blog.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/06/td2.jpg"><img decoding="async" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-11490" src="https://blog.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/06/td2.jpg" alt="td2" width="650" height="438" srcset="https://blog.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/06/td2.jpg 650w, https://blog.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/06/td2-300x202.jpg 300w" sizes="(max-width: 650px) 100vw, 650px" /></a></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>The user is instructed to send a specified code to the e-mail address provided.</p>
<p>To summarize, a Troldesh infection displays these characteristics:</p>
<ul>
<li>A warning message on the user’s screen</li>
<li>Regular files replaced by the encrypted files with the <em>.xbtl</em> extension</li>
<li>README[number].txt files for information and contact data</li>
<li></li>
</ul>
<p><a href="http://blog.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/06/td3.jpg"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-11491" src="https://blog.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/06/td3.jpg" alt="td3" width="779" height="262" srcset="https://blog.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/06/td3.jpg 779w, https://blog.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/06/td3-300x101.jpg 300w" sizes="(max-width: 779px) 100vw, 779px" /></a></p>
<h2><strong>How I Got a Discount From the Hackers</strong></h2>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>I was very interested to learn more about the ransom and tried to start a correspondence with the attackers. As required, I sent the specified code to the e-mail address provided, one that is registered on the most  famous Russian domain.</p>
<p><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-11492" src="https://blog.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/06/td4.jpg" alt="td4" width="586" height="190" srcset="https://blog.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/06/td4.jpg 586w, https://blog.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/06/td4-300x97.jpg 300w" sizes="(max-width: 586px) 100vw, 586px" /></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>After several minutes I received an answer with my next instructions.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><a href="http://blog.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/06/td6.jpg"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-11493" src="https://blog.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/06/td6.jpg" alt="td6" width="647" height="299" srcset="https://blog.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/06/td6.jpg 647w, https://blog.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/06/td6-300x139.jpg 300w" sizes="(max-width: 647px) 100vw, 647px" /></a></p>
<p>The extortionists said to send them one encrypted file to prove they could decrypt it. They demanded 250 euros to decrypt all of the files.</p>
<p>Something about this transaction bothered me. Was their answer generated automatically or was there a real person on the other end? To find out, I decided to accept the hackers’ “generous” offer and send them an encrypted file for decryption.  At the same time, I tried to start a conversation with them to see whether I could persuade them to give me the key for free, or at least get a decent discount.</p>
<p><a href="http://blog.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/06/td7.jpg"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-11494" src="https://blog.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/06/td7.jpg" alt="td7" width="644" height="268" srcset="https://blog.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/06/td7.jpg 644w, https://blog.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/06/td7-300x125.jpg 300w" sizes="(max-width: 644px) 100vw, 644px" /></a></p>
<p>To my great surprise, after a minute I got an answer from a real person who was open to discussion! Since the answer and all of the following conversation were in Russian, a translation is provided under each screenshot.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><a href="http://blog.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/06/td8.jpg"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-11495" src="https://blog.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/06/td8.jpg" alt="td8" width="670" height="291" srcset="https://blog.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/06/td8.jpg 670w, https://blog.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/06/td8-300x130.jpg 300w" sizes="(max-width: 670px) 100vw, 670px" /></a></p>
<p><em>“The guarantee is our word of honor. You can pay in rubles, 12000 RUB.”</p>
<p></em></p>
<p>I checked the currency exchange rate and saw that I received a discount of approximately 15% (~35 euro). A decrypted version of the encrypted file I sent earlier was attached to the same e-mail.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><a href="http://blog.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/06/td9.jpg"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-11496" src="https://blog.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/06/td9.jpg" alt="td9" width="462" height="323" srcset="https://blog.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/06/td9.jpg 462w, https://blog.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/06/td9-300x210.jpg 300w" sizes="(max-width: 462px) 100vw, 462px" /></a></p>
<p>I continued asking about payment methods and if there was a specific time frame.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><a href="http://blog.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/06/td11.jpg"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-11497" src="https://blog.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/06/td11.jpg" alt="td11" width="664" height="225" srcset="https://blog.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/06/td11.jpg 664w, https://blog.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/06/td11-300x102.jpg 300w" sizes="(max-width: 664px) 100vw, 664px" /></a></p>
<p><em>“How can I pay? I don’t see any requisites. Are there any time frames?”</em></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><a href="http://blog.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/06/td12.jpg"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-11498" src="https://blog.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/06/td12.jpg" alt="td12" width="677" height="241" srcset="https://blog.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/06/td12.jpg 677w, https://blog.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/06/td12-300x107.jpg 300w" sizes="(max-width: 677px) 100vw, 677px" /></a></p>
<p><em>“The payment should be done to the QIWI purse, </em><em>requisites </em><em>are changing frequently</em><em>. As soon as you will be ready to pay, write me, and I’ll send an actual requisites.</em></p>
<p><em>12000 RUB is a sum with discount!<br />
You have only 2 days to pay.”</em></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>I took a break at this point and after almost a week wrote them again. I still had hopes of getting the key for free.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><a href="http://blog.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/06/td13.jpg"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-11499" src="https://blog.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/06/td13.jpg" alt="td13" width="691" height="209" srcset="https://blog.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/06/td13.jpg 691w, https://blog.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/06/td13-300x91.jpg 300w" sizes="(max-width: 691px) 100vw, 691px" /></a></p>
<p><em>“I ask you: please, return my data – this is almost all of my life for the last several years!</em></p>
<p><em>I really don’t have much money to pay you!</em></p>
<p><em>Be humane!!!”</em></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><a href="http://blog.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/06/td14.jpg"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-11500" src="https://blog.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/06/td14.jpg" alt="td14" width="688" height="272" srcset="https://blog.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/06/td14.jpg 688w, https://blog.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/06/td14-300x119.jpg 300w" sizes="(max-width: 688px) 100vw, 688px" /></a></p>
<p><em>“The best I can do is to bargain”</em></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><a href="http://blog.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/06/td15.jpg"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-11501" src="https://blog.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/06/td15.jpg" alt="td15" width="686" height="250" srcset="https://blog.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/06/td15.jpg 686w, https://blog.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/06/td15-300x109.jpg 300w" sizes="(max-width: 686px) 100vw, 686px" /></a></p>
<p><em>“Please send me the key.</em></p>
<p><em>Anyway, I can’t pay, neither with bargain, nor without it. Even one thousand rubles is a big sum for me.</em></p>
<p><em>The case in which I’ll lose all of my personal and work(!) files will not make your life easier…”</em></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><a href="http://blog.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/06/td16.jpg"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-11502" src="https://blog.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/06/td16.jpg" alt="td16" width="683" height="211" srcset="https://blog.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/06/td16.jpg 683w, https://blog.checkpoint.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/06/td16-300x93.jpg 300w" sizes="(max-width: 683px) 100vw, 683px" /></a></p>
<p><em>“7000 is a minimal cost for you</em></p>
<p><em>Decide for yourself</em></p>
<p><em>There is no way to get the key for free”</em></p>
<p>By the end of our correspondence, I managed to get a discount of 50%. Perhaps if I had continued bargaining, I could have gotten an even bigger discount.</p>
