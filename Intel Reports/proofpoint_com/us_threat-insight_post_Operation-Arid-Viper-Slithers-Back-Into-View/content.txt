    <div class="node-full__body blog-content__body">  <p>Earlier this year, researchers published analyses of targeted attack known as Operation Arid Viper <a href="http://www.trendmicro.com/cloud-content/us/pdfs/security-intelligence/white-papers/wp-operation-arid-viper.pdf">[1]</a> (aka Desert Falcons <a href="https://securelist.com/blog/research/68817/the-desert-falcons-targeted-attacks/">[2]</a>, aka DHS) directed primarily at organizations in the Middle East. Delivering a backdoor and spyware, this campaign was designed to steal information from infected systems using a malware client capable of filtering out “uninteresting” files, and spread primarily via a targeted phishing email usually promising a pornographic video.</p>

<p>The infection chain described in the initial analyses was fairly straightforward: To access the video content, the recipient had to open an attached RAR archive file – or less frequently, click a link to a RAR – that extracted an <a href="https://blog.malwarebytes.org/fraud-scam/2014/11/rogue-scr-file-links-circulating-in-steam-chat/">SCR (Windows screensaver)</a> file, which in turn drops two files: a malicious EXE with the name of a legitimate file (such as “skype.exe”), and a video format file, usually FLV or MPG.</p>

<p>Despite the apparent severity and extent of this threat, little has been written about it in the intervening months, and the operation appeared to be dormant. However, Proofpoint researchers recently intercepted and analyzed phishing emails distributing Arid Viper malware payloads with some noteworthy updates.</p>

<p>As with the originally documented examples, these messages were part of narrow campaigns targeting specific industry verticals: telecoms, high tech, and business services, primarily in Israel.</p>

<p>In these samples, the spear-phishing email contained a link to a RAR file hosted on MediaFire – no attachments were observed. Instead of a pornographic video, the actors showed a change in TTP by using as lure a video of a fiery automobile accident.</p>

<p>התאונה בכביש // Road accident</p>

<p>וידיו מזעזע דלפ תקשורת // And his shocking leaked to the media</p>

<p><img alt="Example network traffic during data exfiltration" height="265" src="https://www.proofpoint.com/sites/default/files/arid-viper-slithers-6.png?itok=9FK6YfYO" width="480" loading="lazy"></p>

<p>Although the data that is exfiltrated and the manner in which it is gathered remain largely the same as in previously documented versions, the final result that is transmitted to an attacker-controlled server has changed significantly. In an older version of this backdoor (md5: aefea9d795624da16d878dc9bb81bf87), exfiltrated data was simply base64-encoded using a slightly modified base64 alphabet (“-” instead of “+”). In the newer version, prior to base64 encoding the exfiltrated data is first encrypted with AES-256 in CBC mode. The encryption process is depicted in Figure 5 and explained below.</p>

<p><img alt="Arid Viper encryption process for data exfiltration" height="319" src="https://www.proofpoint.com/sites/default/files/arid-viper-slithers-7.png?itok=9uBStUML" width="480" loading="lazy"></p>

<p><em>Figure 5: Arid Viper encryption process for data exfiltration</em></p>

<p>To generate the key/IV pair, first the malware randomly generates 60 bytes of data. From this, the first 32-bytes are used for the key, the next byte is skipped, and the following 16 bytes are used for the IV. After encryption, the key, separator byte, IV, leftover bytes and padding are then encoded into a 512-byte block of data and prepended to the encrypted data. The encoded key/IV and encrypted data are then base64-encoded using the same modified alphabet. Just like in the older version, this data is then appended to the final variable in the POST’s HTTP client body and sent to an attacker-controlled server.</p>

<p><strong>Reinventing the wheel</strong></p>

<p>Numerous examples over the years have served to remind us that designing your own cryptography implementation is difficult and usually ill-advised. The authors of the updated Arid Viper backdoor seem to have overlooked this lesson for, although certain measures have been taken to protect the generated secret keys and IVs, their implementation is susceptible to a brute force attack, often capable of finding the correct key/IV combination in less than one second. Cracking the encryption scheme applied to the traffic in Figure 4 reveals the following decrypted cleartext. (Fig. 6)</p>

<p><img alt="Decrypted data network traffic" height="135" src="https://www.proofpoint.com/sites/default/files/arid-viper-slithers-8.png?itok=A2qfZm_j" width="480" loading="lazy"></p>

<p><em>Figure 6: Decrypted data from example network traffic in Figure 4</em></p>

<p>Determining the encryption scheme that is applied to the updated Arid Viper backdoor’s exfiltrated data enables us to better detect C2 communication while also rapidly determining the extent and impact of the data breach carried out by the malware client.</p>

<p><strong>Conclusion</strong></p>

<p>In summary, this update to Operation Arid Viper demonstrates that despite its relatively low profile since February the Arid Viper / Desert Falcons threat still has teeth and remains a risk for organizations in Israel and elsewhere. While the overall attack profile observed in recent examples remains similar to the originally documented campaigns, the recent campaigns exhibit several important updates:&nbsp;</p>

<ul>
	<li>Use of links instead of attachments</li>
	<li>New lures: still using pornographic video but most recent detections also included lures for auto accident footage</li>
	<li>New executable name: originally reported using “skype.exe” (and variations on “skype”), the recent samples used “chrome.exe”</li>
	<li>New C2 domains</li>
	<li>Added encryption for exfiltrated data</li>
</ul>

<p>&nbsp;</p>

<p>The return of Operation Arid Viper shows that targeted attacks can remain a threat even – and especially – when they are no longer in the headlines,&nbsp;</p>
