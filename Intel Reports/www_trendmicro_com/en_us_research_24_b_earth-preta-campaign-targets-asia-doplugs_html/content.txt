			<h1><span class="body-subhead-title">Introduction</span></h1>
<p>In July 2023, Check Point <a href="https://research.checkpoint.com/2023/chinese-threat-actors-targeting-europe-in-smugx-campaign/">disclosed</a> a campaign called SMUGX, which focused on European countries and was attributed to the advanced persistent threat (APT) group <a href="/en_us/research/23/f/behind-the-scenes-unveiling-the-hidden-workings-of-earth-preta.html">Earth Preta</a> (also known as Mustang Panda and Bronze President). In the same year, we obtained a phishing email targeting the Taiwanese government that contained a piece of customized PlugX malware — the same one used in the SMUGX campaign. As most previous discussions from other researchers focus on the European attacks, we would instead like to shed light on the Asian side of the campaign. After months of investigation, we discovered more SMUGX campaign-related samples targeting not only Taiwan, but also Vietnam, Malaysia, and other Asian countries in 2022 and 2023.</p>
<p>This kind of customized PlugX malware has been active since 2022, with related research being published by <a href="https://www.secureworks.com/blog/bronze-president-targets-government-officials">Secureworks</a>, <a href="https://go.recordedfuture.com/hubfs/reports/cta-2022-1223.pdf">Recorded Future</a>, <a href="https://research.checkpoint.com/2023/chinese-threat-actors-targeting-europe-in-smugx-campaign/">Check Point</a>, and <a href="https://lab52.io/blog/mustang-pandas-plugx-new-variant-targetting-taiwanese-government-and-diplomats/">Lab52</a>. During analysis, we observed that the piece of customized PlugX malware is dissimilar to the <a href="https://st.drweb.com/static/new-www/news/2020/july/Study_of_the_APT_attacks_on_state_institutions_in_Kazakhstan_and_Kyrgyzstan_en.pdf">general type</a> of the PlugX malware that contains a completed backdoor command module, and that the former is only used for downloading the latter. Due to its different functionality, we decided to give this piece of customized PlugX malware a new name: DOPLUGS.</p>
<p>Upon investigation, we found that the DOPLUGS malware uses the KillSomeOne module, a USB worm that was first disclosed by a <a href="https://news.sophos.com/en-us/2020/11/04/a-new-apt-uses-dll-side-loads-to-killlsomeone/">Sophos report</a> in November 2020. However, <a href="https://www.avira.com/en/blog/new-wave-of-plugx-targets-hong-kong">an entry</a> from January 2020 mentioned a USB worm; this entry was also the first report that analyzed a piece of PlugX malware integrated with KillSomeOne behavior.</p>
<p>In this blog entry, we focus on the Earth Preta campaign, providing an analysis of the DOPLUGS malware variant that the group used, including backdoor command behavior, integration with the KillSomeOne module, and its evolution.</p>
<h1><span class="body-subhead-title">Decoys and victims</span></h1>
<p>Based on noteworthy DOPLUGS files we’ve found since July 2023 (Table 1), we can determine that the victims, at least for the attacks that employed these specific samples, are from Taiwan and Mongolia. Based on the file names, it seems the files used for social engineering were related to current events, such as the Taiwanese presidential election that occurred in January 2024.</p>

<h3><span class="body-subhead-title"><span class="rte-sub-menu-text">Backdoor behavior</span></span><b></b></h3>
<p>Since 2018, Earth Preta has constantly updated the backdoor command sets in the PlugX malware, which has at least four generations according to our observations:</p>

<h2><span class="body-subhead-title">Integration with KillSomeOne</span></h2>
<p>While hunting for more DOPLUGS related samples, we came across a DOPLUGS variant with KillSomeOne functionality. The KillSomeOne module is a plug-in specializing in malware distribution, information collection, and document theft via USB. It expands the ability for infection so that initial access methods are not limited to phishing or decoy documents.</p>
<p>The KillSomeOne module was first introduced in a November 2020 Sophos <a href="https://news.sophos.com/en-us/2020/11/04/a-new-apt-uses-dll-side-loads-to-killlsomeone/">report</a>. The DOPLUGS variant with the KillSomeOne module has high similarities with the previous DOPLUGS variant we analyzed, with one of the major differences being the infection method. It has four components: a legitimate executable, a malicious DLL, an encrypted payload, and an encrypted PE file. This variant has an extra launcher file that executes the legitimate executable to perform DLL-sideloading behaviors.</p>

<p>The second behavior is related to USB infection. It applies the API<i> <b>DeviceIoControl</b></i>  with the parameter <i>0x2d1400</i> to identify the USB drive. It then creates three threads in the targeted USB drive, which we detail in the following sections.</p>
<h5>Thread 1: Worm behavior in USB drive (Lateral Movement)</h5>
<p>This thread creates the mutex <i>USB_NOTIFY3_INF_{USB_volume}</i> for mark. Before the worm behavior, these registries are enabled to hide the file extension and the folders that contain malware and stolen documents.</p>

<h5><span class="body-subhead-title"><span class="rte-sub-menu-text">Thread 2: Information or file stealer (Collection)</span></span></h5>
<p>This thread creates the mutex, <i>USB_NOTIFY3_COP_{USB_volume}</i>, for mark. There are two kinds of stealing conditions, each of which we discuss here:</p>
<h6><span class="body-subhead-title"><span class="rte-sub-menu-text">First condition: Steals the document files</span></span><b></b></h6>
<p>If the connection succeeds in connecting to<i> <a href="https://www.microsoft.com/">https://www.microsoft.com/</a></i>, it will check the file extensions in these predefined folders:</p>
<ul>
<li><span class="rte-red-bullet"><i>{USB_volume}:\Kaspersky\Usb Drive\1.0\</i></span></li>
<li><span class="rte-red-bullet"><i>{USB_volume}:\Usb Drive\1.0\</i></span></li>
<li><span class="rte-red-bullet"><i>{USB_volume}:\.System\Device\USB\3.0\Kaspersky\Usb Drive\1.0</i></span></li>
<li><span class="rte-red-bullet"><i>{USB_volume}:\.System\Device\USB\3.0\Usb Drive\1.0\</i></span></li>
</ul>
<p>If the file extensions are not .<i>cmd</i>, .<i>bat</i>, or .<i>dll</i> and the file name is not <i>RECYCLERS.BIN</i>, it will transfer the file to <i>%userprofile%\AppData\Roaming\Render\1.0\</i>  and empty the content of the original file.</p>
<p>We also found another functionality, but it seems that it has not been implemented as of this writing. This functionality collects all files under the same folders and looks for the files with the following extensions:</p>
<ul>
<li><span class="rte-red-bullet">.doc</span></li>
<li><span class="rte-red-bullet">.docx</span></li>
<li><span class="rte-red-bullet">.ppt</span></li>
<li><span class="rte-red-bullet">.pptx</span></li>
<li><span class="rte-red-bullet"> .xls</span></li>
<li><span class="rte-red-bullet">.xlsx</span></li>
<li><span class="rte-red-bullet">.pdf</span></li>
</ul>
<p>Afterward, it will encode the file name with base64, encrypt the file content, and copy the file to the folder of the current process.</p>
<p>Here is the XOR algorithm to encrypt the stolen files:</p>
<p><span class="blockquote">encrypted_contents = []<br />
 <br />
 encrypted_key = 0x6D<br />
 <br />
 for i in range(len(contents)):<br />
 <br />
     encrypted_contents.append(contents[i] ^ encrypted_key)<br />
 <br />
     encrypted_key += 0xAA</span></p>
<h6><span class="body-subhead-title"><span class="rte-sub-menu-text">Second condition: Steals victim information</span></span><b></b></h6>
<p>If the connection fails, the thread checks the value in registry <i>(HKCU|HKLM)\System\CurrentControlSet\Control\Network\Version,</i><b> </b>which does not exist. Afterward, it creates and executes the batch script <i>%temp%\edg{value of QueryPerformanceCounter}.bat</i> to collect the information of the victim.</p>
<p><span class="blockquote">%comspec% /q /c systeminfo &gt;&quot;%~dp0AE353BBEB1C6603E_E.dat&quot;<br />
 <br />
 %comspec% /q /c ipconfig /all &gt;&gt;&quot;%~dp0AE353BBEB1C6603E_E.dat&quot;<br />
 <br />
 %comspec% /q /c netstat -ano &gt;&gt;&quot;%~dp0AE353BBEB1C6603E_E.dat&quot;<br />
 <br />
 %comspec% /q /c arp -a &gt;&gt;&quot;%~dp0AE353BBEB1C6603E_E.dat&quot;<br />
 <br />
 %comspec% /q /c tasklist /v &gt;&gt;&quot;%~dp0AE353BBEB1C6603E_E.dat&quot;<br />
 <br />
 del %0</span></p>
<p>The output data will then be encrypted and dropped <i>to {USB_volume}:\Usb Drive\1.0\ {value of SOFTWARE\CLASSES\ms-pu\CLSID}.dat</i>.</p>
<h5><span class="body-subhead-title"><span class="rte-sub-menu-text">Thread 3: Execute encrypted batch script</span></span></h5>
<p>This thread creates the mutex, <i>USB_NOTIFY_BAT_H3_{USB_volume}</i><b> </b>for mark, which will be executed only under these conditions:</p>
<ul>
<li><span class="rte-red-bullet">When connection with <i>https://www.microsoft.com</i> fails</span></li>
<li><span class="rte-red-bullet">When there is no value in <i>System\\CurrentControlSet\\Control\\Network\\version</i> (this registry is enabled when argument of cmd line = “-net”)</span></li>
</ul>
<p>The thread will search all batch scripts inside the following folders:</p>
<ul>
<li><span class="rte-red-bullet"><i>{USB_volume}:\Usb Drive\1.0\p\</i></span></li>
<li><span class="rte-red-bullet"><i>{USB_volume}:\Kaspersky\Usb Drive\1.0\p\</i></span></li>
<li><span class="rte-red-bullet"><i>{USB_volume}:\.System\Device\USB\3.0\Usb Drive\1.0\p\</i></span></li>
</ul>
<p>If the batch script name does not contain the strings <i>tmpc_</i> or <i>tmp_</i>, the script will be decrypted via XOR algorithm, which is the same as the file encryption in the thread 2 subsection. The new batch will then be created in <i>%temp%\{value of QueryPerformanceCounter}.bat</i> and executed by <i>ShellExecuteW</i> with the following contents:</p>
<p><span class="blockquote">{USB_volume}<br />
 cd &quot;{USB_volume}:\target folder\&quot;<br />
 {decrypted contents in batch file}<br />
 del %0</span></p>
<h4>DOPLUGS backdoor behavior (Command and Control)</h4>

<p>Upon checking backdoor commands of these PlugX malware types, we found an additional variant that serves as the transition from DOPLUGS to Hodur. This version keeps the disk module of the general type of the PlugX malware, although here the customized backdoor command is modified to the improved DOPLUGS type (unlike the original DOPLUGS variant without any module from the general type of the PlugX malware). Another impressive feature is that the KillSomeOne + Hodur variant has two categories of C&amp;C servers for communication: the first one as a regular C&amp;C server to receive backdoor commands, while the second one is designed to download payloads for process injection in <i>svchost.exe</i>.</p>
<h1><span class="body-subhead-title">Conclusion</span></h1>
<p>Earth Preta has primarily focused on targeting government entities worldwide, particularly within the Asia-Pacific region and Europe. Based on our observations, we believe Earth Preta tends to use spear-phishing emails and Google Drive links in its attacks.</p>
<p>We explained the purpose of the DOPLUGS malware (which we believe has been in use since 2022), one of the primary tools Earth Preta uses to download the general type of the PlugX malware. While hunting for other samples, we discovered a DOPLUGS variant that has KillSomeOne module integration and that can be traced back to 2018. This shows that Earth Preta has been refining its tools for some time now, constantly adding new functionalities and features.  </p>
<p>Over the course of our investigations into Earth Preta’s activities, we have observed that the group remains highly active, particularly in Europe and Asia. It is likely that we will hear more from this group in the future, so it is a good idea for security teams to familiarize themselves with how Earth Preta operates.</p>
