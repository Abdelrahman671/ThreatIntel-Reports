<h2>Summary</h2>
<p>This report delves into Doppelgänger information operations conducted by Russian actors, focusing on their activities from early June to late-July 2024. Our investigation was motivated by the unexpected snap general election in France, prompting a closer look at Doppelgänger activities during this period.</p>
<p>While recent activities have been described since<sup id="fnref1_rflast"><a class="footnote-ref" href="#fn_rflast">1</a>,</sup><sup id="fnref1_quriumlast"><a class="footnote-ref" href="#fn_quriumlast">2</a></sup>, our first dive into the information operations topic offers a complementary threat-intelligence analysts&#8217; perspective on the matter, brings additional knowledge on associated infrastructure, tactics and motivation in Europe and the United States.</p>
<h2>Background</h2>
<p>Doppelgänger designates a set of information manipulation operations conducted by Russian actors that leverage social networks and digital media to support Russia&#8217;s interests <sup id="fnref1_maefr"><a class="footnote-ref" href="#fn_maefr">3</a></sup>. The most notable characteristic of these operations is the impersonation of popular news websites to spread disinformation. The term &#8220;Doppelgänger&#8221; is also indiscriminately used in public research to designate the actors, operators and institutions that contribute to these operations.</p>
<p>Since the first public report in 2022<sup id="fnref1_firstref"><a class="footnote-ref" href="#fn_firstref">4</a></sup>, Doppelgänger<sup id="fnref1_dopelprime"><a class="footnote-ref" href="#fn_dopelprime">5</a></sup> has been vastly and publicly described by government organisations<sup id="fnref1_viginumref"><a class="footnote-ref" href="#fn_viginumref">6</a>,</sup><sup id="fnref1_amtderef"><a class="footnote-ref" href="#fn_amtderef">7</a></sup> and private companies<sup id="fnref1_fbcib"><a class="footnote-ref" href="#fn_fbcib">8</a>,</sup><sup id="fnref1_rffirst"><a class="footnote-ref" href="#fn_rffirst">9</a>,</sup><sup id="fnref1_sekoiaref"><a class="footnote-ref" href="#fn_sekoiaref">10</a></sup>.</p>
<p>As a result, and while Doppelgänger operations continue, government organisations, private companies and the civil society can now identify and inform about these operations more rapidly<sup id="fnref2_rflast"><a class="footnote-ref" href="#fn_rflast">1</a></sup><sup id="fnref2_quriumlast"><a class="footnote-ref" href="#fn_quriumlast">2</a></sup>.</p>
<p>Given HarfangLab is based in Europe (France), we were particularly attentive to possible Doppelgänger campaigns during France&#8217;s unexpected June 2024 snap general election.</p>
<h2>(Dis)information chain</h2>
<p>Doppelgänger spreads and amplifies fabricated, fake or even legitimate information across social networks. To do so, social networks accounts post links that initiate an obfuscated chain of redirections leading to final content websites (see Fig. 1).</p>

<p>The overall content distribution approach that we could observe between early June and late-July 2024 has remained consistent, despite continuous rotation of associated infrastructure assets by the operators.</p>
<h3>X/Twitter posts</h3>
<p>Social media appears to be the primary dissemination means for disinformation. We surveiled activity on X/Twitter and were able to identify about 800 acounts which posted links to 1st level redirectors (see Fig. 1). These accounts do not appear to belong to real individuals, may be described as &#8220;bots&#8221;, and often present themselves as Cryptocurrency / Web3 influencers. Based on our observations, the dissemination process exhibits the following characteristics:</p>
<ul>
	<li>Bot posts (see Fig. 2) are unique, possibly AI-generated. Comments never seem to repeat themselves across all bot activity, and use several languages (notably English, French, German, Polish and Ukrainian).</li>
	<li>Different bots post different links to 1st level redirectors.</li>
	<li>All post have abnormally high numbers of engagements compared to each account&#8217;s follower base, making it almost certain that the bot network also artificially inflates interaction metrics.</li>
	<li>It&#8217;s unclear whether these posts are ever able to burst out of the echoing chamber that is the bot network and reach the timeline of real user accounts.</li>
</ul>
<p>Looking at past activities from the bot accounts, it seems like they were used in the context of cryptocurrency scams (i.e, pump and dump schemes, airdrops, etc.). This leads us to believe that Doppelgänger operators are either renting a fleet of Twitter bots from a third party, or that some of their members are also moonlighting as cybercriminals.</p>

<p>While our research focused on X/Twitter, previous reports shared by Meta<sup id="fnref2_fbcib"><a class="footnote-ref" href="#fn_fbcib">8</a></sup> showed that Doppelgänger abused other platforms to spread their content.</p>
<h3>Redirection chain</h3>
<p>In a likely attempt to hinder research and conceal infrastructure, the information flow from social network posts to final content goes through an intricate redirection chain (see Fig. 1). The redirections are imperceptible for the targeted audience: clicking a link transparently leads to the final content website anyway &#8211; but those are making the Doppelgänger infrastructure difficult to identify in real-time and at scale.</p>
<h4>1st level redirectors</h4>
<p>1st level redirectors are a conceptual redirection block comprised of URLs (possibly from pooled domain names) that point to custom redirecting Web pages (on possibly pooled Web servers). Below is an example of such Web page content, as pointed to by <code>hxxps://g5hp0[.]top/i9ro8l</code> on July 17, 2024:</p>
<pre><code class="language-htmlbars">&lt;!DOCTYPE html&gt;
&lt;html&gt;
&lt;head&gt;
&lt;title&gt;Citizenship Doesn't Matter If You Support Biden&lt;/title&gt;
&lt;meta http-equiv="Content-Type" content="text/html; charset=utf-8"&gt;
&lt;meta name="twitter:card" content="summary_large_image"&gt;
&lt;meta property="og:title" content="Citizenship Doesn't Matter If You Support Biden"/&gt;

<h4>Tracker</h4>
<p>The Tracker is the cornerstone of the redirection chain. It is comprised of a domain name, as well as a server hosting an instance of the Keitaro<sup id="fnref1_keitaro"><a class="footnote-ref" href="#fn_keitaro">13</a></sup> platform &#8211; which is described as a tool for content publishers and advertisers, aimed at flexibly handling and analysing Web traffic.</p>
<p>A Tracker server seemingly lasts way longer than 1st and 2nd level redirectors &#8211; the latter being rotated quite frequently, while a single Tracker server can be used for months. It delivers several Javascript files, organised by campaigns, which ultimately redirect the current page to final content websites. Tracker servers are protected behind Cloudflare&#8217;s reverse-proxy, as a light infrastructure concealement technique.</p>

<h2>Final content analysis and targets</h2>
<p>Information manipulation operations are designed to manipulate public opinion to achieve strategic objectives. The most common goals of these campaigns include political influence, social manipulation, eroding public trust in the media, and general destabilization. The timing and content of the Doppelgänger activities indeed make their intentions apparent, actively targeting the public opinion, particularly in the lead-up to elections.</p>
<h3>Political analysis</h3>
<p>We analyzed over 200 of Doppelgänger&#8217;s latest publications from May to July. To conduct this analysis, we employed a large language model (LLM) designed for extensive text analysis and reasoning &#8211; the &#8220;LLaMA-3-Sonar-Large-32k-online&#8221;<sup id="fnref1_LLM"><a class="footnote-ref" href="#fn_LLM">15</a></sup>, to read and score the articles for their political leanings using a political compass<sup id="fnref1_polcompass"><a class="footnote-ref" href="#fn_polcompass">16</a></sup>.</p>
<p>The LLM was tasked with reading the articles and scoring them based on their political leanings. This analysis began with providing an agenda assessment, identifying the primary goal of the content and determining whether it aimed to influence political opinions or promote a specific ideology. The model then evaluated and categorized the political-economic stance of each article, ranging from &#8220;far left&#8221; to &#8220;far right&#8221;. Far-left positions typically advocate for a socialist or communist society, rejecting capitalism and often promoting radical transformation of the existing socio-economic order, while far-right positions emphasize nativism and authoritarianism<sup id="fnref1_populist"><a class="footnote-ref" href="#fn_populist">17</a></sup>.</p>
<p>Additionally, the LLM was directed to assess the &#8220;social score&#8221; of each article, ranging from Libertarian to Authoritarian. This social axis evaluates the article&#8217;s stance on personal freedoms, government control over social issues, and individual rights, with Libertarians advocating for maximum personal freedom and minimal government intervention, while Authoritarians support greater government control.</p>
<p>Finally, the LLM was also tasked with identifying the country of focus within each article.</p>

<h3>Agenda</h3>
<p>Ukraine emerges as the most discussed country, with topics ranging from its handling of conscription, corruption, military capabilities, and readiness for EU membership, often portraying Ukraine&#8217;s governance and policies negatively. France is critiqued for its preparedness for the 2024 Paris Olympics, political unrest, and immigration policies. Germany&#8217;s handling of Ukrainian refugees, energy plans, COVID-19 measures, and defense spending are scrutinized. The United States is criticized for its foreign policy and military readiness under Biden, with support for a more assertive approach and transparent election processes. Poland&#8217;s economic policies under Donald Tusk are criticized as misguided and leading the country to bankruptcy. Israel&#8217;s political maneuvering and humanitarian aid are discussed, with a focus on Netanyahu&#8217;s policies. And last but not least, Russia&#8217;s media regulations and censorship are condemned, advocating for freedom of speech.</p>
<p>The articles generally take a stance critical of mainstream Western policies, often supporting more nationalist or conservative viewpoints. There&#8217;s a tendency to question the actions of the US, NATO, and the EU while sometimes presenting Russia in a more favorable light. The content frequently highlights internal problems within Western countries and criticizes their involvement in foreign conflicts, particularly in Ukraine. Many articles focus on corruption, governance issues, and economic challenges in various countries.</p>
<h3>Targets</h3>
<p>Our analysis reveals a hierarchy in Doppelgänger&#8217;s geographic focus. France emerges as the primary &#8216;target&#8217; and subject of disccussion, followed closely by Germany, the United States and Ukraine. Judging from the recent articles we analyzed, these four countries dominate the narrative landscape, reflecting the current geopolitical dynamics and aligning with temporal trends.</p>
<p>To a lesser extent, Doppelgänger also targets Israel, Poland and Taiwan, indicating a broader, albeit less intense, interest in these regions. Hungary and Switzerland are also mentioned, in relation to Ukraine.</p>
<p>As far as the contents of the articles are concerned, several recurring themes and patterns emerge. The most frequently discussed topics include the Ukraine-Russia conflict, European Union politics, NATO and military affairs, immigration issues, US politics and foreign policy, and various national political situations, particularly in France and Germany.</p>

<p>Converting the political compass data into a heatmap reveals a clear pattern to Doppelgänger articles. The most prominent concentration is observed in the right-wing authoritarian quadrant, indicating a significant bias towards conservative economic policies coupled with socially restrictive viewpoints. Interestingly, there&#8217;s a secondary, although less intense, concentration in the left-libertarian section of the compass.</p>
<p>This dual-focus strategy suggests a nuanced approach to disinformation, aiming to appeal to (or manipulate) diverse political demographics. The presence in opposing quadrants could be an attempt to sow discord across the political spectrum and generally worsen existing divisions.</p>
<p>The scattered spread across other areas of the compass indicates that the group doesn&#8217;t exclusively target the aforementioned stances, but rather adapts its content to cover a range of perspectives, as deemed appropriate, to maximize their reach and impact.</p>
<p>This pattern in the distribution of Doppelgänger articles lends itself to an interpretation through the lens of the horseshoe theory.</p>
<h3>From the horse&#8217;s mouth</h3>
<p>The horseshoe theory<sup id="fnref1_big_pony"><a class="footnote-ref" href="#fn_big_pony">18</a></sup> posits that the far-left and far-right, rather than being at opposite ends of a linear political spectrum, share similarities and can be closer to each other than to the political center.</p>

<p>In the context of information warfare, the horseshoe approach supports Doppelgänger&#8217;s objective of destabilizing target countries by creating artificial discourse. This is achieved by promoting seemingly opposing viewpoints that, in reality, advance the same foreign interests.</p>
<p>It&#8217;s important to note, however, that while the horseshoe theory provides an interesting framework for interpreting the group&#8217;s strategies, it remains controversial and not widely accepted.</p>
<p>Overall, the different mappings show Doppelgänger has a bias towards conservative economic policies coupled with socially restrictive viewpoints, aligning with the group&#8217;s known tactics of exploiting nationalist and traditionalist sentiments. However, rather than adhering strictly to one ideological stance, Doppelgänger also attemps to appeal to left-libertarian viewpoints, although to a lesser extent.</p>

<h2>Doppelgänger amplifies CopyCop</h2>
<p>While collecting Doppelgänger final content URLs, we noticed that Doppelgänger sometimes amplified fake news that were originally published outside of the Doppelgänger&#8217;s scope.</p>

<h2>Conclusion</h2>
<p>Our analysis of Doppelgänger&#8217;s recent information operations reveals a persistent, convoluted and relatively large effort by Russian actors to make room for a foreign narrative in Europe and the United States. This is most likely done to help manipulate public opinion. These operations primarily involve impersonation of legitimate news websites and dissemination of disinformation through a network of bot accounts on social media platforms, notably X/Twitter. The actor&#8217;s dynamic and obfuscated infrastructure alongside the intricate redirection chain makes real-time detection and mitigation challenging.</p>
<p>Our findings indicate that Doppelgänger operations are adaptive, likely leveraging rented or shared cybercrime infrastructure. The political analysis of Doppelgänger&#8217;s content shows a clear bias towards conservative and nationalist viewpoints, with a significant focus on destabilizing Western democracies by exploiting existing societal and political divisions.</p>
<p>As far as future developments are concerned, we discovered days before this publication an AI-generated music video shared by Doppelgänger operators, which indicates that they&#8217;re still in the process of ramping up their efforts and attempting to reach virality through the production of meme-compatible material.</p>
<p>The amplification of content from other disinformation campaigns (i.e., CopyCop) further highlights the interconnected nature of these operations. Despite the challenges in measuring the real-world impact of such campaigns, the continuous and persistent nature of Doppelgänger activities certainly succeed in supporting a foreign narrative and disseminating fabricated news, as well as underscores the need for robust countermeasures and international cooperation to safeguard democratic processes.</p>
