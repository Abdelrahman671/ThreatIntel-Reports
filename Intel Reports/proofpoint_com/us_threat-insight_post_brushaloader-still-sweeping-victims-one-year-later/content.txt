<h1 class="blog-banner__heading">
      <span>BrushaLoader still sweeping up victims one year later</span>

  </h1>

    </div>
  </div>

  <div class="blog-content">
    <div class="blog-content__sharethis sharethis_toolbox sharethis_32x32_style">
      <div class="blog-content__sharethis_label sharethis__label">Share with your network!</div>
      <div class="blog-content__sharethis_buttons sharethis_buttons">
        <div class="sharethis-inline-share-buttons"></div>
        
<span class="addthis_button_subscribe at300b UNCONVERTED" title=Subscribe>
  <span class="at-icon-wrapper block-subscribe-button__trigger block-subscribe-button__addthis"></span>
</span>


      </div>
    </div>
        <div class="blog-content__metadata blog-content__metadata-author">
      <span class="blog-content__date">  <time datetime="2019-07-22T13:00:00Z">July 22, 2019</time>

</span>
        <span class="blog-content__author">
                Kafeine and the Proofpoint Threat Insight Team
  
        </span>
    </div>
    <div class="node-full__body blog-content__body">  <p><strong>Overview</strong></p>

<p>BrushaLoader is one of a growing group of downloaders frequently employed by threat actors to profile infected PCs and then load more robust payloads on devices of interest. Malware like BrushaLoader contributes to the ongoing trend of “quality over quantity” infections and enables threat actors to better stay under the radar than they can with highly disruptive infections like ransomware or when distributing massive malicious spam campaigns with high-profile malware as their primary payload. At the same time, these loaders can also deliver those same disruptive infections if threat actors choose to load ransomware as secondary payloads, a scenario we have observed on multiple occasions recently.</p>

<p>BrushaLoader itself first appeared in June 2018 [1]. Now, just over a year later, we have observed the loader in a number of campaigns by prominent threat actors. We derived the name for this VisualBasic/JavaScript/PowerShell loader from the “Rusha” author of the command and control (C&amp;C) panel.</p>

<p><img alt="BrushaLoader C&amp;C panel: 'Copyright' section" src="/sites/default/files/blf1.png"></p>

<p><em>Figure 1: BrushaLoader C&amp;C panel: "Copyright" section </em></p>

<p><strong>Analysis</strong></p>

<p>Immediately after executing, BrushaLoader receives a PowerShell script called "PowerEnum" [5] (Figure 2).</p>

<p><img alt="HTTP portion of BrushaLoader delivery and post-infection activity" src="/sites/default/files/blf2.png"></p>

<p><em>Figure 2: HTTP portion of BrushaLoader delivery and post-infection activity (PowerEnum activity is not illustrated here); captured February 7, 2019</em></p>

<p>PowerEnum performs extensive fingerprinting on infected devices and sends the data back to the C&amp;C. This communication occurs over a raw TCP "parallel" channel to BrushaLoader. PowerEnum is also used to send tasks, which were originally stored on Dropbox [2][3], and more recently were hosted on Google Drive [4].PowerEnum is integral to BrushaLoader and shares the same C&amp;C infrastructure. Interestingly, we also observed PowerEnum as a Fallout EK payload delivering Danabot Affid "4" (Figure 3)</p>

<p><img alt="Fallout EK dropping PowerEnum" src="/sites/default/files/blf3.png"></p>

<p><em>Figure 3: Fallout EK dropping PowerEnum, which has been observed instructing the download of Danabot Affid 4 and a BackConnect Socks.dll</em></p>

<p><strong>Payloads</strong></p>

<p>BrushaLoader is strongly connected to the <a href="https://www.proofpoint.com/us/threat-insight/post/danabot-new-banking-trojan-surfaces-down-under-0">Danabot banking Trojan</a> Affid "3". However, this connection is not exclusive as we have observed it in conjunction with other malware as well (Figure 4).</p>

<p><img alt="Selection of documented campaigns involving BrushaLoader over the last year" src="/sites/default/files/blf4.png" style="border-width: 0px; border-style: solid;"></p>

<p><em>Figure 4: A selection of documented campaigns involving BrushaLoader over the last year</em></p>

<p>Figure 4 illustrates a number of noteworthy events:</p>

<ul>
	<li>Unusual Payload:
	<ul>
		<li>Ursnif in Italy</li>
		<li>Gootkit in Canada</li>
		<li>Nymaim in Poland</li>
	</ul>
	</li>
	<li>Unusual Spreading:
	<ul>
		<li>TA544 [6], also known as Narwhal Spider [7] on May 14, 2019, in a T-Mobile-themed campaign</li>
	</ul>
	</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>The C&amp;C panel</strong></p>

<p>Early in its distribution, we observed the BrushaLoader C&amp;C panel and were surprised by the success of a “basic” campaign using compressed-VBS attachments. Despite requiring several user interactions, the actors were able to ensnare more than 4,000 computers in 36 hours (Figures 5 and 6).</p>

<p><img alt="BrushaLoader C&amp;C panel" src="/sites/default/files/blf5.png" style="border-width: 0px; border-style: solid;"></p>

<p><em>Figure 5: BrushaLoader C&amp;C panel - Victims a few hours after the beginning of a July 5, 2018 malicious spam campaign</em></p>

<p><img lat="BrushaLoader C&amp;C panel" src="/sites/default/files/blf6.png" style="border-width: 0px; border-style: solid;"></p>

<p><em>Figure 6: BrushaLoader C&amp;C panel - Victims approximately 24 hours after the beginning of a July 5, 2018 campaign (captured July 6, 2018)</em></p>

<p><strong>Conclusion</strong></p>

<p>Though one of many downloaders in regular use, BrushaLoader has emerged in connection with numerous secondary payloads such as DanaBot and prolific actors including TA544. We have observed it in multiple geographies and a variety of campaigns. Moreover, insights from the command and control panel suggest high infection success rates for the loader, enabling deployment of a range of payloads by actors using the malware. While loaders fail to garner headlines like high-profile ransomware attacks, they have emerged as a key element of many threat actors’ toolkits. We will continue to monitor trends around this malware family and BrushaLoader in particular.</p>
