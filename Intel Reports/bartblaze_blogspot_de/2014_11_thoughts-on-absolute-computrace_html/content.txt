Why would this be an issue? First of all, there has been some excellent research by&nbsp;Anibal Sacco and Alfredo Ortega here: <a href="http://corelabs.coresecurity.com/index.php?module=Wiki&amp;action=view&amp;type=publication&amp;name=Deactivate_the_Rootkit" target="_blank">Deactivate the Rootkit</a>, in which they describe attacks on BIOS anti-theft technologies, which Absolute also offers. An excerpt from their paper:<br />
<br />
<blockquote class="tr_bq">
<i>In order to be an effective system, the anti-theft agent must be stealthy, must have complete control of the system, and most importantly, must be highly persistent because wiping of the whole system most often occurs in the case of theft.<br />This activity is also consistent with rootkit behavior, the only difference being that rootkits are generally malicious, while anti-theft technologies act as a form of protection against thieves.</i></blockquote>
<br />
Secondly, there has been research from Kaspersky as well on the subject, read their blog post here:&nbsp;<a href="http://securelist.com/analysis/publications/58278/absolute-computrace-revisited/" target="_blank">Absolute Computrace Revisited</a><br />
<br />
I advise you to read their post, as it provides excellent information as well. I'm not going to repeat their research here, as it's pretty extended. What you should remember however:<br />
<br />
<blockquote class="tr_bq">
<i>While Absolute Software is a legitimate company and information about Computrace product is available on the company's&nbsp;<a href="http://www.absolute.com/ru-ru">official website</a>,
 the owner of the system claimed he had never installed Absolute 
Computrace and didn't even know the software was present on his 
computer. It could be assumed that the software was pre-installed by an 
OEM manufacturer or reseller company, but according to an Absolute 
Software&nbsp;<a href="http://www.absolute.com/en/resources/whitepapers/absolute-persistence-technology">whitepaper</a>&nbsp;this
 should be done by users or their IT service. Unless you have a private 
IT service or your PC vendor took care of you, someone else has full 
access and control over your computer.</i></blockquote>

The machine was freshly bought and the user never ordered, installed or even heard of Computrace software. In this case, the reseller didn't install it either. This leaves the option the manufacterer or a possible previous owner [or someone else] installed Computrace.<br />

Here comes the fun part: even after permanently disabling the Computrace module, the software was still active and running; contacting the server (<i>search.namequery.com</i>) like crazy.<br />
<br />
I decided to contact Absolute Software in order to get an answer as to why this behaviour was occurring. Since neither of us are customers, I used the form <a href="https://www.absolute.com/en/support/contact/security" target="_blank">here</a> to contact them. <br />
<br />
After two days I got a reply from their customer service. In reply as to why permanently disabling didn't seem to work:<br />
<br />
<blockquote class="tr_bq">
<i>It is also worth noting that many used or refurbished devices may have motherboards with a Computrace BIOS module that was activated by the previous owner.&nbsp; In these cases, my recommendation would be the following:<br /><br />1.&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Obtain and install any missing or outdated HECI\Intel Management\IMEI drivers from the manufacturer.&nbsp; Once these drivers are in place, any potential Absolute software installed on the computer will correctly communicate with the BIOS and it should automatically deactivate itself over the course of a few days.<br /><br />2.&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Contact the manufacturer and request a motherboard replacement.&nbsp; Activated motherboards should not be re-sold by manufacturers or retailers if the necessary de-activation steps are not taken first.</i></blockquote>
<br />
Reason for seeing numerous outgoing connections to their server is probably due to their module wanting to receive instructions from the server that the original license should no longer be active, or to download new binaries.<br />

<span style="font-size: large;"><b><span id="BIOS">Binaries &amp; BIOS information &amp; characteristics</span></b></span><br />
<br />
There's already a good list available by Kaspersky which I'm not going to repeat here. You can find that list on <a href="http://securelist.com/analysis/publications/58278/absolute-computrace-revisited/#03" target="_blank">this</a> link.<br />
<br />
However, the following points are worth noting:<br />
<br />
<br />
<ul>
<li>Two new binaries (different hashes) have been identified:<br /><b>ad73c636bb2ead416dfa541a74aea016</b> (wceprv.dll)<br /><b>4011590af6f13a42a869ae57d6174f4f</b> (rpcnetp.exe)</li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li>Several files are packed with <a href="http://upx.sourceforge.net/" target="_blank">UPX</a></li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li>The wceprv.dll module has a Digital Signature which is issued to<i><br />Absolute Software Corp.&nbsp;</i>Serial Number:&nbsp;<span style="white-space: pre-wrap;"><b>35:ba:ec:87:59:d7:84:62:c3:d2:b7:ff:d4:c4:6e:51

</b></span></li>
<li><span style="white-space: pre-wrap;">Machines will have an altered Master Boot Record (<a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Master_boot_record" target="_blank">MBR</a>); this is because Computrace parses the MBR and partition table - it writes some data into the sectors before the primary partition.
According to the patent (<a href="http://www.google.co.in/patents/US20060272020" target="_blank">US 20060272020 A1</a>):</span><br /><span style="white-space: pre-wrap;"><i><i>
In another embodiment, the CLM is stored in a substitute Master Boot Record (MBR), or a combination of the foregoing.</i></i></span></li>
</ul>
<blockquote>
</blockquote>
<br />
<br />
CLM or Computrace&nbsp;Loader Module is one of Computrace's main modules. (besides the Adaptive Installer Module (AIM) and the Communications Driver Agent (CDA) - see the patent for reference)<br />

<span style="font-size: large;"><b><span id="determine">How to determine if you have Absolute Computrace installed</span></b></span><br />
<br />
First things first: check in the BIOS if there's a mention of Absolute Computrace somewhere:<br />
(re)boot your machine and access the BIOS with one of the Function keys on your keyboard.&nbsp; <br />
<br />
Typically, this is <b>F2</b>, but may differ. See here for a complete list:<br />
<a href="http://pcsupport.about.com/od/fixtheproblem/a/biosaccess_pc.htm" target="_blank">BIOS Setup Utility Access Keys for Popular Computer Systems </a><br />
<br />
<br />
Secondly, see if any of the files mentioned in Kasperky's blog post are running or exist on the file system. For the full list see <a href="http://securelist.com/analysis/publications/58278/absolute-computrace-revisited/#17" target="_blank">here</a>, but keep in mind the two new additional hashes added above. <br />
Note that new hashes may pop-up as well.<br />
<br />
<br />
Thirdly, network activity as mentioned in above blog post. <br />
(but mainly to<i> search.namequery.com </i>or <i>209.53.113.223</i>)<br />

<b><span style="font-size: large;"><span id="uninstall">How to remove or uninstall Absolute Computrace</span></span></b><br />
<br />
I won't provide any specific information on how to remove or uninstall Computrace, as its main purpose is still - and I quote:<br />
<br />
<blockquote class="tr_bq">
<i>[...] to perform preemptive and reactive security measures to safeguard a missing, lost, or stolen device and the data it contains. With Computrace Mobile you can determine the location of the device and whether or not it&#8217;s on the move. You can also freeze it to prevent unauthorized access and send a message to the user to validate the status of the device. If the device contains important information, you can remotely retrieve files or delete them immediately. And you can generate an audit log of the data that&#8217;s been removed so you can prove compliance with corporate and government regulations.</i></blockquote>
<br />
<br />
However, should you have bought (what you believe is) a new machine and it is apparent Computrace is active, download the latest drivers fit for your system:<br />
<a href="http://drivers.softpedia.com/get/BIOS/" target="_blank">Download BIOS drivers</a>&nbsp; Also find information on <a href="http://www.wikihow.com/Update-Your-Computer%27s-BIOS" target="_blank">How to Update Your Computer's BIOS.</a><br />
<br />
When correctly executed and the option for Computrace in the BIOS is set to Permanently Disabled, it should correctly disable itself - <b><u>taken into account the original license has expired or the original owner deactivated it</u></b>, if existent.<br />
<br />
<div>
Another option would be to request a motherboard replacement for your machine, as suggested above. Additionally you may reinstall the Operating System afterwards.<br />
