      <h1><span id="hs_cos_wrapper_name" class="hs_cos_wrapper hs_cos_wrapper_meta_field hs_cos_wrapper_type_text" style="" data-hs-cos-general-type="meta_field" data-hs-cos-type="text">The Rising Threat from LockBit Ransomware</span></h1>
      <div class="cr-mln__post-author-share">
      <div id="hubspot-author_data" class="hubspot-editable cr-mln__post-meta" data-hubspot-form-id="author_data" data-hubspot-name="Blog Author">

          <span id="hs_cos_wrapper_post_body" class="hs_cos_wrapper hs_cos_wrapper_meta_field hs_cos_wrapper_type_rich_text" style="" data-hs-cos-general-type="meta_field" data-hs-cos-type="rich_text"><p>LockBit ransomware is the latest threat posing an increased risk for organizations. The ransomware gang has been making headlines recently. LockBit has also <a href="https://www.archyde.com/consulting-giant-accenture-put-under-pressure-by-ransomware-attack/" rel="noopener" target="_blank">reportedly compromised Accenture</a>.</p>
<p>The group reportedly revealed the attack on their site on the DarkWeb, noting: “These people are beyond privacy and security. I really hope that their services are better than what I saw as an insider. If you are interested in buying some databases, reach us.”</p>
<p style="text-align: center; font-size: 16px;"><img src="https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/image%20(31).png?width=489&amp;name=image%20(31).png" alt="image (31)" width="489" loading="lazy" style="width: 489px; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto; display: block;" srcset="https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/image%20(31).png?width=245&amp;name=image%20(31).png 245w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/image%20(31).png?width=489&amp;name=image%20(31).png 489w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/image%20(31).png?width=734&amp;name=image%20(31).png 734w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/image%20(31).png?width=978&amp;name=image%20(31).png 978w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/image%20(31).png?width=1223&amp;name=image%20(31).png 1223w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/image%20(31).png?width=1467&amp;name=image%20(31).png 1467w" sizes="(max-width: 489px) 100vw, 489px"><em>Screenshot from Lockbit site</em></p>
<!--more-->
<h2>What Is LockBit?</h2>
<p>LockBit is a cybercriminal gang that operates using a ransomware-as-a-service (RaaS) model—similar to <a href="/_hcms/analytics/search/conversion?redirect=aHR0cHM6Ly93d3cuY3liZXJlYXNvbi5jb20vYmxvZy9jeWJlcmVhc29uLXZzLWRhcmtzaWRlLXJhbnNvbXdhcmU%3D&amp;ct=SEARCH&amp;pid=3354902&amp;cid=44194973264&amp;t=ZGFya3NpZGU%3D&amp;d=www.cybereason.com&amp;c=2&amp;c=6&amp;rp=1&amp;ab=false&amp;opcid=&amp;rs=UNKNOWN&amp;hs-expires=1660226758&amp;hs-version=1&amp;hs-signature=APUk-v71e9ww5Z2nCFFapeqi2vx677ztig" rel="noopener" target="_blank">DarkSide</a> and <a href="/_hcms/analytics/search/conversion?redirect=aHR0cHM6Ly93d3cuY3liZXJlYXNvbi5jb20vYmxvZy9jeWJlcmVhc29uLXZzLXJldmlsLXJhbnNvbXdhcmUtdGhlLWthc2V5YS1jaHJvbmljbGVz&amp;ct=SEARCH&amp;pid=3354902&amp;cid=50255403605&amp;t=cmV2aWw%3D&amp;d=www.cybereason.com&amp;c=2&amp;c=6&amp;rp=2&amp;ab=true&amp;opcid=&amp;rs=UNKNOWN&amp;hs-expires=1660226780&amp;hs-version=1&amp;hs-signature=APUk-v4c_sdFoIoZ5qbAYShDCayIDu6cZQ" rel="noopener" target="_blank">REvil</a>. LockBit offers its ransomware platform for other entities or individuals to use based on an affiliate model. Any ransom payments received from using LockBit are divided between the customer directing the attack and the LockBit gang.</p>
<p>LockBit is believed to be related to the LockerGoga and MegaCortex malware families. It shares common tactics, techniques, and procedures (TTPs) with these malicious attacks—particularly the ability to propagate automatically to new targets, being used in targeted attacks rather than just spamming or attacking organizations indiscriminately, and the underlying tools it relies on, such as Windows PowerShell and Server Message Block (SMB).</p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><img src="https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/image%20(29).png?width=1545&amp;name=image%20(29).png" alt="image (29)" width="1545" loading="lazy" style="width: 1545px;" srcset="https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/image%20(29).png?width=773&amp;name=image%20(29).png 773w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/image%20(29).png?width=1545&amp;name=image%20(29).png 1545w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/image%20(29).png?width=2318&amp;name=image%20(29).png 2318w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/image%20(29).png?width=3090&amp;name=image%20(29).png 3090w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/image%20(29).png?width=3863&amp;name=image%20(29).png 3863w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/image%20(29).png?width=4635&amp;name=image%20(29).png 4635w" sizes="(max-width: 1545px) 100vw, 1545px"><span style="font-size: 16px;"><em>Screenshot from Lockbit site</em></span></p>
<p>Once a single host is compromised, LockBit can scan the network to locate and infect other accessible devices. It uses tools and protocols that are native to Windows systems—making it more difficult for endpoint security tools to detect or identify the activity as malicious.</p>
<p>The LockBit ransomware continues to adapt and evolve. More recent variants have adopted the double extortion model—locating and exfiltrating valuable data before encrypting systems. The stolen data provides additional incentive for victims to pay the ransom. Even if they can restore data from backups, refusing to pay the ransom may result in sensitive data being published publicly or sold to competitors.</p>
<h2>Rising Threat</h2>
<p>The LockBit gang has been making headlines recently. In the wake of DarkSide and REvil both shutting down operations, it seems like LockBit may be working to fill the void.</p>
<p>Lawrence Abrams <a href="https://www.bleepingcomputer.com/news/security/lockbit-ransomware-recruiting-insiders-to-breach-corporate-networks/" rel="noopener" target="_blank">recently reported</a> that the LockBit ransomware gang is <a href="/blog/lockbit-ransomware-wants-to-hire-your-employees" rel="noopener">actively recruiting insiders</a> to help them breach and encrypt networks. According to Abrams, this may be a shift from the standard ransomware-as-a-service model to cut out the middleman and keep more of the ransom profit for themselves.</p>
<p>The wallpaper displayed on compromised systems now includes text inviting insiders to help compromise systems—promising payouts of millions of dollars.</p>
<h2>Protecting against LockBit Ransomware</h2>
<p>There is no good option for an organization once a ransomware attack has compromised systems and encrypted data. That is especially true in the case of a double extortion attack. Refusing to pay the ransom means going through a painful process of restoring data from backups and trying to regain control and functionality of your systems while also accepting that your data will likely be exposed.</p>
<p>Paying the ransom may allow the victim to be operational quicker and prevent having data published or sold, but <a href="/blog/report-ransomware-attacks-and-the-true-cost-to-business" rel="noopener" target="_blank">research shows that 80% of companies</a> that pay a ransom end up getting attacked again.</p>
<p>It is important to have effective protection in place to prevent the ransomware attack from getting that far in the first place. Organizations need to have an operation-centric view of the attack. The ability to view the entire malicious operation—or <a href="/_hcms/analytics/search/conversion?redirect=aHR0cHM6Ly93d3cuY3liZXJlYXNvbi5jb20vYmxvZy90aGUtY3liZXJlYXNvbi1kaWZmZXJlbmNlLW1hbG9wLWludHJv&amp;ct=SEARCH&amp;pid=3354902&amp;cid=44404671388&amp;t=bWFsb3A%3D&amp;d=www.cybereason.com&amp;c=2&amp;c=6&amp;rp=2&amp;ab=false&amp;opcid=&amp;rs=UNKNOWN&amp;hs-expires=1660229525&amp;hs-version=1&amp;hs-signature=APUk-v78uzg4h3W9xIuiNCmPnCbO8gPDag" rel="noopener" target="_blank">MalOp</a>—and recognize indicators of behavior enables Cybereason to detect and block ransomware attacks and protect against threats like LockBit.</p>
<h2 style="font-weight: normal;">Defending Against Ransomware Attacks</h2>
<p>The only way forward for organizations is to <a href="/blog/accenture-responds-following-lockbit-ransomware-attack" rel="noopener">prevent an infection from occurring</a> in the first place. To do that, they need to invest in an anti-ransomware solution that doesn’t rely on Indicators of Compromise (IOCs), as not every ransomware attack chain is known to the security community. They need a multi-layered platform that uses Indicators of Behavior (IOBs) so that security teams can detect and shut down a ransomware attack chain regardless of whether anyone’s seen it before.</p>
<p>The Cybereason Operation-Centric approach means no data filtering and the ability to detect attacks earlier based on rare or advantageous chains of (otherwise normal) behaviors. <a href="/prevent-ransomware" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>Cybereason is undefeated in the battle against ransomware</span></a> thanks to our multi-layered prevention, detection and response, which includes:</p>
<ul>
<li style="list-style-type: none;">
<ul>
<li aria-level="1"><span style="font-size: 18px;"><a href="/platform/anti-ransomware" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><strong>Anti ransomware prevention and deception</strong></a><strong>:</strong> Cybereason uses a combination of behavioral detections and proprietary deception techniques surface the most complex ransomware threats and end the attack before any critical data can be encrypted.</span></li>
<li aria-level="1"><span style="font-size: 18px;"><strong>Intelligence-Based Antivirus:</strong> Cybereason blocks known ransomware variants leveraging an ever-growing pool of threat intelligence based on previously detected attacks.</span></li>
<li aria-level="1"><span style="font-size: 18px;"><a href="/platform/endpoint-prevention" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><strong>NGAV</strong></a><strong>: </strong>Cybereason NGAV is powered by machine learning and recognizes malicious components in code to block unknown ransomware variants prior to execution.</span></li>
<li aria-level="1"><span style="font-size: 18px;"><strong>Fileless Ransomware Protection:</strong> Cybereason disrupts attacks utilizing fileless and MBR-based ransomware that traditional antivirus tools miss.</span></li>
<li aria-level="1"><span style="font-size: 18px;"><a href="/platform/endpoint-controls" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><strong>Endpoint Controls</strong></a><strong>:</strong> Cybereason hardens endpoints against attacks by managing security policies, maintaining device controls, implementing personal firewalls and enforcing whole-disk encryption across a range of device types, both fixed and mobile.</span></li>
<li aria-level="1"><span style="font-size: 18px;"><strong>Behavioral Document Protection: </strong>Cybereason detects and blocks ransomware hidden in the most common business document formats, including those that leverage malicious macros and other stealthy attack vectors.</span></li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<p><br>Cybereason is dedicated to teaming with defenders to end cyber attacks from endpoints to the enterprise to everywhere - including modern ransomware. <a href="/prevent-ransomware" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>Learn more about ransomware defense here</span></a> or <a href="/request-a-demo" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>schedule a demo</span></a> today to learn how your organization can <a href="/blog/the-cybereason-malop-achieving-operation-centric-security" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span>benefit from an operation-centric approach</span></a> to security.</p></span>
