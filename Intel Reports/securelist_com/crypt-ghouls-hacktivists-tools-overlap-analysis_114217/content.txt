													<p>Last December, we discovered a new group targeting Russian businesses and government agencies with ransomware. Further investigation into this group&#8217;s activity suggests a connection to other groups currently targeting Russia. We have seen overlaps not only in indicators of compromise and tools, but also tactics, techniques, and procedures (TTPs). Moreover, the infrastructure partially overlaps across attacks.</p>
<p>The group under review has a toolkit that includes utilities such as Mimikatz, XenAllPasswordPro, PingCastle, Localtonet, resocks, AnyDesk, PsExec, and others. As the final payload, the group used the well-known ransomware LockBit 3.0 and Babuk. We have dubbed the group &#8220;Crypt Ghouls&#8221;.</p>
<h2 id="delivery-and-persistence">Delivery and persistence</h2>
<p>It was only in two of the group&#8217;s attacks that we were able to determine the initial access vector. The attackers used a contractor&#8217;s login information to connect to the victim&#8217;s internal systems via a VPN. The VPN connections were established from IP addresses associated with a Russian hosting provider&#8217;s network and a contractor&#8217;s network. Nowadays attackers are <a href="https://securelist.com/trusted-relationship-attack/112731/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">increasingly</a> gaining initial access through their targets&#8217; contractors. We suspect that contractors are compromised via VPN services or unpatched vulnerabilities.</p>
<p>To maintain access to the system, the attackers used the NSSM and Localtonet utilities. NSSM creates and manages services on a host, while Localtonet provides an encrypted tunnel for connecting to that host from an external network. Both utilities were downloaded directly from localtonet.com:</p>

<h2 id="file-encryption">File encryption</h2>
<p>The attackers encrypted data with publicly available versions of the popular LockBit 3.0 (for Windows systems) and Babuk (for Linux) malware. The LockBit sample we analyzed was configured with commands to encrypt local drives, terminate specific processes and services, disable Windows Defender, and delete event logs. The ransomware added system directories, as well as a folder named

<p>We noticed something strange about how a victim&#8217;s files were encrypted. First, LockBit encrypted files with specific extensions, as defined in its sample configuration. These are the files that the attackers may find most valuable. Besides these, the malware encrypts files in the recycle bin while inserting random characters in these. Beyond the primary algorithm, we found a cycle that systematically renamed the original file in the recycle bin. This process iterated through every letter of the English alphabet, continuing until it reached the last one. This type of encryption makes it really hard, or even impossible, to recover the user&#8217;s files.</p>

<h2 id="links-to-other-groups">Links to other groups</h2>
<p>We are seeing a lot of overlap in the tools and techniques used by cybercriminals targeting Russian businesses and government agencies. Below, we outline the key similarities we found in attacks by different groups.</p>
<h3 id="morlock">MorLock</h3>
<p>MorLock activities, <a href="https://www.facct.ru/blog/morlock-ransomware/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">as investigated by F.A.C.C.T.</a>, shares many features with several of the attacks we analyzed for this report. The groups share most of the tools they use: SoftPerfect Network Scanner, XenAllPasswordPro, AnyDesk, PingCastle, Localtonet, NSSM, resocks, LockBit 3.0, and Babuk.</p>
<p>The file and folder names used in attacks by both groups also show similarities. Thus we found a resocks utility named &#8220;xfs-healthcheck&#8221;, a name that follows the same template as the resocks names on the <a href="https://github.com/facct-ransomware/Ransomware/tree/main/MorLock" target="_blank" rel="noopener">list of indicators published by F.A.C.C.T.</a>: [xxx]-healthcheck. We noticed a further similarity when studying the XenAllPasswordPro utility: in MorLock attacks, it was located in a directory named &#8220;allinone2023&#8221;.</p>
<p>Furthermore, we checked the MorLock infrastructure as reported by F.A.C.C.T., only to find that the group also used Surfshark VPN and the VDSina hosting services provider.</p>
<h3 id="blackjack">BlackJack</h3>
<p>While investigating the utilities used in Crypt Ghouls attacks, we found an overlap with the toolkit employed by the <a href="https://securelist.com/blackjack-hacktivists-connection-with-twelve/113959/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">BlackJack</a> group, which also used XenAllPasswordPro. This caught our attention, as XenAllPasswordPro is not the most popular tool among cybercriminals despite being freely available.</p>
<h3 id="twelve">Twelve</h3>
<p>We have seen XenAllPasswordPro used in attacks by <a href="https://securelist.com/twelve-group-unified-kill-chain/113877/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Twelve</a> too. Furthermore, we discovered 
			<span id="crayon-671a7ed66ace0652494406" class="crayon-syntax crayon-syntax-inline  crayon-theme-classic crayon-theme-classic-inline crayon-font-monaco" style="font-size: 12px !important; line-height: 15px !important;font-size: 12px !important;"><span class="crayon-pre crayon-code" style="font-size: 12px !important; line-height: 15px !important;font-size: 12px !important; -moz-tab-size:4; -o-tab-size:4; -webkit-tab-size:4; tab-size:4;"><span class="crayon-i">Intellpui</span><span class="crayon-sy">.</span><span class="crayon-i">vbs</span></span></span>, a loader for CobInt, also used by Twelve, on one of the systems attacked by Crypt Ghouls.</p>
<h3 id="shedding-zmiy">Shedding Zmiy</h3>
<p>Shedding Zmiy is a group associated with the (Ex)Cobalt activity cluster. We found a further overlap in a <a href="https://rt-solar.ru/solar-4rays/blog/4333/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">report by Solar 4RAYS on this group-related incidents</a>, namely the use of DLL sideloading with the 
			<span id="crayon-671a7ed66ace1205694065" class="crayon-syntax crayon-syntax-inline  crayon-theme-classic crayon-theme-classic-inline crayon-font-monaco" style="font-size: 12px !important; line-height: 15px !important;font-size: 12px !important;"><span class="crayon-pre crayon-code" style="font-size: 12px !important; line-height: 15px !important;font-size: 12px !important; -moz-tab-size:4; -o-tab-size:4; -webkit-tab-size:4; tab-size:4;"><span class="crayon-i">dismcore</span><span class="crayon-sy">.</span><span class="crayon-i">dll</span></span></span> malicious loader. The report mentioned other familiar utilities and malware: resocks, SoftPerfect Network Scanner, and CobInt. In addition, Shedding Zmiy used VDSina to host its command-and-control servers.</p>
<p>The similarities between the groups described above led to the conclusion that these attacks overlap with the activity of Crypt Ghouls. Our analysis of cybercriminal tools and tactics suggests that different groups may be collaborating, sharing resources, or exchanging intelligence. Other vendors have found evidence of the (Ex)Cobalt cluster members participating in these groups&#8217; activities, and our analysis confirms this. Right now, we cannot say for sure that these campaigns are connected, but we anticipate their number will increase further. We will continue to monitor activity targeting Russian organizations.</p>
<h2 id="victims">Victims</h2>
<p>Russian government agencies as well as mining, energy, finance, and retail companies have fallen victim to the Crypt Ghouls group.</p>
<h2 id="conclusion">Conclusion</h2>
<p>Crypt Ghouls is another group attacking Russia. Cybercriminals are leveraging compromised credentials, often belonging to subcontractors, and popular open-source tools. These include modified configurations of the LockBit 3.0 and Babuk ransomware, whose builders and source code are publicly available. As the number of attackers using leaked builds increases, identifying the perpetrators of threats becomes increasingly difficult. The shared toolkit used in attacks on Russia makes it challenging to pinpoint the specific hacktivist groups involved. In the attacks carried out by the Crypt Ghouls, we have identified components of infrastructure and a variety of popular tools that are also used by many other groups. This suggests that the current actors are not only sharing knowledge but also their toolkits. All of this only makes it more difficult to identify specific malicious actors behind the wave of attacks directed at Russian organizations.</p>
