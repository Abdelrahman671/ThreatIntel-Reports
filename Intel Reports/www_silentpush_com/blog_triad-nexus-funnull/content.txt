<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="Executive-summary">Executive summary</h2>



<p>This is the first in a series of blogs on a deep-dive investigation into <strong>Triad Nexus</strong> &#8211; our term for a large cluster of malicious infrastructure related to FUNNULL&#8217;s CDN.</p>



<p>In the past month, our Threat Research Team has identified more than 200,000<strong> </strong>unique hostnames being proxied through FUNNULL, with more than 95% of the proxied hostnames created using Domain Generation Algorithms (DGAs)—a formula used for creating large amounts of domains.</p>



<p>Silent Push research has also revealed a connection between the Suncity Group &#8211; named in a <a href="https://www.unodc.org/roseap/uploads/documents/Publications/2024/Casino_Underground_Banking_Report_2024.pdf">2024 UN report</a> for laundering millions of dollars for the North Korean Lazarus Group &#8211; and Tether, the cryptocurrency of choice for suspect gambling sites serving East and Southeast Asia.</p>



<p>FUNNULL CDN was caught in a separate scandal earlier in 2024, after acquiring polyfill[.]io &#8211; the popular open source JavaScript library. Months later, they were linked to a supply chain attack against some mobile users on sites that still had the polyfill JS, redirecting them to an online “Asian gambling site.” More than 110,000 websites have been impacted.</p>



<p>Our Threat Analysts have also uncovered a new FUNNULL-hosted phishing scam, focused on retail and luxury fashion companies targeting major brands.</p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="Background">Background</h2>



<p>Silent Push Threat Analysts first observed FUNNULL, a Chinese content delivery network (CDN) company, during research we began in 2022.</p>



<p>Our research uncovered a large cluster of<strong> </strong>fake trading apps impersonating well-known financial organizations that were part of a worldwide financial fraud campaign.</p>



<p>During the course of that research, we tracked FUNNULL hosting investment scams; discovered an alleged association with money laundering organizations; and mapped the hosting of thousands of algorithmically generated domains for low-quality suspect gambling sites, mostly in Mandarin, with the majority based in Macau.</p>



<p>DGAs are formulas used for creating bulk domains. Typically, DGAs result in domains that have a hyper-specific format, for example, apple2peanut4bread6[.]suffix – which would likely be created by a DGA formula that took three simple words/foods from a dictionary and then combined them with a random number between each word.</p>



<p>Other formulas combine a series of letters or numbers in unique ways. A huge portion of the domains on FUNNULL are merely a series of numbers, such as 7419255[.]com.</p>



<p>A large chunk (95%) of the 200,000 FUNNULL domains we discovered during our research are structured in this way.</p>



<p>We also discovered a site template connected to a developer working with FUNNULL, mirrored live (as of this report) on a domain hosted on a FUNNULL-owned IP range. The template promotes a &#8220;money-moving&#8221; network, that appears to be a money laundering service.</p>



<p>Further analysis of Telegram channels used by this network uncovers messages mentioning many of the suspect gambling brands with websites hosted on the FUNNULL CDN.&nbsp;</p>



<p><strong><em>Suncity Group (and other online operators targeting Chinese markets) may be hosting their gambling websites behind randomly generated domain names due to the Great Firewall’s domain-based approach to blocking.</em></strong></p>



<p>It’s also possible that criminals within China are purposefully mirroring their suspect gambling content across thousands of domains to prevent CCP authorities from blocking their websites and, subsequently, their access to cross-border money flows.</p>



<p>Thousands of suspect gambling websites hosted on FUNNULL prominently feature Tether promotions. Frequently used to move money in and out of China—and allegedly for gambling and laundering money—Tether (or USDT) is considered to be the “cryptocurrency of choice” for suspect gambling sites in China. Tether is banned in some countries because of <a href="https://www.occrp.org/en/news/casinos-and-crypto-major-drivers-of-money-laundering-in-east-and-southeast-asia">its appeal to transnational organized crime groups</a>, especially for running suspect gambling in Southeast Asia, where officials struggle to keep up with illicit financial flows.</p>



<p>Silent Push is also following FUNNULL CDN’s acquisition of polyfill[.]io, a JavaScript library that was integrated into 110,000 websites, including some of the largest commercial and government organizations in the world. In June 2024, FUNNULL was accused of propagating supply chain attacks against mobile users, and using polyfill.io in the attack chain.</p>



<p>So-called “bulletproof hosts,” enable malicious infrastructure to remain online by ignoring requests to take down suspicious or malicious sites. FUNNULL CDN does not currently provide a clear process for handling takedown requests on its <a href="https://funnull.com/company/sla/">website</a>, but it does feature an interesting quote to describe its network: “<em><strong>Behind a cute name, there is a violent beast standing</strong></em>.”</p>



<p>We&#8217;ve also uncovered a retail scam campaign, hosted through FUNNULL, that uses phishing login pages to target major retail and luxury Western fashion brands. Targeted brands include <strong>Aldo</strong>, <strong>Asda</strong>, <strong>Bonanza</strong>, <strong>Cartier</strong>, <strong>Chanel</strong>, <strong>Coach</strong>, <strong>eBay</strong>, <strong>Etsy</strong>, <strong>Gilt Groupe</strong>, <strong>Inditex</strong>, <strong>Lotte Mart</strong>, <strong>LVMH</strong>, <strong>Macy’s</strong>, <strong>Michael</strong> <strong>Kors</strong>, <strong>Neiman</strong> <strong>Marcus</strong>, <strong>OnBuy[.]com</strong>, <strong>Rakuten</strong>, <strong>Saks Fifth Avenue</strong>, <strong>Tiffany &amp; Co.</strong>, and <strong>Valentino</strong>.</p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="FUNNULL-and-fake-trading-apps">FUNNULL and fake trading apps</h2>



<p>During a 2022 Silent Push investigation into a domain involved in investment fraud, <a href="https://www.silentpush.com/blog/fake-trading-apps/">our team uncovered a large cluster of fake trading apps impersonating well-known financial organizations</a>, including the <strong>Australian Securities Exchange (ASX)</strong>, <strong>Coinbase</strong>, <strong>CoinSmart</strong>, <strong>eToro</strong>, and <strong>Nasdaq</strong>.</p>



<p>This same investigation uncovered fake financial job scams employing pig butchering techniques, and this is when our analysts first came across several malicious networks hosted on the FUNNULL CDN infrastructure.</p>



<p>Our <strong>Triad Nexus</strong> research discovered this is not a lone cluster of activity but is, in fact, part of a global financial fraud campaign.</p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="FUNNULL’s-CDN,-rising-up-from-corrupted-soil">FUNNULL’s CDN: rising up from corrupted soil</h3>



<p>A large portion of the fake trading apps we reported on were using FUNNULL to host their malicious content, with CNAME records pointing at vk6a2rmn-u.funnull[.]vip.</p>



<p>If we perform a reverse lookup on that CNAME, we see approximately 2,000 domains involved in the 2022 scam. It appears the threat actors are now shifting to 6ce0a6db.u.fn03[.]vip, but its size is considerably smaller.</p>



<p>When updating our FUNNULL research for 2024, we discovered that this same malicious cluster, while reduced in scope, still has a couple of active hostnames, including cmegrouphkpd[.]info (pictured below), which has hosted a fake trading platform abusing CME Group’s brand for the past two years.</p>



<p>Until recently, a near identical version of this site was hosted at hiflyk47344[.]top.</p>



<p>The timeline can be seen via current and historical CNAME records, which for&nbsp;<code>cmegrouphkpd[.]info</code> shows it had a record pointing to <code>vk6a2rmn-u.funnull[.]vip</code> between February and March 2022, changing to <code>vk6a2rmn-u.funnull01[.]vip</code> between March 2022 and June 2024, and since then, switching to <code>6ce0a6db.u.fn03[.]vip.</code></p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="Suncity-Group-connections">Suncity Group connections</h2>



<p>Silent Push Researchers noticed that “Suncity Group” is one of the organizations whose logo and branding is present on a sizeable number (thousands) of gambling websites hosted on FUNNULL.</p>



<p>Suncity Group is alleged to have built a money laundering/underground banking system that <a href="https://macaonews.org/news/city/former-suncity-boss-alvin-chau-sentenced-to-18-years-in-prison/">processed $100 billion USD in illegal bets</a> and <a href="https://www.globaltimes.cn/page/202401/1305066.shtml">laundered $40 billion for criminals</a>.</p>



<p>According to <a href="https://www.reuters.com/article/us-china-casinos/chinese-state-media-target-macaus-suncity-in-online-gambling-report-idUSKCN1U40M7/">Reuters/Hong Kong</a>, in writing about a state-backed report, Suncity is (was) Macau’s biggest junket operator of VIP gambling rooms around Asia.</p>



<p>With the exception of Macau, where Chinese nationals are permitted to bet in physical gambling establishments and a few legal state-sponsored lottery activities, <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gambling_in_China">gambling is illegal</a> in mainland China. The report stated that Suncity enabled Chinese players to bet online, utilizing underground banks to move capital out of the country.</p>



<p>Suncity Group denied having online gaming operations, and issued a <a href="https://www.reuters.com/article/us-china-casinos/chinese-state-media-target-macaus-suncity-in-online-gambling-report-idUSKCN1U40M7/">statement</a> that “It didn&#8217;t operate any online gaming business and all its operations were permitted under local government regulations,” according to Reuters.</p>



<p>Macau&#8217;s gaming regulator stated that <a href="https://www.reuters.com/article/us-china-casinos/chinese-state-media-target-macaus-suncity-in-online-gambling-report-idUSKCN1U40M7/">online gambling in Macau is illegal</a> and violations of local laws and regulations would prompt the bureau to &#8220;take serious action.&#8221;</p>



<p>One report about Suncity Group, “<a href="https://www.unodc.org/roseap/uploads/documents/Publications/2024/Casino_Underground_Banking_Report_2024.pdf"><em><strong>Underground Banking and Money Laundering Methods</strong></em></a><em><strong>,</strong></em>” in the January 2024 United Nations Office on Drugs and Crime (UNODC) goes into great details about the extent of Suncity&#8217;s illegal gaming activities, and further underscores our research.</p>

<p>We soon realized that a chunk of these were pig butchering/investment phishing login pages, such as coroexchange[.]com:</p>

<figure class="aligncenter size-full"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" width="356" height="625" src="https://www.silentpush.com/wp-content/uploads/67-coro-email.png" alt="" class="wp-image-8025"/><figcaption class="wp-element-caption"><em><em>coroexchange[.]com phishing login page</em></em></figcaption></figure></div>


<p>Beyond the pig butchering sites, the only other websites hosted on this CNAME all appeared to be a new retail phishing campaign targeting major Western brands, with phishing login pages such as bonanza.jdfraa[.]com:</p>


<div class="wp-block-image">
<figure class="aligncenter size-full"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" width="1920" height="1080" src="https://www.silentpush.com/wp-content/uploads/68-bonanza-phishing-page.png" alt="" class="wp-image-8026"/><figcaption class="wp-element-caption"><em><em>bonanza.jdfraa[.]com phishing login page</em></em></figcaption></figure></div>


<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="Retail-phishing-websites-have-phishing-kit-similarities">Phishing kit similarities</h3>



<p>The phishing websites attempt to capture user credentials. Many of these phishing pages are hosted within a subdomain and on a specific page—attempting to access the root domain results in a 404 error.</p>



<p>The sites are also built using similar code (we are sharing more details about this privately with our Enterprise customers and law enforcement), and have similar server response codes.</p>



<p>On the Chanel phishing page (cjmall01[.]com/index/user/logins.html), as soon as you enter login credentials and submit the form, a request is made to the “do” endpoint “/do_login.html” within the same /user directory. The request includes a payload with the username and password, and the response includes the message “User doesn’t exist.”</p>


<div class="wp-block-image">
<figure class="aligncenter size-full"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" width="2407" height="1005" src="https://www.silentpush.com/wp-content/uploads/69-chanel-phishing-page.png" alt="" class="wp-image-8027"/><figcaption class="wp-element-caption"><em><em>Chanel phishing page</em></em></figcaption></figure></div>


<p>On the Cartier phishing page (cartier.cartierate[.]com/login), as soon as you enter login credentials and submit the form, a request is made to an “auth” login endpoint (cartier.cartierate[.]com/api/cat/v1/auth/login).</p>



<p>The request includes a payload with the username and password, and the response includes the message &#8220;Account does not exist”, with a code for “tel_not_exist.”</p>


<div class="wp-block-image">
<figure class="aligncenter size-full"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" width="2070" height="915" src="https://www.silentpush.com/wp-content/uploads/70-cartier-phishing-page.png" alt="" class="wp-image-8028"/><figcaption class="wp-element-caption"><em><em>Cartier phishing page</em></em></figcaption></figure></div>


<p>Entering login credentials and submitting the form at the Rakuten phishing page (ebate.ebatshop[.]com/login), makes a request to an “auth” login endpoint (ebate.ebatshop[.]com/api/cat/v1/auth/login).</p>



<p>The page and directory structure of “/API/cat/v1/auth/login”, the request with a payload containing the username and password, and the response message are all the same as those seen on the Cartier phishing page – further indicating a common threat actor behind these FUNNULL-hosted retail phishing websites.</p>


<div class="wp-block-image">
<figure class="aligncenter size-full"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" width="2070" height="1107" src="https://www.silentpush.com/wp-content/uploads/71-rakuten-phishing-page.png" alt="" class="wp-image-8029"/><figcaption class="wp-element-caption"><em><em>Rakuten phishing page</em></em></figcaption></figure></div>


<p>The Aldo phishing page (aldo.shopaldo1[.]com/login) and request results made to the “auth” login endpoint (aldo.shopaldo1[.]com/api/cat/v1/auth/login) also yield the same page, directory structure of “/api/cat/v1/auth/login”, message, and code as seen on the Cartier and Rakuten phishing pages.</p>


<div class="wp-block-image">
<figure class="aligncenter size-full"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" width="2420" height="1120" src="https://www.silentpush.com/wp-content/uploads/72-aldo-phishing-page.png" alt="" class="wp-image-8030"/><figcaption class="wp-element-caption"><em><em>Aldo phishing page</em></em></figcaption></figure></div>


<p>Our Analysts discovered one final commonality between these phishing web pages which further cements our hypothesis that they are operated by the same threat actor.</p>



<p>For operational security purposes, however, we are only sharing this information with our Enterprise clients and law enforcement.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="Polyfill-supply-chain-scandal-affects-110,000+-websites,-raises-additional-questions-about-FUNNULL">Polyfill supply chain scandal affects 110,000+ websites, raises additional questions about FUNNULL</h2>



<p>In <a href="https://www.securityweek.com/polyfill-supply-chain-attack-hits-over-100k-websites/">February 2024</a>, FUNNULL took over <a href="https://x.com/triblondon/status/1761852117579427975">polyfill[.]io</a>, <a href="https://x.com/triblondon/status/1761852117579427975">a legacy domain used for JavaScript code embedded into hundreds of thousands of the biggest websites on the Internet</a>.</p>



<p>FUNNULL modified the JavaScript polyfill[.]js to inject malware onto mobile devices. A few months later, <a href="https://sansec.io/research/polyfill-supply-chain-attack">the first reports of malicious redirects fired through this JavaScript/domain were made public.</a></p>



<p>The Founder of SanSec, Willem de Groot, whose company was the first to report about the malicious polyfill[.]io redirections, <a href="https://x.com/gwillem/status/1805741224189739170">clarified on Twitter that the redirections were to an Asian gambling website</a>, writing, <em>“Except for some laughably bad monetization? They controlled 110k sites, but redirected mobile users to an Asian gambling site.”</em></p>



<p>More than <a href="https://thehackernews.com/2024/06/over-110000-websites-affected-by.html">110,000 websites</a> that have polyfill[.]js library embedded in their systems have been affected by the polyfill[.]io supply chain attack, which redirects some mobile users to an “Asian gambling website.”</p>



<p>While popular CDNs (such as Cloudflare, Fastly, or Amazon Cloudfront) play legitimate roles in routing traffic across the internet, less reputable CDNs can add a layer of complexity to rooting out malicious threat actors. With no clear process provided on its website, FUNNULL CDN makes takedown requests of suspicious sites more difficult – if not impossible – to secure.</p>



<p><a href="https://www.bleepingcomputer.com/news/security/polyfillio-javascript-supply-chain-attack-impacts-over-100k-sites/">Bleeping Computer</a> reported that when polyfill[.]io was purchased, project developer Andrew Betts warned that he never owned the polyfill[.]io site and advised all websites to remove it immediately.&nbsp;To reduce the risk of a potential supply chain attack, <a href="https://cdnjs.cloudflare.com/polyfill/">Cloudflare</a> and <a href="https://community.fastly.com/t/new-options-for-polyfill-io-users/2540">Fastly</a> set up their own mirrors of the polyfill[.]io service so websites could use a trusted service.</p>



<p><a href="https://www.itpro.com/security/millions-of-sites-couldve-been-exposed-in-the-polyfill-bootcdn-bootcss-and-staticfile-attack-and-it-was-all-orchestrated-by-a-single-operator">Google</a> is actively blocking ads for e-commerce sites that use the polyfill[.]js library. It issued a statement about the vulnerability, “Protecting our users is our top priority. We detected a security issue recently that may affect websites using certain third-party libraries. To help potentially impacted advertisers secure their websites, we have been proactively sharing information on how to quickly mitigate the issue.” <strong><mark></mark></strong></p>



<p>Cybersecurity company Wiz <a href="https://threats.wiz.io/all-incidents/funnull-polyfill-supply-chain-attack">mapped out the details of the FUNNULL CDN infrastructure seen in these </a>malicious redirections. The same organization that owned polyfill[.]io also owned four similar sites for hosting developer resources: bootcdn[.]net, bootcss[.]com, staticfile[.]net, and staticfile[.]org. As of this writing, malicious redirects were not documented on the four other domains.</p>


<div class="wp-block-image">
<figure class="aligncenter size-full"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" width="2347" height="1136" src="https://www.silentpush.com/wp-content/uploads/73-polyfill-wiz-map-funnull-cdn-infra.png" alt="" class="wp-image-8031"/><figcaption class="wp-element-caption"><em><em>WIZ map of FUNNULL CDN infrastructure</em></em></figcaption></figure></div>


<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="Additional-information">Additional information</h2>



<p>We&#8217;re continuing to track FUNNULL CDN’s activity, and we&#8217;ll be reporting our findings to the security community in a series of follow-up reports.</p>



<p>We’ve also published a <a href="https://help.silentpush.com/docs/tlp-amber-reports"><strong>TLP Amber report</strong></a> for Enterprise users that contains links to the specific queries we’ve used to identify and traverse Triad Nexus&#8217; FUNNULL CDN’s infrastructure—including proprietary queries that we’ve omitted from this blog for operational security reasons.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="Mitigation">Mitigation</h2>



<p>Silent Push believes all FUNNULL domains offer some level of risk.</p>



<p>Our analysts have constructed a <a href="https://www.silentpush.com/blog/iofa-feed-analytics/">Silent Push IOFA Feed</a> that provides a partial list of FUNNULL Indicator of Future Attack domains focused on their scams, along with an IOFA Feed containing suspect FUNNULL IPs.</p>



<p><em><strong>Silent Push IOFA Feeds are available as part of an Enterprise subscription. Enterprise users can ingest IOFA Feed data into their security stack to inform their detection protocols, or use it to pivot across attacker infrastructure using the Silent Push Console and <a href="https://www.silentpush.com/blog/iofa-feed-analytics/">Feed Analytics</a> screen.</strong></em></p>
