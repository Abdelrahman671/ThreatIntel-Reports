<p><em>Updated 23 Nov 1355 MST: Added some additional observations related to logon spoofing infrastructure.</em></p>



<p>Domain “hunting” is a process of identifying new (or at least, newly identified) network infrastructure associated with threat actors of interest. Such a process does not start in a void, but rather requires understanding tendencies and patterns associated with adversary infrastructure creation and management. This is especially effective when viewing individual network observables &#8211; or indicators &#8211; as <a href="https://www.domaintools.com/resources/blog/analyzing-network-infrastructure-as-composite-objects/">natural composite objects</a>, items that accrue multiple sub-observations relating to the given object’s creation, use, and potentially even <a href="https://www.domaintools.com/resources/blog/extrapolating-adversary-intent-through-infrastructure/">intention</a>.</p>



<p>One historical example of such activity is <a href="https://threatconnect.com/blog/using-fancy-bear-ssl-certificate-information-to-identify-their-infrastructure/">ThreatConnect’s analysis</a> of (then) long-running infrastructure tendencies linked to <a href="https://attack.mitre.org/groups/G0007/">APT28</a>, also known as <a href="https://www.crowdstrike.com/blog/who-is-fancy-bear/">FancyBear</a>, but <a href="https://media.defense.gov/2020/Aug/13/2002476465/-1/-1/0/CSA_DROVORUB_RUSSIAN_GRU_MALWARE_AUG_2020.PDF">associated with Russian Military Intelligence (GRU) 85th Main Special Service Center</a> (GTsSS). ThreatConnect’s reporting publicized patterns used by intelligence professionals for several years prior, using a combination of x509 certificate information, domain registration tendencies, and domain hosting patterns to identify new APT28 infrastructure with high confidence as it was created. Unfortunately, the adversary largely migrated away from these patterns shortly after the blog’s publication, but the overall idea remains a solid mechanism to <a href="https://www.gigamon.com/content/dam/resource-library/english/white-paper/wp-intelligence-driven-threat-hunting-methodology.pdf">systematize external threat hunting</a> as well as implementing an <a href="https://pylos.co/wp-content/uploads/2021/02/pivoting.pdf">intelligence-driven pivoting process</a>.</p>



<p>There are multiple ways to approach domain hunting and tracking. One reasonable mechanism is to utilize internal visibility of newly-observed network objects or external feeds such as <a href="https://www.domaintools.com/products/platform/iris-investigate/">DomainTools</a> to look for infrastructure objects fitting certain patterns based on domain sub-characteristics. Using this methodology, the following domain came to light on 22 November 2022:</p>



<pre class="wp-block-code"><code>msn-imap&#91;.]com</code></pre>



<p>Even at first glance, this appears suspicious given naming conventions, spoofing a combination of <a href="https://www.msn.com/en-us">MSN</a> and <a href="https://support.microsoft.com/en-us/office/what-are-imap-and-pop-ca2c5799-49f9-4079-aefe-ddca85d5b1c9">IMAP</a> services. Further research, in this case using DomainTools Iris Investigate, shows further details that call this item out as likely malicious:</p>



<figure class="wp-block-image"><img decoding="async" src="https://lh4.googleusercontent.com/8uC0qeZgttL27nB91D8c5Cjsk1Z2tR-9rATBZV41WY9jiuawc1QcmmXzQzg-3LOk4EYLqGjzpvA7p_5a0gqxAEJXJ7ZH8B3BZOPKcH1Zo2zUE00KMY5wAR6QoGTi6Pz7JinhKxeQ5LNkQidnvw7RNvKCdQqrsaTgPYG9hZQY1m0osUUdqiEXd__OrESAzg" alt=""/><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">DomainTools Iris Screenshot</figcaption></figure>



<figure class="wp-block-image"><img decoding="async" src="https://lh5.googleusercontent.com/Tntl26QFEdPhgq3CZad6Q1FQnjWGqU_JF5qQY4ij5tFVkX3f7kEhhO-Mjv19cK-8Asv3k0VPIKiPUunPKRtvpenCrtFkN98B0B9B-1BjSbNHMLbN9tNaFNL6EdZ7y_BYRHG3kUiy6Zlu2yWNikSW5_qnSRAByobBkIFGkq3xbuwz3F6q_WTTVrhDFOqsyQ" alt=""/><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">DomainTools Iris ScreenShot</figcaption></figure>



<p>We can spot several items that look suspicious here &#8211; anonymized registration, dedicated hosting (on IP address 92.38.135.213), suspicious authoritative name server use &#8211; but unfortunately there’s very little to pivot on to learn more about this item (or identify related infrastructure) using just domain information.</p>



<p>We can dig further by looking at the hosting address. In this case, using <a href="https://search.censys.io/">Censys Search</a> we can profile this further:</p>



<figure class="wp-block-image"><img decoding="async" src="https://lh4.googleusercontent.com/TuRQuwnfxKsuuKUw4dVkQqAtTzUxLI0pO2C9thOvg16eA-D6QukZIwPMfgxNAjJNVyk4iZ2DHCQr09xPvrdOar2KgVcpSeTKvjg5i30fMoadXTAhCebSBEzR9M0fAdB0nDRS3KZeCrBB5zmeZ3kcg5zLNCUAKmxSUk-7xtSIkWvTP4RYQCUpvkQqPcJhfw" alt=""/><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">Censys Search Screenshot</figcaption></figure>



<figure class="wp-block-image"><img decoding="async" src="https://lh3.googleusercontent.com/0BtqbtK2SMSpDWno1_5mzFP2BBLcW4SxLA_ql7DD8a1Bmx6VA75fKwkjUCDwBcoi7tqmxjCIhHciayKRfK-bPOSiQ_P0YQgogwvgU0Cau__AEewxemovElJoRzdmgz8AqwDmoRJ2zcbNVhUmIGqwRPBQQSuWaTOOGOiUFBFwW1uEzgwYNtvbx5inPJtdjg" alt=""/><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">Censys Search Screenshot</figcaption></figure>



<figure class="wp-block-image"><img decoding="async" src="https://lh4.googleusercontent.com/ZzwZv44vBt9ricPeQGFQ0qC9UF37CUAVSdXo0FLGL1AJxMq_mgJkm0EtRsP2y3o9cSm6YHz_Eqz8atUj0Sv3xRZZCXS2kVyPXUC3_J44bDMs9pfx9u6QC1kF7Sg2nxa9IjEv38161DJ_fhze_L7CSTorPnVshzbcbuhq-5njuYx731fIeTOTxPnwzTfcVw" alt=""/><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">Censys Search Screenshot</figcaption></figure>



<p>Now we’re starting to get more details on how this object might be used by an adversary, as well as other observables that can be used for searching, hunting, and follow-on pivoting. Among other items, we’ve learned the following:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>The adversary’s infrastructure characteristics:
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Ubuntu Linux</li>



<li>Postfix SMTP server</li>



<li>Apache HTTP/HTTPS server</li>



<li>Use of Let’s Encrypt SSL/TLS certificates</li>
</ul>
</li>



<li>JA3S hashes for various TLS services</li>



<li>Additional potential indicators, such as the domain onkrdot[.]info associated with the SMTP server</li>
</ul>



<p>One item that immediately stands out is the SMTP server. Given our original domain’s email theme, we can hypothesize that this server may be utilized for future phishing infrastructure or email relay activity. However, we also have HTTP/HTTPS servers that appear active &#8211; but in a strange way. As seen in the above screenshot, an HTTPS request returns a status code of 200 (success), but the page content (based on the HTML title) says “404 Not Found.” What is going on here?</p>



<p>To simplify our research, we can utilize another service &#8211; <a href="https://urlscan.io/">urlscan.io</a> &#8211; to handle our interactions for us. And this serves up something strange:</p>



<figure class="wp-block-image"><img decoding="async" src="https://lh6.googleusercontent.com/ARGsT7RTj5HSjaiZKpnnosFc8bG3FvszRmDW_mPndlwQlPgZW2qSDDCDB7N6EQ8ak5Lj0nHSXorayv5DhJy18nW1M_t8NC-1bhqwfuwbxwX_wYM9CvAJ6m1ZRKyCJIQ3vOZJ0EGb2E6VZElNhHHgq-ovjz9d3Ktapm4otcbPFxxFE9f0RfOBkkWOH3e5DA" alt=""/><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">Website Capture from URLScan</figcaption></figure>



<p>This may seem unhelpful, but we’ve identified an interesting mismatch. Examination of the server through application fingerprinting indicates we are interacting with an <a href="https://httpd.apache.org/">Apache webserver</a>, while the server itself is displaying a custom webpage modeled off of (but not exactly mirroring) an <a href="https://docs.nginx.com/nginx/admin-guide/web-server/">Ngnix webserver</a> 404 landing page. While not a sign of obvious maliciousness, this mismatch and customization provides an interesting foothold for further exploration. One easy follow-on item lies within urlscan itself, where we can look for instances of similar landing pages based on the content hash of the delivered page &#8211; 9b43f670273b6a12b2b6894a9e29157c1859717594e98ccc5fb3eea05e71f4ed. This reveals something VERY interesting:</p>



<figure class="wp-block-image"><img decoding="async" src="https://lh3.googleusercontent.com/hT_iLV6Kd5a61b3yIHlCXjAD2ZvF5wR-pVF-gC5cZlcbZ7Yma0CzZWJEFtUfo5Hl7QGrFiXp_ZAP86UIhpI_4ppsKuUgqOnoDCUumepR44BGP0QrZVUFT8zsQYUJsN7dxDHs1-O2ry1qdRS8sYTqvLsA31zxHrsFIaxtSKwRaDCBmpqOkRbKCzw-4cm87g" alt=""/><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">URLScan Pivot Results</figcaption></figure>



<p>We seem to have stumbled upon something reasonably unique, and linked to a variety of additional infrastructure &#8211; many of which spoof a variety of legitimate services. Among the items covered in this, we can see:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Korean web portal <a href="https://www.daum.net/">Daum</a>&nbsp;</li>



<li>Korean web portal <a href="https://www.naver.com/">Naver</a></li>



<li>Google services</li>



<li>Various mail, cloud, and certificate themes</li>
</ul>



<p>Infrastructure is overwhelmingly concentrated in Korean or East Asian hosting providers, and all items appear to be created between March and November 2022 (see Table 1 below for a list of all identified indicators).</p>



<p>Additionally, looking at pDNS records (in this case from VirusTotal) for the IP addresses from urlscan shows additional infrastructure of interest likely linked to this campaign:</p>



<figure class="wp-block-image"><img decoding="async" src="https://lh4.googleusercontent.com/ktqG55z0ayy-qcYdjkf_B0ayeB6QVFh-N9KU0KGeggWDDbYLg5goCt7MOD7YYWZjT7qBKbtLfc3ZVEsQIBwDdzoBpfQ0ATw8KExvY5KmhSFqn-xiq4hLxE7mU90yfEQVacKayIvxdmR02sAES80vubZxAtW7rphKwbWlylWp99yhCWNOZhPpfy8oSysn7w" alt=""/><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">VirusTotal pDNS Information</figcaption></figure>



<p>At this stage we’ve collected a lot of information about various infrastructure created (and potentially used) in 2022 with similar themes, characteristics, and other observables. Yet it is important not to lose overall context as to <em>what </em>we might be looking at &#8211; so some external enrichment and research is required to learn more.</p>



<p>With no actual threat to go off of (yet), we can start our search looking for entities that typically host phishing infrastructure (either for sending email, or as landing pages for links) in East Asia (and especially South Korea), that focus on spoofing legitimate services with an emphasis on South Korean major web portals. Based on <a href="https://www.cisa.gov/uscert/ncas/alerts/aa20-301a">multiple</a> <a href="https://www.malwarebytes.com/blog/threat-intelligence/2021/06/kimsuky-apt-continues-to-target-south-korean-government-using-appleseed-backdoor">reports</a> from <a href="https://www.virusbulletin.com/virusbulletin/2020/03/vb2019-paper-kimsuky-group-tracking-king-spearphishing/">various entities</a>, one threat group stands out matching these characteristics: <a href="https://www.cisa.gov/uscert/ncas/alerts/aa20-301a">North Korean-related</a> entity <a href="https://malpedia.caad.fkie.fraunhofer.de/actor/kimsuky">Kimsuky</a>.</p>



<p>While we cannot be certain at this stage, based on an initial suspicious feeling around one suspect domain, we have uncovered an entire ecosystem of related infrastructure that may be related to an in-progress Kimsuky-associated campaign, likely with a focus on website spoofing and phishing. Defenders, especially those with reason to believe they may be targeted by this North Korean-linked threat actor, should take the indicators provided in Table 1 and search historical logs to see if they have interacted with any of these infrastructure items as an initial defensive measure. Going forward, threat intelligence researchers can incorporate the characteristics in infrastructure creation documented in this report and the various linked resources to build a new hunting-and-pivoting profile for infrastructure related to this entity. Overall, network indicator research and refinement can yield fantastic results if you know both where to look, and what to look for.</p>
