      <h1><span id="hs_cos_wrapper_name" class="hs_cos_wrapper hs_cos_wrapper_meta_field hs_cos_wrapper_type_text" style="" data-hs-cos-general-type="meta_field" data-hs-cos-type="text">Royal Rumble: Analysis of Royal Ransomware</span></h1>
      <div class="cr-mln__post-author-share">
      <div id="hubspot-author_data" class="hubspot-editable cr-mln__post-meta" data-hubspot-form-id="author_data" data-hubspot-name="Blog Author">
        
          <span class="descriptor">Written By</span>
          <p><span class="author">Cybereason Global SOC &amp; Cybereason Security Research Teams</span></p>
         
      </div>
    </div>
     
    </div>
    
    <!-- Sticky Author and Social Box -->
    
    <!-- END Sticky Author and Social Box -->
      <div class="container-is-blog columns is-multiline page-center cr-mln__blog-post--body">
        
        <div class="column is-7-fullhd is-7-desktop is-10-tablet is-10-mobile is-offset-1-fullhd is-offset-1-desktop is-offset-1-tablet is-offset-1-mobile">
          <span id="hs_cos_wrapper_post_body" class="hs_cos_wrapper hs_cos_wrapper_meta_field hs_cos_wrapper_type_rich_text" style="" data-hs-cos-general-type="meta_field" data-hs-cos-type="rich_text"><p>The Royal ransomware group emerged in early 2022 and has gained momentum since the middle of the year. Its ransomware, which the group deploys through different TTPs, has impacted multiple organizations across the globe. The group itself is suspected of consisting of former members of other ransomware groups, based on similarities researchers have observed between Royal ransomware and other ransomware operators.</p>
<p><!--more--></p>
<h2>Key Findings</h2>
<ul>
<li aria-level="1">
<p>Unique approach to evade anti-ransomware defenses: Royal ransomware expands the concept of partial encryption, which means it has the ability to encrypt a pre-determined portion of the file content and base its partial encryption on a flexible percentage encryption, which makes detection more challenging for anti-ransomware solutions.</p>
</li>
<li aria-level="1">
<p>Multi-threaded ransomware: Royal ransomware employs multiple threads in order to accelerate the encryption process.</p>
</li>
<li aria-level="1">
<p>Global ransomware operation: Royal ransomware operates around the world, and reportedly on its own. The group doesn’t appear to use ransomware-as-a-service or to target a specific sector or country.</p>
</li>
<li aria-level="1">
<p>Different methods of deployment: Royal ransomware initially starts and deploys in different ways, as described in this report.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<h2>INTRODUCTION</h2>
<p>The Royal ransomware group was first discovered in early 2022. At the time, it <a href="https://www.bleepingcomputer.com/news/security/new-royal-ransomware-emerges-in-multi-million-dollar-attacks/">utilized </a>third-party ransomware, such as BlackCat and custom Zeon ransomware. Since September 2022, the group has started to use its own ransomware. In November 2022, Royal ransomware was <a href="https://twitter.com/ido_cohen2/status/1599404888617758721?t=AasRHMIDIIunfLRi4TEuiA&amp;s=31">reported </a>to be the most prolific ransomware in the e-crime landscape, overtaking Lockbit for the first time in more than a year.</p>
<p>Royal ransomware operations start in different ways. One method is through phishing campaigns and uses one of the common e-crime threat loaders, <a href="https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/security/blog/2022/11/17/dev-0569-finds-new-ways-to-deliver-royal-ransomware-various-payloads/">reportedly </a>BATLOADER and <a href="https://blog.bushidotoken.net/2022/11/the-continuity-of-conti.html">Qbot</a>. The threat loader then downloads a Cobalt Strike payload to continue the malicious operation within the infected environment. This tactic is commonly used by other ransomware operations, including <a href="https://www.cybereason.com/blog/threat-alert-aggressive-qakbot-campaign-and-the-black-basta-ransomware-group-targeting-u.s.-companies">Qbot and BlackBasta</a>.</p>
<p>Since mid-September, the ransomware group has gained momentum and added dozens of victims to its website. The group does not seem to focus on a specific sector, and its victims vary from industrial companies to insurance companies, and more. Although the majority of the group’s victims are based in the U.S., one of its higher profile victims was the <a href="https://cybernews.com/news/silverstone-formula-one-ransomware/">Silverstone Circuit</a>, a motor racing circuit in England.</p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><img src="https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Royal%20Ransomware%20Image%201.png?width=857&amp;height=377&amp;name=Royal%20Ransomware%20Image%201.png" alt="Royal Ransomware Image 1" width="857" height="377" loading="lazy" style="height: auto; max-width: 100%; width: 857px; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto; display: block;" srcset="https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Royal%20Ransomware%20Image%201.png?width=429&amp;height=189&amp;name=Royal%20Ransomware%20Image%201.png 429w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Royal%20Ransomware%20Image%201.png?width=857&amp;height=377&amp;name=Royal%20Ransomware%20Image%201.png 857w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Royal%20Ransomware%20Image%201.png?width=1286&amp;height=566&amp;name=Royal%20Ransomware%20Image%201.png 1286w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Royal%20Ransomware%20Image%201.png?width=1714&amp;height=754&amp;name=Royal%20Ransomware%20Image%201.png 1714w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Royal%20Ransomware%20Image%201.png?width=2143&amp;height=943&amp;name=Royal%20Ransomware%20Image%201.png 2143w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Royal%20Ransomware%20Image%201.png?width=2571&amp;height=1131&amp;name=Royal%20Ransomware%20Image%201.png 2571w" sizes="(max-width: 857px) 100vw, 857px"><em><span style="font-size: 16px;">Screenshot from Royal Ransomware website showing Silverstone Circuit as a victim</span></em></p>
<p>Multiple <a href="https://blog.bushidotoken.net/2022/11/the-continuity-of-conti.html">reports </a>have noted resemblances between the Royal Ransomware group and <a href="https://www.cybereason.com/blog/research/cybereason-vs.-conti-ransomware">Conti</a>, including similarities between the ransom notes each group uses (particularly in Royal’s early stages) and the use of <a href="https://www.bleepingcomputer.com/news/security/callback-phishing-attacks-evolve-their-social-engineering-tactics/">callback phishing </a>attacks. In our research, we have identified additional similarities, such as resemblances in the encryption process decision factors. However, these similarities are not yet clear enough to confirm a direct connection between the two groups.</p>
<h2>TECHNICAL ANALYSIS</h2>
<h4 style="font-size: 30px;">Setting up the ransomware</h4>
<p>When executing, Royal ransomware can take three arguments in its command line:</p>
<ul>
<li aria-level="1">
<p>-path [optional]: The path to be encrypted</p>
</li>
<li aria-level="1">
<p>-ep [optional]: The number that represents the percentage of the file that will be encrypted</p>
</li>
<li aria-level="1">
<p>-id: A 32-digit array</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p>The encryption process decision tree is dependent on the command line arguments. Therefore, factors such as encryption speed, file corruption, and potential detection are directly affected. If no “-id” parameter is given in the command line, the ransomware won’t run.</p>

<p>Royal ransomware encryption is multi-threaded. To choose the number of running threads, the ransomware will use the API call GetNativeSystemInfo to collect the number of processors in a machine. It will then multiply the result by two and will create the appropriate number of threads accordingly.</p>

<p>Next, the ransomware will set the RSA public key, which is embedded in the binary in plain text and will be used for encrypting the AES key.</p>

<p>Before starting the encryption process, Royal ransomware uses the Windows Restart Manager to check if any of the targeted files to be encrypted are being used or blocked by other applications or services. Notably, other ransomware groups including <a href="https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/stamp/stamp.jsp?arnumber=9895237" rel="noopener" target="_blank">Conti</a>, <a href="https://chuongdong.com/reverse%20engineering/2021/01/03/BabukRansomware/" rel="noopener" target="_blank">Babuk</a>, and <a href="https://chuongdong.com/reverse%20engineering/2022/03/19/LockbitRansomware/" rel="noopener" target="_blank">Lockbit</a> use Restart Manager for the same purpose. Royal then uses the API calls RmStartSession to start the session, RmRegisterResources to register the resources (i.e., the targeted files), RmGetList to verify which applications or services are using the resource (excluding “explorer.exe”), and RmShutDown to kill those applications and services using the resource.</p>

<p>Finally, the encryption method will be determined by the size of the file that will be encrypted and in consideration of the -ep parameter.</p>
<ul>
<li aria-level="1">
<p>If the file size is smaller than 5.245 MB or the -ep argument equals 100:, the entire file will be encrypted.</p>
</li>
<li aria-level="1">
<p>If the file size is larger than 5.245 MB and the -ep argument is not equal to 100:, the file will be encrypted approximately by the percentage of the -ep argument.</p>
</li>
<li aria-level="1">
<p>If the file size is larger than 5.245 MB and no -ep argument is given, 50% of the file will be encrypted.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p style="text-align: center;"><span style="font-family: Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif;"><img src="https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog%20Images%20Royal%20Ransomware/Royal%20Ransomware%20Image%2014.png?width=656&amp;height=220&amp;name=Royal%20Ransomware%20Image%2014.png" alt="Royal Ransomware Image 14" width="656" height="220" loading="lazy" style="height: auto; max-width: 100%; width: 656px; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto; display: block;" srcset="https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog%20Images%20Royal%20Ransomware/Royal%20Ransomware%20Image%2014.png?width=328&amp;height=110&amp;name=Royal%20Ransomware%20Image%2014.png 328w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog%20Images%20Royal%20Ransomware/Royal%20Ransomware%20Image%2014.png?width=656&amp;height=220&amp;name=Royal%20Ransomware%20Image%2014.png 656w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog%20Images%20Royal%20Ransomware/Royal%20Ransomware%20Image%2014.png?width=984&amp;height=330&amp;name=Royal%20Ransomware%20Image%2014.png 984w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog%20Images%20Royal%20Ransomware/Royal%20Ransomware%20Image%2014.png?width=1312&amp;height=440&amp;name=Royal%20Ransomware%20Image%2014.png 1312w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog%20Images%20Royal%20Ransomware/Royal%20Ransomware%20Image%2014.png?width=1640&amp;height=550&amp;name=Royal%20Ransomware%20Image%2014.png 1640w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog%20Images%20Royal%20Ransomware/Royal%20Ransomware%20Image%2014.png?width=1968&amp;height=660&amp;name=Royal%20Ransomware%20Image%2014.png 1968w" sizes="(max-width: 656px) 100vw, 656px"></span><span style="font-size: 16px;"><em>Decision making in encryption</em></span></p>
<p>When a targeted file is being encrypted, the ransomware calculates the percentage to encrypt and divides the file content (encrypted and unencrypted) into equal segments. The fragmentation and possibly low percentage of encrypted file content that results lowers the chance of being detected by anti-ransomware solutions.</p>

<p>While <a href="https://www.esecurityplanet.com/threats/ransomware-intermittent-encryption/" rel="noopener" target="_blank">partial encryption</a> is not new, most ransomware base their partial encryption only on the file size, then encrypt a set percentage of the file &nbsp;the same way, each time. In contrast, Royal ransomware lets the operator choose a specific percentage and lower the amount of encrypted data even if the file size is large. This ability to change the amount of the file to be encrypted gives Royal ransomware an advantage when it comes to evading detection by security products.</p>
<p>It’s worth highlighting that Conti ransomware also chooses the file size of 5.24 MB as its threshold for partial encryption. When a file was larger than 5.24MB, Conti would encrypt 50% of the file in a divided manner, much like Royal ransomware. This similarity raises the question of whether the Royal ransomware authors have a connection to the Conti group, but on its own, it is not strong enough to suggest a direct or definitive connection.</p>
<p>As for the encryption algorithm, Royal ransomware uses the OpenSSL library and the AES256 algorithm. Similar to other ransomware, it first reads the targeted file using ReadFile, then it encrypts the content and writes the encrypted data in the designated location using WriteFile and SetFilePointerEx. After finishing encryption, the file extension changes to “.royal” using the API call MoveFileExW.</p>

<h2>CONCLUSION</h2>
<p>When it comes to partial encryption, Royal ransomware seems to give the ransomware operator a more flexible solution for evading detection compared to most ransomware. We assume this flexibility and the evasion potential it enables was a design goal for the creators of Royal ransomware.</p>
<p>As with some reports mentioned above, some ideas that were implemented in Conti ransomware can be found in Royal ransomware.</p>

<p>The Cybereason GSOC &amp; Security Research teams recommend the following actions in the Cybereason Defense Platform:</p>
<ul>
<li aria-level="1">
<p>Enable Application Control to block the execution of malicious files.</p>
</li>
<li aria-level="1">
<p>Enable Anti-Ransomware in your environment’s policies, set the <a href="/platform/ransomware-protection" rel="noopener" target="_blank">Anti-Ransomware</a> mode to Prevent, and enable Shadow Copy detection to ensure maximum protection against ransomware.</p>
</li>
<li aria-level="1">
<p>Enable Variant Payload Prevention with prevent mode on Cybereason Behavioral execution prevention.</p>
</li>
<li aria-level="1">
<p>To hunt proactively, use the Investigation screen in the Cybereason Defense Platform and the queries in the Hunting Queries section to search for machines that are potentially infected with Royal Ransomware.</p>
</li>
<ul>
<li aria-level="2">
<p>Based on the search results, take further remediation actions, such as isolating the infected machines and deleting the payload file.</p>
