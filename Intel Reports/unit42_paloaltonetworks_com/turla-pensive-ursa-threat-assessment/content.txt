function addStyle(styles) {

    css.type = 'text/css';

}

    <span class="wpml-ls-slot-post_translations wpml-ls-item wpml-ls-item-ja wpml-ls-first-item wpml-ls-last-item wpml-ls-item-legacy-post-translations"><a href="https://unit42.paloaltonetworks.jp/turla-pensive-ursa-threat-assessment/" class="wpml-ls-link"><span class="wpml-ls-native" lang="ja">日本語</span><span class="wpml-ls-display"><span class="wpml-ls-bracket"> (</span>Japanese<span class="wpml-ls-bracket">)</span></span></a></span></p><h2><a id="post-129929-_vhls3xaco5nl"></a>Executive Summary</h2>
<p>Turla (aka Pensive Ursa, Uroburos, Snake) is a Russian-based threat group operating since at least 2004, which is <a href="https://www.justice.gov/usao-edny/pr/justice-department-announces-court-authorized-disruption-snake-malware-network">linked to the Russian Federal Security Service (FSB)</a>. In this article, we will cover the top 10 most recently active types of malware in Pensive Ursa’s arsenal: Capibar, Kazuar, Snake, Kopiluwak, QUIETCANARY/Tunnus, Crutch, ComRAT, Carbon, HyperStack and TinyTurla.</p>
<p><a href="https://attackevals.mitre-engenuity.org/enterprise/turla/">Pensive Ursa</a> was chosen to be the main focus for the 2023 MITRE ATT&amp;CK evaluation. MITRE has described Turla as being “known for their targeted intrusions and innovative stealth.” The results of this evaluation, including Palo Alto Networks scoring, will be published in late September 2023.</p>
<p>In addition to describing each type of malware’s functionality and history, we will present their execution through the lens of the Palo Alto Networks Cortex XDR product. We will show how Cortex protects against such malware, and the MITRE ATT&amp;CK mapping of such threats as shown in the Cortex XDR platform.</p>
<p>Palo Alto Networks customers receive protections from Pensive Ursa’s arsenal and the techniques discussed in this blog through <a href="https://docs-cortex.paloaltonetworks.com/p/XDR">Cortex XDR</a>, which provides a multilayer defense that includes behavioral threat protection and exploit protection.</p>
<p>The Advanced WildFire cloud-delivered malware analysis service accurately identifies samples related to Pensive Ursa as malicious. Cloud-Delivered Security Services, including Advanced URL Filtering and DNS Security, identify domains associated with this group as malicious.</p>

<h2><a id="post-129929-_bk7at4ye99bx"></a>Pensive Ursa (aka Turla) Overview</h2>
<p>Over the years, Pensive Ursa has become known as an advanced and elusive adversary. The group has demonstrated a high level of technical expertise, while orchestrating targeted and stealthy attacks.</p>
<p><a href="https://attack.mitre.org/groups/G0010/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">As described by MITRE</a>, Pensive Ursa targeted victims in over 45 countries as well as a wide range of sectors, including government entities, embassies, and military organizations, as well as education, research and pharmaceutical companies. In addition, this threat group had an active part in the Russian-Ukraine conflict that started in February 2022. <a href="https://cert.gov.ua/article/5213167" target="_blank" rel="noopener">According to the Ukraine CERT</a>, Pensive Ursa leveraged espionage attacks against Ukrainian targets, specifically against <a href="https://therecord.media/turla-hackers-targeting-ukraine-defense" target="_blank" rel="noopener">their defense sector</a>.</p>
<p>While Pensive Ursa mainly used their espionage arsenal to target Windows machines, the group also has tools that can attack macOS and Linux machines.</p>
<h2><a id="post-129929-_r70lj73ion26"></a>MITRE ATT&amp;CK Evaluation</h2>
<p>For the 2023 MITRE ATT&amp;CK evaluation, Pensive Ursa was chosen to be the main focus. <a href="https://attackevals.mitre-engenuity.org/enterprise/turla/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">According to MITRE</a>, this threat group is particularly relevant as their actions have global impact.</p>
<p>Below are the top 10 most recently active types of malware in the team’s arsenal. For each type of malware, we provided a short description and analysis, as well as how Cortex XDR detects and prevents the threat.</p>

<h3><a id="post-129929-_tv1mkgy4etjk"></a><strong>Malware:</strong> <strong>Capibar</strong></h3>
<p><strong>Aliases:</strong> DeliveryCheck, GAMEDAY</p>
<p><strong>Malware Type:</strong> Backdoor</p>
<p><strong>First Seen:</strong> 2022</p>
<p><strong>Description:</strong> Capibar (aka DeliveryCheck, GAMEDAY) is a Pensive Ursa backdoor that was first observed in 2022, and used for the purpose of espionage against defense forces in Ukraine. They distributed it via email as documents with malicious macros.</p>
<p>Capibar persists via a scheduled task that downloads and launches the payload in memory. The threat group installed Capibar on compromised MS Exchange servers as a Managed Object Format (MOF) file, granting the attacker full control of the server. <h3><a id="post-129929-_xlj44r7o3oti"></a>Malware: Kazuar</h3>
<p><strong>Malware Type:</strong> Backdoor</p>
<p><strong>First Seen: </strong> 2017</p>
<p><strong>Description: </strong><a href="https://unit42.paloaltonetworks.com/unit42-kazuar-multiplatform-espionage-backdoor-api-access/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Kazuar is a .NET backdoor</a> that was discovered in 2017. Kazuar provides full access to the compromised systems targeted by its operator. Kazuar comes with a powerful command set that includes the ability to remotely load additional plugins to enhance the backdoor’s capabilities.</p>
<p>In 2021, <a href="https://securelist.com/sunburst-backdoor-kazuar/99981/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">researchers found interesting code overlaps</a> and similarities between Kazuar and the notorious SUNBURST backdoor that a Russian threat group used in the <a href="https://www.mandiant.com/resources/blog/evasive-attacker-leverages-solarwinds-supply-chain-compromises-with-sunburst-backdoor" target="_blank" rel="noopener">SolarWinds Operation</a>. In July 2023, the <a href="https://cert.gov.ua/article/5213167" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Ukrainian CERT uncovered</a> an espionage operation where Pensive Ursa used Kazuar as one of the main backdoors. <h3><a id="post-129929-_ae0tf8q81f5t"></a>Malware: Snake</h3>
<p><strong>Malware Type: </strong>Modular backdoor</p>
<p><strong>First Seen: </strong>2003</p>
<p><strong>Description: </strong>The infamous Snake malware is the most complex tool in Pensive Ursa’s tool set, as <a href="https://www.cisa.gov/news-events/cybersecurity-advisories/aa23-129a" target="_blank" rel="noopener">described by CISA</a> in May 2023. The primary purpose of this tool is to achieve persistence for considerable periods of time and exfiltrate data from dedicated targets. It was in active development for 20 years, since 2003.</p>
<p>Snake was detected operating in more than 50 countries worldwide. The United States <a href="https://www.justice.gov/usao-edny/pr/justice-department-announces-court-authorized-disruption-snake-malware-network" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Department of Justice</a> published a statement in which they announced Operation MEDUSA, where they disrupted the Snake malware activity and peer to peer (P2P) network. They did so by using a tool developed by the FBI dubbed PERSEUS, which they used as a kill switch for the Snake malware.</p>
<p>Based on previous analysis, the Snake malware implemented a maintainable code design, which showed that its authors had a high level of software development capability.</p>
<p>Snake implements features such as the following:</p>
<ul>
<li>A custom implementation of communication protocols over HTTP and TCP</li>
<li>A kernel module for stealth</li>
<li>Key logger functionality</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Malware Type: </strong>Backdoor</p>
<p><strong>First Seen: </strong>2017</p>
<p><strong>Description: </strong>Pensive Ursa has been observed using QUIETCANARY since 2019, and the Tomiris group has used this backdoor even earlier. <h3><a id="post-129929-_kieiqepozfx2"></a>Malware: Kopiluwak</h3>
<p><strong>Malware Type: </strong>Spreader/Downloader</p>
<p><strong>First Seen: </strong>2016</p>
<p><strong>Description: </strong>Kopiluwak malware was discovered in <a href="https://securelist.com/kopiluwak-a-new-javascript-payload-from-turla/77429/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">late 2016</a>, and it was delivered as a multilayered JavaScript payload by various types of droppers.</p>
<p>Pensive Ursa dropped the Kopiluwak malware using an MSIL dropper in 2017 in a <a href="https://www.proofpoint.com/us/threat-insight/post/turla-apt-actor-refreshes-kopiluwak-javascript-backdoor-use-g20-themed-attack" target="_blank" rel="noopener">G20-themed attack</a>, and <a href="https://www.mandiant.com/resources/blog/turla-galaxy-opportunity" target="_blank" rel="noopener">as an SFX executable in late 2022</a>.</p>
<p>Kopiluwak’s JavaScript file is depicted in Figure 10 and the code snippet below, dropped under the <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">C:\Windows\Temp\</span> path. Its purpose is gathering valuable initial profiling information on the infected machine, such as the following:</p>
<ul>
<li>Listing files in strategic locations</li>
<li>Retrieving the current running processes</li>
<li>Displaying active network connections</li>
</ul>
<p>The threat actor accomplished this activity by running reconnaissance commands such as <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">systeminfo</span>, <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">tasklist</span>, <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">net</span>, <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">ipconfig</span>, and <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">dir</span>. The results are saved in a file named <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">result2.dat</span>.</p>

<h3><a id="post-129929-_6bl3ekpu6v74"></a>Malware: Crutch</h3>
<p><strong>Malware Type: </strong>Backdoor</p>
<p><strong>First Seen: </strong>2015</p>
<p><strong>Description: </strong>In December 2020, <a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/2020/12/02/turla-crutch-keeping-back-door-open/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">ESET researchers discovered</a> the Crutch backdoor. In line with Pensive Ursa’s tactics, techniques and procedures (TTPs), the threat actor used the backdoor to attack a handful of targets in Europe, including the Ministry of Foreign Affairs of an EU member.</p>
<p>The main purpose of this backdoor was to eventually steal sensitive files and exfiltrate the data to a Dropbox account controlled by Pensive Ursa operators. Using commercial services such as Dropbox for C2 communication is a known (yet effective) technique due to it being a legitimate service, and blending in with other network communication.</p>
<p>This backdoor was attributed to Pensive Ursa due to strong similarities in code and TTPs with another backdoor from Pensive Ursa’s arsenal called Gazer. Crutch is considered to be a second-stage backdoor, and its persistence is achieved using DLL hijacking.</p>

<p><strong>First Seen: </strong>2007</p>
<figure id="attachment_129966" aria-describedby="caption-attachment-129966" style="width: 900px" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><img decoding="async" class="wp-image-129966" src="https://unit42.paloaltonetworks.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/09/word-image-129929-18.png" alt="Image 17 is a screenshot of a diagram in Cortex XDR. ComRAT was reportedly first <a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/2020/05/26/agentbtz-comratv4-ten-year-journey/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">discovered in 2007</a>. Since then it has had many upgrades. As of 2020, the latest iteration of ComRAT is version 4. This threat is developed in C++ and the threat actor has deployed it using PowerShell implants, such as PowerStallion. Figure 17 shows the PowerShell dropper mechanism. The threat actor’s main purpose of operations when using ComRAT was to steal and exfiltrate confidential documents from high value targets.</p>

<h3><a id="post-129929-_wel6em4jagwl"></a>Malware: Carbon</h3>
<p><strong>Malware Type: </strong>Backdoor</p>
<p><strong>First Seen: </strong>2014</p>
<p><strong>Description: </strong><a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/2017/03/30/carbon-paper-peering-turlas-second-stage-backdoor/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Carbon is a modular backdoor framework</a> that has been used by Pensive Ursa for several years. The Carbon framework includes an installer, an orchestrator component, a communication module and a configuration file.</p>

<p><strong>Malware Type: </strong>Backdoor</p>
<p><strong>First Seen: </strong>2018</p>
<p><strong>Description: </strong>HyperStack (aka SilentMoo, BigBoss) is an <a href="https://www.bleepingcomputer.com/news/security/russian-turla-hackers-breach-european-government-organization/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">RPC backdoor</a> that was first observed in 2018, which the threat actor used in operations targeting government entities in Europe. <h3><a id="post-129929-_o2p9fynjl3d1"></a>Malware: TinyTurla</h3>
<p><strong>Malware Type: </strong>Backdoor</p>
<p><strong>First Seen: </strong>2021</p>
<p><strong>Description: </strong>The TinyTurla malware was first <a href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/tinyturla/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">discovered by Talos</a> in 2021. They assumed it was a second stage backdoor, and it has been seen on targets in the US, EU and later in Asia.</p>
<p>TinyTurla’s main features include the following:</p>
<ul>
<li>Downloading additional payloads</li>
<li>Uploading files to the attacker's C2 server</li>
<li>Executing other processes</li>
</ul>
<p>As shown in Figure 23, threat actors install the backdoor via a batch script as a service called Windows Time Service. The batch script is also in charge of writing the C2 server’s data to the registry. Once the backdoor is executed, it reads these values to communicate with its C2. It masquerades as a DLL called <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">w64time.dll</span>, under the <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">system32</span> folder.</p>
<figure id="attachment_129980" aria-describedby="caption-attachment-129980" style="width: 900px" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><img decoding="async" class="wp-image-129980" src="https://unit42.paloaltonetworks.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/09/word-image-129929-25.png" alt="Figure 23 is a screenshot of 10 lines of code. Content of the batch script described above.</figcaption></figure>
<p>Although <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">w32time.dll</span> is a legitimate DLL, and other legitimate DLLs do have both 32- and 64-bit variants, a legitimate <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">w64time.dll</span> does not exist. This naming convention is intended to further distract victims from suspecting anything is amiss.</p>

<h2><a id="post-129929-_tq0kbthefey"></a>Conclusion</h2>
<p>The Pensive Ursa advanced persistent threat (APT) group is known to be a significant and persistent adversary. With their advanced techniques, this Russian-FSB operated group has demonstrated an evasive modus operandi while targeting a wide range of sectors across the globe.</p>
<p>We explored the top 10 types of malware in Pensive Ursa’s arsenal and witnessed their execution through the lens of Palo Alto Networks Cortex XDR product. This demonstrated the importance of using a multilayered protection model against an advanced threat.</p>
<p>The potential damage of falling victim to a Pensive Ursa APT attack can be significant. The consequences extend beyond financial losses and data breaches to the possibility of them reaching critical infrastructure, which could have national security and geopolitical ramifications. Thus, every organization, regardless of its size or industry, must prioritize comprehensive security strategies and invest in multilayer security measurements to safeguard against the growing threat of APT groups like Pensive Ursa.</p>

	function maskEmailAddress (emailAddress) {
		function mask(str) {
			var strLen = str.length;
			if (strLen > 4) {
				return str.substr(0, 1) + str.substr(1, strLen - 1).replace(/\w/g, '*') + str.substr(-1,1);
			}
			return str.replace(/\w/g, '*');
		}
