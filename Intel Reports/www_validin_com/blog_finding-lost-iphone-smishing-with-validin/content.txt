  <h2>How to quickly reveal related domains through detailed DNS history and easy pivoting</h2>
    <p>In this post, Iâll walk through another real-world example of <a href="https://www.fcc.gov/avoid-temptation-smishing-scams">smishing</a>, this time from a <a href="https://www.linkedin.com/posts/leifjensendk_advarsel-en-iphone-bliver-stj%C3%A5let-i-danmark-ugcPost-7172201180025098241-V-QQ/">LinkedIn post</a> (also found on <a href="https://cybervejret.dk/stjaalen-iphone/">this blog</a>) detailing a phone theft experience that nearly resulted in an additional account takeover via a likely targeted phishing campaign. We use DNS history to link the past to the present and discover new, previously unknown malicious domains and IPs.</p>

<h1 id="initial-pivots">Initial Pivots</h1>
<p>We observe two domains from the posted image:</p>
<ul>
<li><code>apple.beliggenhed[.]live</code></li>
<li><code>icloud.adgangskode[.]live</code></li>
</ul>
<p>English translations of the domains are roughly âapple.location[.]liveâ and âicloud.password[.]liveâ</p>
<p>Those URLs resolve to Cloudflare IPs (as of early April 2024). Cloudflare IPs often host millions of domain names as part of Cloudflareâs global proxy and DDOS protection network, making them an unhelpful pivot by themselves.</p>
<figure class="border border-1 p-2"><img src="/images/cloudflare-ip-address.webp"
         alt="Easily 10,000&#43; resolutions on one Cloudflare IP."
         title="Easily 10,000&#43; resolutions on one Cloudflare IP."/><figcaption>
            <p>Easily 10,000+ resolutions on one Cloudflare IP.</p>
        </figcaption>
</figure>

<p>Looking into the history for <code>icloud.beliggenhed[.]live</code>, we see resolutions to another IP address from September 2022 to October 2023: <code>80.76.42[.]89</code>. (The parent domain, <code>beliggenhed[.]live</code>, does not resolve to IPv4 addresses during this period.)</p>
<figure class="border border-1 p-2"><img src="/images/phishing-domain-passive-dns-history-date-filter.webp"
         alt="The date filter on passive DNS domain highlights an interesting IP."
         title="The date filter on passive DNS domain highlights an interesting IP."/><figcaption>
            <p>The date filter on passive DNS domain highlights an interesting IP.</p>

</figure>

<h1 id="second-order-pivoting">Second-order Pivoting</h1>
<p>Pivoting on this IP address reveals a set of related domain names:</p>
<figure class="border border-1 p-2"><img src="/images/phishing-ip-passive-dns-history-reveals-other-phishing-domains.webp"
         alt="Note the many themes related to branded products, location, store, photos, and logins in various languages."
         title="Note the many themes related to branded products, location, store, photos, and logins in various languages."/><figcaption>
            <p>Note the many themes related to branded products, location, store, photos, and logins in various languages.</p>
        </figcaption>
</figure>

<p>Validin observed over 800 unique domains (including subdomains) pointing to <code>80.76.42[.]89</code> at one time or another, with 780 of those using similar phishing themes as the starting point beginning on August 1, 2022. Further, the hosting patterns indicate that the domain owners cycle through domains pointing to this IP over time.</p>
<p>Using Validinâs bulk search, I identified and pivoted through a handful of additional IPs and created a list of 1879 domains that are likely related to the original campaign.</p>
