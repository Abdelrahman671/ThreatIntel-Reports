I am saying they should probably be joined to an entirely separate Active Directory forest, and administrator access should be incredibly tightly controlled.</p><h1 id="3080" class="oz pa gv bf pb pc pd pe pf pg ph pi pj pk pl pm pn po pp pq pr ps pt pu pv pw bk">The impact</h1><h2 id="5b0f" class="qd pa gv bf pb qe qf dy pf qg qh ea pj ni qi qj qk nm ql qm qn nq qo qp qq qr bk">Left adrift</h2><p id="7606" class="pw-post-body-paragraph my mz gv bf b na px nc nd ne py ng nh ni pz nk nl nm qa no np nq qb ns nt nu go bk ra"><span class="l rb rc rd bo re rf rg rh ri fj">L</span>ockerGoga does a few things, some unique:</p><ul class=""><li id="fe24" class="my mz gv bf b na nb nc nd ne nf ng nh ni nj nk nl nm nn no np nq nr ns nt nu on oo op bk">It ends up using every CPU core and thread during encryption and is very, very fast. This is because it spawns hundreds of executables for encryption. On an average system within a few minutes, it is toast.</li><li id="fed0" class="my mz gv bf b na oq nc nd ne or ng nh ni os nk nl nm ot no np nq ou ns nt nu on oo op bk">Additionally, some technical blogs on LockerGoga mention a list of file types that are encrypted which only includes things like Office files — I can say first hand that it also encrypts system files such as DLL files across the C: drive. Since it is deployed as administrator level using Active Directory, it has full control of all files.</li><li id="af89" class="my mz gv bf b na oq nc nd ne or ng nh ni os nk nl nm ot no np nq ou ns nt nu on oo op bk">It depends on the version being run (on VirusTotal you can see different LockerGoga executables with different features) but newer versions use netsh.exe to disable all network cards after encryption is done.</li><li id="a1c9" class="my mz gv bf b na oq nc nd ne or ng nh ni os nk nl nm ot no np nq ou ns nt nu on oo op bk">It then changes every local administrator account password.</li><li id="1b89" class="my mz gv bf b na oq nc nd ne or ng nh ni os nk nl nm ot no np nq ou ns nt nu on oo op bk">It then logs you off, using logoff.exe.</li></ul><h2 id="eff6" class="qd pa gv bf pb qe qf dy pf qg qh ea pj ni qi qj qk nm ql qm qn nq qo qp qq qr bk">Recovery</h2><p id="13b2" class="pw-post-body-paragraph my mz gv bf b na px nc nd ne py ng nh ni pz nk nl nm qa no np nq qb ns nt nu go bk ra"><span class="l rb rc rd bo re rf rg rh ri fj">On</span> laptops and desktops, what you’re left with with is the ability to log back in using domain user accounts (it can’t impact Active Directory accounts) on a cached basis, but your users are off the network, as an administrator you cannot reach the PC remotely to fix it, and all the user can do is read the ransom note. They cannot use email, they cannot read a broadcast from the company on their PC, they cannot work likely as their files are encrypted.</p><p id="9ebc" class="pw-post-body-paragraph my mz gv bf b na nb nc nd ne nf ng nh ni nj nk nl nm nn no np nq nr ns nt nu go bk">You cannot log in at the physical console to recover the system as you do not know the local administrator details any more.</p><p id="56ca" class="pw-post-body-paragraph my mz gv bf b na nb nc nd ne nf ng nh ni nj nk nl nm nn no np nq nr ns nt nu go bk">On servers you have service issues, as changing the local administrator passwords can impact system services using local accounts (such as Microsoft SQL, Sophos etc).</p></div></div></div><div class="ab cb qs qt qu qv" role="separator"><span class="qw by bm qx qy qz"></span><span class="qw by bm qx qy qz"></span><span class="qw by bm qx qy"></span></div><div class="go gp gq gr gs"><div class="ab cb"><div class="ci bh ga gb gc gd"><figure class="ny nz oa ob oc od"><div class="rj jg l fj"><div class="rw rl l"></div></div></figure><h1 id="e256" class="oz pa gv bf pb pc pd pe pf pg ph pi pj pk pl pm pn po pp pq pr ps pt pu pv pw bk">Learning opportunities</h1><p id="beb2" class="pw-post-body-paragraph my mz gv bf b na px nc nd ne py ng nh ni pz nk nl nm qa no np nq qb ns nt nu go bk">Here’s the top ten things I think we can take away from this:</p><h2 id="6338" class="qd pa gv bf pb qe qf dy pf qg qh ea pj ni qi qj qk nm ql qm qn nq qo qp qq qr bk">Governments and industry</h2><p id="cb9b" class="pw-post-body-paragraph my mz gv bf b na px nc nd ne py ng nh ni pz nk nl nm qa no np nq qb ns nt nu go bk">There is a serious lack of open information sharing after ransomware incidents which involve unusual code. Right now we’re super protected against, say, WannaCry — but after Altran, a lack of transparency and openness lead to the same issue elsewhere.</p><p id="6d34" class="pw-post-body-paragraph my mz gv bf b na nb nc nd ne nf ng nh ni nj nk nl nm nn no np nq nr ns nt nu go bk">This will happen again with other threats. In my opinion it is in national and international interests for governments to be informed of technical details of all major business incidents around malicious code causing outages (e.g. wipers, ransomware) and that information should be shared with security vendors and other governments. I know many organisations won’t want to provide this information so governments or regulators might need levers to pull to compel disclosure.</p><h2 id="1bc8" class="qd pa gv bf pb qe qf dy pf qg qh ea pj ni qi qj qk nm ql qm qn nq qo qp qq qr bk">Companies</h2><p id="1d62" class="pw-post-body-paragraph my mz gv bf b na px nc nd ne py ng nh ni pz nk nl nm qa no np nq qb ns nt nu go bk">Organisations should look at how Hydro disclosed and dealt with the issue so far in the public arena. It looks like it may be a textbook example of how incident response <em class="ov">should </em>be done, with transparency and openness. Not only the public and media perception went well, but the business end went well too — people didn’t sell off shares because they felt genuinely informed and that Hydro had a dire situation under control.</p><h2 id="b3f2" class="qd pa gv bf pb qe qf dy pf qg qh ea pj ni qi qj qk nm ql qm qn nq qo qp qq qr bk">Security industry</h2><p id="3700" class="pw-post-body-paragraph my mz gv bf b na px nc nd ne py ng nh ni pz nk nl nm qa no np nq qb ns nt nu go bk">I feel the industry could do a better job at advising what benefits and weak spots of their products have in terms of detection, so customers can make informed choices about where they may need to introduce additional controls and investment.</p><p id="2f04" class="pw-post-body-paragraph my mz gv bf b na nb nc nd ne nf ng nh ni nj nk nl nm nn no np nq nr ns nt nu go bk">I know, I’m naive.</p><p id="8c88" class="pw-post-body-paragraph my mz gv bf b na nb nc nd ne nf ng nh ni nj nk nl nm nn no np nq nr ns nt nu go bk">The cyber security industry is currently going through a hype cycle where some vendors are simply overselling their products — including internally — so you can end up with customers like Hydro which likely had a reasonable security stack, but got wiped out anyway. Hydro aren’t alone here in their journey.</p><p id="60e2" class="pw-post-body-paragraph my mz gv bf b na nb nc nd ne nf ng nh ni nj nk nl nm nn no np nq nr ns nt nu go bk">I’ve seen vendors talking about Artificial Intelligence made malware — where AI creates the malware itself — which isn’t even a real thing.
