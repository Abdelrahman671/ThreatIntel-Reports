	<h1 class="article-details__title">Operation ENDTRADE: Multi-Stage Backdoors that TICK</h1>
	<p class="article-details__description">We found cyberespionage group TICK targeting critical systems and enterprises to steal information. In this research brief, we show the group&#39;s activities and technical analyses of the new malware families, modified tools, and upgraded routines.</p>
	<p class="article-details__author-by">By: Joey Chen, Kakara Hiroyuki, Shoji Masaoki
		
			<time class="article-details__date">November 29, 2019</time>
		
		
		<span>Read time:&nbsp;</span><span class="eta"></span> (<span class="words"></span> words)

		<div>
			<p>While we have been following cyberespionage group TICK (a.k.a. “BRONZE BUTLER” or “REDBALDKNIGHT”) since 2008, we noticed an unusual increase in malware development and deployments towards November 2018. We already know that the group uses previously deployed malware and modified tools for obfuscation, but we also found TICK developing new malware families capable of detection evasion for initial intrusion, as well as escalation of administrative privileges for subsequent attacks and data collection. We also found the group using legitimate email accounts and credentials for the delivery of the malware, zeroing in on industries with highly classified information: defense, aerospace, chemical, and satellite industries with head offices in Japan and subsidiaries in China. Given their targets, we have named this campaign “Operation ENDTRADE,” and identified some of the findings in our research <i>“</i><a href="https://documents.trendmicro.com/assets/pdf/Operation-ENDTRADE-TICK-s-Multi-Stage-Backdoors-for-Attacking-Industries-and-Stealing-Classified-Data.pdf"><i>Operation ENDTRADE: TICK’s Multi-Stage Backdoors for Attacking Industries and Stealing Classified Data</i></a><i>”</i>.</p>
<p>This research paper was submitted and presented for the DeepINTEL Security Intelligence 2019 Conference on November 27, 2019 in Vienna, Austria.</p>
<p><b><span class="body-subhead-title">Targeting and malware delivery</span></b><a href="/content/dam/trendmicro/global/en/migrated/security-intelligence-migration-spreadsheet/trendlabs-security-intelligence/2019/11/figure1_timeline_v2.jpg"><img src="/content/dam/trendmicro/global/en/migrated/security-intelligence-migration-spreadsheet/trendlabs-security-intelligence/2019/11/figure1_timeline_v2.jpg"/></a></p>
<p style="text-align: center;">Figure 1. Operation ENDTRADE’s timeline</p>
<p>As part of their attacks in January 2019, TICK was conducting their research by compromising a Japanese economic research company and a public relations (PR) agency to steal email credentials and files as decoy documents. These email addresses were used for spear phishing, prompting potential victim organizations to open the attachments with malware payloads. Meanwhile, the documents were embedded with malware, and sent to individuals and companies knowledgeable in Japanese or Chinese, and interested in the Chinese economy. The emails had the following features:</p>
<ul>
<li><span class="rte-red-bullet">They were sent from legitimate email accounts</span></li>
<li><span class="rte-red-bullet">They were written as legitimate reports and prompted the users to open the attachments</span></li>
<li><span class="rte-red-bullet">They contained subject topics related to “salary rate increase” or “job market,” or with special interests in the economic affairs of China such as the US-China trade mandates</span></li>
</ul>
<p style="text-align: center;"><a href="/content/dam/trendmicro/global/en/migrated/security-intelligence-migration-spreadsheet/trendlabs-security-intelligence/2019/11/figure2_spear-phishing.png"><img src="/content/dam/trendmicro/global/en/migrated/security-intelligence-migration-spreadsheet/trendlabs-security-intelligence/2019/11/figure2_spear-phishing.png"/></a></p>
<p style="text-align: center;">Figure 2. Spear phishing sample in fluent Japanese</p>
<p style="text-align: left;">Based on the language that was hardcoded in the samples we found, TICK appeared to be targeting Japanese organizations with subsidiaries in China to serve as footholds for intrusion: TICK hard-coded two code pages 932 and 936, referring to Japanese and Simplified Chinese characters respectively. Moreover, we found successful transfers of malicious executable files in the shared folder from a Chinese subsidiary with an infected desktop, and an employee in Japan that executed the said file.<a href="/content/dam/trendmicro/global/en/migrated/security-intelligence-migration-spreadsheet/trendlabs-security-intelligence/2019/11/figure10_scode-downloader.png"><img src="/content/dam/trendmicro/global/en/migrated/security-intelligence-migration-spreadsheet/trendlabs-security-intelligence/2019/11/figure10_scode-downloader.png"/></a></p>
<p style="text-align: center;">Figure 3. Language code pages</p>
<p>While we found intrusions in a large number of companies in the abovementioned industries before May 2019, further analysis revealed that one of the main targets was the defense sector. We found TICK trying to steal military-related documents from the victim network during an extended assistance for incident response in the region. However, TICK seemed to shift their attention to the chemical industry by mid-May, which may indicate the group’s sponsor organization’s goal: To steal proprietary and classified information such as military data and advanced materials.</p>
<p><b><span class="body-subhead-title">Malware Analysis</span></b></p>
<p>Our research lists some of the new and adjusted malware routines we found from Operation ENDTRADE, which we named based on their characteristic program database (PDB) strings. For a complete list and analyses of the trojans, downloaders, and modified tools, you may access the research brief <a href="https://documents.trendmicro.com/assets/pdf/Operation-ENDTRADE-TICK-s-Multi-Stage-Backdoors-for-Attacking-Industries-and-Stealing-Classified-Data.pdf">here</a>.</p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><a href="/content/dam/trendmicro/global/en/migrated/security-intelligence-migration-spreadsheet/trendlabs-security-intelligence/2019/11/figure8_downloader-relations.jpg"><img src="/content/dam/trendmicro/global/en/migrated/security-intelligence-migration-spreadsheet/trendlabs-security-intelligence/2019/11/figure8_downloader-relations.jpg"/></a></p>
<p style="text-align: center;">Figure 4. New downloaders and trojans</p>
<p><i><b>DATPER</b></i></p>
<p>While this backdoor routine has been associated with TICK’s weapons arsenal, the sample we derived from this campaign had two adjusted mutex objects — <i>d0ftyzxcdrfdqwe</i> and <i>*&amp;Hjgfc49gna-2-tjb </i>— that retrieve information from the victim’s machine. The latest variant also has a new set of parameters that allow it to evade anti-virus (AV) product pattern detections, implying the ease by which the group can change their routines to suit their goals.<a href="/content/dam/trendmicro/global/en/migrated/security-intelligence-migration-spreadsheet/trendlabs-security-intelligence/2019/11/figure14_datper-new-mutex-parameters.png"><img src="/content/dam/trendmicro/global/en/migrated/security-intelligence-migration-spreadsheet/trendlabs-security-intelligence/2019/11/figure14_datper-new-mutex-parameters.png"/></a></p>
<p style="text-align: center;">Figure 5. DATPER’s new mutex with separate parameters</p>
<p><b><i>down_new</i></b></p>
<p>This malware combines features of existing trojans in the malware family’s development, based on the adjustments TICK made as we analyzed their test versions. It adds features (listed below) that can be found separately on previous iterations:</p>
<ul>
<li><span class="rte-red-bullet">Adds Autorun to the registry.</span></li>
<li><span class="rte-red-bullet">Gets MAC address and volume information to send back to the C&amp;C.</span></li>
<li><span class="rte-red-bullet">Executes only during working hours (8:00AM-6:00PM, using <i>kernel32.GetLocalTime</i> API)</span></li>
<li><span class="rte-red-bullet">Uses AES encryption and base64 encoding method to encrypt the call back message.</span></li>
<li><span class="rte-red-bullet">Uses legitimate websites for the C&amp;C server.</span></li>
<li><span class="rte-red-bullet">Detects anti-virus products and processes.</span></li>
</ul>
<p><a href="/content/dam/trendmicro/global/en/migrated/security-intelligence-migration-spreadsheet/trendlabs-security-intelligence/2019/11/figure35_Command-func-down_new.png"><img src="/content/dam/trendmicro/global/en/migrated/security-intelligence-migration-spreadsheet/trendlabs-security-intelligence/2019/11/figure35_Command-func-down_new.png"/></a></p>
<p style="text-align: center;">Figure 6. Code showing down_new’s command function</p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><a href="/content/dam/trendmicro/global/en/migrated/security-intelligence-migration-spreadsheet/trendlabs-security-intelligence/2019/11/Table-3.jpg"><img src="/content/dam/trendmicro/global/en/migrated/security-intelligence-migration-spreadsheet/trendlabs-security-intelligence/2019/11/Table-3.jpg"/></a></p>
<p style="text-align: center;">Table 1. down_new command list</p>
<p>As we studied its processes to compare with the others, the call back information stood out: The HTTP post header is hard-coded in the sample, getting the infected machine’s specific information to single out the identity of the users. As a cyber-espionage group with specific goals based on their sponsoring organization’s objectives, TICK only goes after specific targets and only uses other non-targeted individuals and enterprises as footholds to meet their purposes.<a href="/content/dam/trendmicro/global/en/migrated/security-intelligence-migration-spreadsheet/trendlabs-security-intelligence/2019/11/figure36_down_new-data-url.png"><img src="/content/dam/trendmicro/global/en/migrated/security-intelligence-migration-spreadsheet/trendlabs-security-intelligence/2019/11/figure36_down_new-data-url.png"/></a></p>
<p style="text-align: center;">Figure 7. down_new collects home phone data and URL path</p>
<p><b><i>Avenger</i></b></p>
<p>Our analysis found that Avenger has a number of variants and versions depending on their targets. For example, some variants have autorun functions while others execute a sleep mode upon system infection. We found that the downloader has three stages:</p>
<ol>
<li>The first stage collects volume information, AV product, and OS bits version from the host, and sends it to the command and control (C&amp;C) server to ensure that the host is the intended target.</li>
</ol>
<p><a href="/content/dam/trendmicro/global/en/migrated/security-intelligence-migration-spreadsheet/trendlabs-security-intelligence/2019/11/figure39_avenger-phone-home-first-stage-info.png"><img src="/content/dam/trendmicro/global/en/migrated/security-intelligence-migration-spreadsheet/trendlabs-security-intelligence/2019/11/figure39_avenger-phone-home-first-stage-info.png"/></a></p>
<p style="text-align: center;">Figure 8. First stage: Information collection</p>
<ul>
<li>2. It then checks if the host matches their C&amp;C server reference. Avenger collects the victim’s detailed information from the system by browsing the folders, files, and domain information.<a href="/content/dam/trendmicro/global/en/migrated/security-intelligence-migration-spreadsheet/trendlabs-security-intelligence/2019/11/figure40_dl-avenger-second-stage-info-write.png"><img src="/content/dam/trendmicro/global/en/migrated/security-intelligence-migration-spreadsheet/trendlabs-security-intelligence/2019/11/figure40_dl-avenger-second-stage-info-write.png"/></a></li>
</ul>
<p style="text-align: center;">Figure 9. Second stage: Collected information is written into a .txt file</p>
<ul>
<li>3. If the host doesn’t exist, Avenger will download an image with an embedded malware hidden via steganography and extract a backdoor.<a href="/content/dam/trendmicro/global/en/migrated/security-intelligence-migration-spreadsheet/trendlabs-security-intelligence/2019/11/figure41_dl-avenger-send-back-encrypted-info.png"><img src="/content/dam/trendmicro/global/en/migrated/security-intelligence-migration-spreadsheet/trendlabs-security-intelligence/2019/11/figure41_dl-avenger-send-back-encrypted-info.png"/></a></li>
</ul>
<p style="text-align: center;">Figure 10. Third stage: Sending the encrypted file to the C&amp;C</p>
<p>While steganography is always used as part of TICK’s malware techniques, we found that the group used a more sophisticated steganography technique in this campaign.<a href="/content/dam/trendmicro/global/en/migrated/security-intelligence-migration-spreadsheet/trendlabs-security-intelligence/2019/11/figure43_extract-backdoor-fr-pic.png"><img src="/content/dam/trendmicro/global/en/migrated/security-intelligence-migration-spreadsheet/trendlabs-security-intelligence/2019/11/figure43_extract-backdoor-fr-pic.png"/></a></p>
<p style="text-align: center;">Figure 11. Backdoor found in the steganography image</p>
<p><a href="/content/dam/trendmicro/global/en/migrated/security-intelligence-migration-spreadsheet/trendlabs-security-intelligence/2019/11/figure44_dl-avenger-steganography-algo.png"><img src="/content/dam/trendmicro/global/en/migrated/security-intelligence-migration-spreadsheet/trendlabs-security-intelligence/2019/11/figure44_dl-avenger-steganography-algo.png"/></a></p>
<p style="text-align: center;">Figure 12. Upgraded steganography technique</p>
<p>We found a newer version of Avenger with a clearer code structure and internal IP testing URL (aptly named Avenger2 in the PDB strings), though the rest of the components had minimal differences with the previous version.<a href="/content/dam/trendmicro/global/en/migrated/security-intelligence-migration-spreadsheet/trendlabs-security-intelligence/2019/11/figure45_dl-avenger-internal-URL.png"><img src="/content/dam/trendmicro/global/en/migrated/security-intelligence-migration-spreadsheet/trendlabs-security-intelligence/2019/11/figure45_dl-avenger-internal-URL.png"/></a></p>
<p style="text-align: center;">Figure 13. Avenger2 with internal URL</p>
<p><b><i>Casper</i></b></p>
<p>Casper is a modified version of the Cobalt Strike backdoor, showing the team server SHA1 hash if the controller connects to the C&amp;C. If accessed by the client, Cobalt Strike confirms with the user if they recognize and match the SHA1 hash of a specific team server’s SSL certificate.<a href="/content/dam/trendmicro/global/en/migrated/security-intelligence-migration-spreadsheet/trendlabs-security-intelligence/2019/11/figure49_casper-backdoor-CC-Cobalt-fingerprint.png"><img src="/content/dam/trendmicro/global/en/migrated/security-intelligence-migration-spreadsheet/trendlabs-security-intelligence/2019/11/figure49_casper-backdoor-CC-Cobalt-fingerprint.png"/></a></p>
<p style="text-align: center;">Figure 14. Casper C&amp;C with Cobalt Strike’s server fingerprint</p>
<p>The backdoor is usually hidden in the steganography photo and uses several techniques and tools to bypass AV detection. One technique involves launching itself with a legitimate Windows application with Dynamic Link Library (DLL) side loading techniques. Another involves injecting the backdoor’s shellcode into <i>svchost.exe</i>.<a href="/content/dam/trendmicro/global/en/migrated/security-intelligence-migration-spreadsheet/trendlabs-security-intelligence/2019/11/figure46_shellcode-inject-svchost.png"><img src="/content/dam/trendmicro/global/en/migrated/security-intelligence-migration-spreadsheet/trendlabs-security-intelligence/2019/11/figure46_shellcode-inject-svchost.png"/></a></p>
<p style="text-align: center;">Figure 15. Shellcode injected to svchost.exe</p>
<p><b><span class="body-subhead-title">Publicly available RATs and modified tools</span></b></p>
<p style="text-align: left;">Included in all the malware routines, we also found TICK using publicly available remote access trojans (RATs) and ope n source tools, and either modified or imported the techniques into their malware. For instance, they cloned <a href="https://github.com/werkamsus/Lilith/issues/3">Lilith RAT</a> from GitHub, studied and implemented its features into their customized backdoor under continued development. The list of modified tools the group used include Mimikatz, RAR compression tool, port mapping tool, and screen capture.</p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><a href="/content/dam/trendmicro/global/en/migrated/security-intelligence-migration-spreadsheet/trendlabs-security-intelligence/2019/11/figure55_screen-capture-tool.png"><img src="/content/dam/trendmicro/global/en/migrated/security-intelligence-migration-spreadsheet/trendlabs-security-intelligence/2019/11/figure55_screen-capture-tool.png"/></a></p>
<p style="text-align: center;">Figure 16. Modified screen capture tool</p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><a href="/content/dam/trendmicro/global/en/migrated/security-intelligence-migration-spreadsheet/trendlabs-security-intelligence/2019/11/figure56_modified-mimikatz.png"><img src="/content/dam/trendmicro/global/en/migrated/security-intelligence-migration-spreadsheet/trendlabs-security-intelligence/2019/11/figure56_modified-mimikatz.png"/></a></p>
<p style="text-align: center;">Figure 17. Modified Mimikatz</p>
<p><b><span class="body-subhead-title">Conclusion</span></b></p>
<p>TICK is an organized and persistent cyber espionage group specialized in targeting high-value individuals and organizations, with the skills and resources needed to coordinate sophisticated attacks.</p>
<p>This operation not only highlights the need for stronger monitoring systems foremost in countries’ critical infrastructures and multinational enterprises, but also firmer operational chains of command and redundant security policies established. Persistent criminal groups will continue to target enterprises, and will look for security gaps to exploit to gain unauthorized entry. Organizations with foreign subsidiaries can make it difficult to take control and implement security procedures and policies, making monitoring, isolating, investigating, incident response, and recovery more difficult. To top it all, employees’ security awareness and consciousness will remain a significant part of making sure the security measures in place are maintained for regular operations.</p>
