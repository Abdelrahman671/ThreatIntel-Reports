			<h1 class="entry-title">NetWalker Ransomware in 1 Hour</h1>
		<div class="entry-meta">
			<span class="posted-on"><a href="https://thedfirreport.com/2020/08/31/netwalker-ransomware-in-1-hour/" rel="bookmark"><time class="entry-date published" datetime="2020-08-31T00:02:08+00:00">August 31, 2020</time></a></span>		</div><!-- .entry-meta -->

		
	</header><!-- .entry-header -->

	<div class="entry-content">
		
<p>The threat actor logged in through RDP, attempted to run a Cobalt Strike Beacon, and then dumped memory using ProcDump and Mimikatz. Next, they RDPed into a Domain Controller, minutes before using PsExec to run the NetWalker ransomware payload on all Domain joined systems. The entire intrusion took ~1 hour.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">What is NetWalker?</h2>



<blockquote class="wp-block-quote is-layout-flow wp-block-quote-is-layout-flow"><p>NetWalker, as a ransomware strain, first appeared in August 2019. In its initial version, the ransomware went by the name of Mailto but rebranded to NetWalker towards the end of 2019.</p><p>The ransomware operates as a closed-access RaaS &#8212; a ransomware-as-a-service portal. Other hacker gangs sign up and go through a vetting process, after which they are granted access to a web portal where they can build custom versions of the ransomware.</p><p>The distribution is left to these second-tier gangs, known as affiliates, and each group deploys it as they see fit.</p><cite><a href="https://www.zdnet.com/meet-the-team/us/catalin.cimpanu/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">Catalin Cimpanu</a> &#8211; <a href="https://www.zdnet.com/article/netwalker-ransomware-gang-has-made-25-million-since-march-2020/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">https://www.zdnet.com/article/netwalker-ransomware-gang-has-made-25-million-since-march-2020/</a></cite></blockquote>



<p>For more info on NetWalker check out the following posts:</p>



<div class="wp-block-group"><div class="wp-block-group__inner-container is-layout-flow wp-block-group-is-layout-flow">
<p><a rel="noreferrer noopener" href="https://labs.sentinelone.com/netwalker-ransomware-no-respite-no-english-required/" target="_blank">https://labs.sentinelone.com/netwalker-ransomware-no-respite-no-english-required</a><a href="https://labs.sentinelone.com/netwalker-ransomware-no-respite-no-english-required/" data-type="URL" data-id="https://labs.sentinelone.com/netwalker-ransomware-no-respite-no-english-required/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">/</a></p>
</div></div>

<p><a href="https://news.sophos.com/en-us/2020/05/27/netwalker-ransomware-tools-give-insight-into-threat-actor/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">https://news.sophos.com/en-us/2020/05/27/netwalker-ransomware-tools-give-insight-into-threat-actor/</a></p>

<p><a href="https://go.crowdstrike.com/rs/281-OBQ-266/images/ReportCSIT-20081e.pdf" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">https://go.crowdstrike.com/rs/281-OBQ-266/images/ReportCSIT-20081e.pdf</a></p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Exploitation</h2>



<p>We saw multiple RDP logins around the time of the attack but we believe <a rel="noreferrer noopener" href="https://www.ultratools.com/tools/ipWhoisLookupResult" data-type="URL" data-id="https://www.ultratools.com/tools/ipWhoisLookupResult" target="_blank">198.181.163[.]103</a> (possibly IPVanish VPN) to be the source of this intrusion. We will include other IPs that logged into the honeypot on this day in the IOCs section.</p>



<p>The threat actor logged in using the DomainName\Administrator account.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Command &amp; Control</h2>



<p>c37.ps1 was dropped and run about 16 minutes after initial login. There didn&#8217;t appear to be any network connections made while running this script which makes us wonder if the script works or not.</p>



<p>The script is heavily obfuscated but still looks like Cobalt Strike. When we uploaded the script to VT, Thor said it may also contain Windshield or SplinterRAT.</p>



<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img fetchpriority="high" decoding="async" width="1024" height="303" src="https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/image-30-1024x303.png" alt="" class="wp-image-773" srcset="https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/image-30-1024x303.png 1024w, https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/image-30-300x89.png 300w, https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/image-30-768x227.png 768w, https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/image-30-1536x455.png 1536w, https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/image-30.png 1584w" sizes="(max-width: 1024px) 100vw, 1024px" /></figure>



<p>c37.ps1 has a very low detection rate even after 7+ days.</p>



<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img decoding="async" width="1024" height="364" src="https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/image-39-1024x364.png" alt="" class="wp-image-808" srcset="https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/image-39-1024x364.png 1024w, https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/image-39-300x107.png 300w, https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/image-39-768x273.png 768w, https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/image-39.png 1278w" sizes="(max-width: 1024px) 100vw, 1024px" /></figure>



<p>Minutes later they ran c37.exe, which copies itself to a temp directory and then stops. This binary includes Neshta as well as many capabilities as seen below:</p>



<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img decoding="async" width="893" height="380" src="https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/image-12.png" alt="" class="wp-image-739" srcset="https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/image-12.png 893w, https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/image-12-300x128.png 300w, https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/image-12-768x327.png 768w" sizes="(max-width: 893px) 100vw, 893px" /><figcaption><a href="https://www.fireeye.com/blog/threat-research/2020/07/capa-automatically-identify-malware-capabilities.html" data-type="URL" data-id="https://www.fireeye.com/blog/threat-research/2020/07/capa-automatically-identify-malware-capabilities.html" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">capa</a></figcaption></figure>



<p>After further analysis and a comment from <a href="https://twitter.com/GaborSzappanos">@GaborSzappanos</a>, we were able to confirm that both of these are indeed Cobalt Strike and connect to 173.232.146[.]37 over 443.</p>



<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" width="634" height="274" src="https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/image-1.png" alt="" class="wp-image-855" srcset="https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/image-1.png 634w, https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/image-1-300x130.png 300w" sizes="auto, (max-width: 634px) 100vw, 634px" /></figure>



<p>The Cobalt Strike server at 173.232.146.37 is using the <a rel="noreferrer noopener" href="https://www.recordedfuture.com/cobalt-strike-servers/" data-type="URL" data-id="https://www.recordedfuture.com/cobalt-strike-servers/" target="_blank">default cert </a>(<a href="https://community.riskiq.com/search/certificate/serialNumber/146473198" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">146473198</a>) and oddly enough could not be MiTM. We tried to MiTM this connection multiple times and kept getting an error stating SSL session did not authentication successfully.</p>



<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" width="481" height="486" src="https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/image-2.png" alt="" class="wp-image-857" srcset="https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/image-2.png 481w, https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/image-2-297x300.png 297w" sizes="auto, (max-width: 481px) 100vw, 481px" /></figure>



<p>We attempted to run c37.ps1 and c37.exe in a few sandboxes and none of them captured the network traffic which tells us that these Beacons include sandbox evasion techniques. Here are a couple runs &#8211; <a href="https://capesandbox.com/analysis/54494/">https://capesandbox.com/analysis/54494/</a> <a rel="noreferrer noopener" href="https://app.any.run/tasks/4524fb0c-8e17-4255-8582-35b0e206ff3f/" target="_blank">https://app.any.run/tasks/4524fb0c-8e17-4255-8582-35b0e206ff3f/</a> <a rel="noreferrer noopener" href="https://capesandbox.com/analysis/54493/" target="_blank">https://capesandbox.com/analysis/54493/</a></p>



<p>The c37.exe binary includes shared code from Neshta, poison, BazarBackdoor, XMRig and a large portion from CobaltStrike according to Intezer.</p>



<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" width="1024" height="434" src="https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/image-1024x434.png" alt="" class="wp-image-854" srcset="https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/image-1024x434.png 1024w, https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/image-300x127.png 300w, https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/image-768x325.png 768w, https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/image-1536x650.png 1536w, https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/image.png 1566w" sizes="auto, (max-width: 1024px) 100vw, 1024px" /></figure>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Discovery</h2>



<p>AdFind was dropped alongside a script named adf.bat. We&#8217;ve seen this script in the past and wrote about it <a rel="noreferrer noopener" href="https://thedfirreport.com/2020/05/08/adfind-recon/" target="_blank">here</a>.</p>



<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" width="502" height="251" src="https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/image-19.png" alt="" class="wp-image-749" srcset="https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/image-19.png 502w, https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/image-19-300x150.png 300w" sizes="auto, (max-width: 502px) 100vw, 502px" /></figure>



<p>We can see from these lnk files that they opened a few of the txt files output by AdFind. We can also see that domains.txt and ips.log were opened minutes after AdFind being run.</p>



<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" width="841" height="258" src="https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/image-34.png" alt="" class="wp-image-792" srcset="https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/image-34.png 841w, https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/image-34-300x92.png 300w, https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/image-34-768x236.png 768w" sizes="auto, (max-width: 841px) 100vw, 841px" /><figcaption><a href="https://binaryforay.blogspot.com/2016/02/introducing-lecmd.html" data-type="URL" data-id="https://binaryforay.blogspot.com/2016/02/introducing-lecmd.html" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">LECmd </a>&#8211; Tool by EricZimmerman</figcaption></figure>



<p>A few minutes after AdFind was run, a command prompt was opened and the following commands were either copy and pasted slowly or manually typed.</p>



<pre class="wp-block-preformatted">nltest /dclist:
net group "Domain Computers" /DOMAIN
net groups "Enterprise Admins" /domain
net user Administrator</pre>



<p>Shortly after that, a script named pcr.bat was dropped and executed.</p>



<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" width="906" height="97" src="https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/image-23.png" alt="" class="wp-image-759" srcset="https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/image-23.png 906w, https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/image-23-300x32.png 300w, https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/image-23-768x82.png 768w" sizes="auto, (max-width: 906px) 100vw, 906px" /></figure>



<p>This script pings a list of hostnames (domains.txt) and writes the output to ips.log. The ping command they use sends one ping and forces IPv4. This domains.txt file most likely came from the above AdFind command using the domainlist parameter.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Credential Access</h2>



<p>Mimikatz was dropped and then a minute later procdump64.exe was dropped. The threat actors then used Procdump to dump lsass using the following command:</p>



<pre class="wp-block-preformatted">procdump64.exe -ma lsass.exe lsass.dmp</pre>



<p>This procdump64 binary appears to be compiled with Delphi and does not match known hashes. It appears the threat actors rolled their own but included the original instructions.</p>



<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" width="510" height="244" src="https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/image-46.png" alt="" class="wp-image-847" srcset="https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/image-46.png 510w, https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/image-46-300x144.png 300w" sizes="auto, (max-width: 510px) 100vw, 510px" /></figure>



<p>Mimikatz was run about a minute later.</p>



<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" width="563" height="164" src="https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/image-20.png" alt="" class="wp-image-755" srcset="https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/image-20.png 563w, https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/image-20-300x87.png 300w" sizes="auto, (max-width: 563px) 100vw, 563px" /></figure>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Lateral Movement</h2>



<p>The threat actor RDPed into a Domain Controller (DC) after dumping credentials. Shortly after accessing the DC they dropped ip.list.txt, P100119.ps1, and PsExec.</p>



<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" width="558" height="377" src="https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/image-38.png" alt="" class="wp-image-807" srcset="https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/image-38.png 558w, https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/image-38-300x203.png 300w" sizes="auto, (max-width: 558px) 100vw, 558px" /></figure>



<p>The threat actor was now ready to execute it&#8217;s objective.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Objectives</h2>



<p>The threat actor used PsExec to mount a share on all systems as the Domain Administrator and then execute the ransomware payload using PowerShell. NetWalker was delivered to all online Domain joined systems in the honeypot via the below command:</p>



<p>C:\psexec.exe @ip-list.txt -d cmd /c &#8220;(net use q: /delete /y &amp;; net use q: \\DomainController\DomainName /user:DomainName\administrator ThisWasThePassword &amp;; powershell -ExecutionPolicy ByPass -NoLogo -NoProfile -windowstyle hidden -NoExit -File q:\P100119.ps1&#8221;</p>



<p>After the PowerShell script runs you are left with the following ransom note.</p>



<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" width="1023" height="378" src="https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/image-40.png" alt="" class="wp-image-810" srcset="https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/image-40.png 1023w, https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/image-40-300x111.png 300w, https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/image-40-768x284.png 768w" sizes="auto, (max-width: 1023px) 100vw, 1023px" /></figure>



<p>The NetWalker operators asked for $50k within 7 days or $100k after. They were talked down to $35k after the time expired.</p>



<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" width="736" height="446" src="https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/image-44.png" alt="" class="wp-image-841" srcset="https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/image-44.png 736w, https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/image-44-300x182.png 300w" sizes="auto, (max-width: 736px) 100vw, 736px" /></figure>
