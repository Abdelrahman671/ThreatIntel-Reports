					<h1 class="entry-title">Malicious Infrastructure as a Service</h1>                    <div class='ep-post-terms term-tag'><a class='ep-item-term ep-item-term-threat' href='https://www.silentpush.com/tag/threat/' >threat</a></div>					<div class="single-post-meta">
												<div class="single-post-date">
							April 25, 2021						</div>
					</div>

												<div class="single-post-thumbnail "><img width="1200" height="800" src="https://www.silentpush.com/wp-content/uploads/Silent-Push-Malicious-Infrastructure-As-Service-Blog.jpg" class="wp-image-1565 avia-img-lazy-loading-1565 attachment-post-thumbnail size-post-thumbnail wp-post-image" alt="Old abandoned house after a fire" decoding="async" loading="lazy" /></div>
						
				</div>
				
<p class="has-medium-font-size">Domains created for malicious purposes are rarely registered on their own. When you have identified such a domain, it is therefore always a good idea to look for other domains used in the same campaign.</p>



<p class="has-medium-font-size">Sometimes finding such a domain is easy. For example, you may notice a very similar domain (such as the .net version of a .com domain) registered with the same registrar on the same day. At other times you will need to look for other evidence, for example find them hosted on the same IP address.</p>



<p class="has-medium-font-size">In general, however, linking two domains through a single IP address isn’t strong enough evidence that the domains themselves are linked. The link becomes a lot stronger though when two or more domains were seen moving through the same set of IP addresses simultaneously.</p>



<p class="has-medium-font-size">In previous blog posts, this was used to find five domains that used the <a href="https://silentpush.com/blog/pivoting-finding-malware-domains-without-seeing-malicious-activity/3">same infrastructure</a> and that were made to look like they belonged to a content delivery network, as well as the infrastructure of a <a href="https://silentpush.com/blog/more-lodarat-infrastructure-targeting-bangladesh-uncovered/5">LodaRAT campaign</a> targeting Bangladesh.</p>



<p class="has-medium-font-size">In this blog post, a few more examples showing sets of malicious domains that moved simultaneously through the same set of IP addresses will be examined. The sets of domains are linked and there is some evidence to suggest that the infrastructure belongs to a bulletproof hosting service.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading has-large-font-size">Magecart</h2>

Magecart is an umbrella term use to refer to more than a dozen groups that insert code into websites’ payment pages that steals credit card data. A common trick used by Magecart groups is to make their domains look like those from which code is regularly included into web pages. A website owner looking at the source code on a webpage may thus incorrectly assume the inclusion of third-party JavaScript is harmless.</p>

The same applies to the examples below.</em></p>

Though no direct link between these two actors is known, recently URLs of the type that previously served Qakbot started to <a href="https://www.malware-traffic-analysis.net/2021/02/22/index.html" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">serve</a> IcedID instead.</p>



<p class="has-medium-font-size">This suggests that it is another actor that handles the spam campaigns that delivers either malware, an example of the increased commoditization of cybercrime. This would also explain why the domains listed above are different from the IcedID command and control domains written about <a href="https://silentpush.com/blog/icedid-command-and-control-infrastructure/7">recently</a>, which use a different hosting infrastructure.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading has-large-font-size">Ursnif and phishing</h2>



<p class="has-medium-font-size">A third set of domains also cycled through a set of IP addresses:</p>



<pre class="wp-block-code has-medium-font-size"><code>aodacrtsrytuce&#91;.]com
ashguq&#91;.]com
chonlinedocstorage&#91;.]com

<p class="has-medium-font-size">The IP addresses in this case are:</p>



<pre class="wp-block-code has-medium-font-size"><code>188.227.58&#91;.]120
45.143.136&#91;.]43
188.227.86&#91;.]64

<p class="has-medium-font-size">All these domains were registered through Eranet, a registrar based in Hong Kong, and again used&nbsp; DNSPod’s nameservers. Two of the IP addresses, marked in bold, were also used by the Magecart domains, suggesting a possible link.</p>



<p class="has-medium-font-size">Interestingly, there are two kinds of domains in the list. One the one hand, there are random looking domains which, as with the IcedID/Qakbot domains above, could suggest a <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain_generation_algorithm" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">domain generation algorithm</a> (DGA). On the other hand, domains like docusign-cloud[.]com and upsdocstorage[.]com of which one can be all but certain they have been used in phishing campaigns: both DocuSign and UPS are commonly used in phishing lures.</p>



<p class="has-medium-font-size">It is not surprising therefore that these latter domains were taken down, often within a week after becoming active: lookalike domains are actively hunted by the affected organisations.</p>



<p class="has-medium-font-size">As for the DGA-like domains, one of them, uidacrtsppxece[.]com, has been <a href="https://urlhaus.abuse.ch/url/991115/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">linked</a> to Ursnif, another common malware delivered in email campaigns.</p>



<p class="has-medium-font-size">It is unclear whether there is a direct link between Ursnif and the phishing domains beyond the use of the same infrastructure, or even whether all DGA-like domains have served Ursnif.&nbsp; &nbsp;</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading has-large-font-size">Other domains</h2>



<p class="has-medium-font-size">There are many other domains that have used the same infrastructure, including the use of the DNSPod DNS provider.</p>



<p class="has-medium-font-size">For example, the following domains&#8230;</p>



<pre class="wp-block-code has-medium-font-size"><code>ie-kbc&#91;.]net
ie-kbc&#91;.]org
kbc-ie&#91;.]net
www.kbcbanking&#91;.]net</code></pre>



<p class="has-medium-font-size">will no doubt have been used to impersonate KBC, an Irish bank, while authorise-eebilling[.]com has likely targeted customers of UK mobile provider EE. There are also several more domains that suggest a DGA.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading has-large-font-size">Conclusion: a bulletproof hosting provider?</h2>



<p class="has-medium-font-size">The similarities among the various sets described above, such as the use of DNSPod and the sharing of IP addresses, suggests the campaigns described all use the same infrastructure, likely that of a bulletproof hosting service.</p>



<p class="has-medium-font-size">A bulletproof hoster serves a similar function as a content-delivery network (CDN) does for legitimate domains: making it harder for a denial-of-service attack. The “attack” in this case would come from law enforcement and security researchers.</p>



<p class="has-medium-font-size">In the past, bulletproof hosters ran their own networks, which often led to the whole ASN being blocklisted. More modern bulletproof hosters rent servers at cloud providers and set these up as proxies for their customers’ content. By rotating through a set of IP addresses, the content is less vulnerable to being blocked based on the IP address.</p>



<p class="has-medium-font-size">Intel471 recently <a href="https://intel471.com/blog/bulletproof-hosting-fast-flux-dns-double-flux-vps/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">wrote about</a> bulletproof hosters and in particular mentioned DNSPod.</p>



<p class="has-medium-font-size">Of course, we cannot be 100% certain that this is a bulletproof hoster, or even that the various campaigns do use the same infrastructure: the sharing of IP addresses may be a coincidence, or because there is another party involved in renting the servers.</p>



<p class="has-medium-font-size">But this is yet another example that shows how understanding the context of a domain name can help one find a lot of related infrastructure that is worth blocking, even without having seen evidence of actual malicious activity.</p>
