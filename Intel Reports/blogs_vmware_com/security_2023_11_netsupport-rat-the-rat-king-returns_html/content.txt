<h2></h2>
<h2><span style="font-weight: 400;">Executive Summary</span></h2>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">For years, threat actors have been using legitimate software for illegitimate or malicious purposes.  One such software is NetSupport Manager &#8211; a remote control application used for remote systems management.  In recent years, however, threat actors have repurposed this software as a Remote Access Trojan (RAT) to infiltrate systems and utilize them as a launching point for subsequent attacks.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">The Carbon Black Managed Detection &amp; Response team, in collaboration with our Threat Analysis Unit, has observed over 15 new infections related to NetSupport RAT in the last few weeks. From the increase we noticed that the majority of the infections were from the Education, Government, and Business Services sectors. In this article we will delve into our methods for detecting and preventing this malware, along with providing valuable insights and resources for defenders.</span></p>
<h2></h2>
<h2><span style="font-weight: 400;">History</span></h2>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">NetSupport Manager began as genuine software 30 years ago for remote technical support use.  The tool allowed file transfers, support chat, inventory management, and remote access.  While it is legitimate software, threat actors have been using it in recent years as a Remote Access Trojan (RAT) &#8211; most notably spread in </span><a href="https://www.bleepingcomputer.com/news/security/world-health-organization-warns-of-coronavirus-phishing-attacks/"><span style="font-weight: 400;">2020 via a massive COVID-19 phishing campaign</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">.  The delivery mechanisms for the NetSupport RAT encompass fraudulent updates, drive-by downloads, utilization of malware loaders (such as GhostPulse), and various forms of phishing campaigns.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Due to its legitimate nature and widespread availability, NetSupport Manager is not exclusive to a singular threat actor.  Multiple malicious entities, including the notorious TA569 &#8211; recognized for its SocGholish malware, incorporate this tool into their arsenal.  Its accessibility renders it susceptible to use by a spectrum of threat actors, ranging from novice hackers to sophisticated adversaries.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Older variations of NetSupport RAT were seen utilizing .BAT and .VBS files, often used as decoys.  Only one of the many BAT files being dropped would be responsible for executing the RAT and establishing persistence.  We have not observed these newer variants utilizing older methods.</span></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h2><span style="font-weight: 400;">Carbon Black Detection &amp; Attack Chain </span></h2>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">In recent attacks, the NetSupport RAT has been observed to be downloaded onto a victim&#8217;s computer via deceptive websites and fake browser updates.  Initial infection, however, can vary depending on the threat actor.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">The following infection showcases the victim getting tricked into downloading a fake browser update after visiting a compromised website.  These infected websites host a PHP script which displays a seemingly authentic update.  When the victim clicks on the download link, an additional Javascript payload is downloaded onto the endpoint.</span></p>
<p><a href="https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/11/NetSupportRAT_Figure-1-Chrome-NSR.png"><img decoding="async" class="aligncenter wp-image-83762 size-full" src="https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/11/NetSupportRAT_Figure-1-Chrome-NSR.png" alt="" width="871" height="591" srcset="https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/11/NetSupportRAT_Figure-1-Chrome-NSR.png 871w, https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/11/NetSupportRAT_Figure-1-Chrome-NSR-300x204.png 300w, https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/11/NetSupportRAT_Figure-1-Chrome-NSR-768x521.png 768w, https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/11/NetSupportRAT_Figure-1-Chrome-NSR-600x407.png 600w" sizes="(max-width: 871px) 100vw, 871px" /></a></p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Figure 1: Fake chrome browser update presented to victim</span></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">In this example, </span><b>Update_browser_10.6336.js</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> is the downloaded payload from the fake browser update and can be seen making external network connections to</span> <span style="font-weight: 400;">implacavelvideos[.]com</span></p>
<p><a href="https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/11/NetSupportRAT_Figure-2-NSR.png"><img decoding="async" loading="lazy" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-83763" src="https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/11/NetSupportRAT_Figure-2-NSR.png" alt="" width="761" height="238" srcset="https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/11/NetSupportRAT_Figure-2-NSR.png 761w, https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/11/NetSupportRAT_Figure-2-NSR-300x94.png 300w, https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/11/NetSupportRAT_Figure-2-NSR-600x188.png 600w" sizes="(max-width: 761px) 100vw, 761px" /></a></p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Figure 2: Update_browser_10.6336.js establishing connection to implacavelvideos[.]com</span></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><b>Update_browser_10.6336.js </b><span style="font-weight: 400;">then invokes</span><b> powershell.exe</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> to execute obfuscated commands which then connects to </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">kgscrew[.]com</span><span style="font-weight: 400;"> </span></p>
<p><a href="https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/11/NetSupportRAT_Figure-3-NSR-powershell-cmd.png"><img decoding="async" loading="lazy" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-83764" src="https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/11/NetSupportRAT_Figure-3-NSR-powershell-cmd.png" alt="" width="626" height="421" srcset="https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/11/NetSupportRAT_Figure-3-NSR-powershell-cmd.png 626w, https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/11/NetSupportRAT_Figure-3-NSR-powershell-cmd-300x202.png 300w, https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/11/NetSupportRAT_Figure-3-NSR-powershell-cmd-600x404.png 600w" sizes="(max-width: 626px) 100vw, 626px" /></a></p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Figure 3: Obfuscated Powershell Command </span></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Powershell.exe is then utilized to pass a Base64 snippet in memory, then decodes and stores the contents in a file called </span><b>p.zip</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">The contents of </span><b>p.zip</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> are then extracted into the directory: \</span><span style="font-weight: 400;">appdata\roaming\divx-429\</span><span style="font-weight: 400;"><br />
</span><span style="font-weight: 400;"><br />
</span></p>
<p><a href="https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/11/NetSupportRAT_Figure-4-NSR-pzip.png"><img decoding="async" loading="lazy" class="aligncenter wp-image-83765 size-medium" src="https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/11/NetSupportRAT_Figure-4-NSR-pzip-280x300.png" alt="" width="280" height="300" srcset="https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/11/NetSupportRAT_Figure-4-NSR-pzip-280x300.png 280w, https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/11/NetSupportRAT_Figure-4-NSR-pzip-956x1024.png 956w, https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/11/NetSupportRAT_Figure-4-NSR-pzip-768x823.png 768w, https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/11/NetSupportRAT_Figure-4-NSR-pzip-600x643.png 600w, https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/11/NetSupportRAT_Figure-4-NSR-pzip.png 1062w" sizes="(max-width: 280px) 100vw, 280px" /></a></p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Figure 4: PowerShell connecting to the C2 for additional payload and </span><b>p.zip</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> download.</span></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Multiple NetSupport dependencies/DLL’s as well as the NetSupport Manager are contained within this decompressed file.</span></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Once installed on a victim&#8217;s device, NetSupport is able to monitor behavior, transfer files, manipulate computer settings, and move to other devices within the network.</span></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><a href="https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/11/NetSupportRAT_Figure-5-NSR-kgscrew.png"><img decoding="async" loading="lazy" class="aligncenter size-large wp-image-83766" src="https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/11/NetSupportRAT_Figure-5-NSR-kgscrew-1024x485.png" alt="" width="1024" height="485" srcset="https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/11/NetSupportRAT_Figure-5-NSR-kgscrew-1024x485.png 1024w, https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/11/NetSupportRAT_Figure-5-NSR-kgscrew-300x142.png 300w, https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/11/NetSupportRAT_Figure-5-NSR-kgscrew-768x364.png 768w, https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/11/NetSupportRAT_Figure-5-NSR-kgscrew-600x284.png 600w, https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/11/NetSupportRAT_Figure-5-NSR-kgscrew.png 1352w" sizes="(max-width: 1024px) 100vw, 1024px" /></a></p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Figure 5: Numerous NetSupport files being dropped after the connection to </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">kgscrew[.]com</span></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Persistence is then established by adding </span><b>client32.exe</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> to the HKCU Run registry key in</span><span style="font-weight: 400;">:</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">\SOFTWARE\Microsoft\Windows\CurrentVersion\Run\DIVXX </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">or</span><span style="font-weight: 400;"> \SOFTWARE\Microsoft\Windows\CurrentVersion\Run\DIVX</span></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><a href="https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/11/NetSupportRAT_Figure-6-NSR-Divxx-registry.png"><img decoding="async" loading="lazy" class="aligncenter size-large wp-image-83767" src="https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/11/NetSupportRAT_Figure-6-NSR-Divxx-registry-1024x115.png" alt="" width="1024" height="115" srcset="https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/11/NetSupportRAT_Figure-6-NSR-Divxx-registry-1024x115.png 1024w, https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/11/NetSupportRAT_Figure-6-NSR-Divxx-registry-300x34.png 300w, https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/11/NetSupportRAT_Figure-6-NSR-Divxx-registry-768x86.png 768w, https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/11/NetSupportRAT_Figure-6-NSR-Divxx-registry-600x67.png 600w, https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/11/NetSupportRAT_Figure-6-NSR-Divxx-registry.png 1424w" sizes="(max-width: 1024px) 100vw, 1024px" /></a></p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Figure 6: PowerShell creates persistence in HKCU run registry</span></p>

<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">When the MDR team received this alert we observed a </span><b>powershell.exe</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> process with a very suspicious command line.  Given that the </span><b>.JS</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> file was also named “</span><b>update_browser_10.6336.js</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">”, we immediately identified this as NetSupport RAT.</span></p>

<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">When an affected endpoint makes a network connection to the compromised URL, the payload is downloaded; observed in Figure 10.  The downloaded payload is the GET response of the obfuscated script from the compromised URL (</span><span style="font-weight: 400;">gamefllix.com/111.php</span><span style="font-weight: 400;">)</span><span style="font-weight: 400;">. </span></p>
