			<h1 class="entry-title">Emotet is Back</h1><p class="wordcount"><span class="black">5 min read</span></p>			<p> <a href="https://blogs.cisco.com/author/merquiag" title="Posts by Maria Jose Erquiaga" rel="author">Maria Jose Erquiaga</a>			</p>
		</div>		
	</div> <!-- .blog-post-header -->
	<div class="entry-content">
		<p><em>The text below is a joint work of Maria Jose Erquiaga, Onur Erdogan and Adela Jezkova from Cisco Cognitive team</em></p>
<hr />
<p style="font-weight: 400;">Emotet (also known as Geodo and Heodo) is a banking trojan, but it is also a modular malware that can be used to download other malware as Trickbot and IcedID [8, 9, 13]. Emotet was observed for the first time in 2014 [9]. In January 2021, in a combined effort by Interpol and Eurojust, Emotet was taken down [12]. However, Emotet rose again in November 2021, and it has shown more activity since 2022) [6, 7].</p>
<p style="font-weight: 400;">Even though Emotet was born as a banking trojan, it evolved in time and became highly modular threat. This evolution granted adversaries a tool for different purposes. Emotet can be used as an initial payload and remain inactive for extended periods of time until the adversaries decide to leverage it [10]. This feature of Emotet gives the adversaries the flexibility to carry out a multi-stage infection process. This means that Emotet can act as banking trojan, but also has been observed to drop additional malware in the infected systems [1]. Emotet has the capability to gathering information of the infected systems and the adversaries can evaluate the value of the asset [14, 15] Some analysis shows that Emotet can drop CobaltStrike, which then drops ransomware [11]. For example, one of the ransomware dropped by Emotet is Ryuk [9].</p>
<p style="font-weight: 400;">In the past few months, Emotet malware has been observed in the wild, and its detection growth considerably [1]. Even though this Emotet re-appearance happened at (almost) the same time as Log4J vulnerability was discovered, there is not enough evidence that these two things are related. However, CobaltStrike, which is known to be related to Emotet, was using Log4J vulnerability [4].</p>
<p style="font-weight: 400;">The reappearance of Emotet motivated our deeper research and effort to update the detection ability for Global Threat Alerts  customers. As a result of it, the customers of Cisco Secure Network Analytics and Secure Endpoint using GTA get better coverage of the threat now.</p>
<p style="font-weight: 400;">We summarize in this blog Emotet threat, it’s lifecycle and typical detectable patterns. In the second part of the blog we show how to use GTA to detect the Emotet.</p>
<h2><strong><span style="color: #6abf4b;">Summary of Emotet characteristics</span></strong></h2>
<ul>
<li>Modular banking trojan</li>
<li>Downloader/Dropper</li>
<li>Polymorphic – can evade signature-based detection</li>
<li>Virtual machine aware</li>
</ul>
<h2><strong><span style="color: #6abf4b;">Emotet behavior</span></strong></h2>
<p style="font-weight: 400;">The attack flow is detailed in Figure 1. According to the analysis presented by Brad Duncan [2], the attack vector seems to be phishing, via an email with an attached file (1). The file contained in the phishing email, is an Office document (2). When the victims open the office document files and enable macros (3) the Emotet DLL is downloaded in the victim&#8217;s device (4). After downloaded, this DLL file is executed (5) and it generates the connection with Emotet Command and control (6) [5, 7].</p>
<figure id="attachment_395631" aria-describedby="caption-attachment-395631" style="width: 640px" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><img class="wp-image-395631 size-medium_large" src="https://storage.googleapis.com/blogs-images-new/ciscoblogs/1/2022/03/Picture1-768x450.jpg" sizes="(max-width: 768px) 100vw, 768px" srcset="https://storage.googleapis.com/blogs-images-new/ciscoblogs/1/2022/03/Picture1-300x176.jpg 300w, https://storage.googleapis.com/blogs-images-new/ciscoblogs/1/2022/03/Picture1-768x450.jpg 768w" alt="" width="640" height="375" /><figcaption id="caption-attachment-395631" class="wp-caption-text">Figure 1. Emotet attack flow</figcaption></figure>
<h2 style="font-weight: 400;"><strong><span style="color: #6abf4b;">Attached files and PowerShell execution</span></strong></h2>
<p style="font-weight: 400;">Once the victim opens and executes the infected files and enables the macros (mainly with docx or xml extensions), a command is executed to obtain and execute a HTML application. The pattern of the URL observed for this step is the following:</p>
<p>hxxp://{IP address}/[yy]/[y].{html|png}</p>
<p style="font-weight: 400;">Where “yy” are usually two alphabetical characters.</p>
<p style="font-weight: 400;">For example, one of the of the URLs founded in the wild:</p>
<p style="font-weight: 400;">hxxp://91.240.118[.]172/hh/hello.png</p>
<p style="font-weight: 400;">Then, it downloads PowerShell payload then it leads to downloading Emotet binary, which is a dll file from any of the given URLs contained in the URL described above. The format, in this case can vary, some of the URL&#8217;s patterns look like this:</p>
<p style="font-weight: 400;">http://ttisecurity[.]com/cgi/7RFeiqkgymCs/</p>
<p style="font-weight: 400;">Where the regex is:<br />
.*/(gci/){0,1}[a-z0-9\_]{3,20}$</p>
<p style="font-weight: 400;">Another pattern related to Emotet was</p>
<p style="font-weight: 400;">.*/(wp-admin/){0,1}[a-z0-9\_]{3,20}$</p>
<p style="font-weight: 400;">During the download of the Emotet payload, user agent pattern was, Mozilla/5.0 (Windows NT; Windows NT %; en-US) WindowsPowerShell/5.1.%</p>
<p style="font-weight: 400;"><strong>DLL execution and Emotet C2</strong></p>
<p style="font-weight: 400;">Once the DLL files is in the infected system, it downloads a PE file and then establishes a communication with its Command and Control, using HTTP or HTTPS protocols, on ports 80, 8080 and 443 [2]. Even though some researchers claim there is no relationship between Log4J vulnerability and Emotet, there are some common behaviours, as the use of the same IPs for C2. For example, those IP addresses are both related to Emotet and Log4j:</p>
<ul>
<li>250.21[.]2 and 116.124.128[.]206 founded in [4]</li>
<li>94.252[.]3</li>
<li>31.163[.]17</li>
<li>178.186[.]134</li>
<li>79.205[.]117</li>
</ul>
<h2><strong><span style="color: #6abf4b;">Detecting Emotet with Global Threat Alerts</span></strong></h2>
<p style="font-weight: 400;">GTA (Global Threat Alerts) detects Emotet as a High-risk threat. The threat description includes the MITRE software code and the techniques used by Emotet.</p>
<figure id="attachment_395635" aria-describedby="caption-attachment-395635" style="width: 908px" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><img loading="lazy" class="wp-image-395635 size-full" src="https://storage.googleapis.com/blogs-images-new/ciscoblogs/1/2022/03/cfcfcgfcg.png" sizes="(max-width: 908px) 100vw, 908px" srcset="https://storage.googleapis.com/blogs-images-new/ciscoblogs/1/2022/03/cfcfcgfcg-300x44.png 300w, https://storage.googleapis.com/blogs-images-new/ciscoblogs/1/2022/03/cfcfcgfcg-768x113.png 768w, https://storage.googleapis.com/blogs-images-new/ciscoblogs/1/2022/03/cfcfcgfcg.png 908w" alt="emotet" width="908" height="134" /><figcaption id="caption-attachment-395635" class="wp-caption-text">Figure 2. Detail of Emotet description in GTA</figcaption></figure>
<p style="font-weight: 400;">The threat detail (see <u>Figure 3) </u>contains also extra information regarding the files that could have been modified, deleted, or created by a particular threat. This information is enriched with the analysis of Emotet samples in Cisco Threat Grid [16].  The patterns of the files that could have been modified by Emotet, the probability of the malware behaviour, and the severity level for each one of the events are provided. This extra information helps network administrators and security teams to mitigate the threat not only in the network, but also in the devices.</p>
<figure id="attachment_395636" aria-describedby="caption-attachment-395636" style="width: 898px" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><img loading="lazy" class="wp-image-395636 size-full" src="https://storage.googleapis.com/blogs-images-new/ciscoblogs/1/2022/03/fcgfgcgc.png" sizes="(max-width: 898px) 100vw, 898px" srcset="https://storage.googleapis.com/blogs-images-new/ciscoblogs/1/2022/03/fcgfgcgc-300x146.png 300w, https://storage.googleapis.com/blogs-images-new/ciscoblogs/1/2022/03/fcgfgcgc-768x373.png 768w, https://storage.googleapis.com/blogs-images-new/ciscoblogs/1/2022/03/fcgfgcgc.png 898w" alt="emotet" width="898" height="436" /><figcaption id="caption-attachment-395636" class="wp-caption-text">Figure 3. In the first case, the asset established communication with the hostnames 201.213.32[.]59, 45.55.82[.]2 and 89.32.150[.]160 (Figure 4). In the second example, the asset communicated with the hostnames robertmchilespe[.]com and vbaint[.]com (Figure 5). In the third example, the detection found communication to the domain 104.131.148[.]38 (Figure 6).</p>

<h2><strong><span style="color: #6abf4b;">Emotet mitigation</span></strong></h2>
<p style="font-weight: 400;">To prevent Emotet, we suggest the following measures:</p>
<ul>
<li>Block emails with any attachment files that are suspicious</li>
<li>Scan suspicious files before opening them</li>
<li>Isolate the infected devices from the rest of the network to avoid spreading</li>
<li>Restrict the use of PowerShell and remote tools if possible</li>
<li>Reset all the user&#8217;s passwords in the infected devices</li>
<li>Consider use 2FA (such as Cisco DUO)</li>
</ul>
<h2><strong><span style="color: #6abf4b;">Conclusions</span></strong></h2>
<p style="font-weight: 400;">We conducted research to find not only new IOCs (IPs, domains and samples) but also URL patters related to this new Emotet wave to keep our customers up to date on the latest threats evolutions. The processed IOCs are also seeds to machine learning GTA algorithms which help to further enrich the detections. GTA users of Secure Endpoint and Secure Network Analytics can detect Emotet in their systems, execute mitigation actions and stay safe from the evolution of this threat.</p>
