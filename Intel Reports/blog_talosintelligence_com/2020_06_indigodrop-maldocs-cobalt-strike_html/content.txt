    <div class="text-center m-1">
        <div class="post-author">

    <section class="post-content-wrapper mt-5">
        <div class="post-content">
            <p><em>By <a href="https://twitter.com/asheermalhotra">Asheer Malhotra</a>.</em></p><h3></h3><ul><li>Cisco Talos has observed a malware campaign that utilizes military-themed malicious Microsoft Office documents (maldocs) to spread Cobalt Strike beacons containing full-fledged RAT capabilities.</li><li>These maldocs use malicious macros to deliver a multistage and highly modular infection.</li><li>This campaign appears to target military and government organizations in South Asia.</li><li>Network-based detection, although important, should be combined with endpoint protections to combat this threat and provide multiple layers of security.</li></ul><h3 id="what-s-new">Whats new?</h3><p>Cisco Talos has recently discovered a new campaign distributing a multistage attack used to infect target endpoints with customized <a href="https://www.cobaltstrike.com">Cobalt Strike</a> beacons. Due to the theme of the malicious documents (maldocs) employed, it is highly likely that military and government organizations in South Asia were targeted by this attack.</p><h3 id="how-did-it-work">How did it work?</h3><p>The attack consists of a highly modular dropper executable were calling IndigoDrop dropped to a victims endpoint using maldocs. IndigoDrop is responsible for obtaining the final payload from a download URL for deployment. The final payloads currently observed by Talos are Cobalt Strike beacons.<br></p><p>In this post, we illustrate the core technical capabilities of the maldocs, IndigoDrop and the Cobalt strike beacons components including: <br></p><ul><li>The maldocs-based infection chain.</li><li>IndigoDrops functionality.</li><li>Communication mechanisms and infrastructure used to download infection artifacts.</li><li>Detailed configurations of the Cobalt Strike beacons.<br></li></ul><h3 id="so-what">So what?</h3><p>This attack demonstrates how the adversary operates a targeted attack that:<br></p><ul><li>Uses legitimate-looking lures to trick the target into infecting themselves.</li><li>Employs a highly modular infection chain (implemented in the IndigoDrop) to instrument the final payload.</li><li>Uses an existing offensive framework (Cobalt Strike) to establish control and persist in the targets network without having to develop a bespoke remote access trojan (RAT).<br></li></ul><p>Analysis of recently discovered attack-chain variations provides insights into the evolution of this threat. These evolutions indicate the changes in tactics and techniques of the attackers used to continue attacks while trying to bypass detections. This campaign also shows us that while network-based detection is important, it should be complemented with system behavior analysis and endpoint protections for additional layers of security.<br></p><h3 id="analysis-of-maldocs">Analysis of maldocs</h3><p>This attack uses two techniques to deliver malicious macros to be executed on the targets endpoint:<br><br></p><ul><li>Malicious macros already embedded, ready to execute.</li><li>Malicious macro downloaded as part of an <a href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/2017/07/template-injection.html">externally linked template that is then injected</a> into the original lure maldoc.<br></li></ul><h4 id="maldoc-lures">Maldoc lures</h4><p>This attack consisted of maldocs masquerading as internal government or military documents. For example, some of the maldocs discovered by Talos masquerade as Incident Action Plan (IAP) documents dictating safeguard procedures for the IT infrastructure of the Indian Air Force (IAF).<br></p><p>These documents are aptly named:<br></p><ul><li>IAP39003.doc - Contains embedded malicious macro.</li><li>IAP39031.docx - Uses template injection.<br></li></ul><p>Sample content of the maldoc lures:</p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card"><img src="https://lh6.googleusercontent.com/jIVWUP0HzUCP00bBjggdV4C9i_uHBR7T23n4b1C_LGcgQx9nZVR_kctL_Gk1UyOybMDXHNH9ErS141Zgm3QrKdndx7MikvwA2qHEGGRuRi5dqhwAdgNorQeFNixibjOVduYMjvJhyiqiNLxX9_cC2yYUTYIsbsgQEnyDzRvdlaisQLqBqs14Sru12x7P" class="kg-image" alt loading="lazy" width="557" height="713"></figure><p><br><br></p><h5 id="fake-document-or-weaponized-copy">Fake document or weaponized copy?</h5><p>Many targeted attacks employing maldocs observed by Talos usually consist of utmost a couple of pages of decoy content to make them look legitimate. The documents used in this attack, however, contain legitimate content (~64 pages, ~15k words), making them seem even more bonafide. Talos also found a benign copy of one of the maldocs indicating that it is highly likely that the attackers weaponized and distributed it to targets</p><p>(Benign copy hash: 0d16b15972d3d0f8a1481db4e2413a2c519e8ac49cd2bf3fca02cfa3ff0be532).<br></p><h4 id="malicious-vba-analysis">Malicious VBA Analysis</h4><p>This section consists of an analysis of malicious macros used to carry out the attacks using:</p><ul><li>Locally embedded malicious macros and</li><li>Malicious macros embedded in the injected templates downloaded from a remote location.<br></li></ul><p>The malicious macros carry out the following malicious activities:<br></p><ol><li>Parse a Windows executables hardcoded bytes into bytes that can be written to a file on disk.<br></li><li>The parsed bytes are written to an EXE file in the currently logged in users Startup directory: E.g.</li></ol><blockquote>%userprofile%\AppData\Roaming\Microsoft\Windows\Start Menu\Programs\Startup\anything.exe<br></blockquote><ol><li>Once the malicious EXE (second-stage payload  IndigoDrop ) has been written to the users Startup directory the macro quits execution without executing the actual second-stage payload.<br></li><li>The attack relies on the user either logging in again or restarting the system to activate the second-stage payload on the infected endpoint.<br></li><li>The second-stage payload in this attack is a custom dropper (IndigoDrop) that carries out a variety of tasks.<br></li></ol><p>Malicious macro code:</p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card"><img src="https://lh6.googleusercontent.com/SI0MnjPZQmRAum6MUVRPbVVxiz6VLnWfsr8_uI9fYq-QJ8G9f4rxT1R_FvpWbbrmV1R-9Yyg_SGcHMfRVtVR0nXfWnkIwV3dRfqbp1uEUAV8Qt6ggFvaZ-2Jgn1bLzhQ4zAGZVzVgRT_gULGDiq1yU77VzU1xoS5Tv3U90tSBxMOtHC81hWQt4anWAaF" class="kg-image" alt loading="lazy" width="624" height="315"></figure><p><br><br></p><h4 id="maldoc-distribution">Maldoc distribution</h4><p>One of the maldocs disclosed here was referred to by a Bit.ly-shortened URL (created Jan. 23, 2020)  hxxp://bit[.]ly/iaf-guidelines  which redirects to hxxp://tecbeck[.]com/IAP39031[.]docx.<br></p><p>It is highly likely that the attackers hosted the maldocs on a public server and distributed the direct or Bit.ly links to the targets in the form of spear-phishing emails. This may be done to bypass detection systems that scan email attachments for malware.<br></p><h3 id="stage-2-dropper-binary-indigodrop">Stage 2: Dropper binary  IndigoDrop<br></h3><p>The second-stage binary dropped to disk by the maldocs is a malicious dropper/loader were calling IndigoDrop designed to download and activate a customized Cobalt Strike beacon (final payload DLL) from another remote location.<br></p><p>Before we delve into a detailed analysis, some of the key operational features of IndigoDrop are:<br></p><ul><li>Highly modular in nature: IndigoDrop usually consists of three hardcoded locations that can be used to download and activate the next payload.</li><li>In this attack, IndigoDrop utilizes both attacker-operated remote locations and public data hosting platforms such as pastebin[.]com to host the next stage payloads.It is highly likely that this dropper may download the final payload from these remote locations (likely done in other variants of the attack).</li><li>However this instance of the attack downloads a Metasploit shellcode from the hardcoded remote locations. We will refer to this Metasploit shellcode as Stage 2A in this post (detailed later-on).The Metasploit shellcode (Stage 2A) is hosted in the form of a Base64 encoded string on the download locations. This shellcode is base64 decoded, unhexlified and executed on the endpoint as part of IndigoDrops execution.<br></li></ul><p>Base64-encoded Metasploit shellcode:</p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card"><img src="https://lh3.googleusercontent.com/NKRQeAaP75R4BHSnXX5bHmzqivDVLMpMqohzOwHzIlXc2s0nHRl4_y_SfWbzO0Vdl4uZ6ukQT2iq_eeIQvjF2Fc2TFwvqHmtIdB81xyhz-mIoskBM_sKvJYSlMf3kJPLBjsUnJgRzmfy_qJcRQcolrwEw_n5gq-D7FV0kMmRycSGsAihSNCWhqFTr32m" class="kg-image" alt loading="lazy" width="624" height="193"></figure><p><br></p><p>Base64-decoded Metasploit shellcode:</p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card"><img src="https://lh4.googleusercontent.com/SY05VNai6ISckzbIic9NsGvnYNIX8z0ha6WlaAhNbRKWhCkTVLg69T9BOoxQ_iSre8CC2o0toUHKzFC8hWn5tV5H3viF19r4xP0-f1Rf78v2B-P5Rh_irF7WoAcs0El9loRkPcPhhEQ9TAu7ig5RX7ld2pnJLZftn_vcXOMs-efyNEh7Jo60t2OapExp" class="kg-image" alt loading="lazy" width="624" height="265"></figure><p><br></p><h4 id="indigodrop-analysis">IndigoDrop analysis</h4><p>This dropper performs the following actions on the endpoint:<br></p><ul><li>Establish persistence using the registry Run key for itself in location:</li></ul><blockquote>HKCU\Software\Microsoft\Windows\CurrentVersion\Run | iexplorer = cmd /c &lt;file_path_of_Dropper&gt; /onboot -hide</blockquote><p>E.g.</p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card"><img src="https://lh6.googleusercontent.com/ZwssFq9tUQ6Dc0WfR3a4Bvk_TYWqg3MvVNP0IgryRr7nKovnro7Dq2OhoZhknnzmAmmZlWIGHZwdHKnNGfATsxXRONYe97xk_GY8QOuwwHQY3K91pWC9PjFs2KT1qQyi5JeNnJMHTdg0cmliM64ZZeqiOw9Gk5LJpmDouESsYLYmKGIixM0up68pSmvh" class="kg-image" alt loading="lazy" width="624" height="44"></figure><p><br></p><ul><li>Download and execute the Stage 2A Metasploit shellcode.</li><li>Anti-Infection Checks: Check the current Username, Computername, parent folder name, MAC addresses, Public IP addresses against a list of blocked values. If any of the values match, IndigoDrop quits (Blocked values listed in the IOC section).<br></li></ul><h3 id="stage-2a-metasploit-msf-downloader-shellcode">Stage 2A: Metasploit (MSF) downloader shellcode</h3><p>The Metasploit shellcode is a modified reverse HTTP stager meant to download a malicious file from the specified download location. This shellcode (stage 2A) is usually hosted on a public hosting site such as pastebin[.]com.<br></p><p>The malicious file downloaded is usually a copy of a trojanized jquery[.]min.js file. A variety of infection artifacts are utilized ranging from bespoke tools (IndigoDrop) to customizable adversarial tools (Cobalt Strike beacons). The attackers also use a combination of public and private servers to host their malicious payloads with a growing trend towards the sole usage of public servers.</p><p>The use of military-themed maldocs (lures) indicates that government and military organizations in South Asia may be the targets of this threat actor. The earliest observable campaigns of this actor date back to April 2018 and continue to operate today along with the most recent evolutions of the attacks. Evidence of rapid ideation, testing and production of new and diversified modules and IndigoDrop iterations indicates highly motivated and agile adversaries. The use of adversarial frameworks like Cobalt Strike suggests that the attackers are looking to expand their malicious arsenal at a significant rate with self-authored and customizable artifacts.</p><p>Modern-day malware attack chains consist of multiple stages and operational entities. These artifacts and entities may be hosted locally or on remote servers. For example, this attack consists of multiple shellcodes hosted on remote locations downloaded by a local component (IndigoDrop) during runtime to instrument the attack chain. Thus, while network-based detection is important, it should be complemented with system behavior analysis and endpoint protections.<br></p><h3 id="coverage">Coverage</h3><p>Ways our customers can detect and block this threat are listed below.<br></p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card"><img src="https://lh6.googleusercontent.com/kkz7Tb5IBw05n6CSUI3bjGvYYScRULeG_G4bxpx7o3gjVpL-58BahPVKS1QzY7pXuKGmudbIW1d8w9GlX59FJKLtxAPgtfuvEDTlu_bscKZ5HwXWeWino9AG6lyY9zxZIX8HvHjwqmjYCe2rDb1s9wDH_f_QZMt6vDQjP3f9aJqDWW289V1BgnHMLF5q" class="kg-image" alt loading="lazy" width="314" height="320"></figure><p>Advanced Malware Protection (<a href="https://www.cisco.com/c/en/us/products/security/advanced-malware-protection">AMP</a>) is ideally suited to prevent the execution of the malware detailed in this post.
