    <div class="node-full__body blog-content__body">  <h3 paraeid="{22b2f6b8-450f-4cee-bfbc-47e97b700f29}{245}" paraid="1569680177">Key takeaways&nbsp;</h3>

<ul>
	<li paraeid="{22b2f6b8-450f-4cee-bfbc-47e97b700f29}{251}" paraid="1645222094">Proofpoint has observed an increase in cryptocurrency fraud that impersonates various organizations to target users with fake job lures.&nbsp;&nbsp;</li>
	<li paraeid="{debb8a04-5fac-4d7b-b297-57b5a508c753}{2}" paraid="513050408">Researchers assess with high confidence this fraud is conducted by actors who conduct Pig Butchering, or romance-based cryptocurrency investment fraud.&nbsp;</li>
	<li paraeid="{debb8a04-5fac-4d7b-b297-57b5a508c753}{8}" paraid="1125238713">The job fraud has smaller but more frequent returns for the fraudsters compared to Pig Butchering.&nbsp;&nbsp;</li>
	<li paraeid="{debb8a04-5fac-4d7b-b297-57b5a508c753}{14}" paraid="472372456">The activity leverages popular brand recognition in place of a long, romance-based confidence scam.&nbsp;</li>
	<li paraeid="{debb8a04-5fac-4d7b-b297-57b5a508c753}{20}" paraid="1059034063">The activity uses a flexible platform to tailor the scam to a variety of lure types.&nbsp;</li>
	<li paraeid="{debb8a04-5fac-4d7b-b297-57b5a508c753}{26}" paraid="964053411">The activity is initiated through social media, SMS&nbsp;and messaging apps like WhatsApp and Telegram.&nbsp;&nbsp;</li>
</ul>

<h3 paraeid="{debb8a04-5fac-4d7b-b297-57b5a508c753}{32}" paraid="166967578">Overview&nbsp;</h3>

<p paraeid="{debb8a04-5fac-4d7b-b297-57b5a508c753}{38}" paraid="1712375674">For years, <a data-entity-substitution="canonical" data-entity-type="node" data-entity-uuid="ca1f69b5-88dd-4046-a096-1892012392d8" href="/us/blog/threat-insight/broken-dreams-and-piggy-banks-pig-butchering-crypto-fraud-growing-online" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank" title="Broken Dreams and Piggy Banks: Pig Butchering Crypto Fraud Growing Online">Pig Butcher scammers </a>have swindled victims out of <a href="https://www.wired.com/story/pig-butchering-fbi-ic3-2022-report/" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">billions</a> of dollars. Typically, they lure victims in with long-winded confidence scams that eventually direct victims to a fake cryptocurrency investment platform. Once a target’s initial small investment starts turning into a large (but fake) “profit,” the scammers pressure the victim to invest vast sums of money. Once the victim’s money leaves their digital wallet, the unrealized gains are replaced with a sinking realization—they have lost everything.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>

<p paraeid="{debb8a04-5fac-4d7b-b297-57b5a508c753}{60}" paraid="750875528">While quite <a href="https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=4742235" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">profitable</a> for the pig butchers, these scams take a long time and offer many opportunities for the victim to realize something might be off. For this business model to be successful, they need to target victims with money to lose. This leaves a large market of financially insecure victims—that provide the scammers with smaller payout, but a larger pool of people—virtually untapped. Recently, however, Proofpoint observed these types of fraudsters launch a new business venture to tap into this market: job scamming.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>

<p paraeid="{debb8a04-5fac-4d7b-b297-57b5a508c753}{71}" paraid="343532692">In June 2024, the U.S. Federal Bureau of Investigation published a <a href="https://www.ic3.gov/Media/Y2024/PSA240604" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">public service announcement</a> warning the public of these fraudulent job scams originating on mobile devices.&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>

<h3 paraeid="{debb8a04-5fac-4d7b-b297-57b5a508c753}{82}" paraid="591572934">Job duties may include&nbsp;</h3>

<p paraeid="{debb8a04-5fac-4d7b-b297-57b5a508c753}{88}" paraid="1348339363">The scam typically begins with an unsolicited message on a social media platform or other messaging application, like SMS, WhatsApp, Telegram, etc. It starts out like a typical job recruiter message (see Figures 1 and 2). They specifically highlight the job as a work from home (WFH) opportunity. Despite recent pushes for return to office (RTO), remote jobs remain very popular with <a href="https://arstechnica.com/tech-policy/2024/08/remote-workers-are-27-percent-more-likely-to-look-forward-to-work/" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">job seekers</a>.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>

<p paraeid="{debb8a04-5fac-4d7b-b297-57b5a508c753}{105}" paraid="1110535817"><img alt="Figure 1" data-entity-type="file" data-entity-uuid="8f5df176-82ee-4de4-8e41-5969e0b5abd3" height="111" src="/sites/default/files/inline-images/Screenshot%202024-10-23%20at%2012.52.10%E2%80%AFPM.png" width="712" loading="lazy"></p>

<p paraeid="{debb8a04-5fac-4d7b-b297-57b5a508c753}{110}" paraid="2104121089"><em>Figure 1: Fake Glassdoor recruiter.&nbsp;&nbsp;</em></p>

<p paraeid="{debb8a04-5fac-4d7b-b297-57b5a508c753}{125}" paraid="1939394144">The fraudster then launches into a brief description of the company “work platform.” The specific job and brand impersonation can vary quite a bit from boosting popular music streams on Spotify to reviewing fake TikTok Shop products and services, or even giving hotel reviews. Essentially click farming.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>

<p paraeid="{debb8a04-5fac-4d7b-b297-57b5a508c753}{139}" paraid="1211249506"><img alt="Figure 2" data-entity-type="file" data-entity-uuid="abf4e8ed-3d58-4f32-a7ed-1b02b28053f8" height="282" src="/sites/default/files/inline-images/Screenshot%202024-10-23%20at%2012.52.04%E2%80%AFPM.png" width="704" loading="lazy"></p>

<p paraeid="{debb8a04-5fac-4d7b-b297-57b5a508c753}{144}" paraid="1223299031"><em>Figure 2: Hotel ratings job scam pitch.&nbsp;</em></p>

<p paraeid="{debb8a04-5fac-4d7b-b297-57b5a508c753}{163}" paraid="1450086850">Once the target agrees to take the job, the threat actor instructs them to register on a website, which is malicious. To register, the target is given a referral code. The page content is not viewable unless a user is registered. This makes surfacing and taking down these websites slightly more challenging for defenders, as domain takedown requests often require a substantial amount of evidence. Luckily, <a href="https://www.proofpoint.com/us/products/advanced-threat-protection/et-intelligence" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">Emerging Threats</a> was up to the challenge and has developed signatures to identify these new job scam sites.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>

<p paraeid="{debb8a04-5fac-4d7b-b297-57b5a508c753}{182}" paraid="1792709496"><img alt="Figure 3" data-entity-type="file" data-entity-uuid="031fa778-32c0-4743-8cf6-430f2252890f" height="245" src="/sites/default/files/inline-images/Screenshot%202024-10-23%20at%2012.51.58%E2%80%AFPM.png" width="904" loading="lazy"></p>

<p paraeid="{debb8a04-5fac-4d7b-b297-57b5a508c753}{182}" paraid="1792709496"><em>Figure 3: Directions to register.&nbsp;</em></p>

<p paraeid="{debb8a04-5fac-4d7b-b297-57b5a508c753}{202}" paraid="2107799295">After the victim has registered with the site, the job scammer—or “handler”—will ask that the target shares a screenshot of their profile page so they can create a training account.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>

<p paraeid="{debb8a04-5fac-4d7b-b297-57b5a508c753}{202}" paraid="2107799295"><img alt="Figure 4" data-entity-type="file" data-entity-uuid="c86fea57-9588-4c24-992d-a5bb1bb8055c" height="409" src="/sites/default/files/inline-images/Screenshot%202024-10-23%20at%2012.51.05%E2%80%AFPM.png" width="902" loading="lazy"></p>

This part will vary a bit depending on the platform theme—like submitting product or service reviews, placing product orders or booking hotels—but the core characteristics are the same. The user must click a button to submit a review, sales data, play music or some other task. <p paraeid="{cc7c7789-3e60-44c6-ae82-1a2e9f2029c9}{103}" paraid="1377073974">The number of times a user must click to get “paid” will vary from 30-50 times per job session, based on instructions from their “handler.” After clicking for a bit (usually a little over halfway through), the user will encounter an error that will not allow them to continue. The fake commission account balance will show a negative amount. The handler will explain that the user has encountered a “lucky” event and will be super jazzed.</p>

<p paraeid="{cc7c7789-3e60-44c6-ae82-1a2e9f2029c9}{103}" paraid="1377073974"><img alt="Figure 7" data-entity-type="file" data-entity-uuid="031664e6-295c-4e6f-bb60-64ea3a8c5b62" src="/sites/default/files/inline-images/Screenshot%202024-10-23%20at%2012.50.54%E2%80%AFPM.png" width="1260" height="254" loading="lazy"></p>

<p paraeid="{cc7c7789-3e60-44c6-ae82-1a2e9f2029c9}{147}" paraid="1723413444">The handler does not give a clear explanation as to why the account balance was reduced to zero, but claims it is good for the victim. The fraudster explains that once the user brings the account back out of the negative, they will unlock a multiplier on their income. For training, the handler will pay it this time, but the user will get to reap the rewards once they start on their own account. They will instruct the user to reach out to a support agent on the platform to get the cryptocurrency wallet address and they will bring the account out of the negative and ask that you continue your work. Once work has been completed, they will direct the user to log back into their own account. It will typically show a balance of $70 to $80. And if they try to start working again, the webpage with the fake account balance details will inform the user that to use the platform they will need to bring the account up to a minimum of $100. The handler will urge them to do so or lose out on the “5x bonus” received during training. The scammer will also inform the target about their “VIP commission tiers” (see Figure 8) where workers can invest between $100-$5,000 to increase the number of commissions received and how many tasks can be submitted in a day.&nbsp;</p>

<h3 paraeid="{cc7c7789-3e60-44c6-ae82-1a2e9f2029c9}{188}" paraid="1256040371">Click, click, boom&nbsp;</h3>

<p paraeid="{cc7c7789-3e60-44c6-ae82-1a2e9f2029c9}{194}" paraid="880237757">So, what happens next? Let’s assume the victim tops off the account using their own money and starts happily clicking away. After a couple days of building up their commission check, they will get “lucky” and have a negative balance again like in the training session. Their handler will urge them to pay into it and reassure them that it will be worth it. After sending the cryptocurrency to the designated wallet, the victim will indeed start seeing their balance start to climb as predicted. Much like the related Pig Butcher scam, at this point the victim may even be allowed to withdraw their first “paycheck.” The withdrawal is never allowed to be more than they have put into the platform, but they will be allowed to take out some. The platforms do not process any payments. Instead, they are processed by the “customer service” reps or the handler (assuming the handler thinks they will continue to keep paying out, that is). This vicious cycle will continue as long as the scammers think the victim will keep paying into the system. If they suspect their victim has become wise to the scam, they will lock their account and ghost them. While losses are typically lower than your run-of-the-mill Pig Butcher cryptocurrency investment scam, losses can easily climb into the <a href="https://www.nzherald.co.nz/nz/hotel-review-scam-student-loses-42k-works-48-hour-weekends-after-putting-parents-landlord-in-debt/6AQQ2GIAKFGGXERNIS3HILCJO4/" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">tens of thousands of dollars.</a>&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>

<h3 paraeid="{1ee443f4-c779-45f0-ac1b-7298e5a797c5}{42}" paraid="1647151338">Danger in numbers&nbsp;</h3>

<p paraeid="{1ee443f4-c779-45f0-ac1b-7298e5a797c5}{48}" paraid="132797203">While many of these scams involve a one-on-one relationship between the victim and the handler, Proofpoint researchers have identified some victims being directed to a large group chat on Telegram or WhatsApp. This allows them to fill the chat with confederates who will gaslight the victims and brag about how much money they have made. We see this type of group chat frequently with Pig Butcher scams. If you are suddenly added to any group chat on these types of platforms, be very wary. Proofpoint researchers have observed the group and individual chats operating in multiple countries and languages.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>

<h3 paraeid="{1ee443f4-c779-45f0-ac1b-7298e5a797c5}{78}" paraid="1132576798">“I would never fall for that!”&nbsp;</h3>

<p paraeid="{1ee443f4-c779-45f0-ac1b-7298e5a797c5}{84}" paraid="1672743916">At first glance, this scam seems very unlikely to work. Having to pay a company money to get paid is absurd, you might think to yourself. But scams work because of emotions and desires. And for many job seekers, especially those in great need of an income, the prospect of a well-paying job with limited job requirements and seemingly quick payouts may seem alluring enough to ignore potential signs of a scam.&nbsp; &nbsp;</p>

<p paraeid="{1ee443f4-c779-45f0-ac1b-7298e5a797c5}{104}" paraid="221926003">To get a better understanding of why and how people may fall for these scams, our threat researchers asked Proofpoint resident psychologist Dr. Bob Hausmann what factors are at play that could make the scam so effective. According to Dr. Hausmann, there are likely three psychological mechanisms at work:&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>

<ol>
	<li paraeid="{1ee443f4-c779-45f0-ac1b-7298e5a797c5}{110}" paraid="293193464">Sunk cost fallacy&nbsp;</li>
	<li paraeid="{1ee443f4-c779-45f0-ac1b-7298e5a797c5}{116}" paraid="131255455">Loss aversion&nbsp;&nbsp;</li>
	<li paraeid="{1ee443f4-c779-45f0-ac1b-7298e5a797c5}{122}" paraid="1023455207">Principle of reciprocity&nbsp;</li>
</ol>

<p paraeid="{1ee443f4-c779-45f0-ac1b-7298e5a797c5}{128}" paraid="1583937273">The sunk cost fallacy is the idea that people will not abandon something or stop doing something when they have invested heavily in it, whether it be time or money, despite the investment not returning positive results. This concept, as part of the scam, is also fueled by what behavioral psychologists call l<a href="https://www.investopedia.com/terms/l/loss-psychology.asp" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">oss aversion</a>—the idea that the fear of losing outweighs the feeling of obtaining an equivalent amount of money. In this case, the job scam victim likely feels they have put too much time and effort into making a perceived sum, and so they will pay into the fake website in order to prevent the loss of what they believe to be hard-earned gains.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>

<p paraeid="{1ee443f4-c779-45f0-ac1b-7298e5a797c5}{139}" paraid="2077730889">Additionally, whether they realize it or not, the scammer relies on the principle of reciprocity to get their target to pay into the fake platform. By paying the initial sum when the balance went below zero, the handler appears to do the target a favor by paying the negative balance, and then the target is indebted to them. In this case, the target is willing to go along with the next cycle and pay the negative balance themselves.&nbsp;</p>

<p paraeid="{1ee443f4-c779-45f0-ac1b-7298e5a797c5}{171}" paraid="459277840">Unfortunately, all these psychological factors are part of why the scam is so successful.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>

<p paraeid="{1ee443f4-c779-45f0-ac1b-7298e5a797c5}{177}" paraid="1305824239">According to our colleagues at the cryptocurrency investigation company <a href="https://www.chainalysis.com/" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">Chainalysis</a>, thousands of people have decided to pay into fake job platforms. Proofpoint researchers shared public cryptocurrency wallet addresses associated with identified job scams to see how effective the schemes were. Chainalysis’ research showed that the cryptocurrency wallet for daptonerecordsmusicalbums[.]com—a fake job site impersonating a record label—had only been active for two months but made over $300,000 in Bitcoin and Ethereum. Funds from this wallet have been going into an account shared by short-term trade investment scams (aka Pig Butchers), and other job scam sites, according to Chainalysis’ findings.&nbsp;</p>

<p paraeid="{1ee443f4-c779-45f0-ac1b-7298e5a797c5}{222}" paraid="181332874"><img alt="Figure 9" data-entity-type="file" data-entity-uuid="d2128d81-2b02-4733-8d4d-2d424c8a5821" height="331" src="/sites/default/files/inline-images/Screenshot%202024-10-23%20at%2012.49.09%E2%80%AFPM.png" width="510" loading="lazy"></p>

<p paraeid="{1ee443f4-c779-45f0-ac1b-7298e5a797c5}{227}" paraid="1060031794"><em>Figure 9: Pig Butcher and job scam cryptocurrency flow. In this case, the actors impersonated Daptone Records.&nbsp;</em></p>

<p paraeid="{1ee443f4-c779-45f0-ac1b-7298e5a797c5}{241}" paraid="25353375">Here is a summary of how much cryptocurrency was stolen by the fake job scam websites identified by our researchers in collaboration with Chainalysis:&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>

<h3 paraeid="{15808f62-fa1f-4455-b106-dc4d5e14c674}{68}" paraid="1944380762">Conclusion&nbsp;</h3>

<p paraeid="{15808f62-fa1f-4455-b106-dc4d5e14c674}{74}" paraid="792698832">Cryptocurrency investment and job-related scams are extremely prevalent and use sophisticated social engineering techniques to convince people they are legitimate. <p paraeid="{15808f62-fa1f-4455-b106-dc4d5e14c674}{88}" paraid="920828574">To protect yourself, friends and family against these types of scams, Proofpoint recommends the following:&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>

	<li paraeid="{15808f62-fa1f-4455-b106-dc4d5e14c674}{112}" paraid="555013341">Never provide any money to a person who claims to be an employer. 	<li paraeid="{15808f62-fa1f-4455-b106-dc4d5e14c674}{123}" paraid="926349390">Remember the old adage: If it seems too good to be true, it probably is.&nbsp;</li>
	<li paraeid="{15808f62-fa1f-4455-b106-dc4d5e14c674}{137}" paraid="1390448612">Spread the word.&nbsp;Knowledge is power when fighting scams.&nbsp;</li>
