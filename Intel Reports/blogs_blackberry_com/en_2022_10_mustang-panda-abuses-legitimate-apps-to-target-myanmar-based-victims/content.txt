    <i><b>Update 12.06.22</b>:&nbsp;<a title="https://blogs.blackberry.com/en/2022/10/mustang-panda-abuses-legitimate-apps-to-target-myanmar-based-victims" href="https://blogs.blackberry.com/en/2022/12/mustang-panda-uses-the-russian-ukrainian-war-to-attack-europe-and-asia-pacific-targets" target="_blank">Mustang Panda</a>&nbsp;is targeting countries across Europe and Asia Pacific, utilizing geopolitical events to their advantage. Their attack chain remains consistent, with the continued use of archive files, shortcut files, malicious loaders, and the use of PlugX malware. The goal of this particular operation appears to be collecting sensitive information from European countries and states from Asia, which might be supporting Western countries. <a href="https://blogs.blackberry.com/en/2022/12/mustang-panda-uses-the-russian-ukrainian-war-to-attack-europe-and-asia-pacific-targets" target="_blank">Learn more</a>.<br>

    <h2>Executive Summary</h2>
<p>The BlackBerry Research &amp; Intelligence Team recently uncovered a campaign by an advanced persistent threat (APT) group called Mustang Panda that is leveraging the PlugX malware family to target the Southeast Asian state of Myanmar.</p>
<p>Our team analyzed the samples in question and found their embedded configurations revealed a set of command-and-control (C2) domains that masquerade as Myanmar news outlets. This is not the first time a campaign targeting this state has impersonated <a href="https://securelist.com/apt-luminousmoth/103332/" target="_blank">Myanmar news outlets</a> or used PlugX malware.</p>
<p>These tactics, techniques, and procedures (TTPs), along with other corroborating evidence – such as a <a href="https://twitter.com/AvastThreatLabs/status/1404864965584977922" target="_blank">previous indication</a> that the group was active in this location – lead us to assert with <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytic_confidence" target="_blank">reasonable confidence</a> that the China-based threat group known as Mustang Panda is responsible for this campaign</p>
<h2>Mustang Panda: an Origin Story</h2>
<p>Mustang Panda (aka HoneyMyte, Bronze President or Red Delta) is a prolific APT group that has been publicly attributed as being based in China. This group conducted malware campaigns as far back as <a href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/2022/05/mustang-panda-targets-europe.html" target="_blank">2012</a>, which primarily related to cyber-espionage.</p>
<p>Their targets have included Government and Non-Government Organizations (NGO) in many locations around the world, from various states in Southeast Asia to the European Union to the U.S. and beyond.</p>

    
    
</div>

<h3><b>Mustang Panda Attack Vector</b></h3>
<p>Mustang Panda typically sends phishing emails with malicious document attachments as an initial infection vector. These documents are usually designed to mimic those of the targeted country or organization, or even current world affairs applicable to that region.</p>
<p>Once threat actors gain a foothold within a target organization, they typically deploy one of a variety of payloads such as <a href="https://blogs.blackberry.com/en/2021/11/all-your-beacon-are-belong-to-us-new-blackberry-book-cracks-code-of-cobalt-strike-threat-actors" target="_blank">Cobalt Strike</a>, Poison Ivy, or <a href="https://blogs.blackberry.com/en/2016/06/cylanceprotect-vs-plugx" target="_blank">PlugX</a>, the latter of which is used most extensively.</p>
<h3><b>Initial Thread</b></h3>
<p>In late May of this year, BlackBerry detected some unusual network traffic to a domain&nbsp;<i>–</i> www[.]myanmarnewsonline[.]org. At first glance, this URL appeared to be a Myanmar news website.</p>
<p>The files found to be communicating with this site were encompassed in several .RAR files. These files had a relatively low detection ratio on VirusTotal (VT), and as shown in Figure 2, they followed a naming convention designed to make them appear to be legitimate utilities relating to Hewlett-Packard (HP) printers.</p>

    
    
</div>

<p>The RAR archives contained a legitimate signed utility from HP, along with a DLL loader and a DAT file that is an encrypted PlugX payload.</p>
<p>One of the legitimate utilities (“HPCustParticUI.exe” – SHA256 8857232077b4b0f0e4a2c3bb5717fd65079209784f41694f8e1b469e34754cf6) was previously used in a similar fashion as part of a PlugX execution chain, which was documented by <a href="https://www.trellix.com/en-us/about/newsroom/stories/threat-labs/operation-harvest-a-deep-dive-into-a-long-term-campaign.html" target="_blank">another vendor</a> in September 2021.</p>
<p>In early June of this year, a tweet from the user @<b>kienbigmummy </b>(shown in Figure 3) mentioned an additional .RAR file titled “service Log.rar” that was linked with a sub-domain of the previously mentioned website – images[.]myanmarnewsonline[.]org – that was associated with PlugX and the Mustang Panda APT group.</p>

<h3><b>What is PlugX?</b></h3>
<p>PlugX is a remote access tool (RAT) used by <a href="https://attack.mitre.org/software/S0013/" target="_blank">several</a> threat groups. It is the malware of choice for the Chinese APT group Mustang Panda. This group delivers the PlugX implant in the form of an encrypted data blob, which is typically paired with a DLL loader as well as a benign application.</p>
<p>This actor has <a href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/2022/05/mustang-panda-targets-europe.html" target="_blank">commonly employed the stealthy technique of side-loading</a> the malicious DLLs into legitimate applications during execution. This action then deploys the PlugX implant into memory.</p>
<p>We noted threat actors had used three separate legitimate applications within our RAR files; A free VPN service, and two legitimate HP applications related to HP’s Digital Imaging. Each legitimate application was bundled with a DLL and a data file. In two out of the three RAR files, the DAT file masqueraded as a different file format, such as JSON or CHM.</p>
<p>Upon execution of the legitimate application, the threat loads a malicious DLL loader in a specific set order, which the threat actor has strategically placed in the same folder to replace a legitimate one. This proceeds to side-load the DLL by abusing the DLL search order, which is a technique also known as <a href="https://attack.mitre.org/techniques/T1574/001/" target="_blank">DLL Search Order Hijacking</a>. The malicious DLL is then loaded into the legitimate application, where it decrypts, loads and deploys the malicious PlugX implant. This execution chain is shown in Figure 5.</p>

<h2>Technical Analysis</h2>
<p>The DLL loader is heavily obfuscated and employs dynamic API resolution upon runtime. It retrieves a handle to the encrypted PlugX implant, then reads the data into a newly allocated region within memory. Execution is then passed to the implant, where the shellcode is executed, and it XOR decrypts the embedded payload, as shown in Figure 6. Once decryption is complete, <b>RtlDecompressBuffer </b>is called to decompress the decrypted payload to its final form as shown in Figure 7. </p>

<h2>Conclusion</h2>
<p>Mustang Panda, which is publicly known as a Chinese-affiliated APT group, has an established history of using the PlugX malware and targeting nations throughout South-East Asia. This threat actor has been previously linked to campaigns targeting Myanmar government entities <a href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/2022/05/mustang-panda-targets-europe.html" target="_blank">using custom lures</a> and compromising the website of the office of <a href="https://twitter.com/ESETresearch/status/1400165767488970764" target="_blank">Myanmar</a>’s president.</p>
<p>The TTPs associated with the campaign covered in this report align with those of Mustang Panda. We observed a typical attack chain employed by the group, where attackers used a benign executable to side-load a malicious DLL loader, which then decrypts and loads the PlugX implant. We have also confirmed the C2 infrastructure associated with this campaign has been used to target entities in Myanmar, including a government VPN portal, from early March onwards.</p>
