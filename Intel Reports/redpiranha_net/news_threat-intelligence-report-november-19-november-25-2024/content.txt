<h3><u><strong><span style="color:#ffffff;">SafePay Ransomware Group</span></strong></u></h3>

<p><span style="color:#ffffff;">Based on the latest Analysis on 21-Nov-2024, In the latest analysis conducted by Red Piranha, our team has identified a concerning new ransomware variant named SafePay that has emerged in the threat landscape. First observed in October 2024, this previously unreported ransomware strain has been identified in multiple incidents across different business sectors and geographical locations.</span></p>

<p><span style="color:#ffffff;">Red Piranha analysts documented two distinct SafePay ransomware deployment incidents. These attacks targeted organisations in different business verticals and geographical locations, indicating a potentially widespread campaign. The key identifying characteristics of these attacks include:</span></p>

<ul>
	<li><span style="color:#ffffff;">Encrypted files appended with&nbsp;.safepay&nbsp;extension</span></li>
	<li><span style="color:#ffffff;">Ransom note filename:&nbsp;readme_safepay.txt</span></li>
	<li><span style="color:#ffffff;">No prior public reporting or documentation of this variant</span></li>
</ul>

<p><br>
<span style="color:#ffffff;"><u><strong>Detailed TTPs</strong></u></span></p>

<p><strong><span style="color:#ffffff;">Initial Access and Defence Evasion:</span></strong></p>

<ul>
	<li><span style="color:#ffffff;">Primary access vector: Remote Desktop Protocol (RDP)</span></li>
	<li><span style="color:#ffffff;">Initial attempt to execute&nbsp;</span><a href="https://github.com/darkoperator/Veil-PowerView/blob/master/PowerView/functions/Invoke-ShareFinder.ps1"><span style="color:#ffffff;">Sparpfinder.ps1</span></a><span style="color:#ffffff;">&nbsp;was detected and blocked by Windows Defender</span></li>
	<li><span style="color:#ffffff;">Threat actors successfully disabled Windows Defender using a series of LOLBin commands&nbsp;</span></li>
</ul>

<blockquote>
<div><span style="color:#ffffff;">"C:\Windows\system32\SystemSettingsAdminFlows.exe" Defender DisableEnhancedNotifications 1</span></div>

<div><span style="color:#ffffff;">"C:\Windows\system32\SystemSettingsAdminFlows.exe" Defender SubmitSamplesConsent 0</span></div>

<div><span style="color:#ffffff;">"C:\Windows\system32\SystemSettingsAdminFlows.exe" Defender SpynetReporting 0</span></div>

<div><span style="color:#ffffff;">"C:\Windows\system32\SystemSettingsAdminFlows.exe" Defender RTP 1</span></div>
</blockquote>

<ul>
	<li><span style="color:#ffffff;">Successfully executed ShareFinder.ps1 after disabling security controls</span></li>
</ul>

<p><br>
<span style="color:#ffffff;"><strong>Privilege Escalation Analysis</strong></span></p>

<p><span style="color:#ffffff;">The adversary likely employed a widely recognised&nbsp;UAC Bypass Privilege Escalation technique, frequently used by ransomware groups such as&nbsp;LockBit&nbsp;and&nbsp;BlackCat/ALPHV. This method involves creating an elevated process via a specified&nbsp;COM Object, enabling the execution of malicious commands or binaries.</span></p>

<p><span style="color:#ffffff;">In this instance, the parent process observed was DllHost.exe, with the&nbsp;CLSID&nbsp;of the COM Object (CMSTPLUA) visible in the command line. Although such activity may occasionally occur for legitimate reasons, it is uncommon&mdash;particularly in scenarios involving:</span></p>

<ul>
	<li><strong><span style="color:#ffffff;">Unsigned binaries.</span></strong></li>
	<li><strong><span style="color:#ffffff;">System binaries&nbsp;</span></strong><span style="color:#ffffff;">leveraged for proxy execution.</span></li>
	<li><strong><span style="color:#ffffff;">Scripting interpreters&nbsp;</span></strong><span style="color:#ffffff;">(e.g., CMD or PowerShell) as child processes.</span></li>
</ul>

<p><br>
<span style="color:#ffffff;">This behaviour typically warrants further scrutiny to identify potential misuse or privilege escalation attempts.</span></p>

<p><span style="color:#ffffff;">Ransomware note:&nbsp;</span><a href="https://gist.github.com/gleeda/988da614e6740fac66dbaa6d92121302" target="_blank"><span style="color:#e74c3c;">Safeplay Message!</span></a></p>

<p><span style="color:#e74c3c;"><img alt="GitHubGist" data-entity-type="" data-entity-uuid="" src="/sites/default/files/2024-11/GitHubGist.png" style="width: 100%;" data-pagespeed-url-hash="2117233075" onload="pagespeed.CriticalImages.checkImageForCriticality(this);"></span></p>

<p><strong><span style="color:#ffffff;">Process Termination</span></strong></p>

<p><span style="color:#ffffff;">The malware employs&nbsp;ZwTerminateProcess&nbsp;to terminate specific processes, targeting applications and services critical for system operations, databases, and productivity tools. The processes targeted for termination include:</span></p>

<ul>
	<li><span style="color:#ffffff;"><strong>Database &amp; Sync Services:</strong> sql, oracle, ocssd, dbsnmp, synctime, agntsvc, isqlplussvc.</span></li>
	<li><span style="color:#ffffff;"><strong>Backup &amp; Security Services:</strong> xfssvccon, mydesktopservice, ocautoupds, encsvc.</span></li>
	<li><span style="color:#ffffff;"><strong>Web &amp; Email Clients:</strong> firefox, tbirdconfig, thunderbird, thebat.</span></li>
	<li><span style="color:#ffffff;"><strong>Office Suite &amp; Productivity Tools:</strong> excel, infopath, msaccess, mspub, onenote, outlook, powerpnt, visio, winword.</span></li>
	<li><span style="color:#ffffff;"><strong>Miscellaneous Applications:</strong> steam, notepad, wordpad, wuauclt, onedrive, sqlmangr.</span></li>
</ul>

<p><br>
<span style="color:#ffffff;"><strong>Service Termination</strong></span></p>

<p><span style="color:#ffffff;">The ransomware leverages&nbsp;ControlService&nbsp;to stop services, focusing on those related to backups, antivirus software, and system operations, aiming to disable protective mechanisms and hinder data recovery efforts. Targeted services include:</span></p>

<ul>
	<li><span style="color:#ffffff;"><strong>System &amp; Backup Services:</strong> vss, sqlsvc, memtas, backup, GxVss, GxBlr, GxFWD, GxCVD, GxCIMgr.</span></li>
	<li><span style="color:#ffffff;"><strong>Email &amp; Exchange Services:</strong> mepocs, msexchange.</span></li>
	<li><span style="color:#ffffff;"><strong>Antivirus &amp; Security Tools</strong>: Sophos, Veeam.</span></li>
</ul>

<p><br>
<span style="color:#ffffff;">This dual strategy of terminating both processes and services ensures the ransomware can maximise its impact while reducing resistance from the system's defences.</span></p>

<p><strong><span style="color:#ffffff;">Data Exfiltration Phase:</span></strong></p>

<ul>
	<li><span style="color:#ffffff;">Approximately 40 minutes after initial access, threat actors began data archiving operations</span></li>
	<li><span style="color:#ffffff;">Utilised WinRAR.exe with specific command parameters for data collection:</span></li>
</ul>

<blockquote>
<div><span style="color:#ffffff;">WinRAR.exe&nbsp;&nbsp;a -v5g -ed -r -tn1000d -m0 -mt5 -x*.rar -x*.JPEG -x*.RAW -x*.PSD -x*.TIFF -x*.BMP -x*.GIF -x*.JPG -x*.MOV -x*.pst -x*.FIT -x*.FIL -x*.mp4 -x*.avi -x*.mov -x*.mdb -x*.iso -x*.exe -x*.dll -x*.bak -x*.msg -x*.png -x*.zip -x*.ai -x*.7z -x*.DPM -x*.log -x*.dxf -x*.insp -x*.upd -x*.db -x*.dwg -x*.nc1 -x*.metadata -x*.dg -x*.inp -x*.dat -x*.TIFF -x*.tiger -x*.pcp -x*.rvt -x*.rws -x*.nwc -x*.tif -x*.frx -x*.dyf -x*.rcs -x*.diff C:\[redacted].rar&nbsp;\\[redacted]\C$\Users\</span></div>
</blockquote>

Following the installation, both&nbsp;filezilla.exe&nbsp;and&nbsp;fzsftp.exe&nbsp;were&nbsp;executed. However, the application was promptly uninstalled afterwards.</span></p>

<p><span style="color:#ffffff;">Most of the strings throughout the binary are obfuscated with a simple three-step XOR loop consisting of a random single-byte key, the index of the character, and the first byte of&nbsp;kernel32.dll&nbsp;(&lsquo;M&rsquo;).</span></p>

<p><span style="color:#ffffff;"><u><strong>Mitigation:</strong></u></span></p>

<ol>
	<li><span style="color:#ffffff;">Restrict Remote Desktop Protocol (RDP) Access: Use VPNs, enable multi-factor authentication (MFA), and enforce strong password policies</span></li>
	<li><span style="color:#ffffff;">Monitor PowerShell and LOLBins Usage.</span></li>
	<li><span style="color:#ffffff;"><strong>Monitor UAC Bypass Attempts:</strong> Audit and alert on the creation of processes with elevated privileges, especially those leveraging DllHost.exe or COM Objects.</span></li>
	<li><span style="color:#ffffff;">Ensure the least privileged access is enforced for all users and processes.</span><br>
	&nbsp;</li>
</ol>

<p><u><strong><span style="color:#ffffff;">Ransomware Victims Worldwide</span></strong></u></p>

<p><span style="color:#ffffff;">A recent ransomware analysis reveals a striking 48.78% of global incidents targeted the United States, making it the hardest-hit country by far. Trailing behind, the United Kingdom experienced 5.69% of attacks, while Canada reported 4.88%. Australia, France, and India each faced 4.07%, reflecting notable vulnerabilities in these regions.</span></p>

<p><span style="color:#ffffff;">Germany and Italy were also significantly impacted, contributing 3.25% each, followed by Brazil at 2.44%. Other countries like the Netherlands, Spain, and Argentina recorded smaller shares, each at 1.63%.</span></p>

<p><span style="color:#ffffff;">Meanwhile, a diverse group of nations, including Estonia, Belgium, Oman, China, Egypt, Peru, and Mexico, saw limited impacts, each representing just 0.81% of the total incidents.</span></p>

<p><span style="color:#ffffff;">This analysis highlights the widespread nature of ransomware attacks, with North America and Europe facing the brunt of these persistent threats. These numbers underscore the urgent need for robust cybersecurity measures worldwide.</span></p>

<p><u><strong><span style="color:#ffffff;">Ransomware Victims Industry-wise</span></strong></u></p>

<p><span style="color:#ffffff;">Ransomware attacks continue to disrupt industries across the board, with the Retail sector emerging as the hardest hit, making up a significant 13.01% of all incidents. Education, Construction, and Business Services are also high on the radar, each seeing 8.94% of attacks, underlining their growing vulnerability.</span></p>

<p><span style="color:#ffffff;">The Healthcare and Finance sectors are not far behind, each accounting for 6.5%, while Manufacturing faces its own challenges at 4.88%. Even industries like Software and Energy are feeling the impact, with each recording 3.25% of incidents.</span></p>

<p><span style="color:#ffffff;">Mid-level impacts were observed in areas like Food &amp; Beverage, Consumer Services, and Architecture, all reporting 2.44% of attacks. Meanwhile, sectors like Automotive Services, Hospitality, and Law Firms experienced smaller yet notable shares of 1.63%.</span></p>

<p><span style="color:#ffffff;">A wide range of industries, including Fitness, Airlines, Oil, Gambling, and Internet Service Providers, were less affected, each seeing 0.81% of the total incidents.</span></p>

<p><span style="color:#ffffff;">These numbers paint a clear picture: ransomware isn&rsquo;t just targeting one or two industries. It&rsquo;s spreading across the spectrum, hitting both critical and smaller sectors. This makes it more important than ever for organisations in every field to bolster their cybersecurity strategies and protect against these growing threats.</span></p>
