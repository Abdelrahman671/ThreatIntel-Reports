          <span class="f6 mt0" dir="ltr"><a href="https://citizenlab.ca/category/research/" class="breadcrumbs link"><a href="https://citizenlab.ca/category/research/" class="breadcrumbs link">Research</a><span class="fa-solid fa-chevron-right mh2" aria-hidden="true"></span></a><a href="https://citizenlab.ca/category/research/targeted-threats/" class="breadcrumbs link">Targeted Threats</a></span>
            <h1 itemprop="headline" rel="bookmark" class="ma0 mt5 lh-title">
            	<!-- Title -->
	<span class="db f2 f1-ns black lh-solid">Keep Calm and (Don’t) Enable Macros</span>
	<!-- Subtitle -->
	<span class="db f4 f2-ns mid-gray mt2 lh-title oswald-regular"> A New Threat Actor Targets UAE Dissidents</span>
	            </h1>
            <div dir="ltr" class="mt2">
              
            <div class="f5 mr4 b dark-gray dib">By 
            <a href="https://citizenlab.ca/author/bmarczak/" title="Posts by Bill Marczak" class="author url fn" rel="author">Bill Marczak</a> and <a href="https://citizenlab.ca/author/jsrailton/" title="Posts by John Scott-Railton" class="author url fn" rel="author">John Scott-Railton</a></div>

          <!-- Display other versions of the post -->
                      </div>

                <section itemprop="articleBody" class="article-body mb4 mt4 pt2 bt b--light-gray">
                  <!DOCTYPE html PUBLIC "-//W3C//DTD HTML 4.0 Transitional//EN" "http://www.w3.org/TR/REC-html40/loose.dtd">
<html><body><h2 class=" lh-solid mb3"><b style="">1. Executive Summary</b></h2>
<p class="mt0"><span style="font-weight: 400;">This report describes a campaign of targeted spyware attacks carried out by a sophisticated operator, which we call </span><b>Stealth Falcon</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">. &nbsp;The attacks have been conducted from 2012 until the present, against Emirati journalists, activists, and dissidents. &nbsp;We discovered this campaign when an individual purporting to be from an apparently fictitious organization called &ldquo;The Right to Fight&rdquo; contacted Rori Donaghy. &nbsp;Donaghy, a UK-based journalist and founder of the Emirates Center for Human Rights, received a spyware-laden email in November 2015, purporting to offer him a position on a human rights panel. &nbsp;Donaghy has written critically of the United Arab Emirates (UAE) government in the past,<sup><a href="#1" class="pointer">1</a></sup></span><span style="font-weight: 400;"> and had recently published a series of articles based on leaked emails involving members of the UAE government.<sup><a href="#2" class="pointer">2</a></sup></span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Circumstantial evidence suggests a link between Stealth Falcon and the UAE government. &nbsp;We traced digital artifacts used in this campaign to links sent from an activist&rsquo;s Twitter account in December 2012, a period when it appears to have been under government control. &nbsp;We also identified other bait content employed by this threat actor. &nbsp;We found 31 public tweets sent by Stealth Falcon, 30 of which were directly targeted at one of 27 victims. &nbsp;Of the 27 targets, 24 were obviously linked to the UAE, based on their profile information (e.g., photos, &ldquo;UAE&rdquo; in account name, location), and at least six targets appeared to be operated by people who were arrested, sought for arrest, or convicted in absentia by the UAE government, in relation to their Twitter activity.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">The attack on Donaghy &mdash; and the Twitter attacks &mdash; involved a malicious URL shortening site. &nbsp;When a user clicks on a URL shortened by Stealth Falcon operators, the site profiles the software on a user&rsquo;s computer, perhaps for future exploitation, before redirecting the user to a benign website containing bait content. &nbsp;We queried the URL shortener with every possible short URL, and identified 402 instances of bait content which we believe were sent by Stealth Falcon, 73% of which obviously referenced UAE issues. &nbsp;Of these URLs, only the one sent to Donaghy definitively contained spyware. &nbsp;However, we were able to trace the spyware Donaghy received to a network of 67 active command and control (C2) servers, suggesting broader use of the spyware, perhaps by the same or other operators.</span></p>

<h2 class=" lh-solid mb3"><b style="">2. Background</b></h2>
<p class="mt0"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Rori Donaghy<sup><a href="#3" class="pointer">3</a></sup></span><span style="font-weight: 400;"> is a London-based journalist who currently works for UK news organization </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">Middle East Eye</span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;">, a website that covers news in the Middle East.<sup><a href="#4" class="pointer">4</a></sup></span><span style="font-weight: 400;"> &nbsp;</span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">Middle East Eye</span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;"> has recently published a series of articles about UAE foreign policy, based on leaked emails involving members of the UAE government.</span><span style="font-weight: 400;"> &nbsp;Previously, Donaghy led the Emirates Center for Human Rights,<sup><a href="#5" class="pointer">5</a></sup></span><span style="font-weight: 400;"> an organization he founded to </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">&ldquo;promote the defence of human rights in the United Arab Emirates &hellip; through building strong relationships with the media, parliaments and other relevant organisations outside the UAE</span></i><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">&rdquo;.</span></i><sup><a href="#6" class="pointer">6</a></sup></p>
<h3 class=" lh-solid mb3"><b style="">2.1. Political and Human Rights Situation in the UAE</b></h3>
<p class="mt0"><span style="font-weight: 400;">In its most recent (2015) Freedom in the World ranking, Freedom House classified the UAE as </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">&ldquo;not free,&rdquo;</span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;"> and noted that the UAE continues to </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">&ldquo;suppress dissent&rdquo;</span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;">.<sup><a href="#7" class="pointer">7</a></sup></span><span style="font-weight: 400;"> &nbsp;Human Rights Watch stated in its most recent (2016) country report, that the UAE has </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">&ldquo;continued &hellip; to arbitrarily detain and in some cases forcibly disappear individuals who criticized the authorities&rdquo;.</span></i><sup><a href="#8" class="pointer">8</a></sup><span style="font-weight: 400;"> &nbsp;Amnesty International says that UAE courts have </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">&ldquo;accepted evidence allegedly obtained through torture&rdquo;.</span></i><sup><a href="#9" class="pointer">9</a></sup></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Specifically in the online realm, there is evidence that the UAE government has previously conducted malware attacks against civil society. <p class="mt0"><span style="font-weight: 400;">The document attempts to execute code on the recipient&rsquo;s computer, using a macro. &nbsp;The macro passes a Base64-encoded command to Windows PowerShell, which gathers system information via Windows Management Instrumentation (WMI), and attempts to determine the installed version of .NET by querying the registry (full script available in </span><b><a href="https://citizenlab.ca/2016/05/stealth-falcon-appendices/" class="pointer">Appendix A: Stage One PowerShell Command</a></b><span style="font-weight: 400;">).</span></p>
<h4 class=" lh-solid mb3"><b style="">3.3.2 Communication &amp; Obtaining a Shell</b></h4>
<p class="mt0"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Gathered information is returned to </span><b>http://adhostingcache.com/ehhe/eh4g4/adcache.txt</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">, and the server&rsquo;s response is executed as a PowerShell command. &nbsp;At the time, </span><b>adhostingcache.com</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> resolved to </span><b>95.215.44.37</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">. &nbsp;The domain was apparently deleted on November 30th 2015 (Donaghy received the malicious Word Document on November 24th 2015). &nbsp;A new domain, </span><b>adhostingcaches.com</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">, was registered on December 3rd, which points to the same IP address. &nbsp;The deletion of </span><b>adhostingcache.com</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> may reflect operator suspicion that the file received by Donaghy had been sent to security researchers.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">The server response is a PowerShell command that decodes and materializes an invocation of a Base64-encoded PowerShell command to disk as </span><b>IEWebCache.vbs</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">, and creates a scheduled task entitled </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">&ldquo;IE Web Cache&rdquo;</span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;"> that executes the file hourly (full script available in </span><b><a href="https://citizenlab.ca/2016/05/stealth-falcon-appendices/" class="pointer">Appendix B: Stage Two PowerShell Command</a></b><span style="font-weight: 400;">).</span></p>
<p><b>IEWebCache.vbs</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> runs a Base64-encoded PowerShell command, which periodically POSTs a unique identifier to </span><b>https://incapsulawebcache.com/cache/cache.nfo</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> (via HTTPS without verifying the server certificate, and with a hardcoded user-agent header matching Internet Explorer 10.6). &nbsp;The script executes server responses as PowerShell commands, responding back to the server with the exit status of, output of, or any exceptions generated by the commands. &nbsp;</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">This gives the operator control over the victim&rsquo;s computer, and allows the operator to install additional spyware or perform other activities. &nbsp;All commands and responses are encrypted using RC4 with a hardcoded key, and the encrypted message is prefixed with a hardcoded value.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Despite some similarities in functionality to the Empire backdoor,<sup><a href="#18" class="pointer">18</a></sup></span><span style="font-weight: 400;"> we were unable to identify any shared code, and we suspect that the backdoor is custom-made.</span></p>
<h3 class=" lh-solid mb3"><b style="">3.4. Technical Analysis: aax.me Browser Profiling</b></h3>

<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">In November 2015, when we accessed the link in the second email that Donaghy received, </span><b>http://aax.me/a6faa</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">, we found that it redirected directly to </span><b>https://cloud.openmailbox.org/index.php/s/ujDNWMmg8pdG3AL/authenticate</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> via an HTTP 302 redirect. &nbsp;When we accessed the link in the first email that Donaghy received, </span><b>http://aax.me/d0dde</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">, the server responded with the following page:</span></p>

<li style="font-weight: 400;" class="mt2"><span style="font-weight: 400;">For Internet Explorer, it attempts to create several instances of </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">ActiveXObject</span><span style="font-weight: 400;"> to get the versions of Flash, Shockwave, Java, RealPlayer, Windows Media Player, and Microsoft Office (classified as either </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">2003</span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;">, </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">2007</span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;">, or </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">2010</span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">).</span></span></li>
<li style="font-weight: 400;" class="mt2"><span style="font-weight: 400;">For non-Internet Explorer browsers, it attempts to get a list of enabled plugins from </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">navigator.mimeTypes</span><span style="font-weight: 400;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">.</span></span></li>
<li style="font-weight: 400;" class="mt2"><span style="font-weight: 400;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">For all browsers, it captures the user agent, whether cookies are enabled, the OS, the size of the browser window, and the timezone. &nbsp;It classifies browsers into different versions, denoted by letters, based on the existence and behavior of certain JavaScript methods.</span></span></li>
<li style="font-weight: 400;" class="mt2"><span style="font-weight: 400;">The script attempts to exploit an information leak in older versions of Tor Browser. &nbsp;We explore the technique used in </span><b>Section 3.5</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">.</span></span></li>
<li style="font-weight: 400;" class="mt2"><span style="font-weight: 400;">For Windows browsers (except Opera, and versions of Internet Explorer before IE9), it sends a series of XMLHttpRequests to 127.0.0.1, which we believe are designed to deduce if the computer is running any one of several specific antivirus programs. &nbsp;The code for this appears to be borrowed from the </span><b>JS-Recon</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> port scanning tool.<sup><a href="#21" class="pointer">21</a></sup></span><span style="font-weight: 400;"> &nbsp;The creator of JS-Recon presented the tool at BlackHat Abu Dhabi in 2010.<sup><a href="#22" class="pointer">22</a></sup></span><span style="font-weight: 400;"> &nbsp;We explore such techniques in more detail in </span><b>Section 3.6</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">.</span></li>
</ul>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">We were unfamiliar with the website </span><b>aax.me</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">, so we investigated it further. &nbsp;We found that the main page of </span><b>aax.me</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> purported to be a public URL shortening service, powered by YOURLS,<sup><a href="#23" class="pointer">23</a></sup></span><span style="font-weight: 400;"> an open source PHP framework allowing anyone to set up their own URL shortening service. &nbsp;We are unable to ascertain whether the site actually uses any YOURLS code. &nbsp;We also noted that the homepage contains a typo (&ldquo;Shortend [sic] URL&rdquo;).</span></p>

<h3 class=" lh-solid mb3"><b style="">3.5. Technical Analysis: aax.me Tor Deanonymization Attempt</b></h3>
<p class="mt0"><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">The aax.me site appears to attempt to deanonymize users of Tor Browser. &nbsp;While the technique the operators used was out-of-date at the time we observed the attack, the attempted Tor deanonymization speaks to their motivations and potential targets.</span></i></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">The script first detects Tor Browsers by checking whether </span><b>navigator.buildID</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> is set to zero (all testing was conducted on English, Windows builds of Tor Browser). &nbsp;Versions of Tor Browser &nbsp;before 2.3.25-12 (released on 13 August 2013) had their buildID set to zero. &nbsp;This behavior was originally introduced in TorButton,<sup><a href="#25" class="pointer">25</a></sup></span><span style="font-weight: 400;"> in support of the goal of making Tor users appear homogenous.<sup><a href="#26" class="pointer">26</a></sup></span><span style="font-weight: 400;"> &nbsp;Current Tor Browser versions have navigator.buildID set to a different distinctive value, 20000101000000.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">When the script detects a Tor Browser, it attempts to deduce the version of Tor Browser by checking for the existence and behavior of certain JavaScript methods. &nbsp;Once a browser is determined to be </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">older </span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;">than a certain version of Tor Browser, the script exploits a now-fixed bug to get the disk path of the browser installation.<sup><a href="#27" class="pointer">27</a></sup></span><span style="font-weight: 400;"> &nbsp;The disk path may contain the target&rsquo;s username, which may include the target&rsquo;s real name.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">The bug in Tor Browser was first disclosed at Defcon 17, which took place in August 2009.<sup><a href="#28" class="pointer">28</a></sup></span><span style="font-weight: 400;"> &nbsp;The bug was first fixed on on 25 May 2012 in Tor Browser release 2.2.35-13.<sup><a href="#29" class="pointer">29</a></sup></span><span style="font-weight: 400;"> &nbsp;The bug was, however, later reintroduced into Tor Browser on 18 December 2013 with the release of Tor Browser 3.5, and subsequently fixed again in Tor Browser 3.6 on 29 April 2014.<sup><a href="#30" class="pointer">30</a></sup></span><span style="font-weight: 400;"> &nbsp;However, unfortunately for the operators, they failed to update their profiling script to reflect Tor Browser&rsquo;s </span><b>navigator.buildID</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> change (before the bug was reintroduced). &nbsp;Thus, the profiling script did not detect Tor Browsers with the reintroduced bug as Tor Browsers, so it did not try to exploit them. &nbsp;Even if it had been updated to reflect the navigator.buildID change, the version check in the Tor Browser exploitation code would also have to be updated to select the versions with the reintroduced bug for exploitation.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">The version of Tor Browser (as determined by JavaScript checks) is submitted back to the server, along with the value of </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">navigator.oscpu</span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;"> <span style="text-decoration: line-through;">(which reveals the version of the OS on which Tor Browser is running &mdash; e.g., the latest version of Tor Browser on OSX El Capitan reveals: </span></span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">&ldquo;Intel Mac OS X 10.11&rdquo;</span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;">) which is set to &ldquo;Windows NT 6.1&rdquo; in the latest Tor browser, </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">navigator.vendor</span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;"> (which appears blank in the latest Tor Browser), and any data gathered about the installation path.</span></p>
<h3 class=" lh-solid mb3"><b style="">3.6. Technical Analysis: aax.me Antivirus Profiling</b></h3>
<p class="mt0"><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">Interestingly, aax.me also attempts to determine the presence of various antivirus products on a target&rsquo;s machine.</span></i></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">We expand on the probing of antivirus programs which we observed on </span><b>aax.me</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">, as we were unfamiliar with this technique. &nbsp;The technique appears to work on any modern version of Windows, with the latest versions of Chrome, Firefox, and IE/Edge (though, the profiling script excludes IE versions less than IE9 from the profiling, using the </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">vertical tab test</span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;">).<sup><a href="#31" class="pointer">31</a></sup></span><span style="font-weight: 400;"> &nbsp;Specifically, the script conducts GET XMLHttpRequests (one at a time) to 127.0.0.1/ on the following ports: 12993, 44080, 24961, 1110, 6646, 6999, 30606. &nbsp;The script stops conducting these requests if it finds one request whose </span><b>readyState</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> is set to 4 less than 20ms after the request was initiated (200ms for port 6646), and submits the number of this port to the server.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">The latest versions of Internet Explorer/Edge, Chrome, and Firefox (except Tor Browser) will all perform these XMLHttpRequests to 127.0.0.1 on behalf of any site. &nbsp;Of course, the </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">result</span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;"> of such a request will most likely not be available to the script, due to the same-origin policy, and likely absence of a CORS<sup><a href="#32" class="pointer">32</a></sup></span><span style="font-weight: 400;"> header in the response. &nbsp;Indeed, the script does not attempt to read the results of its requests. &nbsp;Rather, it leverages the fact that the web browser makes the </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">status</span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;"> of the request sent available, via the </span><b>readyState</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> parameter of an XMLHttpRequest instance (</span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">1</span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;"> approximately represents TCP SYN sent, and </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">4</span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;"> represents HTTP response received or TCP connection terminated). &nbsp;For a closed port, Windows will issue an RST/ACK for each SYN sent. &nbsp;However, it appears that Windows&rsquo; TCP stack will not consider an outgoing connection it is initiating to be terminated until it has sent 3 SYNs, and received three corresponding RST/ACKs (or timeouts).</span></p>
<figure class="center mw-100 ba b--light-gray" style="width:690px;"><div class="tc pa2 bg-white flex justify-center"><a href="https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/webpc-passthru.php?src=https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2016/05/image07.png&amp;nocache=1" class="pointer"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="wp-image-27540 aligncenter" src="https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/webpc-passthru.php?src=https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2016/05/image07-1024x113.png&amp;nocache=1" alt="image07" width="690" height="76" title="Keep Calm and (Don’t) Enable Macros: A New Threat Actor Targets UAE Dissidents 7" srcset="https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/webpc-passthru.php?src=https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2016/05/image07-1024x113.png&amp;nocache=1 1024w, https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/webpc-passthru.php?src=https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2016/05/image07-300x33.png&amp;nocache=1 300w, https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/webpc-passthru.php?src=https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2016/05/image07-768x84.png&amp;nocache=1 768w, https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/webpc-passthru.php?src=https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2016/05/image07-180x20.png&amp;nocache=1 180w, https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/webpc-passthru.php?src=https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2016/05/image07-605x66.png&amp;nocache=1 605w, https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/webpc-passthru.php?src=https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2016/05/image07-297x33.png&amp;nocache=1 297w, https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/webpc-passthru.php?src=https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2016/05/image07.png&amp;nocache=1 1984w" sizes="(max-width: 690px) 100vw, 690px" /></a></div><figcaption class="mt1 f5 pa2 bg-light-gray"><p style="text-align: center;"><strong><em>Figure 7: Three RST/ACKs required until Windows considers outgoing TCP connection terminated</em></strong></p></figcaption></figure>

<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">When testing with a TCP connection from Windows to a remote host, we can clearly see that Windows transmits the second SYN ~500ms after the first RST/ACK, and the third SYN ~500ms after the second RST/ACK.</span></p>

<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Thus, the </span><b>readyState</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> value for a request to a closed port on 127.0.0.1 will not be set equal to 4 until approximately 1000ms after the request is issued. &nbsp;In summary, one can use this technique to distinguish between a closed port (</span><b>readyState</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> set to 4 at around 1000ms), an open port (</span><b>readyState</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> set to 4 before 1000ms), and a filtered port (</span><b>readyState</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> set to 4 long after 1000ms).</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">This script was apparently designed to detect the presence of certain components of Avast, Avira, ESET, Kaspersky, and Trend Micro antivirus products. &nbsp;We were not able to determine which program the probing of port 24961 was designed to detect. &nbsp;We verified that the latest version of Avast can be detected by this script, as it opens TCP port 12993, which is associated with its </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">Mail Shield</span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;"> component for scanning email traffic; port 6999 is opened by Trend Micro&rsquo;s </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">tmproxy</span></i><sup><a href="#33" class="pointer">33</a></sup><span style="font-weight: 400;"> which scans web and email traffic; port 1110 is used by Kaspersky<sup><a href="#34" class="pointer">34</a></sup></span><span style="font-weight: 400;"> to scan web and email traffic; it appears that Avira&rsquo;s </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">Web Protection</span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;"> component for scanning web traffic used to open port 44080,<sup><a href="#35" class="pointer">35</a></sup></span><span style="font-weight: 400;"> though we observed it opening 44081 instead; port 30606 appears to have been used by ESET to scan web and email traffic,<sup><a href="#36" class="pointer">36</a></sup></span><span style="font-weight: 400;"> but we did not observe this port open while testing the latest version of ESET; port 6646 may be used by McAfee, though we did not test this.<sup><a href="#37" class="pointer">37</a></sup></span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">The code for the port scanning appears to be adapted from the </span><b>JS-Recon</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> port scanning tool.<sup><a href="#38" class="pointer">38</a></sup></span><span style="font-weight: 400;"> &nbsp;JS-Recon is a generic tool that enumerates all open ports on 127.0.0.1 in a range; it does not specifically target anti-virus programs. &nbsp;The </span><b>scan_xhr</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> and </span><b>check_ps_xhr</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> functions in the </span><b>aax.me</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> profiling script are similar to the </span><b>scan_ports_xhr</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> and </span><b>check_ps_xhr</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> functions in JS-Recon. &nbsp;The creator of JS-Recon seems to have first presented the tool at BlackHat Abu Dhabi in 2010.<sup><a href="#39" class="pointer">39</a></sup></span></p>
<figure class="center mw-100 ba b--light-gray" style="width:690px;"><div class="tc pa2 bg-white flex justify-center"><a href="https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/webpc-passthru.php?src=https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2016/05/image09.png&amp;nocache=1" class="pointer"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="wp-image-27542 aligncenter" src="https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/webpc-passthru.php?src=https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2016/05/image09.png&amp;nocache=1" alt="image09" width="690" height="134" title="Keep Calm and (Don’t) Enable Macros: A New Threat Actor Targets UAE Dissidents 9" srcset="https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/webpc-passthru.php?src=https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2016/05/image09.png&amp;nocache=1 717w, https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/webpc-passthru.php?src=https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2016/05/image09-300x58.png&amp;nocache=1 300w, https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/webpc-passthru.php?src=https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2016/05/image09-180x35.png&amp;nocache=1 180w, https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/webpc-passthru.php?src=https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2016/05/image09-605x117.png&amp;nocache=1 605w, https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/webpc-passthru.php?src=https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2016/05/image09-297x58.png&amp;nocache=1 297w" sizes="(max-width: 690px) 100vw, 690px" /></a></div><figcaption class="mt1 f5 pa2 bg-light-gray"><p style="text-align: center;"><em><b>Figure 9: Image from the author of JS-Recon showing how long WebSocket and XMLHttpRequest (&ldquo;COR&rdquo;) connections remain in their initial readyState on Windows.</b></em><sup><a href="#40" class="pointer">40</a></sup></p></figcaption></figure>

<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Note that this technique can be generalized to any remote content timing side channel (e.g, the </span><b>onerror</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> event for an </span><b>Image</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">). &nbsp;Additionally, one can identify the presence of an open port on 127.0.0.1 that speaks HTTP without using timing information, and thus without the Windows TCP behavior assumption (e.g., by handling the </span><b>onerror</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> and </span><b>oncomplete</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> events of certain types of</span><b> link</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> elements). </span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">We are unsure whether the purpose of the antivirus profiling is to identify potentially exploitable antivirus software running on a target&rsquo;s computer, or for evasion of antivirus products. &nbsp;In December 2015, Google Security discovered a critical vulnerability in Avast&rsquo;s antivirus product, which involved a webpage sending HTTP requests to a port that Avast opens on 127.0.0.1. &nbsp;Google Security demonstrated that the vulnerability allowed exfiltration of arbitrary files from a victim&rsquo;s disk.<sup><a href="#41" class="pointer">41</a></sup></span><span style="font-weight: 400;"> &nbsp;In January 2016, Google Security discovered a critical vulnerability in Trend Micro&rsquo;s antivirus product, which similarly involved a web page sending HTTP requests to a port that Trend Micro opens on 127.0.0.1. &nbsp;Google Security demonstrated that the vulnerability allowed arbitrary command execution.</span></p>

<h2 class=" lh-solid mb3"><b style="">5. Stealth Falcon&rsquo;s Widespread Targeting of UAE Figures</b></h2>
<p class="mt0"><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">This section describes how we identified additional Stealth Falcon victims and bait content, and traced Stealth Falcon&rsquo;s spyware to additional C2 servers.</span></i></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Given Stealh Falcon&rsquo;s use of public Twitter mentions to contact individuals, we searched Google and Twitter for instances of </span><b>aax.me</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> links. &nbsp;The links we found indicated that we could easily probe </span><b>aax.me</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> to get a comprehensive list of all currently active short URLs, and their corresponding long URLs. &nbsp;Our findings point to a UAE-focused operator, whose bait content and targets are linked to the Emirates. &nbsp;Furthermore, we were able to connect this attack to case from December 2012, where an anonymous UAE activist contacted us and claimed to have received a suspicious link from a Twitter account that was purportedly under government control.</span></p>

<h3 class=" lh-solid mb3"><b style="">5.2. Ennumerating aax.me for Bait Content</b></h3>
<p class="mt0"><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">This section describes how we probed every conceivable short URL on aax.me, and found 402 pieces of bait content that we believe were sent by Stealth Falcon.</span></i></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">All of the public </span><b>aax.me</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> links we found, as well as the links sent to Donaghy, matched the regular expression </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">/aax\.me\/[0-9a-f]{5}/</span><span style="font-weight: 400;">. &nbsp;Assuming all links shortened via </span><b>aax.me</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> match this regular expression, there are only 16</span><span style="font-weight: 400;">5</span><span style="font-weight: 400;"> (1,048,576) possible short URLs. &nbsp;We sent a request to </span><b>aax.me</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> for each possible URL, and observed the returned page or redirect. &nbsp;We found 57 URLs that exhibited the </span><b>redirect.php</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> profiling behavior, and 524 URLs that returned an HTTP 302 redirect to an expanded URL. &nbsp;The other 1,047,995 </span><b>aax.me</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> links returned a HTTP 302 redirect to the </span><b>aax.me</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> homepage; we assume these short URLs were unassigned to an expanded URL, as of the time of our scan.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">We coded the long URLs where the URLs were still active, or where we could find an archived copy of, or some information about, the URL. &nbsp;We were able to code 535 URLs, and failed to code 46 URLs as the corresponding websites were down, and we could not find reliable information about what content the URLs contained. &nbsp;See </span><b><a href="https://citizenlab.ca/2016/05/stealth-falcon-appendices/" class="pointer">Appendix E: Results of aax.me Scan</a></b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> for details. &nbsp;We coded 133 URLs as &ldquo;advertisement&rdquo; (25% of all coded URLs), as they appeared to represent an advertisement for a product. &nbsp;The vast majority of these advertisements seemed to be products typically marketed via spam (e.g., &ldquo;dietary supplement&rdquo; or &ldquo;green coffee&rdquo;). &nbsp;We suspect that these links may have been shortened by spammers, as the </span><b>aax.me</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> URL shortening page is pubilcly accessible and indexed by Google, and YOURLS advises that publicly accessible URL shorteners will receive spam.<sup><a href="#83" class="pointer">83</a></sup></span><span style="font-weight: 400;"> &nbsp;All &ldquo;advertisement&rdquo; links were 302 redirects, and none were </span><b>redirect.php</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> links. &nbsp;This is consistent with our observation that the </span><b>aax.me</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> public interface only permits visitors to shorten links using the 302 redirect method.</span></p>

<h3 class=" lh-solid mb3"><b style="">5.3. A Connection to an Account Potentially Under UAE Government Control</b></h3>
<p class="mt0"><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">This section describes a case from December 2012 where an Emirati activist said he received links connected to aax.me from an account that may have been &nbsp;under UAE government control.</span></i></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">In December 2012, an author of this report was contacted by an Emirati activist, who reported that an account, </span><b>@WeldBudhabi</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">, had sent him a link on 14 December 2012 via Twitter direct message that took him to a page on </span><b>a7rarelemarat.com</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">. &nbsp;A report by BBC notes that UAE authorities on 14 December 2012 arrested an individual who they believed to be associated with </span><b>@WeldBudhabi</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">, and that the account was </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">&ldquo;reportedly hacked by the authorities&rdquo;</span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;"> on the same day.<sup><a href="#84" class="pointer">84</a></sup></span><span style="font-weight: 400;"> &nbsp;The Emirati activist told us that he later contacted </span><b>@WeldBudhabi</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">, who reported that he did not send the link.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">This link provides the strongest connection between Stealth Falcon and the UAE Authorities that we are aware of.</span></p>

<h3 class=" lh-solid mb3"><b style="">5.4. Infrastructure Analysis of Stealth Falcon Command &amp; Control</b></h3>
<p class="mt0"><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">This section describes how we traced Stealth Falcon&rsquo;s spyware to live C2 servers and domain names.</span></i></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">We fingerprinted the behavior of </span><b>adhostingcache.com</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> (the C2 server for the Stage One spyware that Donaghy received) and traced it to a series of 14 active IP adresses and 11 domains (using PassiveTotal<sup><a href="#85" class="pointer">85</a></sup></span><span style="font-weight: 400;">). &nbsp;Nine domains are named like generic Internet backend servers (e.g., <b>simpleadbanners.com</b>, <b>clickstatistic.com</b>), whereas two appear to be thematically related to travel (<b>bestairlinepricetags.com</b>, <b>fasttravelclearance.com</b>), perhaps indicative of travel-themed targeting or targets.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">We fingerprinted the behavior of </span><b>incapsulawebcache.com</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> (the C2 server for the Stage Two spyware that Donaghy receied) and scanned the Internet (including historical scanning results<sup><a href="#86" class="pointer">86</a></sup></span><span style="font-weight: 400;">) for servers that matched our fingerprint. &nbsp;We also used Passive DNS to correlate IP addresses to domains. &nbsp;In total, we associated 67 active (and 30 historical) IP addresses with the Stage Two spyware. &nbsp;Using PassiveTotal, we linked 69 domain names to these IP addresses, the earliest registered on 28 January 2013, and the most recent registered on 19 April 2016. &nbsp;The vast majority of the domains are named like generic Internet backend servers. &nbsp;One domain name appears to be travel-themed (<b>airlineadverts.com</b>), and two appear to be news and/or government themed (</span><span style="font-weight: 400;"><b>ministrynewschannel.com</b>, <b>ministrynewsinfo.com</b></span><span style="font-weight: 400;">).</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">The earliest date we found an IP addresses matching our Stage Two fingerprint was 21 July 2014, as recorded by </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">sonar-ssl</span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;"> scans. &nbsp;It is possible that the operator used a different configuration of spyware between January 2013 and July 2014.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">We traced several additional domains to Stealth Falcon using WHOIS information, or Passive DNS. &nbsp;Of these, one was designed to impersonate a China-based provider of VoIP solutions (<b>yeastarr.com</b>), and two appeared to perhaps contain the Arabic word for security, &ldquo;amn,&rdquo; (<b>amnkeysvc.com</b>, <b>amnkeysvcs.com</b>). &nbsp;Full scan results and other indicators of targeting can be found in </span><b><a href="https://citizenlab.ca/2016/05/stealth-falcon-appendices/" class="pointer">Appendix F: Indicators of Targeting</a></b><span style="font-weight: 400;">.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">The domain names we found were typically registered with WHOIS privacy providers. &nbsp;Although, in some cases, we were able to obtain the true registration email through historical WHOIS. &nbsp;Typically, the operators practiced disciplined operational security: we rarely found an email address that was used to register two domains, and we rarely found two domains linked to the same IP address.</span></p>

<h2 class=" lh-solid mb3"><b style="">6. Tip of the Iceberg: Possibly Related Attacks</b></h2>
<p class="mt0"><span style="font-weight: 400;">We suspect that the activity we have observed is simply the tip of the iceberg in ongoing attacks against dissidents in the UAE. &nbsp;Reuse of tactics, techniques and procedures and general carelessness by operators can often lead to discovery of links between campaigns. &nbsp;We briefly discuss some instances of potentially related attacks below.</span></p>

<h3 class=" lh-solid mb3"><b style="">6.2. A fake file sharing site?</b></h3>
<p class="mt0"><span style="font-weight: 400;">We identified one </span><b>aax.me</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> link (<b>http://aax.me/4b708</b>) that points to <b>http://velocityfiles.com/download.php?id=a81abdd8a0c0cd1d5d3b6baadcc9eb18</b>. &nbsp;We visited this link in February 2016, and were served a blank page. &nbsp;VelocityFiles appears to have been disabled in March 2016.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">We found that the site purported to be a file hosting site, where users could register and upload files. &nbsp;However, the registration and signup pages are currently blank, and were blank as of the Internet Archive&rsquo;s oldest capture of the pages in December 2013.<sup><a href="#89" class="pointer">89</a></sup></span><span style="font-weight: 400;"> &nbsp;We were unable to identify any links to velocityfiles.com from Twitter, or any pages indexed by Google.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">The design of VelocityFiles appeared to be a loosely modified version of a public website design template.<sup><a href="#90" class="pointer">90</a></sup></span><span style="font-weight: 400;"> &nbsp;Given that the site appears to be designed to pose as a public file sharing service, has no obvious public functionality, and was linked to through aax.me, we suspect that it may have been an attack site.</span></p>

<h3 class=" lh-solid mb3"><b style="">6.3. Fake web forums?</b></h3>
<p class="mt0"><span style="font-weight: 400;">We found an </span><b>aax.me</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> link<sup><a href="#91" class="pointer">91</a></sup></span><span style="font-weight: 400;"> that pointed to </span><b><span style="font-weight: 400;">https://call4uaefreedom.com/vb</span></b><span style="font-weight: 400;">. &nbsp;The domain was registered on 5/15/2013 and expired on 5/15/2015. &nbsp;We were unable to find any webpages or tweets linking to this website. &nbsp;A Google search for &ldquo;call4uaefreedom&rdquo; reveals a blog, containing five posts, all within a 30 minute span on 4 June 2013, and an empty Twitter account @call4uaefreedom, created in May 2013. &nbsp;Given the suspicious activity associated with the alias &ldquo;call4uaefreedom,&rdquo; this may have been created by operators.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">While searching for domains with similar domain names, we came across </span><b>uaefreedom.com</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">. &nbsp;The domain name was first registered on 11 June 2010 by the administrators of UAE Hewar,<sup><a href="#92" class="pointer">92</a></sup></span><span style="font-weight: 400;"> an online discussion forum founded in 2009 that was a frequent government target. &nbsp;The domain name expired on 11 June 2011, but was re-registered by a different registrant on 7 October 2012.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">On 16 October 2012, we find the only tweet linking to </span><b>uaefreedom.com</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">. &nbsp;A Google search yields no links to the site and we found no passive DNS data available for this domain. &nbsp;The tweet was sent from account </span><b>@FreeUAE2012</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">, directed at @uaemot. &nbsp;An individual based in Qatar was convicted in absentia on 25 December 2013 for running @uaemot.<sup><a href="#93" class="pointer">93</a></sup></span></p>

<h2 class=" lh-solid mb3"><b style="">7. Attribution</b></h2>
<p class="mt0"><span style="font-weight: 400;">In this section, we analyze two competing hypotheses about the identity of Stealth Falcon, and conclude that the balance of evidence suggests Stealth Falcon may be linked to the UAE government.</span></p>
<h3 class=" lh-solid mb3"><b style="">Hypothesis 1: Stealth Falcon is State Sponsored</b></h3>
<p class="mt0"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Stealth Falcon is a sophisticated threat actor, capable of deploying a wide range of technical and social engineering techniques against a potential target. The operations targeting Donaghy are linked to a series of primarily UAE-focused campaigns against UAE dissidents, starting in January 2012. &nbsp;While there is no &ldquo;smoking gun,&rdquo; several pieces of evidence suggest a connection between Stealth Falcon and the UAE Government. &nbsp;</span></p>
<h4 class=" lh-solid mb3"><b style="">UAE Focused Targeting, Links to Arrests</b></h4>
<p class="mt0"><span style="font-weight: 400;">The majority (73%) of bait content on aax.me was focused on UAE-related political issues (</span><b>Section 5.2</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">). Furthermore, of the 27 victim Twitter accounts we linked to public Stealth Falcon targeting, 24 </span><b>primarily engaged in political activities, or were otherwise critical of the UAE government </b><span style="font-weight: 400;">(</span><b>Section 5.1</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">). &nbsp;Of these 24, we were able to find a subsequent arrest or a conviction in absentia by the UAE government.</span></p>
<h4 class=" lh-solid mb3"><b style="">Tweets During a Period of Government Control</b></h4>
<p class="mt0"><span style="font-weight: 400;">A reported case in which a Twitter account apparently under UAE Government control shared a Stealth Falcon link also suggests a connection. </span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">In December 2012, an activist contacted us and asserted that an </span><b>a7rarelemarat.com</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> link was sent to him in a private message from the </span><b>@WeldBudhabi</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> account the same day that an individual accused of operating the account was arrested, and while the account was </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">&ldquo;reportedly hacked by authorities&rdquo;</span></i>.<sup><a href="#96" class="pointer">96</a></sup> <span style="font-weight: 400;"> The activist asserted that he contacted an owner of the account, who claimed he did not send that link. &nbsp;The Twitter account associated with </span><b>a7rarelemarat.com</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">, </span><b>@a7rarelemarat</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">, appears to have been under the control of Stealth Falcon at some point during October 2012 (and possibly before and after), as the account sent several </span><b>aax.me</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> links in October 2012.</span></p>
<h4 class=" lh-solid mb3"><b style="">Sophisticated Target Knowledge and Operational Security</b></h4>
<p class="mt0"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Stealth Falcon demonstrates some familiarity with the patterns of behavior, interests, and activities of its targets, suggesting that the operators may have been working with other sources of information about their targets&rsquo; behaviors. &nbsp;In addition, Stealth Falcon displayed above-average operational security throughout the campaign. &nbsp;</span><span style="font-weight: 400;">Some of the social engineering was highly intricate, particularly the email from Andrew Dwight about his ski holiday. &nbsp;Stealth Falcon also shows familiarity with creating and maintaining a range of fictitious personas, and registering and managing a significant amount of attack and C2 infrastructure with concern for operational security. </span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">The infrastructure behind the malware attacks showed good compartmentalization of identities. We rarely found the same (fake) registration information used for more than one C2 domain. Stealth Falcon operators also appear to have deleted one of their attack domains, </span><b>adhostingcache.com</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> when they realized their attempt to target Donaghy had failed. &nbsp;We also noted that the (self-signed) SSL certificates on the C2 domains were changed several times as we monitored the infrastructure, perhaps in an attempt to thwart fingerprinting of their infrastructure via SSL certificates.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">This level of sophistication is consistent with a state sponsored attacker. &nbsp;Importantly, we found little evidence that indicate criminal or other motivation for the attack, with no evidence of financial or industry targeting.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">We also note that while some Stealth Falcon domains were registered on anonymousbitcoindomains.com, which is linked to APT28 activities, we found no evidence to support such a connection. &nbsp;See </span><b><a href="https://citizenlab.ca/2016/05/stealth-falcon-appendices/" class="pointer">Appendix G: No Evidence of APT28 Connection</a></b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> for more details.</span></p>
<h3 class=" lh-solid mb3"><b style="">Hypothesis 2: Stealth Falcon is Not State Sponsored</b></h3>
<p class="mt0"><span style="font-weight: 400;">We have considered the possibility that Stealth Falcon&rsquo;s operators are not state sponsored, but ultimately find little evidence to support this possibility.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Stealth Falcon&rsquo;s attacks show no evidence of cyber criminal motivations, like financial theft or fraud, nor is there any evidence of attempts to steal intellectual property or conduct other forms of economic espionage. Instead, the targets are politically engaged individuals and public figures. Furthermore, the activity of targets we have been able to identify often concerns domestic UAE issues. &nbsp;Therefore, we would need to posit an operator with an interest in individuals known for their engagement in domestic UAE issues.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Other potential motivations might include blackmail or extortion. If this were the case, however, we might expect follow-up interactions between attackers and successful victims, and we would also expect attackers to use off-the-shelf Remote Access Tools (RATs), rather than apparently coding a general-purpose RAT from scratch. This would save them the trouble of needing to load additional malware to exfiltrate files or other material. &nbsp;We are aware of no evidence of follow-up interactions between the operators and successful victims as part of any extortion attempts. &nbsp;Furthermore, Stealth Falcon&rsquo;s use of JavaScript to profile and de-anonymize victims seems inconsistent with a primary motivation of collecting information that could be used for blackmail.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">The strongest scenario for a non-state sponsored attacker is thus a politically motivated group. &nbsp;Stealth Falcon targets are primarily individuals known for their criticism of the UAE government. It is perhaps conceivable that a group of pro-government hackers might, without coordination, target these individuals.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">There are, however, several features of Stealth Falcon&rsquo;s activities that tell against this possibility. First, there is limited existing evidence that such autonomous groups exist and are active in the UAE. &nbsp;Given what is known about this kind of group, we might expect such a group to have engaged in defacements, public boasting, or other public-facing activities related to Stealth Falcon&rsquo;s campaign. &nbsp;Furthermore, it seems unlikely that a previously unknown political group would have the resources to develop and maintain Stealth Falcon&rsquo;s fictitious personas and compartmentalized infrastructure.</span></p>
<h3 class=" lh-solid mb3"><b style="">Evaluation of Hypotheses</b></h3>
<p class="mt0"><span style="font-weight: 400;">We evaluated both hypotheses and found </span><b>Hypothesis 1: Stealth Falcon is State Sponsored</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> to be the best at explaining the many elements that we have observed. Stealth Falcon&rsquo;s tactics, resources, and targets all fit with the profile of a state sponsored attacker. &nbsp;Furthermore, the circumstantial evidence we have presented in this report is suggestive of a link between Stealth Falcon and an entity within the UAE Government.</span></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h2 class=" lh-solid mb3"><b style="">8. Conclusion: The Big Picture</b></h2>
<p class="mt0"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Stealth Falcon appears to be a new, state sponsored threat actor. &nbsp;As an operator, Stealth Falcon is distinguished by well informed and sophisticated social engineering, combined with moderately sophisticated<sup><a href="#97" class="pointer">97</a></sup></span><span style="font-weight: 400;"> technical attempts to deanonymize and monitor political targets working on the UAE, and relatively simple malcode.<sup><a href="#98" class="pointer">98</a></sup></span></p>
<h3 class=" lh-solid mb3"><b style="">Social Engineering and the Achilles Heel of Civil Society</b></h3>
<p class="mt0"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Stealth Falcon&rsquo;s technical approach may not be cutting edge, but the operators are neither unsophisticated or ineffective. Analyzed holistically as an operation, Stealth Falcon is a logical and multi-pronged approach to compromising and unmasking a class of targets. &nbsp;Stealth Falcon&rsquo;s campaign highlights the power of social engineering, once a technical bar has been met, in conducting a large scale campaign. </span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Contemporary social movements and civil society groups rely heavily on the internet for both their core operations, as well as advocacy activities. &nbsp;Yet these groups are often operating outside a centrally managed IT environment. &nbsp;The constant sharing of links and materials, as well as regular communications with journalists makes them especially vulnerable to targeting with social engineering.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">However, the emphasis on social engineering can also cut in the other direction. Many modern attack techniques require an attacker to interact with a target. &nbsp;When operators like Stealth Falcon send malicious e-mails and tweets, there are a range of opportunities for retrospective investigation. &nbsp;As this report shows, the inboxes of targets, for example, are often a more efficient object of investigation than computers themselves, especially once features of a particular campaign are recognized. </span></p>
<h3 class=" lh-solid mb3"><b style="">The Growing Trend of Impersonating Journalists</b></h3>
<p class="mt0"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Stealth Falcon is only the latest example of civil society-focused threat actors impersonating NGOs and journalists to conduct espionage operations. The tactic has been used by a wide range of actors, including Bahrain&rsquo;s government,<sup><a href="#99" class="pointer">99</a></sup></span><span style="font-weight: 400;"> Packrat in Latin America,<sup><a href="#100" class="pointer">100</a></sup></span><span style="font-weight: 400;"> Iranian groups</span><span style="font-weight: 400;">,<sup><a href="#101" class="pointer">101</a></sup> and China related groups,<sup><a href="#102" class="pointer">102</a></sup></span><span style="font-weight: 400;"> among others. Threat actors seem to gravitate towards this tactic because interacting with journalists is an essential part of civil society activity. It is common for journalists to send unsolicited messages to activists and civil society organizations asking for information, and there is typically a strong incentive for the organization to engage. Indeed, even Western law enforcement agencies have occasionally adopted the approach.<sup><a href="#103" class="pointer">103</a></sup></span><span style="font-weight: 400;"> &nbsp;The reporter-source relationship is protected in many jurisdictions, based on the understanding that protecting this trust is important to a healthy and vibrant civil society. &nbsp;Tactics that play on this trust are risky, and can quickly contribute to eroding the trust on which civil society is based.</span></p>
<p><b>Final Note: A Plea for More Research</b></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Importantly, while we were unable to identify evidence of a conclusive link between Stealth Falcon and a particular sponsor, we have assembled a body of circumstantial evidence that points to an alignment of interests between Stealth Falcon and the UAE Security Forces. &nbsp;We hope that other researchers will draw from our findings and work to identify additional cases. &nbsp;Finally, we urge anyone who recalls receiving a link to &ldquo;aax.me,&rdquo; or an email from &ldquo;Andrew Dwight&rdquo; to contact the authors of this report for further investigation.</span></p>
