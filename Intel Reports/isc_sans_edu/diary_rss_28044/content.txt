<p><em><strong>Introduction</strong></em></p>

<p>Back in January 2021, law enforcement and judicial authorities worldwide <a href="https://www.europol.europa.eu/newsroom/news/world%E2%80%99s-most-dangerous-malware-emotet-disrupted-through-global-action">took down the Emotet botnet</a>.&nbsp; Although some Emotet emails still went out in the weeks after that, those were remnants from the inactive botnet infrastructure.&nbsp; We hadn&#39;t seen any <u><em>new</em></u> Emotet since then.</p>

<p>But on Monday 2021-11-15, we saw indicators that <a href="https://twitter.com/Cryptolaemus1/status/1460302706954981385">Emotet has returned</a>.&nbsp; This diary reviews activity from a recent Emotet infection.</p>

<p><a href="https://isc.sans.edu/diaryimages/images/2021-11-15-ISc-diary-image-01.jpg"><img alt="" src="https://isc.sans.edu/diaryimages/images/2021-11-15-ISc-diary-image-01a.jpg" style="border-width: 2px; border-style: solid;" /></a><br />
<em>Shown above:&nbsp; Chain of events for Emotet infection on Monday 2021-11-15.</em></p>

<p><em><strong>Emails</strong></em></p>

<p>We found some emails from a newly-revived Emotet botnet on Monday 2021-11-15 that have one of three types of attachments:</p>

<ul>
	<li>Microsoft Excel spreadsheet</li>
	<li>Microsoft Word document</li>
	<li>Password-protected zip archive (password: BMIIVYHZ) containing a Word document</li>
</ul>

<p>These emails were all spoofed replies that used data from stolen email chains, presumably gathered from previously infected Windows hosts.</p>

<p><a href="https://isc.sans.edu/diaryimages/images/2021-11-15-ISc-diary-image-02.jpg"><img alt="" src="https://isc.sans.edu/diaryimages/images/2021-11-15-ISc-diary-image-02a.jpg" style="border-width: 2px; border-style: solid;" /></a><br />
