    <div class="node-full__body blog-content__body">  <p>In December 2019,&nbsp;Proofpoint researchers&nbsp;observed&nbsp;email campaigns widely distributing&nbsp;a new&nbsp;version&nbsp;of the&nbsp;ZLoader&nbsp;banking malware, which appears to be under active development. We have seen over 100 campaigns since January 1, 2020 with recipients in the United States, Canada, Germany, Poland, and Australia. The fraudulent email lures include a variety of subjects, including COVID-19 scam prevention tips, COVID-19 testing, and invoices.</p>

<p>ZLoader, a variant of the infamous Zeus banking malware, has&nbsp;been around since&nbsp;2006.&nbsp;It is a typical banking malware that makes use of&nbsp;webinjects&nbsp;to steal credentials and other&nbsp;private information&nbsp;from users of targeted financial institutions. The malware&nbsp;can&nbsp;also steal passwords and cookies stored in&nbsp;victim’s&nbsp;web browsers.&nbsp;With the stolen information&nbsp;in hand, the malware can use the VNC (Virtual Network Computing) client it downloads to allow threat actors&nbsp;to connect to the victim’s system and make illicit financial&nbsp;transactions from the banking user’s legitimate device.&nbsp;</p>

<p>Almost two&nbsp;years after ZLoader’s last activity in 2018,&nbsp;we started&nbsp;observing&nbsp;campaigns using a new&nbsp;banking malware&nbsp;that&nbsp;exhibited&nbsp;functionality&nbsp;and network traffic&nbsp;similar&nbsp;to&nbsp;the&nbsp;original&nbsp;2016-2018&nbsp;ZLoader.&nbsp;However,&nbsp;during our analysis&nbsp;we noticed&nbsp;that&nbsp;it was missing the code&nbsp;obfuscation, string encryption, and&nbsp;a few&nbsp;other&nbsp;advanced&nbsp;features&nbsp;of the original&nbsp;ZLoader.&nbsp;Hence,&nbsp;the new malware&nbsp;does not&nbsp;appear to be&nbsp;a continuation of the 2018 strain, but&nbsp;likely&nbsp;a&nbsp;fork of an earlier version.</p>

<p>In&nbsp;this post, we&nbsp;analyze&nbsp;the&nbsp;new malware&nbsp;version&nbsp;and&nbsp;provide&nbsp;several&nbsp;examples&nbsp;of&nbsp;the most interesting&nbsp;email&nbsp;campaigns&nbsp;spreading it.&nbsp;We&nbsp;will continue&nbsp;to&nbsp;track&nbsp;this new malware as a “ZLoader&nbsp;variant” which has&nbsp;caught on in the&nbsp;<a href="https://twitter.com/hashtag/zloader">wider community</a>.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>

<p><strong>Background</strong>&nbsp;</p>

<p>From&nbsp;<a href="https://www.fortinet.com/blog/threat-research/the-curious-case-of-an-unknown-trojan-targeting-german-speaking-users.html">June 2016</a>&nbsp;to&nbsp;February 2018,&nbsp;a&nbsp;banking malware&nbsp;known as&nbsp;ZLoader&nbsp;(also known as&nbsp;DELoader&nbsp;or&nbsp;Terdot)&nbsp;spread in the wild.&nbsp;The&nbsp;letter&nbsp;“Z”&nbsp;in its name&nbsp;was given because it is a variant of&nbsp;the Zeus malware.&nbsp;The “loader”&nbsp;part of its name&nbsp;is due&nbsp;to its distinguishing feature: it was distributed as a downloader&nbsp;component,&nbsp;which would download and&nbsp;run the&nbsp;main&nbsp;banking malware&nbsp;component&nbsp;and other modules&nbsp;from&nbsp;its&nbsp;command and control (C&amp;C)&nbsp;server.&nbsp;</p>

<p>While there were multiple threat actors using the malware at the time, TA511 (Hancitor) was one of the most prevalent.&nbsp;In approximately November 2017,&nbsp;TA511 switched from&nbsp;ZLoader&nbsp;to Panda Banker. Other threat actors started following suit and switched&nbsp;away from&nbsp;ZLoader&nbsp;to other&nbsp;malware. The last&nbsp;email&nbsp;campaign we saw using the original&nbsp;ZLoader&nbsp;was in February 2018.&nbsp;</p>

<p>The&nbsp;next&nbsp;anti-analysis&nbsp;mechanism&nbsp;is the&nbsp;encryption&nbsp;of&nbsp;strings. Most of&nbsp;ZLoader’s&nbsp;important strings&nbsp;are encrypted&nbsp;using XOR and a hardcoded string&nbsp;(e.g. “7Gl5et#0GoTI5VV94”).&nbsp;An example&nbsp;IDAPython&nbsp;script to decrypt strings in&nbsp;the sample we analyzed is available on our&nbsp;<a href="https://github.com/EmergingThreats/threatresearch/blob/master/zloader/str_decrypt.py">GitHub</a>.&nbsp;</p>

<p>The last anti-analysis measure&nbsp;we will&nbsp;mention&nbsp;is not&nbsp;built into the&nbsp;malware&nbsp;client&nbsp;but&nbsp;implemented in the C&amp;C server&nbsp;instead. While it varies based on the&nbsp;campaign,&nbsp;we&nbsp;noticed aggressive blacklisting of&nbsp;sandboxes and&nbsp;malware analysis systems&nbsp;and&nbsp;significant&nbsp;blocking based on geography of the connecting source&nbsp;IP&nbsp;address.&nbsp;</p>

<p><strong>Configuration</strong>&nbsp;</p>

<p>ZLoader&nbsp;continues the Zeus tradition of using a data structure known as the&nbsp;“<a href="https://github.com/Visgean/Zeus/blob/translation/source/common/generateddata.h#L58">BaseConfig</a>”&nbsp;to store its&nbsp;initial&nbsp;configuration.&nbsp;Figure 2&nbsp;shows an example of the&nbsp;BaseConfig&nbsp;decryption function:&nbsp;</p>

<p><img alt="ZLoader_2" data-entity-type="file" data-entity-uuid="ff92f8bc-6c41-4b93-b352-c780ab502164" height="183" src="/sites/default/files/inline-images/ZLoader_2.jpg" width="815" loading="lazy"></p>

<p class="MsoCaption"><em>Figure 2 Example of a BaseConfig decryption function</em></p>

<p>It uses RC4 with a hardcoded key&nbsp;(e.g. “quxrfjxtmedqretawrxg”).&nbsp;An example plaintext config is shown&nbsp;in&nbsp;Figure 3:&nbsp;</p>

<p><img alt="ZLoader_3" data-entity-type="file" data-entity-uuid="505ad190-2404-42be-a6ac-c73753f2d380" height="327" src="/sites/default/files/inline-images/ZLoader_3.jpg" width="825" loading="lazy"></p>

<p class="MsoCaption"><em>Figure 3 Example of a plaintext BaseConfig</em></p>

<p>The plaintext data is interpreted as a binary structure&nbsp;and includes:&nbsp;</p>

<ul>
	<li>DWORD used in C&amp;C communications (e.g.&nbsp;0x83)&nbsp;</li>
	<li>Botnet name (e.g.&nbsp;“1”)&nbsp;</li>
	<li>Campaign name (e.g. “07/04”)&nbsp;</li>
	<li>Up to 10 C&amp;C URLs (e.g.&nbsp;“hxxps://xyajbocpggsr\.site/wp-config.php”&nbsp;and&nbsp;“hxxps://ooygvpxrb\.pw/wp-config.php”)&nbsp;</li>
	<li>RC4 key used in C&amp;C communications (e.g.&nbsp;“41997b4a729e1a0175208305170752dd”)&nbsp;</li>
	<li>Miscellaneous timeouts and flags&nbsp;</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Command and Control</strong>&nbsp;</p>

<p>ZLoader&nbsp;uses HTTP(S) POST requests for command and control.&nbsp;The POST data&nbsp;is encrypted&nbsp;in two layers.&nbsp;The first&nbsp;layer&nbsp;is RC4 using the key from the&nbsp;BaseConfig.&nbsp;The second layer&nbsp;is an XOR-based encryption typical in Zeus variants known as “<a href="https://github.com/Visgean/Zeus/blob/translation/source/common/crypt.cpp#L207">Visual Encrypt</a>.”&nbsp;</p>

<p>The plaintext data&nbsp;is structured&nbsp;using a traditional Zeus data structure known as “BinStorage.”&nbsp;BinStorage&nbsp;consists of&nbsp;a&nbsp;<a href="https://github.com/Visgean/Zeus/blob/translation/source/common/binstorage.h#L42">header</a>&nbsp;and a variable number of&nbsp;<a href="https://github.com/Visgean/Zeus/blob/translation/source/common/binstorage.h#L51">data items</a>. The header is 48-bytes in size and&nbsp;contains:</p>

<ul>
	<li>Random data (20-bytes)&nbsp;</li>
	<li>Size of data items (DWORD)&nbsp;</li>
	<li>Flags (DWORD)&nbsp;</li>
	<li>Number of data items (DWORD)&nbsp;</li>
	<li>MD5 hash of data items (16-bytes)&nbsp;</li>
</ul>

<p>Each data item starts with a 16-byte header containing:&nbsp;</p>

<ul>
	<li>Id&nbsp;(DWORD)&nbsp;--&nbsp;also&nbsp;known as “CFGID”&nbsp;</li>
	<li>Flags (DWORD)&nbsp;</li>
	<li>Size of data (compressed) (DWORD)&nbsp;--&nbsp;ZLoader&nbsp;does not&nbsp;use compression&nbsp;</li>
	<li>Size of data (uncompressed) (DWORD)&nbsp;</li>
</ul>

<p>The&nbsp;response data&nbsp;is&nbsp;encrypted&nbsp;similarly to requests.&nbsp;Once decrypted, it also&nbsp;typically&nbsp;uses the&nbsp;BinStorage&nbsp;structure.&nbsp;We will&nbsp;look at three requests:&nbsp;initial&nbsp;“hello,”&nbsp;main&nbsp;component&nbsp;download, and configuration&nbsp;update.&nbsp;</p>
