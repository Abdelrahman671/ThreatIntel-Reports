<p>In September this year the security researcher Orange Tsai <a rel="noreferrer noopener nofollow" aria-label="published  (opens in a new tab)" href="https://blog.orange.tw/2020/09/how-i-hacked-facebook-again-mobileiron-mdm-rce.html" target="_blank">published </a>various vulnerabilities and P0Cs related to the MobileIron&#8217;s mobile Device Management (MDM) solution. </p>



<p> The <a rel="noreferrer noopener" aria-label="Tarlogic Blue Team (opens in a new tab)" href="https://www.tarlogic.com/blog/blue-team/" target="_blank">Tarlogic Blue Team</a> has identified the use of <a rel="noreferrer noopener nofollow" aria-label="CVE-2020-15505 (opens in a new tab)" href="https://github.com/iamnoooob/CVE-Reverse" target="_blank">CVE-2020-15505</a> by a certain group of attackers to download and run Kaiten </p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading"><span class="ez-toc-section" id="Kaiten_aka_Tsunami"></span>Kaiten (aka Tsunami)<span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h2>



<p>Through the <a href="https://www.tarlogic.com/blog/log4shell-vulnerability-cve-2021-44228/">JNDI injection</a> related to said CVE, the attackers are downloading the well-known Kaiten. This family of malware has been used by multiple actors for <a rel="noreferrer noopener nofollow" aria-label="more than 15 years (opens in a new tab)" href="https://pen-testing.sans.org/resources/papers/gcih/sql-snake-port-1433-threats-support-cyber-defense-initiative-103976" target="_blank">more than 15 years</a> (its beginnings date back to 2002) mainly as an offensive tool to generate DoS attacks and, currently, for the mining of cryptocurrencies.</p>



<p>There are dozens of variants associated with this <a rel="noreferrer noopener nofollow" aria-label="malicious code (opens in a new tab)" href="https://malpedia.caad.fkie.fraunhofer.de/details/elf.tsunami" target="_blank">malicious code</a>; possibly as a result of the publication of its source code. In <a rel="noreferrer noopener nofollow" aria-label="February 2016 (opens in a new tab)" href="https://securelist.com/beware-of-backdoored-linux-mint-isos/73893/" target="_blank">February 2016</a>, a variant of Kaiten was distributed by a group of cybercriminals through malicious ISO images after compromising an instance of Linux Mint WordPress and modify its download URLs. Another variant, dubbed Amnesia in April 2017 by PaloAlto, was related to the infection of multiple <a href="https://unit42.paloaltonetworks.com/unit42-new-iotlinux-malware-targets-dvrs-forms-botnet/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener nofollow" aria-label="CCTV-DVR systems (opens in a new tab)">CCTV-DVR systems</a> around the world by taking advantage of a certain RCE vulnerability that affected more than 70 vendors.</p>



<p>In April 2018, <a href="https://threatpost.com/muhstik-botnet-exploits-highly-critical-drupal-bug/131360/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener nofollow" aria-label="Netlab 360 researchers (opens in a new tab)">Netlab 360 researchers</a> identified a botnet (nicknamed <strong>Muhstik</strong>) also linked to this malicious code that used a certain Drupal vulnerability as the input vector.</p>



<p>The capabilities of this malware are mainly focused on denial of service attacks by implementing various functions to do TCP/UDP flooding to the victims; all instructed by means of the IRC protocol. Attackers also have the ability to execute commands and download files.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading"><span class="ez-toc-section" id="Malware_characteristics"></span>Malware characteristics:<span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h2>



<p>The binary identified in one of our clients corresponds to <a rel="noreferrer noopener nofollow" aria-label="969013b23e440fe31be70daac6d7edb2 (opens in a new tab)" href="https://www.virustotal.com/gui/file/0c27c64fc118ef56048b7d994162c4a0d008b4582c5eeb6923949a286f45ec52/detection" target="_blank">969013b23e440fe31be70daac6d7edb2</a>. Its download originates from a certain <em>dropper</em> developed in bash whose goal is, in the first place, to kill multiple processes related to miners and services that require a high level of CPU.</p>



<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><a href="https://www.blackarrow.net/wp-content/uploads/2020/10/1_bot_kill_function.png"><img fetchpriority="high" decoding="async" width="840" height="808"   src="data:image/gif;base64,R0lGODlhAQABAAAAACH5BAEKAAEALAAAAAABAAEAAAICTAEAOw==" data-src="https://www.blackarrow.net/wp-content/uploads/2020/10/1_bot_kill_function.png" alt="bot_kill function" class="wp-image-1682 teaser lazyload"/><noscript><img decoding="async" width="840" height="808"   src="https://www.blackarrow.net/wp-content/uploads/2020/10/1_bot_kill_function.png" alt="bot_kill function" class="wp-image-1682"/></noscript></a><figcaption> Figure 1. bot_kill function </figcaption></figure>



<p>Once these processes are finished, the script downloads, via “curl”, the Kaiten malware from the URL <em>https://lib.pygensim.com/gensim</em> in the directory defined by the INSTALL variable (<em>/var/tmp/systemd-private-c15c0d5284bd838c15fd0d6c5c2b50bb-systemd-resolved.service-xCkB12/jf2fa44a/aPs52s/jKal2d</em>), it sets execution permissions and finally runs it under the name of “kworker”.</p>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><a href="https://www.blackarrow.net/wp-content/uploads/2020/10/5_Function_structure.png"><img decoding="async" width="1024" height="195"   src="data:image/gif;base64,R0lGODlhAQABAAAAACH5BAEKAAEALAAAAAABAAEAAAICTAEAOw==" data-src="https://www.blackarrow.net/wp-content/uploads/2020/10/5_Function_structure-1024x195.png" alt="Function structure" class="wp-image-1690 teaser lazyload"/><noscript><img decoding="async" width="1024" height="195"   src="https://www.blackarrow.net/wp-content/uploads/2020/10/5_Function_structure-1024x195.png" alt="Function structure" class="wp-image-1690"/></noscript></a><figcaption> Figure 5. Function structure </figcaption></figure>



<p>The binary, after executing, makes a <em>fork() </em>call and later tries to establish communication with the control server using the IRC protocol. To do this, it generates a random nickname/user and connects to certain channel waiting to receive the instructions from their operators.</p>



<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><a href="https://www.blackarrow.net/wp-content/uploads/2020/10/6_Fork_y_CandC_connection.png"><img decoding="async" width="1024" height="151"   src="data:image/gif;base64,R0lGODlhAQABAAAAACH5BAEKAAEALAAAAAABAAEAAAICTAEAOw==" data-src="https://www.blackarrow.net/wp-content/uploads/2020/10/6_Fork_y_CandC_connection.png" alt="C2 connection " class="wp-image-1691 teaser lazyload"/><noscript><img decoding="async" width="1024" height="151"   src="https://www.blackarrow.net/wp-content/uploads/2020/10/6_Fork_y_CandC_connection.png" alt="C2 connection " class="wp-image-1691"/></noscript></a><figcaption> Figure 6. Fork y C&amp;C connection </figcaption></figure>



<p>The code implements various functions to carry out different types of <a href="https://www.tarlogic.com/dos-test/">denial of service attacks</a> (SYN / UDP flooding, etc.). The following image shows the logic to execute one of them, specifically, the so-called Tsunami attack. The operators will instruct the bots to execute, for a certain time (set in seconds), a DOS TCP attack playing with various flags of this protocol.</p>



<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><a href="https://www.blackarrow.net/wp-content/uploads/2020/10/7_Tsunami_dos.png"><img decoding="async" width="1024" height="375"   src="data:image/gif;base64,R0lGODlhAQABAAAAACH5BAEKAAEALAAAAAABAAEAAAICTAEAOw==" data-src="https://www.blackarrow.net/wp-content/uploads/2020/10/7_Tsunami_dos-1024x375.png" alt="Tsunami (DOS)" class="wp-image-1692 teaser lazyload"/><noscript><img decoding="async" width="1024" height="375"   src="https://www.blackarrow.net/wp-content/uploads/2020/10/7_Tsunami_dos-1024x375.png" alt="Tsunami (DOS)" class="wp-image-1692"/></noscript></a><figcaption> Figure 7. Tsunami (DOS) </figcaption></figure>



<p>The malicious code also has the ability to execute commands on the victim via the “SH” command. To do this, first, it adds the command to execute in the $PATH env variable and then  makes use of <em>popen()</em> to run it.</p>



<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><a href="https://www.blackarrow.net/wp-content/uploads/2020/10/8_Command_execution.png"><img decoding="async" width="1024" height="688"   src="data:image/gif;base64,R0lGODlhAQABAAAAACH5BAEKAAEALAAAAAABAAEAAAICTAEAOw==" data-src="https://www.blackarrow.net/wp-content/uploads/2020/10/8_Command_execution-1024x688.png" alt="Command execution" class="wp-image-1693 teaser lazyload"/><noscript><img decoding="async" width="1024" height="688"   src="https://www.blackarrow.net/wp-content/uploads/2020/10/8_Command_execution-1024x688.png" alt="Command execution" class="wp-image-1693"/></noscript></a><figcaption> Figure 8. <h2 class="wp-block-heading"><span class="ez-toc-section" id="Indicators_of_compromise"></span>Indicators of compromise<span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h2>



<p>Yara rule:</p>



<pre class="EnlighterJSRAW" data-enlighter-language="python" data-enlighter-theme="" data-enlighter-highlight="" data-enlighter-linenumbers="" data-enlighter-lineoffset="" data-enlighter-title="" data-enlighter-group="">rule Tsunami {
    meta:
        author = "BlackArrow Unit (Tarlogic)"
        description = "Detection of Tsunami/Kaiten sample based on embeded strings"
        md5 = "969013b23e440fe31be70daac6d7edb2"
        sha1 = "5369a0122fd3b75ffdd110cc86ccc2d8ae2fa130"
    strings:
        $elf = { 7f 45 4c 46 }

        $x1 = "= Kills the client"
        $x2 = "Kaiten wa goraku"
        $x3 = "syn flooder that will kill most"
        $x4 = "NOTICE %s :Killing pid"       
        $x5 = ":Removed all spoofs"
        $x6 = "TSUNAMI &lt;target>"
        $x7 = "Do something like: 169.40"
        $x8 = ":Spoofs: %d.%d.%d.%d"
        $x9 = "NOTICE %s :UDP &lt;target>"           
        $x10 = "NOTICE %s :GET &lt;http address> "           
        $x11 = "NOTICE %s :NICK &lt;nick>"  
        $x12 = "NOTICE %s :UNKNOWN &lt;target>"
        $x13 = "NOTICE %s :KILLALL"  
        $x14 = "GETSPOOFS"  

    condition:
        $elf in (0..4) and 6 of ($x*) and filesize &lt; 120KB
}
</pre>



<p>It is recommended to filter the domain linked to the C&amp;C (lib.pygensim.com) and establish rules in the corresponding networking devices (firewalls, IDS / IPS) to identify outgoing IRC traffic as this is a protocol rarely used in business environments. In the case of using SNORT, consider the detection rules listed at: <em><a href="https://www.snort.org/search?query=irc&amp;submit_search=" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener nofollow" aria-label=" (opens in a new tab)">https://www.snort.org/search?query=irc&amp;submit_search=</a></em></p>
