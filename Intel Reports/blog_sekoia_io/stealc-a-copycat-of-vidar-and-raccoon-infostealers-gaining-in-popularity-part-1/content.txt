<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Context</h2>



<p>In January 2023, through our Dark Web monitoring routine, Sekoia.io identified a <strong>new information stealer advertised as Stealc</strong> by its alleged developer, going by the handle <em>Plymouth</em>. The threat actor presents Stealc as a fully featured and ready-to-use stealer, whose development relied on Vidar, Raccoon, Mars and Redline stealers. This information suggests that this newcomer could be a serious competitor to the popular widespread malware families mentioned above.</p>



<p>In early February 2023, <strong>Sekoia.io identified a new malware family</strong> when tracking infrastructures distributing information stealers. The <a href="https://blog.sekoia.io/adversary-c2-infrastructures-tracked-in-2023/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">Command and Control (C2) communications</a> of the associated samples share similarities with those of Vidar and Raccoon. Further analysis by Sekoia.io allowed us to <strong>associate this new malware family with Stealc</strong>.</p>



<p>The investigation led us to discover several dozens of Stealc samples distributed in the wild, and more than 40 Stealc C2 servers, certainly an indication that this new infostealer became widespread and popular among cybercriminals distributing stealers. Sekoia.io therefore conducted an in-depth analysis of this emerging threat.</p>



<p>This blog post aims at presenting the activities of the Stealc’s alleged developer, a technical analysis of the malware and its C2 communications, and how to track it. We also share details on Stealc capabilities (Annex 1) and an infection chain distributing it (Annex 2).</p>



<p><a href="https://blog.sekoia.io/stealc-a-copycat-of-vidar-and-raccoon-infostealers-gaining-in-popularity-part-2/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">In a follow-up blog post, we will share a write-up on the reverse engineering of Stealc</a> to take a look at the different techniques implemented by the malware.&nbsp;</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-a-successful-entry-into-the-cybercrime-market">A successful entry into the cybercrime market</h2>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-first-stealc-advertisement">First Stealc advertisement</h3>



<p>On 9 January, 2023, <em>Plymouth</em> advertised the Stealc information stealer for the first time on XSS and BHF Russians-speaking underground forums. The threat actor published a detailed description of the new malware to list its wide stealing capabilities, the fully featured and well designed administration panel, and some technical characteristics.</p>



<figure class="wp-block-image alignwide"><img decoding="async" src="data:image/svg+xml,%3Csvg%20xmlns='http://www.w3.org/2000/svg'%20viewBox='0%200%200%200'%3E%3C/svg%3E" alt="Advertisement for Stealc stealer on XSS, published by Plymouth on 9 January, 2023." data-lazy-src="https://lh3.googleusercontent.com/Jwjgn5MsLxf_Jm5hft4YKT9EYY5wJ8VdRJ3sz1gESVJ4wjvGDBSQKIOYZyVXqMOLnSsT5CANY3uHdLeX0aalQTVnKRNaRvt4lazlkBmzLmcq0s1zfV3lv90JGkDDAXAY4MkkaxDcdYWtB2lk2ASdIw4"/><noscript><img decoding="async" src="https://lh3.googleusercontent.com/Jwjgn5MsLxf_Jm5hft4YKT9EYY5wJ8VdRJ3sz1gESVJ4wjvGDBSQKIOYZyVXqMOLnSsT5CANY3uHdLeX0aalQTVnKRNaRvt4lazlkBmzLmcq0s1zfV3lv90JGkDDAXAY4MkkaxDcdYWtB2lk2ASdIw4" alt="Advertisement for Stealc stealer on XSS, published by Plymouth on 9 January, 2023."/></noscript><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">Figure 1. Advertisement for Stealc stealer on XSS, published by <em>Plymouth</em> on 9 January, 2023&nbsp;</figcaption></figure>



<p>By default, Stealc targets sensitive data from most used <strong>web browsers</strong>, <strong>browser extensions for cryptocurrency wallets</strong>, <strong>desktop cryptocurrency wallets</strong> and information from additional applications, including<strong> email client and messenger</strong> software. Compared to other stealers Sekoia.io analysed, the data collection configuration can be customised to tailor the malware to the customer needs.</p>



<p>Stealc also implements a <strong>customisable file grabber</strong>, allowing its customers to steal files matching their grabber rules. The stealer also has <strong>loader capabilities</strong> that would be usually expected for an information stealer sold as a Malware-as-a-Service (MaaS). A complete list of Stealc capabilities is shared in Annex 1.</p>



<p>The administration panel is also fully featured and allows its users (<em>i.e.</em> threat actors distributing the stealer), to:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>set up the malware configuration;</li>



<li>parse, display, filter, sort and analyse the stolen data;</li>



<li>download the logs (stolen data) with several options.</li>
</ul>



<p>Sekoia.io observed that <strong>logs handling is a key feature for all information stealers</strong> entering the MaaS market. Threat actors are likely to sell the stolen data on logs marketplace and therefore need to<strong> download it in a personalised way</strong>. In addition, they need to <strong>identify and extract the valuable credentials and files</strong> from the large amounts of collected data. Thus, we assess <em>Plymouth</em>, the Stealc presumed developer, almost certainly dedicated<strong> a great effort to develop the administration panel on sorting and downloading logs features</strong>.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-plymouth-s-activity-carried-out-in-a-professional-manner"><em>Plymouth</em>’s activity carried out in a professional manner</h3>



<p>After the first publication on 9 January, 2023 on XSS and BHF, <em>Plymouth</em> continued to advertise its infostealer to reach a larger audience on additional channels, including Exploit hacking forum and Telegram messaging application.</p>



<p>To <strong>gain the trust of potential customers</strong>, developers often <strong>offer free malware tests to cybercrime forum users</strong> to collect reviews and possibly positive feedback on their product. This is considered as a <strong>guarantee of quality</strong>, similarly to a <strong>Bitcoin deposit</strong> on a cybercrime forum. On some forums, it is even <strong>required to make a deposit or have relevant feedback</strong> from an administrator, moderator or experienced user to sell a product or service.</p>



<p>As shown in the following figure, <em>Plymouth</em> fulfils both: a 0.02 Bitcoin deposit (around $400 at the time of deposit), and free weekly tests offered to XSS users. We assess with high confidence that its alleged developer quickly established itself as a reliable threat actor, and its malware gained the trust of cybercriminals dealing with infostealers.</p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-technical-overview-of-stealc-sample">Technical overview of Stealc sample</h3>



<p>Sekoia.io reverse engineered Stealc and will publish an in-depth analysis to share further details. In the meantime, here is an overview of the main steps of Stealc execution.</p>



<p>Once executed, Stealc deobfuscates all its <strong>RC4-encrypted and base64-encoded strings</strong>. It then compares the system date to the hardcoded date in the obfuscated strings. If the execution occurs after the hardcoded date, the malware stops. This check is likely implemented by the stealer developer to limit the customer’s activity to the licence validity period.&nbsp;</p>



<p>Stealc also checks for virtual or sandbox environments by comparing the machine name to <em>HAL9TH</em> and the user name to <em>JohnDoe</em>, solely used by Microsoft Defender emulator.</p>

Here are the characteristic strings included in all standalone samples:</p>



<figure class="wp-block-table"><table><tbody><tr><td><code>ASCII: ------ &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; paddr: 69704, 69726<br>ASCII: \..\ &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; paddr: 69844<br>ASCII: block&nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; paddr: 69856<br>ASCII: Network Info:&nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; paddr: 69864<br>ASCII: - IP: IP?&nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; paddr: 69881<br>ASCII: - Country: ISO?&nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; paddr: 69893<br>ASCII: - Display Resolution:&nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; paddr: 69913<br>ASCII: User Agents: &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; paddr: 69936</code></td></tr></tbody></table></figure>



<p>We can also sign the malware function that loops over the obfuscated strings to deobfuscate them. A YARA rule based on both methods is shared in IoCs &amp; Technical Details.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-packed-samples">Packed samples</h3>



<p>YARA signatures based on the malware strings or functions are not efficient when the sample is packed using a commercial packer, a custom loader, embedded in a shellcode, or else. In that scenario, dynamic detection is a valid option.</p>



<p>To this end, we can use a YARA rule for VirusTotal Livehunt to detect the specific commands executed by Stealc or the specific C2 communications, including:<br></p>



<figure class="wp-block-table"><table><tbody><tr><td><code>/c timeout /t 5<br>del /f /q "%SAMPLEPATH%"<br>del "%ProgramData%\*.dll""<br>/sqlite3.dll<br>.php</code></td></tr></tbody></table></figure>



<p>As we did above to pivot on this malware family, we can correlate these specific behaviours in a YARA rule using VirusTotal Livehunt. A YARA rule is shared in IoCs &amp; Technical Details.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-c2-servers">C2 servers</h2>



<p>Tracking the Stealc C2 servers can be done using the HTTP and HTML default responses which seem to be characteristic. Most of the scanned C2 servers responds an HTTP 200 status code with an HTML page containing a “404 Forbidden” Apache server on the port 80, as shown below:</p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-conclusion">Conclusion</h2>



<p>Stealc is <strong>another fully featured infostealer sold as a MaaS</strong> which emerged on underground forums in early 2023. <em>Plymouth</em> drew on the today’s trendy infostealers on the market (Vidar, <a href="https://blog.sekoia.io/raccoon-stealer-v2-part-1-the-return-of-the-dead/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">Raccoon</a>, Redline and <a href="https://blog.sekoia.io/mars-a-red-hot-information-stealer/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">Mars</a>) to develop a malware that quickly became popular among Russian-speaking cybercriminals.</p>



<p>Since customers of the Stealc MaaS own a build of its administration panel to host the stealer C2 server and generate stealer samples themselves, it is <strong>likely that the build will leak into the underground communities</strong> in the medium term. For that matter Sekoia.io further assess the <strong><em>Plymouth</em> business possibly will not be viable over several years</strong>, as Vidar or Raccoon projects are. However, it is <strong>likely that a cracked version of the Stealc build may be released</strong> in the future which may be used for many years to come.</p>



<p>However, we expect that the <strong>Stealc infostealer will become widespread in the near term</strong>, as multiple threat actors add the malware to their arsenal while it is poorly monitored. Companies facing stealer compromise need to be aware of this malware.</p>



<p>To provide our customers with actionable intelligence, Sekoia.io analysts will continue to monitor <a href="https://blog.sekoia.io/tag/stealer/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">emerging and prevalent infostealers</a>, including Stealc.</p>
