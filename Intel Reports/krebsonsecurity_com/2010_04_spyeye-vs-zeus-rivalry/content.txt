					<h1 class="entry-title">SpyEye vs. ZeuS Rivalry</h1>
				</div>

	<div id="primary" class="site-content">

					<article id="post-2092" class="post-2092 post type-post status-publish format-standard hentry category-sunshine category-web-fraud-2-0 tag-secureworks tag-spyeye tag-symantec tag-vmprotect tag-zeus">

	<div class="btm-wrap">
	<div class="below-title-meta">
		<div class="adt">
			
			
				
				<span class="date updated">April 1, 2010</span>
					
        </div>
		<div class="adt-comment">
		<span><a class="link-comments" href="https://krebsonsecurity.com/2010/04/spyeye-vs-zeus-rivalry/#comments">20 Comments</a></span>

							<div class="entry-content">
												<p>It&#8217;s common for malware writers to taunt one another with petty insults nested within their respective creations. Competing crime groups also often seek to wrest infected machines from one another. A very public turf war between those responsible for maintaining the <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Netsky_%28computer_worm%29" target="_blank">Netsky</a> and <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bagle_%28computer_worm%29" target="_blank">Bagle</a> worms back in 2005, for example, caused a substantial increase in the volume of threats generated by both gangs.</p>
<p>The latest rivalry appears to be budding between the authors of the <a href="http://www.krebsonsecurity.com/?s=zeus&amp;x=0&amp;y=0" target="_blank">Zeus Trojan</a> &#8212; a crime kit used by a large number of cyber thieves &#8212; and &#8220;SpyEye,&#8221; a relatively new kit on the block that is taking every opportunity to jeer at, undercut and otherwise siphon market share from the mighty Zeus.</p>
<p><strong>Symantec</strong> alluded to this in <a href="http://www.symantec.com/connect/blogs/spyeye-bot-versus-zeus-bot" target="_blank">a February blog post</a> that highlighted a key selling point of the SpyEye crimeware kit:  If the malware created with SpyEye lands on a computer that is already infected with Zeus, it will hijack and/or remove the Zeus infection.</p>
<p><a href="https://www.krebsonsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2010/04/spyeyeshot.jpg"><img decoding="async" class="alignright size-medium wp-image-2210" title="spyeyeshot" src="https://www.krebsonsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2010/04/spyeyeshot-300x145.jpg" alt="" width="300" height="145" srcset="https://krebsonsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2010/04/spyeyeshot-300x145.jpg 300w, https://krebsonsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2010/04/spyeyeshot-1024x497.jpg 1024w, https://krebsonsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2010/04/spyeyeshot.jpg 1152w" sizes="(max-width: 300px) 100vw, 300px" /></a>Now, just a few months later, the SpyEye author is releasing a new update (v. 1.1) that he claims includes the ability to inject content into <strong>Firefox</strong> and <strong>Internet Explorer</strong> browsers, just as Zeus does (this screen shot shows the result of a demo configuration file on the left, which instructs the malware to inject SpyEye and &#8220;Zeuskiller&#8221;  banner ads into a live Bank of America Web site). It is precisely this injection ability that allows thieves using Zeus to <a href="http://www.krebsonsecurity.com/2010/03/crooks-crank-up-volume-of-e-banking-attacks/" target="_blank">defeat</a> the <a href="http://www.krebsonsecurity.com/2010/02/comerica-phish-foiled-2-factor-protection/" target="_blank">security tokens</a> that many banks require commercial customers to use for online banking.</p>
<p>The new version comes as the Zeus author is pushing out his own updates (v. 1.4), along with a hefty price tag hike. The old Zeus kit started at around $4,000, while the base price of the newer version is double that. According to <a href="http://www.secureworks.com/research/threats/zeus/" target="_blank">research</a> from Atlanta-based security firm <strong>SecureWorks</strong>, Zeus plug-ins that offer additional functionality raise the price even more. For example:</p>
<p><span id="more-2092"></span></p>
<p>-Windows7/Vista compatibility module &#8211; $2,000<br />
-Backconnect module (lets criminals connect back to victim and make bank transactions through that PC) &#8211; $1,500<br />
-Firefox form grabbing (copies out any data entered into a form field, such as a user name and password) &#8211; $2,000<br />
-Jabber notification (a form of instant message) &#8211; $500<br />
-FTP clients saved credentials grabbing module &#8211; $2,000<br />
-VNC module &#8212; $10,000 (like GoToMyPC for the bad guys, reportedly no longer being sold/supported)</p>
<p>The SpyEye author declined to be interviewed for this story. But it&#8217;s clear from his Flash banner ads reproduced here that he plans to keep up the public relations campaign against Zeus, with a focus on the relatively low price: SpyEye costs just $500 (although the new Firefox injection tool runs an extra $1,000).</p>

<p>SecureWorks has noted that the latest versions of Zeus include anti-piracy technology that uses a hardware-based licensing system that can only be run on one computer. &#8220;Once you run it, you get a code  from the  specific computer, and then the author gives you a key just for that   computer,&#8221; SecureWorks wrote. &#8220;This is the first time we have  seen this level of control  for malware.&#8221;</p>
<p>Not to be outdone, the SpyEye author now claims his malware builder also includes a hardware lock, using <strong>VMProtect</strong>, a Russian commercial software protection package.</p>

<p>It got so bad even the help desk people in the state capitol couldn&#8217;t use it on the whole enterprise. Nobody had the skill to configure it to meet HIPAA regulations I suspect. Or at least that was the way our CIO interpreted it. </p>
<p>I&#8217;d say we had a pretty smart CIO!</p>
