												<div class="c-wysiwyg">
													<p>Angry Likho (referred to as Sticky Werewolf by some vendors) is an APT group we&#8217;ve been monitoring since 2023. It bears a strong resemblance to Awaken Likho, <a href="https://securelist.com/awaken-likho-apt-new-implant-campaign/114101/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">which we&#8217;ve analyzed before</a>, so we classified it within the Likho malicious activity cluster. However, <a href="https://securelist.ru/angry-likho-another-campaign-with-darktrack-rat/111139/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Angry Likho&#8217;s attacks tend to be targeted</a>, with a more compact infrastructure, a limited range of implants, and a focus on employees of large organizations, including government agencies and their contractors. Given that the bait files are written in fluent Russian, we infer that the attackers are likely native Russian speakers.</p>
<p>We&#8217;ve identified hundreds of victims of this attack in Russia, several in Belarus, and additional incidents in other countries. We believe that the attackers are primarily targeting organizations in Russia and Belarus, while the other victims were incidental—perhaps researchers using sandbox environments or exit nodes of Tor and VPN networks.</p>
<p>At the beginning of 2024, several cybersecurity vendors published reports on Angry Likho. However, in June, we detected new attacks from this group, and in January 2025, we identified malicious payloads confirming their continued activity at the moment of our research.</p>
<h2 id="technical-details">Technical details</h2>
<h3 id="initial-attack-vector">Initial attack vector</h3>
<p>The initial attack vector used by Angry Likho consists of standardized spear-phishing emails with various attachments. Below is an example of such an email containing a malicious RAR archive.</p>

<p>The content of this document is almost identical to the body of the phishing email.</p>
<p>This example illustrates how the attackers gain access to victims&#8217; systems. All these emails (and others like them in our collection) date back to April 2024. We observed no further activity from this group until we discovered an unusual implant, described below. Based on our telemetry, the attackers operate periodically, pausing their activities for a while before resuming with slightly modified techniques.</p>
<h3 id="previously-unknown-angry-likho-implant">Previously unknown Angry Likho implant</h3>
<p>In June 2024, we discovered a very interesting implant associated with this APT. The implant was distributed under the name FrameworkSurvivor.exe from the following URL:</p>
<p><strong>hxxps://testdomain123123[.]shop/FrameworkSurvivor.exe</strong></p>
<p>This implant was created using the legitimate open-source installer, Nullsoft Scriptable Install System, and functions as a self-extracting archive (SFX). We&#8217;ve previously observed this technique in multiple <a href="https://securelist.com/awaken-likho-apt-new-implant-campaign/114101/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Awaken Likho</a> campaigns.</p>
<p>Below are the contents of the archive, opened using the 7-Zip archiver.</p>
<div id="attachment_115685" style="width: 730px" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><a href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2025/02/21080127/angry-likho-3.png" class="magnificImage"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" aria-describedby="caption-attachment-115685" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2025/02/21080127/angry-likho-3.png" alt="Contents of the malicious SFX archive" width="720" height="761" class="size-full wp-image-115685" srcset="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2025/02/21080127/angry-likho-3.png 720w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2025/02/21080127/angry-likho-3-284x300.png 284w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2025/02/21080127/angry-likho-3-331x350.png 331w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2025/02/21080127/angry-likho-3-265x280.png 265w" sizes="auto, (max-width: 720px) 100vw, 720px" /></a><p id="caption-attachment-115685" class="wp-caption-text">Contents of the malicious SFX archive</p></div>
<p>The archive contains a single folder, $INTERNET_CACHE, filled with many files without extensions.</p>

<p>The Helping.cmd script launches a legitimate AutoIt interpreter (Child.pif) with the file i.a3x as a parameter. The i.a3x file contains a compiled AU3 script. With that in mind, we can assume that this script implements the core logic of the malicious implant.</p>
<h4 id="au3-script">AU3 script</h4>
<p>To recover the original AU3 file used when creating the i.a3x file, we created a dummy executable with a basic AutoIt script, swapped its content with i.a3x, and used a specialized tool to extract the original AU3 script.<br />
We ended up with the original AU3 file:</p>
<div id="attachment_115676" style="width: 1034px" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><a href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2025/02/20113359/angry-likho9.png" class="magnificImage"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" aria-describedby="caption-attachment-115676" class="size-large wp-image-115676" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2025/02/20113359/angry-likho9-1024x340.png" alt="Restored AU3 script" width="1024" height="340" srcset="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2025/02/20113359/angry-likho9-1024x340.png 1024w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2025/02/20113359/angry-likho9-300x99.png 300w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2025/02/20113359/angry-likho9-768x255.png 768w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2025/02/20113359/angry-likho9-1536x509.png 1536w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2025/02/20113359/angry-likho9-1055x350.png 1055w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2025/02/20113359/angry-likho9-740x245.png 740w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2025/02/20113359/angry-likho9-844x280.png 844w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2025/02/20113359/angry-likho9-800x265.png 800w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2025/02/20113359/angry-likho9.png 1728w" sizes="auto, (max-width: 1024px) 100vw, 1024px" /></a><p id="caption-attachment-115676" class="wp-caption-text">Restored AU3 script</p></div>
<p>The script is heavily obfuscated, with all strings encrypted. After deobfuscating and decrypting the code, we analyzed it. The script begins with a few verification procedures:</p>
<div id="attachment_115677" style="width: 1034px" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><a href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2025/02/20113440/angry-likho10.png" class="magnificImage"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" aria-describedby="caption-attachment-115677" class="size-large wp-image-115677" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2025/02/20113440/angry-likho10-1024x196.png" alt="The AU3 script checks the environment" width="1024" height="196" srcset="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2025/02/20113440/angry-likho10-1024x196.png 1024w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2025/02/20113440/angry-likho10-300x58.png 300w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2025/02/20113440/angry-likho10-768x147.png 768w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2025/02/20113440/angry-likho10-740x142.png 740w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2025/02/20113440/angry-likho10-800x153.png 800w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2025/02/20113440/angry-likho10.png 1345w" sizes="auto, (max-width: 1024px) 100vw, 1024px" /></a><p id="caption-attachment-115677" class="wp-caption-text">The AU3 script checks the environment</p></div>
<p>The script checks for artifacts associated with emulators and research environments of security vendors. If a match is found, it either terminates or executes with a 10,000 ms delay to evade detection.</p>
<p>Interestingly, we&#8217;ve seen similar checks in the Awaken Likho implants. This suggests that the attackers behind these two campaigns share the same technology or are the same group using different tools for different targets and tasks.</p>
<p>The script next sets an error-handling mode by calling SetErrorMode() from the kernel32.dll with the flags SEM_NOALIGNMENTFAULTEXCEPT, SEM_NOGPFAULTERRORBOX, and SEM_NOOPENFILEERRORBOX, thus hiding system error messages and reports. If this call fails, the script terminates.</p>
<p>Afterward, the script deletes itself from disk by calling FileDelete(&#8220;i&#8221;) and generates a large text block, as shown below.</p>
<div id="attachment_115678" style="width: 656px" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><a href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2025/02/20113552/angry-likho11.png" class="magnificImage"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" aria-describedby="caption-attachment-115678" class="size-full wp-image-115678" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2025/02/20113552/angry-likho11.png" alt="Code for generating &quot;shellcode&quot;" width="646" height="552" srcset="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2025/02/20113552/angry-likho11.png 646w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2025/02/20113552/angry-likho11-300x256.png 300w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2025/02/20113552/angry-likho11-410x350.png 410w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2025/02/20113552/angry-likho11-328x280.png 328w" sizes="auto, (max-width: 646px) 100vw, 646px" /></a><p id="caption-attachment-115678" class="wp-caption-text">Code for generating &#8220;shellcode&#8221;</p></div>
<p>This block is presumably shellcode that will be loaded into memory and executed. However, it is also packed and encrypted. Once unpacked and decrypted, the AU3 script attempts to inject the malicious payload into the legitimate AutoIt process.</p>
<div id="attachment_115679" style="width: 1034px" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><a href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2025/02/20113643/angry-likho12.png" class="magnificImage"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" aria-describedby="caption-attachment-115679" class="size-large wp-image-115679" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2025/02/20113643/angry-likho12-1024x51.png" alt="Final activity of the AU3 script" width="1024" height="51" srcset="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2025/02/20113643/angry-likho12-1024x51.png 1024w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2025/02/20113643/angry-likho12-300x15.png 300w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2025/02/20113643/angry-likho12-768x38.png 768w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2025/02/20113643/angry-likho12-740x37.png 740w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2025/02/20113643/angry-likho12-800x40.png 800w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2025/02/20113643/angry-likho12.png 1516w" sizes="auto, (max-width: 1024px) 100vw, 1024px" /></a><p id="caption-attachment-115679" class="wp-caption-text">Final activity of the AU3 script</p></div>
<h4 id="main-payload">Main payload</h4>
<p>To obtain the shellcode, we saved a dump of the decrypted and unpacked payload once the AU3 malicious script had fully processed it. After removing unnecessary bytes from the dump, we recovered the original payload of the attack. It turned out to be not shellcode but a full-fledged MZ PE executable file.</p>
<div id="attachment_115691" style="width: 1034px" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><a href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2025/02/21103409/angry-likho-13-1.png" class="magnificImage"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" aria-describedby="caption-attachment-115691" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2025/02/21103409/angry-likho-13-1-1024x223.png" alt="The decrypted and unpacked payload—an MZ PE file" width="1024" height="223" class="size-large wp-image-115691" srcset="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2025/02/21103409/angry-likho-13-1-1024x223.png 1024w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2025/02/21103409/angry-likho-13-1-300x65.png 300w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2025/02/21103409/angry-likho-13-1-768x167.png 768w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2025/02/21103409/angry-likho-13-1-740x161.png 740w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2025/02/21103409/angry-likho-13-1-800x174.png 800w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2025/02/21103409/angry-likho-13-1.png 1126w" sizes="auto, (max-width: 1024px) 100vw, 1024px" /></a><p id="caption-attachment-115691" class="wp-caption-text">The decrypted and unpacked payload—an MZ PE file</p></div>
<p>Our products detect this payload with the following verdicts:</p>
<ul>
<li>HEUR:Trojan.MSIL.Agent.pef</li>
<li>HEUR:Trojan.Win32.Generic</li>
</ul>
<p>We examined this payload and concluded that it is the Lumma Trojan stealer (Trojan-PSW.Win32.Lumma).</p>
<p>The Lumma stealer gathers system and installed software information from the compromised devices, as well as sensitive data such as cookies, usernames, passwords, banking card numbers, and connection logs. It also steals data from 11 browsers, including Chrome, Chromium, Edge, Kometa, Vivaldi, Brave, Opera Stable, Opera GX Stable, Opera Neon, Mozilla Firefox and Waterfox, as well as cryptocurrency wallets such as Binance and Ethereum. Additionally, it exfiltrates data from cryptowallet browser extensions (MetaMask) and authenticators (Authenticator), along with information from applications such as the remote access software AnyDesk and the password manager KeePass.</p>

<h2 id="new-activity">New activity</h2>
<p>We&#8217;ve been tracking the attacks of this campaign since June 2024. However, in January 2025, the attackers showed a new surge in activity, as <a href="https://habr.com/ru/companies/F6/news/873762/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">reported</a> by our colleagues from F6 (previously known as F.A.C.C.T.). We analyzed the indicators of compromise they published and identified signs of a potential new wave of attacks, likely in preparation since at least January 16, 2025:</p>
<div id="attachment_115681" style="width: 1034px" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><a href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2025/02/20113920/angry-likho14.png" class="magnificImage"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" aria-describedby="caption-attachment-115681" class="size-large wp-image-115681" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2025/02/20113920/angry-likho14-1024x195.png" alt="Files found in Angry Likho's payload repositories" width="1024" height="195" srcset="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2025/02/20113920/angry-likho14-1024x195.png 1024w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2025/02/20113920/angry-likho14-300x57.png 300w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2025/02/20113920/angry-likho14-768x146.png 768w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2025/02/20113920/angry-likho14-1536x293.png 1536w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2025/02/20113920/angry-likho14-740x141.png 740w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2025/02/20113920/angry-likho14-1468x280.png 1468w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2025/02/20113920/angry-likho14-800x153.png 800w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2025/02/20113920/angry-likho14.png 1762w" sizes="auto, (max-width: 1024px) 100vw, 1024px" /></a><p id="caption-attachment-115681" class="wp-caption-text">Files found in Angry Likho&#8217;s payload repositories</p></div>
<p>We managed to download malicious files hosted in repositories seen in the January Angry Likho attack while they were still accessible. Analysis of the files test.jpg and test2.jpg revealed that they contained the same .NET-based payload, encoded using Base64. Last year, we <a href="https://securelist.ru/angry-likho-another-campaign-with-darktrack-rat/111139/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">documented</a> Angry Likho attacks that used image files containing malicious code. Moreover, the filenames match those of the samples we recently discovered.</p>
<p>This further confirms that the Angry Likho group, responsible for these attacks, remains an active threat. We are continuing to monitor this threat and providing up-to-date <a href="https://www.kaspersky.com/enterprise-security/threat-intelligence?icid=gl_securelist_acq_ona_smm__onl_b2b_securelist_lnk_sm-team_______a0eb1db6ecb23c0d" target="_blank" rel="noopener">cyber intelligence data</a> about it and the TTPs used by the group.</p>
<h2 id="victims">Victims</h2>
<p>At the time of our investigation, our telemetry data showed hundreds of victims in Russia and several in Belarus. Most of the SFX archives had filenames and bait documents in Russian, thematically linked to government institutions in Russia. These institutions and their contractors are the primary targets of this campaign.</p>
<h2 id="attribution">Attribution</h2>
<p>We attribute this campaign to the APT group Angry Likho with a high degree of confidence. It shares certain similarities with findings from our colleagues at <a href="https://bi.zone/expertise/blog/shpiony-sticky-werewolf-atakuyut-gosudarstvennye-organizatsii-rossii-i-belarusi/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">BI.ZONE</a> and <a href="https://habr.com/ru/companies/F6/news/873762/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">F6</a>, as well as previous attacks by the group:</p>
<ol>
<li>The same initial implant structure (an archive with similar contents, sent in an email).</li>
<li>Similar bait documents with the same naming patterns and themes, mostly written in Russian.</li>
<li>Command files and AutoIt scripts used to install the implant are obfuscated similarly. Newer versions contain more sophisticated installation scripts, with extra layers of obfuscation to complicate analysis.</li>
<li>The implant described in this report contains a known payload—the Lumma stealer (Trojan-PSW.Win32.Lumma). We have not previously seen this tool used in Angry Likho campaigns, but earlier attacks showed similar data exfiltration tactics, suggesting the group is still targeting cryptowallet files and user credentials.</li>
</ol>
<h2 id="conclusion">Conclusion</h2>
<p>We are continuing to monitor the activity of the Angry Likho APT, which targets Russian organizations. The group&#8217;s latest attacks use the Lumma stealer, which collects a vast amount of data from infected devices, including browser-stored banking details and cryptowallet files. As before, the complex infection chain was contained in a self-extracting archive distributed via email. We believe that the attackers crafted spear-phishing emails tailored to specific users, attaching bait files designed to attract their interest. Additionally, we identified more malicious samples linked to this campaign based on common command servers and repositories.<br />
Let&#8217;s sum up by highlighting the notable features of this campaign and other similar ones:</p>
<ol>
<li>The attack techniques remain relatively consistent over time, with only minor modifications. Despite this, the attackers are successfully achieving their objectives.</li>
<li>The attackers occasionally pause their activity, only to return with a new wave of attacks after a certain period.</li>
<li>The group relies on readily available malicious utilities obtained from darknet forums, rather than developing its own tools. The only work they do themselves is writing mechanisms of malware delivery to the victim&#8217;s device and crafting targeted phishing emails.</li>
</ol>

The campaigns in this article rely on phishing emails as the initial attack vector, highlighting the importance of <a href="https://www.kaspersky.com/small-to-medium-business-security/security-awareness-platform?icid=gl_securelist_acq_ona_smm__onl_b2b_securelist_lnk_sm-team___kasap____ffbd8ff4936461b3" target="_blank" rel="noopener">regular employee training and awareness programs</a> for corporate security.</p>
