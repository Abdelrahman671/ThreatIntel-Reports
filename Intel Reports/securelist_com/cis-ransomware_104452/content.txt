													<h3 id="introduction">Introduction</h3>
<p>These days, when speaking of cyberthreats, most people have in mind ransomware, specifically cryptomalware. In 2020–2021, with the outbreak of the pandemic and the emergence of several major cybercriminal groups (Maze, REvil, Conti, DarkSide, Avaddon), an entire <a href="https://securelist.com/ransomware-world-in-2021/102169/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">criminal ecosystem</a> took shape, leading to a mounting worldwide wave of attacks on large organizations with pockets deep enough to pay a ransom in the hundreds of thousands, even millions, of US dollars.</p>
<p>This year, after a series of high-profile ransomware incidents, such as the attacks on Colonial Pipeline (the operator of the largest fuel pipeline in the US), JBS and Kaseya, and the heightened scrutiny from the US and other authorities that followed, the ransomware market has undergone some major changes: some groups have shut up shop, others have rebranded.</p>
<p>Most of the groups you might read about in the news today tend to operate outside the Commonwealth of Independent States (CIS). That said, companies in this region still cannot relax, since they are the target of dozens of lesser-known groups.</p>
<p>This roundup spotlights the ransomware Trojan families that most actively attacked businesses in the CIS in H1 2021, and their technical characteristics.</p>

<h3 id="ransomware-families-at-a-glance">Ransomware families at a glance</h3>
<h5 id="bigbobross-thedmr">BigBobRoss/TheDMR</h5>
<p>This ransomware became active at the back end of 2018 and remains current. According to our data, its main vector of distribution is <a href="https://encyclopedia.kaspersky.com/glossary/brute-force/?utm_source=securelist&amp;utm_medium=blog&amp;utm_campaign=termin-explanation" target="_blank" rel="noopener">cracking RDP passwords</a>.</p>
<p>When launched, BigBobRoss shows the operator technical information, including the key for subsequent file decryption. The malware also sends a message with this information via Telegram.</p>

<h5 id="crysis-dharma">Crysis/Dharma</h5>
<p>Crysis is an old piece of cryptomalware known since 2016. It is known to be <a href="https://securelist.com/ksb-story-of-the-year-2017/83290/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">deactivated and then revived</a>. Currently, it is still active. The Trojan&#8217;s code has remained unchanged for several years, and today it is distributed through a <a href="https://encyclopedia.kaspersky.com/glossary/ransomware-as-a-service-raas/?utm_source=securelist&amp;utm_medium=blog&amp;utm_campaign=termin-explanation" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Ransomware-as-a-Service</a> (RaaS) affiliate program.</p>
<p>Crysis is written in C/C ++ and compiled in MS Visual Studio. The malware encrypts files using the AES-256 algorithm in CBC mode. Upon launch, the Trojan generates a 256-bit AES key that is encrypted using the RSA-1024 algorithm, with the attacker&#8217;s public key contained in the Trojan&#8217;s body.</p>
<p>Each file is encrypted using the aforementioned AES key, as well as the freshly generated 128-bit initialization vector (IV). Besides the encrypted content, the encrypted file stores the IV, the RSA-encrypted AES key, and auxiliary information, including the attacker&#8217;s label (a string value), the SHA1 hash of the used RSA public key, the original file name, the encryption type (the part of the file to be encrypted is chosen differently for small and large files) and the checksum.</p>
<p style="text-align: center"><a href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142200/CIS_ransomware_07.png" class="magnificImage"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="aligncenter size-large wp-image-104466" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142200/CIS_ransomware_07-1024x812.png" alt="Crysis ransom note" width="1024" height="812" srcset="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142200/CIS_ransomware_07-1024x812.png 1024w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142200/CIS_ransomware_07-300x238.png 300w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142200/CIS_ransomware_07-768x609.png 768w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142200/CIS_ransomware_07-221x175.png 221w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142200/CIS_ransomware_07-370x293.png 370w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142200/CIS_ransomware_07-353x280.png 353w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142200/CIS_ransomware_07-800x634.png 800w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142200/CIS_ransomware_07.png 1124w" sizes="(max-width: 1024px) 100vw, 1024px" /></a></p>
<p style="text-align: center"><strong><em>Crysis ransom note</em></strong></p>
<p>The typical Crysis attack vector is unauthorized RDP access. The attacker cracks the credentials (through a dictionary/brute-force attack or ready lists bought from other cybercriminals), connects remotely to the victim&#8217;s computer, and runs the Trojan manually.</p>
<h5 id="phobos-eking">Phobos/Eking</h5>
<p>This ransomware has been around since 2017. At the conceptual level (code structure, approaches used by the developers), Phobos is similar to Crysis in many ways. This suggests that either the Trojans share the same developer, or the authors of Phobos are familiar with how Crysis works. However, we found no direct borrowing of code; in other words, these are different families of Trojans assembled from different sources.</p>
<p>Like most modern ransomware, Phobos is distributed through a RaaS affiliate program. The main vector of infection is unauthorized RDP access.</p>
<p>Phobos is written in C/C++ and compiled in MS Visual Studio. It uses the AES-256-CBC algorithm to encrypt the victim&#8217;s files, while the AES key is encrypted using the RSA-1024 public key contained in the body of the malware.</p>

<h5 id="cryakl-crylock">Cryakl/CryLock</h5>
<p>Cryakl is probably the oldest ransomware featured in this post. The first version was detected back in April 2014. However, it seems that in modern versions of this Trojan, not a single line of code is left over from that time. Cryakl has been rewritten many times, and changes are introduced with each new version, often significant ones.</p>
<p>It is distributed through an affiliate program. Currently, its most common attack vector is via RDP. For the attacker&#8217;s convenience, the Trojan supports a graphical interface. The operator configures the necessary settings manually in the program window.</p>
<p style="text-align: center"><a href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142255/CIS_ransomware_09.png" class="magnificImage"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="aligncenter size-large wp-image-104468" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142255/CIS_ransomware_09-1024x695.png" alt="Cryakl settings window" width="1024" height="695" srcset="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142255/CIS_ransomware_09-1024x695.png 1024w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142255/CIS_ransomware_09-300x204.png 300w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142255/CIS_ransomware_09-768x521.png 768w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142255/CIS_ransomware_09-258x175.png 258w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142255/CIS_ransomware_09-370x251.png 370w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142255/CIS_ransomware_09-413x280.png 413w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142255/CIS_ransomware_09-800x543.png 800w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142255/CIS_ransomware_09.png 1067w" sizes="(max-width: 1024px) 100vw, 1024px" /></a></p>
<p style="text-align: center"><strong><em>Cryakl settings window</em></strong></p>
<p>Cryakl is written in Delphi. The modern version of Cryakl uses a custom symmetric cipher to encrypt the victim&#8217;s files, and the RSA algorithm to encrypt the key.</p>
<p>An interesting feature of the current versions of Cryakl, not seen in other ransomware, is advanced processing of archive file formats.</p>
<p>Archives can be large, and encrypting them in their entirety takes a long time. And if only an arbitrary piece of a file is encrypted, it is possible to recover some of the content without decryption.</p>
<p>Cryakl features specialized procedures for handling the ZIP, 7z, TAR, CAB and RAR (old versions and RAR5) formats. It parses each of these formats and encrypts only the critical parts of the archive, delivering high performance and preventing data recovery without decryption.</p>

<h5 id="cryptconsole">CryptConsole</h5>
<p>CryptConsole was first spotted in January 2017 and is still encountered today. It is written in C# and uses .NET libraries for encryption. The main vector of distribution is cracking RDP passwords.</p>
<p style="text-align: center"><a href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142401/CIS_ransomware_12.png" class="magnificImage"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-104471" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142401/CIS_ransomware_12.png" alt="CryptConsole note" width="562" height="292" srcset="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142401/CIS_ransomware_12.png 562w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142401/CIS_ransomware_12-300x156.png 300w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142401/CIS_ransomware_12-337x175.png 337w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142401/CIS_ransomware_12-370x192.png 370w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142401/CIS_ransomware_12-539x280.png 539w" sizes="(max-width: 562px) 100vw, 562px" /></a></p>
<p style="text-align: center"><strong><em>CryptConsole note</em></strong></p>
<p>For encryption, two key and IV pairs are generated. These are written to a text file, along with a <em>size</em> parameter that reflects how much of the user&#8217;s file is to be encrypted, and placed on the desktop. The name of this text file is a 40-character string that matches the user&#8217;s unique identifier (Personal ID in the note). It is assumed that the malware operator, having gained access via RDP, runs the ransomware and saves this file for themselves, then deletes it from the victim&#8217;s device. It may prove possible to recover the file, but there is no guarantee. Interestingly, the size of the encrypted part of the file (the <em>size</em> parameter) is a random value in the range [5485760, 10485760].</p>
<p style="text-align: center"><a href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142420/CIS_ransomware_13.png" class="magnificImage"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-104472" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142420/CIS_ransomware_13.png" alt="File with keys left by the ransomware" width="567" height="214" srcset="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142420/CIS_ransomware_13.png 567w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142420/CIS_ransomware_13-300x113.png 300w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142420/CIS_ransomware_13-464x175.png 464w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142420/CIS_ransomware_13-370x140.png 370w" sizes="(max-width: 567px) 100vw, 567px" /></a></p>
<p style="text-align: center"><strong><em>File with keys left by the ransomware</em></strong></p>
<p>The encryption scheme is also curious. As mentioned above, the ransomware generates two random pairs: key+IV and key2+IV2. The file size is then compared to the previously generated random <em>size</em> value. If the file is greater than <em>size</em>, only the part of the file that is less than or equal to this value is encrypted, before which a buffer with <em>size</em> bytes of random data is written to the file.</p>
<p style="text-align: center"><a href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142433/CIS_ransomware_14.png" class="magnificImage"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="aligncenter size-large wp-image-104473" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142433/CIS_ransomware_14-1024x289.png" alt="Generating the key/IV pairs, ID, and size" width="1024" height="289" srcset="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142433/CIS_ransomware_14-1024x289.png 1024w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142433/CIS_ransomware_14-300x85.png 300w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142433/CIS_ransomware_14-768x217.png 768w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142433/CIS_ransomware_14-620x175.png 620w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142433/CIS_ransomware_14-370x104.png 370w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142433/CIS_ransomware_14-993x280.png 993w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142433/CIS_ransomware_14-800x226.png 800w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142433/CIS_ransomware_14.png 1248w" sizes="(max-width: 1024px) 100vw, 1024px" /></a></p>
<p style="text-align: center"><strong><em>Generating the key/IV pairs, ID, and size</em></strong></p>
<p>Encryption is performed using the symmetric AES algorithm. First, a <em>size</em> bytes chunk of the file is encrypted using key and IV, then the encrypted buffer is reversed and encrypted again, this time using key2 and IV2. This is how the dual encryption scheme works.</p>
<p style="text-align: center"><a href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142508/CIS_ransomware_15.png" class="magnificImage"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="aligncenter size-large wp-image-104474" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142508/CIS_ransomware_15-1024x131.png" alt="Dual encryption scheme for small files" width="1024" height="131" srcset="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142508/CIS_ransomware_15-1024x131.png 1024w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142508/CIS_ransomware_15-300x38.png 300w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142508/CIS_ransomware_15-768x98.png 768w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142508/CIS_ransomware_15-1030x132.png 1030w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142508/CIS_ransomware_15-370x47.png 370w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142508/CIS_ransomware_15-800x103.png 800w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142508/CIS_ransomware_15.png 1077w" sizes="(max-width: 1024px) 100vw, 1024px" /></a></p>
<p style="text-align: center"><strong><em>Dual encryption scheme for small files</em></strong></p>
<p>Large files, as mentioned before, are first filled with <em>size</em> bytes of arbitrary data. Only after that is the encrypted data appended.</p>
<p style="text-align: center"><a href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142528/CIS_ransomware_16.png" class="magnificImage"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="aligncenter size-large wp-image-104475" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142528/CIS_ransomware_16-1024x431.png" alt="Dual encryption scheme with arbitrary data writing" width="1024" height="431" srcset="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142528/CIS_ransomware_16-1024x431.png 1024w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142528/CIS_ransomware_16-300x126.png 300w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142528/CIS_ransomware_16-768x323.png 768w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142528/CIS_ransomware_16-416x175.png 416w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142528/CIS_ransomware_16-370x156.png 370w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142528/CIS_ransomware_16-665x280.png 665w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142528/CIS_ransomware_16-800x337.png 800w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142528/CIS_ransomware_16.png 1100w" sizes="(max-width: 1024px) 100vw, 1024px" /></a></p>
<p style="text-align: center"><strong><em>Dual encryption scheme with arbitrary data writing</em></strong></p>
<h5 id="fonix-xinof">Fonix/XINOF</h5>
<p>Fonix ransomware appeared in the summer of 2020. In January 2021, its creators announced the closure of the project and even published the master key, which we used to build a <a href="https://www.kaspersky.com/blog/fonix-decryptor/38646/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">decryptor</a> for victims of this Trojan.</p>
<p>However, that was not the end of the Fonix story. A few months later (in June 2021), we detected attacks by a new version of Fonix, which doesn&#8217;t use the old master key.</p>
<p>This version of Fonix mimics the Crysis and Phobos Trojans, using the same extensions and naming scheme for encrypted files.</p>
<p>If the files affected by earlier versions of Fonix had names like picture.jpg.Email=[actor@mail.tld]ID=[B49D8EF5].XINOF, now they are indistinguishable from the names of the files encrypted by Crysis (picture.jpg.id-523E8573.[actor@mail.tld].harma) or Phobos (picture.jpg.ID-70AB2875.[actor@mail.tld].eking).</p>
<p>The path to the project&#8217;s PDB file, preserved in the Trojan sample, likewise speaks of deliberate masking: the line &#8220;DharmaVersion&#8221; points unambiguously to the Dharma family (an alternative name for the Crysis ransomware).</p>
<p style="text-align: center"><a href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142554/CIS_ransomware_17.png" class="magnificImage"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-104476" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142554/CIS_ransomware_17.png" alt="PDB path" width="920" height="78" srcset="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142554/CIS_ransomware_17.png 920w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142554/CIS_ransomware_17-300x25.png 300w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142554/CIS_ransomware_17-768x65.png 768w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142554/CIS_ransomware_17-370x31.png 370w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142554/CIS_ransomware_17-800x68.png 800w" sizes="(max-width: 920px) 100vw, 920px" /></a></p>
<p style="text-align: center"><strong><em>PDB path</em></strong></p>
<p>Fonix is written in C++ using the CryptoPP library and compiled into a 64-bit executable file in MS Visual Studio. It is distributed using the RaaS scheme, with the main method of delivery to the victim&#8217;s system being via spam with a malicious attachment.</p>
<p>After each infection, the ransomware sends a notification to its operator via Telegram, which, incidentally, is nothing new and was first <a href="https://securelist.com/the-first-cryptor-to-exploit-telegram/76558/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">seen</a> several years ago.</p>
<p style="text-align: center"><a href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142616/CIS_ransomware_18.png" class="magnificImage"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="aligncenter size-large wp-image-104477" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142616/CIS_ransomware_18-1024x549.png" alt="Sending a notification in Telegram" width="1024" height="549" srcset="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142616/CIS_ransomware_18-1024x549.png 1024w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142616/CIS_ransomware_18-300x161.png 300w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142616/CIS_ransomware_18-768x412.png 768w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142616/CIS_ransomware_18-1536x824.png 1536w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142616/CIS_ransomware_18-326x175.png 326w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142616/CIS_ransomware_18-370x198.png 370w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142616/CIS_ransomware_18-522x280.png 522w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142616/CIS_ransomware_18-800x429.png 800w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142616/CIS_ransomware_18.png 1734w" sizes="(max-width: 1024px) 100vw, 1024px" /></a></p>
<p style="text-align: center"><strong><em>Sending a notification in Telegram</em></strong></p>
<p>Upon infecting the host, Fonix also checks the victim&#8217;s geolocation by IP and, if launched in Iran, ceases its activity without encryption.</p>
<p style="text-align: center"><a href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142646/CIS_ransomware_19.png" class="magnificImage"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="aligncenter size-large wp-image-104478" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142646/CIS_ransomware_19-1024x671.png" alt="Country check in Fonix" width="1024" height="671" srcset="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142646/CIS_ransomware_19-1024x671.png 1024w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142646/CIS_ransomware_19-300x197.png 300w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142646/CIS_ransomware_19-768x503.png 768w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142646/CIS_ransomware_19-267x175.png 267w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142646/CIS_ransomware_19-370x242.png 370w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142646/CIS_ransomware_19-427x280.png 427w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142646/CIS_ransomware_19-800x524.png 800w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142646/CIS_ransomware_19.png 1213w" sizes="(max-width: 1024px) 100vw, 1024px" /></a></p>
<p style="text-align: center"><strong><em>Country check in Fonix</em></strong></p>
<p>To encrypt user files, it uses the ChaCha or Salsa algorithms (depending on the file size). The ChaCha/Salsa keys are encrypted by RSA with a session public key generated when the Trojan is launched. The session private key is encrypted by RSA using the public master key contained in the body of the malware.</p>
<p>Early versions of Fonix had their own design of ransom notes.</p>
<p style="text-align: center"><a href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142710/CIS_ransomware_20.png" class="magnificImage"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="aligncenter size-large wp-image-104479" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142710/CIS_ransomware_20-1024x681.png" alt="Fonix ransom note (early version)" width="1024" height="681" srcset="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142710/CIS_ransomware_20-1024x681.png 1024w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142710/CIS_ransomware_20-300x200.png 300w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142710/CIS_ransomware_20-768x511.png 768w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142710/CIS_ransomware_20-1536x1022.png 1536w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142710/CIS_ransomware_20-263x175.png 263w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142710/CIS_ransomware_20-370x246.png 370w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142710/CIS_ransomware_20-421x280.png 421w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142710/CIS_ransomware_20-800x532.png 800w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142710/CIS_ransomware_20.png 1665w" sizes="(max-width: 1024px) 100vw, 1024px" /></a></p>
<p style="text-align: center"><strong><em>Fonix ransom note (early version)</em></strong></p>
<p>In modern samples, meanwhile, we see the look of some versions of Crysis&#8217; and Phobos&#8217; ransom notes being copied.</p>

<h5 id="limbozar-voidcrypt">Limbozar/VoidCrypt</h5>
<p>This ransomware appeared in mid-2019. Some versions of it are also known as Limbo, Legion, Odveta and Ouroboros. Limbozar is distributed through an affiliate program (RaaS). Currently, the main vector of distribution is unauthorized RDP access. Limbozar is written in C++, compiled in MS Visual Studio and uses the CryptoPP library to implement cryptographic functions.</p>
<p>The cryptographic scheme has changed several times throughout the family&#8217;s history. When launched, modern versions of Limbozar generate an RSA-2048 session key pair, followed by a 256-bit key and a 96-bit initialization vector for the AES algorithm in GCM mode. The private RSA session key is encrypted with the AES algorithm and saved locally. Next, the key+IV pair for AES is encrypted with one of the several public RSA master keys contained in the Trojan&#8217;s body, and is also saved to the local drive.</p>
<p>After this preparatory phase, Limbozar searches for the victim&#8217;s files and encrypts them with the AES-GCM algorithm, generating for each file a unique key+IV pair, which, in turn, it encrypts with the RSA session public key.</p>
<p>After encryption, the malware leaves the cybercriminals&#8217; demands in the Decrypt-info.txt files.</p>
<p style="text-align: center"><a href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142758/CIS_ransomware_22.png" class="magnificImage"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-104481" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142758/CIS_ransomware_22.png" alt="Limbozar ransom note" width="946" height="636" srcset="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142758/CIS_ransomware_22.png 946w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142758/CIS_ransomware_22-300x202.png 300w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142758/CIS_ransomware_22-768x516.png 768w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142758/CIS_ransomware_22-260x175.png 260w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142758/CIS_ransomware_22-370x249.png 370w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142758/CIS_ransomware_22-416x280.png 416w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142758/CIS_ransomware_22-800x538.png 800w" sizes="(max-width: 946px) 100vw, 946px" /></a></p>
<p style="text-align: center"><strong><em>Limbozar ransom note</em></strong></p>
<p>Upon full encryption, Limbozar also sends a notification about the new victim to its C&amp;C server using a POST request. To implement network communication, the SFML library (libsfml-network) is used.</p>
<p style="text-align: center"><a href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142821/CIS_ransomware_23.png" class="magnificImage"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-104482" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142821/CIS_ransomware_23.png" alt="Notification about a new Limbozar infection" width="914" height="645" srcset="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142821/CIS_ransomware_23.png 914w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142821/CIS_ransomware_23-300x212.png 300w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142821/CIS_ransomware_23-768x542.png 768w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142821/CIS_ransomware_23-248x175.png 248w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142821/CIS_ransomware_23-370x261.png 370w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142821/CIS_ransomware_23-397x280.png 397w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142821/CIS_ransomware_23-800x565.png 800w" sizes="(max-width: 914px) 100vw, 914px" /></a></p>
<p style="text-align: center"><strong><em>Notification about a new Limbozar infection</em></strong></p>
<h5 id="thanos-hakbit">Thanos/Hakbit</h5>
<p>Thanos became active in late April 2020, although information about it first appeared in January when it was presented as RaaS on a hacker forum. The ransomware is written in C#. According to our information, its main vector of distribution is cracking RDP passwords.</p>
<p style="text-align: center"><a href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142847/CIS_ransomware_24.png" class="magnificImage"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="aligncenter size-large wp-image-104483" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142847/CIS_ransomware_24-1024x659.png" alt="Desktop wallpaper of an infected machine displaying a ransom note" width="1024" height="659" srcset="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142847/CIS_ransomware_24-1024x659.png 1024w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142847/CIS_ransomware_24-300x193.png 300w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142847/CIS_ransomware_24-768x494.png 768w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142847/CIS_ransomware_24-272x175.png 272w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142847/CIS_ransomware_24-370x238.png 370w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142847/CIS_ransomware_24-435x280.png 435w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142847/CIS_ransomware_24-800x515.png 800w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142847/CIS_ransomware_24.png 1063w" sizes="(max-width: 1024px) 100vw, 1024px" /></a></p>
<p style="text-align: center"><strong><em>Desktop wallpaper of an infected machine displaying a ransom note</em></strong></p>
<p>Since the distribution model is RaaS, the ransomware is distributed through a builder, enabling the customization of the Trojan itself and a decryptor for it.</p>
<p>There are many different settings in the builder: both basic (extension of encrypted files, name and content of ransom note, payment address) and more advanced (code obfuscation, self-delete, disabling Windows Defender, bypassing the Antimalware Scan Interface (AMSI), unlocking files occupied by other processes, protecting the ransomware process, preventing sleep, execution delay, fast encryption mode for large files, setting extensions of the files to be encrypted, selecting a victim notification method). The leaked constructor can be found online. Most likely, it was uploaded by the operator who bought it. For protection, it features a built-in HWID check, suggesting it was assembled for the specific device of the operator.</p>
<p>The decryptor can decrypt files using the user ID, which is an RSA-encrypted key for a symmetric encryption algorithm (different versions have different symmetric algorithms).</p>
<p style="text-align: center"><a href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142914/CIS_ransomware_25.png" class="magnificImage"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-104484" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142914/CIS_ransomware_25.png" alt="Decryptor for Thanos" width="289" height="366" srcset="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142914/CIS_ransomware_25.png 289w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142914/CIS_ransomware_25-237x300.png 237w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142914/CIS_ransomware_25-138x175.png 138w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142914/CIS_ransomware_25-221x280.png 221w" sizes="(max-width: 289px) 100vw, 289px" /></a></p>
<p style="text-align: center"><strong><em>Decryptor for Thanos</em></strong></p>
<p>The ransomware can employ a range of encryption schemes. In various samples of the ransomware, we came across the following:</p>
<ul>
<li>One key for all files; Salsa20 encryption</li>
<li>Different keys for all files; Salsa20 encryption</li>
<li>One key for all files passed through PBKDF2 function; AES-256 CBC encryption</li>
<li>One key for all files passed through PBKDF2 function (1000 iterations for small files and 50,000 iterations for large (&gt;15 MB) files), then AES-256 CBC encryption</li>
</ul>
<p>An illustration of one of the encryption schemes (static key + PBKDF2 + AES-256 CBC) and the code obfuscation method are given below. The obfuscation is rather weak, which makes it possible to recover the original code.</p>
<p style="text-align: center"><a href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142938/CIS_ransomware_26.png" class="magnificImage"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="aligncenter size-large wp-image-104485" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142938/CIS_ransomware_26-1024x127.png" alt="One of the blocks of code used for encryption" width="1024" height="127" srcset="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142938/CIS_ransomware_26-1024x127.png 1024w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142938/CIS_ransomware_26-300x37.png 300w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142938/CIS_ransomware_26-768x95.png 768w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142938/CIS_ransomware_26-1536x191.png 1536w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142938/CIS_ransomware_26-1030x128.png 1030w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142938/CIS_ransomware_26-370x46.png 370w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142938/CIS_ransomware_26-1600x198.png 1600w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142938/CIS_ransomware_26-800x99.png 800w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06142938/CIS_ransomware_26.png 2048w" sizes="(max-width: 1024px) 100vw, 1024px" /></a></p>
<p style="text-align: center"><strong><em>One of the blocks of code used for encryption</em></strong></p>
<p>The ransom note does not differ much. As usual, the purpose is to leave contact details and intimidate the user.</p>
<p style="text-align: center"><a href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06143002/CIS_ransomware_27.png" class="magnificImage"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-104486" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06143002/CIS_ransomware_27.png" alt="Thanos ransom note" width="964" height="617" srcset="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06143002/CIS_ransomware_27.png 964w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06143002/CIS_ransomware_27-300x192.png 300w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06143002/CIS_ransomware_27-768x492.png 768w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06143002/CIS_ransomware_27-273x175.png 273w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06143002/CIS_ransomware_27-370x237.png 370w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06143002/CIS_ransomware_27-437x280.png 437w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06143002/CIS_ransomware_27-800x512.png 800w" sizes="(max-width: 964px) 100vw, 964px" /></a></p>
<p style="text-align: center"><strong><em>Thanos ransom note</em></strong></p>
<p>Thanos implements a rather flexible attack scheme, allowing the operator to independently select the ransomware&#8217;s features and generate it to suit their specific needs.</p>
<h5 id="xmrlocker">XMRLocker</h5>
<p>XMRLocker was first noticed in early August 2020. It is written in C# and uses .NET libraries for encryption.</p>
<p>Encryption is performed using a generated password of random length of 65–101 characters. A fixed alphabet, which includes English upper- and lower-case letters plus some special characters, is used to generate the password.</p>
<p style="text-align: center"><a href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06143127/CIS_ransomware_28.png" class="magnificImage"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="aligncenter size-large wp-image-104487" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06143127/CIS_ransomware_28-1024x512.png" alt="Password generation in XMRLocker" width="1024" height="512" srcset="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06143127/CIS_ransomware_28-1024x512.png 1024w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06143127/CIS_ransomware_28-300x150.png 300w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06143127/CIS_ransomware_28-768x384.png 768w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06143127/CIS_ransomware_28-350x175.png 350w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06143127/CIS_ransomware_28-370x185.png 370w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06143127/CIS_ransomware_28-560x280.png 560w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06143127/CIS_ransomware_28-800x400.png 800w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06143127/CIS_ransomware_28.png 1153w" sizes="(max-width: 1024px) 100vw, 1024px" /></a></p>
<p style="text-align: center"><strong><em>Password generation in XMRLocker</em></strong></p>
<p>Encryption uses the AES algorithm with a key length of 256 bits in CFB mode and with PKCS7 padding. The pre-generated password is passed through the PBKDF2 function with 50,000 iterations, and the result is converted to a key and IV for further encryption. PBKDF2 uses a 32-byte random salt, which gets written to the beginning of each file. A single key is generated for all files. It is saved in a text file named HWID, which is sent to the C&amp;C server hosted on Tor network and then deleted.</p>
<p style="text-align: center"><a href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06143157/CIS_ransomware_29.png" class="magnificImage"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="aligncenter size-large wp-image-104488" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06143157/CIS_ransomware_29-1024x252.png" alt="Encryption function" width="1024" height="252" srcset="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06143157/CIS_ransomware_29-1024x252.png 1024w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06143157/CIS_ransomware_29-300x74.png 300w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06143157/CIS_ransomware_29-768x189.png 768w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06143157/CIS_ransomware_29-712x175.png 712w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06143157/CIS_ransomware_29-370x91.png 370w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06143157/CIS_ransomware_29-800x197.png 800w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06143157/CIS_ransomware_29.png 1087w" sizes="(max-width: 1024px) 100vw, 1024px" /></a></p>
<p style="text-align: center"><strong><em>Encryption function</em></strong></p>
<p>After encryption, the machine is shut down. Upon next startup, the user is greeted with a mocking description of what has happened and the cybercriminals&#8217; details.</p>
<p style="text-align: center"><a href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06143210/CIS_ransomware_30.png" class="magnificImage"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-104489" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06143210/CIS_ransomware_30.png" alt="Message after startup" width="486" height="215" srcset="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06143210/CIS_ransomware_30.png 486w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06143210/CIS_ransomware_30-300x133.png 300w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06143210/CIS_ransomware_30-396x175.png 396w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06143210/CIS_ransomware_30-370x164.png 370w" sizes="(max-width: 486px) 100vw, 486px" /></a></p>
<p style="text-align: center"><strong><em>Message after startup</em></strong></p>
<p>The ransomware note, as usual, contains contact details and an ID. The only surprising element is the words &#8220;files encrypted with Base-64 algorithm,&#8221; since this is not an encryption algorithm and is not used at all by this ransomware.</p>
<p style="text-align: center"><a href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06143221/CIS_ransomware_31.png" class="magnificImage"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-104490" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06143221/CIS_ransomware_31.png" alt="Note left by the ransomware" width="909" height="365" srcset="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06143221/CIS_ransomware_31.png 909w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06143221/CIS_ransomware_31-300x120.png 300w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06143221/CIS_ransomware_31-768x308.png 768w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06143221/CIS_ransomware_31-436x175.png 436w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06143221/CIS_ransomware_31-370x149.png 370w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06143221/CIS_ransomware_31-697x280.png 697w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/10/06143221/CIS_ransomware_31-800x321.png 800w" sizes="(max-width: 909px) 100vw, 909px" /></a></p>
<p style="text-align: center"><strong><em>Note left by the ransomware</em></strong></p>
<h3 id="takeaways">Takeaways</h3>
<p>Both well-known and relatively new business-oriented ransomware is present in the CIS. Many of these threats are actively developing, and some, since being discovered, have been shut down only to reappear on the market. Cybercriminals use various encryption techniques, some of them quite curious, such as dual encryption in CryptConsole and archive processing in Cryakl.</p>
<p>Although there are different vectors of malware distribution, most of the current crop of ransomware threats targeting businesses in the CIS penetrate the victim&#8217;s network via RDP. To counter this, it is important to create strong passwords for domain accounts and change them regularly. It is also advised to block RDP access from the internet and use a VPN to connect to the corporate network instead.</p>
