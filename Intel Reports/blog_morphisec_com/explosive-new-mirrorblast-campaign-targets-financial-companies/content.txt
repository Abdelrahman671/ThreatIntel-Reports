<h2>Introduction</h2>
<p>Financial organizations are historically among the most targeted by threat actors. There are many reasons for this, not least of which is the trove of customer data the financial sector holds, as well as the funds to pay large sums of money to regain access to encrypted data.&nbsp;</p>
<p>The Morphisec Labs team has tracked a new version of a campaign targeting financial organizations. Dubbed “MirrorBlast” by <a href="https://twitter.com/ET_Labs/status/1441147196317974532?s=20">ET Labs</a>, the current attack campaign the Labs team has tracked began in early September. There was similar activity in April 2021 as well, but the current campaign began more recently.&nbsp;</p>
<p>The attack chain of the infection bears a similarity to the tactics, techniques, and procedures commonly used by the allegedly Russia-based threat group TA505. The similarities extend to the attack chain, the GetandGo functionality, the final payload, and similarities in the domain name pattern.&nbsp;</p>
<p><a href="https://attack.mitre.org/groups/G0092/" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">TA505</a> has been active since at least 2014 and, as far as analysts can ascertain, has a financial motivation for their actions. As a group, TA505 is most known for frequently changing the malware they use as well as driving global trends in malware distribution.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>
<p>In this blog post, we will examine the new <span style="font-weight: bold;">MirrorBlast phishing campaign</span>, from the initial delivery of a malicious Excel file to the end result of loading an additional payload.&nbsp;</p>

<h2>Technical introduction</h2>
<p>In September we observed a malspam campaign delivering Excel documents as an attachment. This campaign targets multiple sectors from Canada, the United States, Hong Kong, Europe, and more.</p>
<p>The attack chain starts with an email attachment document, but at a later stage, it changes to use the Google feedproxy URL with SharePoint and OneDrive lure, which poses as a file share request. These URLs lead to a compromised SharePoint or a fake OneDrive site that the attackers use to evade detection, in addition to a sign-in requirement (SharePoint) that helps to evade sandboxes.</p>

<h3>Excel Document</h3>
<p><span style="color: #000000;">The Excel document is weaponized with an extremely lightweight macro code.</span></p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><span style="color: #000000;"><img src="https://blog.morphisec.com/hs-fs/hubfs/FUD%20Excel%20doc.png?width=587&amp;height=128&amp;name=FUD%20Excel%20doc.png" alt="FUD Excel doc" width="587" height="128" loading="lazy" style="height: auto; max-width: 100%; width: 587px;" srcset="https://blog.morphisec.com/hs-fs/hubfs/FUD%20Excel%20doc.png?width=294&amp;height=64&amp;name=FUD%20Excel%20doc.png 294w, https://blog.morphisec.com/hs-fs/hubfs/FUD%20Excel%20doc.png?width=587&amp;height=128&amp;name=FUD%20Excel%20doc.png 587w, https://blog.morphisec.com/hs-fs/hubfs/FUD%20Excel%20doc.png?width=881&amp;height=192&amp;name=FUD%20Excel%20doc.png 881w, https://blog.morphisec.com/hs-fs/hubfs/FUD%20Excel%20doc.png?width=1174&amp;height=256&amp;name=FUD%20Excel%20doc.png 1174w, https://blog.morphisec.com/hs-fs/hubfs/FUD%20Excel%20doc.png?width=1468&amp;height=320&amp;name=FUD%20Excel%20doc.png 1468w, https://blog.morphisec.com/hs-fs/hubfs/FUD%20Excel%20doc.png?width=1761&amp;height=384&amp;name=FUD%20Excel%20doc.png 1761w" sizes="(max-width: 587px) 100vw, 587px"></span><span style="color: #000000;"><br></span><span style="font-size: 16px;"><strong><span style="color: #000000;">Figure 4: </span></strong><span style="color: #000000;">A totally FUD Excel document.</span></span></p>
<p><span style="color: #000000;">The macro code can be executed only on a 32-bit version of Office due to compatibility reasons with ActiveX objects (</span><a href="https://docs.microsoft.com/en-us/office/client-developer/shared/compatibility-between-the-32-bit-and-64-bit-versions-of-office#activex-control-and-com-add-in-compatibility"><span>ActiveX control compatibility</span></a><span style="color: #000000;">). The macro code performs anti sandboxing by checking if the following queries are true:</span></p>
<ul>
<li style="color: #000000;" aria-level="1"><span style="color: #000000;">Computer name is equal to the user domain.</span></li>
<li style="color: #000000;" aria-level="1"><span style="color: #000000;">Username is equal to </span><strong><span style="color: #000000;">admin </span></strong><span style="color: #000000;">or </span><strong><span style="color: #000000;">administrator</span></strong><span style="color: #000000;">.</span></li>
</ul>
<p><span style="color: #000000;">We have observed different variants of the document, in the first variants there wasn’t any anti-sandboxing and the macro code was hidden behind the </span><strong><span style="color: #000000;">Language </span></strong><span style="color: #000000;">and </span><strong><span style="color: #000000;">Code </span></strong><span style="color: #000000;">document information properties, later it moved to the sheet cells. Additionally, the code has been added one more obfuscation layer on top of the previous obfuscation</span></p>

<h3>MSI package</h3>
<p><span style="color: #000000;">We have observed two variants of the MSI installer: KiXtart and REBOL. Both variants are generated using the </span><strong><span style="color: #000000;">Windows Installer XML Toolset (WiX) version</span></strong><span style="color: #000000;"> - </span><strong><span style="color: #000000;">3.11.0.1528; </span></strong><span style="color: #000000;">once executed they drop two files into a random directory in ProgramData. One of them is the legitimate software language interpreter executable (KiXtart or REBOL) and the other is the malicious script.</span></p>

<h2>Conclusions</h2>
<p><span style="color: #000000;">TA505 is one of many financially motivated threat groups currently active in the marketplace. They are also one of the most creative, as they have a tendency to constantly shift the attacks they leverage to achieve their goals. This new cyberattack for MirrorBlast is no exception for TA505 or for other innovative threat groups.&nbsp;</span></p>
<p><span style="color: #000000;">If anything, the shift in the attack chain is a further indication that organizations can ill afford to take a defensive, reactive approach to their security. They must remain constantly vigilant, iterating on security procedures to ensure they are not caught off-guard when new TTPs are deployed to breach their defenses.&nbsp;</span></p>
<p><span style="color: #000000;">The ability of the <span style="font-weight: bold;">MirrorBlast attack</span> to have very low detections in VirusTotal is also indicative of the focus most groups have on evading detection-centric solutions. Yet again, it is clear that the market’s reliance on detection and response leaves them open to more attacks than it resolves. A new way forward is needed.</span></p>
