                                <div class="box"><p class="perex">Andromeda is one of the longest running and most prevalent malware families to have existed.</p>


                                    <span id="hs_cos_wrapper_post_body" class="hs_cos_wrapper hs_cos_wrapper_meta_field hs_cos_wrapper_type_rich_text" style="" data-hs-cos-general-type="meta_field" data-hs-cos-type="rich_text"><h1 style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-size: 14px;">Andromeda is one of the longest running and most prevalent malware families to have existed. Andromeda was first discovered in late 2011 and it probably evolved from ngrBot/DorkBot. Throughout its existence, the groups behind Andromeda have used various methods to spread the malware and infect users.</span></h1>
<p style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">We have seen Andromeda spread via spam email campaigns with infected files attached (doc, xls, pdf, zip.), through illegal download sites, </span><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Warez"><span style="font-weight: 400;">warez</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> (infected cracks, keygens, ..), or infecting users via other phishing campaigns.</span></p>
<h2 style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Infection vector</span></h2>
<p style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">In recent months, the authors have mainly focused on spreading Andromeda via exploit kits (Neutrino, Nuclear, Angler,..) located on compromised websites or advertisement services. These exploit kits are mainly found on a dubio</span>us sites (p0rn, warez, video streaming sites, share sites etc.) but occasionally appear on trusted sites as well.</p>
<p style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-weight: 400;"><img src="https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/infection_scheme_gamarue.png?width=640&amp;name=infection_scheme_gamarue.png" alt="infection_scheme_Andromeda.png" title="infection_scheme_Andromeda.png" data-constrained="true" style="width: 640px;" width="640" srcset="https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/infection_scheme_gamarue.png?width=320&amp;name=infection_scheme_gamarue.png 320w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/infection_scheme_gamarue.png?width=640&amp;name=infection_scheme_gamarue.png 640w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/infection_scheme_gamarue.png?width=960&amp;name=infection_scheme_gamarue.png 960w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/infection_scheme_gamarue.png?width=1280&amp;name=infection_scheme_gamarue.png 1280w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/infection_scheme_gamarue.png?width=1600&amp;name=infection_scheme_gamarue.png 1600w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/infection_scheme_gamarue.png?width=1920&amp;name=infection_scheme_gamarue.png 1920w" sizes="(max-width: 640px) 100vw, 640px"><!--more--></span></p>
<p style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Andromeda binary files are almost always stored on hacked websites, but we have also discovered files hosted on a few dedicated servers that only host malware. Not only have we seen Andromeda appear on hacked websites, but we have also seen its plugins being distributed on SourceForge.net, a repository that hosts 7zip, VLC player, OpenOffice, FileZilla and other popular open source&nbsp;projects.</span></p>
<h2 style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Andromeda’s core anatomy</span></h2>
<p style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">This analysis covers the latest variant of Andromeda samples, which began spreading since the beginning of this year. The authors have not made many changes to Andromeda’s core binary file, but &nbsp;they are constantly changing the PE packer/obfuscator in the top most layer. Andromeda uses various PE packers of different quality to avoid AV detections. Some packers also contain other anti-vm/emul/debug tricks. We’ve seen a packer very similar to Zbot (based on its source code), obfuscated Visual Basic and .NET binaries and even a few custom packers reminiscent of Dridex included in the Andromeda variant.</span></p>
<p style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Andromeda’s authors put a lot of effort into diversifying their portfolio of infection droppers and to disable, or at least complicate the sample submission and exchange between AV companies and their regular process used to scan and thoroughly analyze files. To achieve this, they update the custom packers daily and as a bonus, they bloat the binaries with more than 70 MB of garbage. This strategy can either significantly prolong the sample upload (on a slow connection) or cause an overflow of scan/submit limits of some antivirus scanning engines (or online scanning services respectively). On the other hand, this trick is suspicious and it can help to heuristically detect the file.</span></p>
<h2 style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Zbot-like packer in detail</span></h2>
<p style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Andromeda’s top-layer packer is interesting and deserves a closer look. The packer is very similar to that of Zbot, based on the source code. The encrypted payload is stored inside the “.rsrc” section as the “raw data”.</span></p>
<p style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-weight: 400;"><img src="https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/zbot-rsrc.png?width=461&amp;name=zbot-rsrc.png" alt="zbot-rsrc.png" title="zbot-rsrc.png" data-constrained="true" style="width: 461px;" width="461" srcset="https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/zbot-rsrc.png?width=231&amp;name=zbot-rsrc.png 231w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/zbot-rsrc.png?width=461&amp;name=zbot-rsrc.png 461w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/zbot-rsrc.png?width=692&amp;name=zbot-rsrc.png 692w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/zbot-rsrc.png?width=922&amp;name=zbot-rsrc.png 922w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/zbot-rsrc.png?width=1153&amp;name=zbot-rsrc.png 1153w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/zbot-rsrc.png?width=1383&amp;name=zbot-rsrc.png 1383w" sizes="(max-width: 461px) 100vw, 461px"></span></p>
<p style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">The Andromeda payload is twice encrypted with custom encryption and compressed by the RtlCompressBuffer API function with LZ compression (0x002 - COMPRESSION_FORMAT_LZNT1). The custom encryption uses random seed values and generic obfuscation with lots of SMC (self-modificated code) and junk instructions.</span></p>
<p style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">First payload custom encryption:</span></p>
<p style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-weight: 400;"><img src="https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/zbotpack-1layer.png?width=543&amp;height=637&amp;name=zbotpack-1layer.png" alt="zbotpack-1layer.png" title="zbotpack-1layer.png" width="543" height="637" srcset="https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/zbotpack-1layer.png?width=272&amp;height=319&amp;name=zbotpack-1layer.png 272w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/zbotpack-1layer.png?width=543&amp;height=637&amp;name=zbotpack-1layer.png 543w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/zbotpack-1layer.png?width=815&amp;height=956&amp;name=zbotpack-1layer.png 815w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/zbotpack-1layer.png?width=1086&amp;height=1274&amp;name=zbotpack-1layer.png 1086w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/zbotpack-1layer.png?width=1358&amp;height=1593&amp;name=zbotpack-1layer.png 1358w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/zbotpack-1layer.png?width=1629&amp;height=1911&amp;name=zbotpack-1layer.png 1629w" sizes="(max-width: 543px) 100vw, 543px"></span></p>
<p style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Second encryption:</span></p>
<strong><strong><img src="https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/zbotpack-2layer.png?width=413&amp;height=394&amp;name=zbotpack-2layer.png" alt="zbotpack-2layer.png" title="zbotpack-2layer.png" width="413" height="394" srcset="https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/zbotpack-2layer.png?width=207&amp;height=197&amp;name=zbotpack-2layer.png 207w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/zbotpack-2layer.png?width=413&amp;height=394&amp;name=zbotpack-2layer.png 413w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/zbotpack-2layer.png?width=620&amp;height=591&amp;name=zbotpack-2layer.png 620w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/zbotpack-2layer.png?width=826&amp;height=788&amp;name=zbotpack-2layer.png 826w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/zbotpack-2layer.png?width=1033&amp;height=985&amp;name=zbotpack-2layer.png 1033w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/zbotpack-2layer.png?width=1239&amp;height=1182&amp;name=zbotpack-2layer.png 1239w" sizes="(max-width: 413px) 100vw, 413px"><br></strong></strong>
<p style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">The decrypted data is then ready for a decompression via the significant RtlDecompressBuffer API function.</span></p>
<p style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-weight: 400;"><img src="https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/zbotpack-before-rtldecompress.png?width=640&amp;name=zbotpack-before-rtldecompress.png" alt="zbotpack-before-rtldecompress.png" title="zbotpack-before-rtldecompress.png" style="width: 640px;" width="640" srcset="https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/zbotpack-before-rtldecompress.png?width=320&amp;name=zbotpack-before-rtldecompress.png 320w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/zbotpack-before-rtldecompress.png?width=640&amp;name=zbotpack-before-rtldecompress.png 640w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/zbotpack-before-rtldecompress.png?width=960&amp;name=zbotpack-before-rtldecompress.png 960w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/zbotpack-before-rtldecompress.png?width=1280&amp;name=zbotpack-before-rtldecompress.png 1280w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/zbotpack-before-rtldecompress.png?width=1600&amp;name=zbotpack-before-rtldecompress.png 1600w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/zbotpack-before-rtldecompress.png?width=1920&amp;name=zbotpack-before-rtldecompress.png 1920w" sizes="(max-width: 640px) 100vw, 640px"></span></p>
<h2><span style="font-weight: 400;">Payload Loader</span></h2>
<p style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Under all of the obfuscated layers, we found a typical Andromeda payload loader binary. The entire loader is very minimalistic (~20kB) and includes the final malware payload in compressed (Aplib) and encrypted (RC4) form and hardcoded config structure.</span></p>
<h2 style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Loader config structure</span></h2>
<p style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">The structure is hardcoded right before the encrypted payload that is 0x28h (40) bytes long and it contains seven values: </span></p>
<ul style="text-align: left;">
<li style="font-weight: 400;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">RC4 key for payload decryption (first 16 bytes).</span></li>
<li style="font-weight: 400;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Payload size (dword).</span></li>
<li style="font-weight: 400;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Payload CRC32 hash (dword).</span></li>
<li style="font-weight: 400;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Heap allocation size for decompressed payload data (dword).</span></li>
<li style="font-weight: 400;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Entry point of decompressed payload (dword).</span></li>
<li style="font-weight: 400;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Pointer to decompressed payload data section (dword).</span></li>
<li style="font-weight: 400;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Size of decompressed payload data section (dword). This value is unused by loader.</span></li>
</ul>
<p><img src="https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/loader_config.png?width=640&amp;name=loader_config.png" alt="loader_config.png" title="loader_config.png" style="width: 640px;" width="640" srcset="https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/loader_config.png?width=320&amp;name=loader_config.png 320w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/loader_config.png?width=640&amp;name=loader_config.png 640w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/loader_config.png?width=960&amp;name=loader_config.png 960w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/loader_config.png?width=1280&amp;name=loader_config.png 1280w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/loader_config.png?width=1600&amp;name=loader_config.png 1600w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/loader_config.png?width=1920&amp;name=loader_config.png 1920w" sizes="(max-width: 640px) 100vw, 640px"></p>
<p style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Entire config structure is located at the beginning of &nbsp;“.rdata” section (VA offset: 0x00402000h).</span></p>
<h2 style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Loader API hashes</span></h2>
<p style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">It’s interesting that Andromeda’s loader binary has no imports (in PE directories). The payload loader uses only the ntdll.dll library and all imported API functions are hardcoded as custom hash values.</span></p>
<p style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">The malware obtains a handle of the ntdll.dll library via a PEB_LDR_DATA (contains the base address of ntdll and kernel32) trick, well known from many shellcodes:</span></p>
<strong><strong><img src="https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/loader_peb_trick.png?width=640&amp;name=loader_peb_trick.png" alt="loader_peb_trick.png" title="loader_peb_trick.png" style="width: 640px;" width="640" srcset="https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/loader_peb_trick.png?width=320&amp;name=loader_peb_trick.png 320w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/loader_peb_trick.png?width=640&amp;name=loader_peb_trick.png 640w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/loader_peb_trick.png?width=960&amp;name=loader_peb_trick.png 960w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/loader_peb_trick.png?width=1280&amp;name=loader_peb_trick.png 1280w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/loader_peb_trick.png?width=1600&amp;name=loader_peb_trick.png 1600w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/loader_peb_trick.png?width=1920&amp;name=loader_peb_trick.png 1920w" sizes="(max-width: 640px) 100vw, 640px"><br></strong></strong>
<p style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Hashing algorithm is trivial and combines XOR and ROL operations over API names (ASCII). </span></p>
<strong><strong><img src="https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/custom_hash.png?width=640&amp;name=custom_hash.png" alt="custom_hash.png" title="custom_hash.png" style="width: 640px;" data-constrained="true" width="640" srcset="https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/custom_hash.png?width=320&amp;name=custom_hash.png 320w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/custom_hash.png?width=640&amp;name=custom_hash.png 640w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/custom_hash.png?width=960&amp;name=custom_hash.png 960w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/custom_hash.png?width=1280&amp;name=custom_hash.png 1280w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/custom_hash.png?width=1600&amp;name=custom_hash.png 1600w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/custom_hash.png?width=1920&amp;name=custom_hash.png 1920w" sizes="(max-width: 640px) 100vw, 640px"><br></strong></strong>
<p style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">All API hashes are stored at the beginning of “.text” section (VA offset 0x00401000h) as DWORD values.</span></p>
<strong><strong><img src="https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/loader_api_hashes.png?width=640&amp;name=loader_api_hashes.png" alt="loader_api_hashes.png" title="loader_api_hashes.png" style="width: 640px;" width="640" srcset="https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/loader_api_hashes.png?width=320&amp;name=loader_api_hashes.png 320w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/loader_api_hashes.png?width=640&amp;name=loader_api_hashes.png 640w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/loader_api_hashes.png?width=960&amp;name=loader_api_hashes.png 960w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/loader_api_hashes.png?width=1280&amp;name=loader_api_hashes.png 1280w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/loader_api_hashes.png?width=1600&amp;name=loader_api_hashes.png 1600w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/loader_api_hashes.png?width=1920&amp;name=loader_api_hashes.png 1920w" sizes="(max-width: 640px) 100vw, 640px"><br></strong></strong>
<p style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">The authors seem to be very experienced native subsystem and low-level programmers and have deep knowledge of the AV detection methods. This malware uses very uncommon API functions in low-level form (Nt/Rtl), which is probably used to avoid standard API monitors/tracers, sandboxes and other dynamic analysis tools with predefined API lists or well known API combinations patterns.</span></p>

<h2 style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Payload encryption &amp; compression</span></h2>
<p style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">The final Andromeda payload is compressed with Aplib and encrypted with RC4 stream cipher.&nbsp;</span>The encrypted payload is verified with a hardcoded CRC32 hash and proceeds to decryption if this check passes.</p>
<strong><strong><img src="https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/payload_crc32_check.png?width=600&amp;name=payload_crc32_check.png" alt="payload_crc32_check.png" title="payload_crc32_check.png" style="width: 600px;" data-constrained="true" width="600" srcset="https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/payload_crc32_check.png?width=300&amp;name=payload_crc32_check.png 300w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/payload_crc32_check.png?width=600&amp;name=payload_crc32_check.png 600w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/payload_crc32_check.png?width=900&amp;name=payload_crc32_check.png 900w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/payload_crc32_check.png?width=1200&amp;name=payload_crc32_check.png 1200w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/payload_crc32_check.png?width=1500&amp;name=payload_crc32_check.png 1500w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/payload_crc32_check.png?width=1800&amp;name=payload_crc32_check.png 1800w" sizes="(max-width: 600px) 100vw, 600px"><br></strong></strong>

<h2 style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Final payload fixups</span></h2>
<p style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Once the payload is decrypted and unpacked, it’s necessary to relocate it to its new base address, because it is not a position independent code. This is done through another uncommon API call - LdrProcessRelocationBlock - which is a function used only internally by the system to relocate loaded PE modules.</span></p>
<p style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-weight: 400;"><img src="https://blog.avast.com/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/payload_api_ldr.png" alt="payload_api_ldr.png" title="payload_api_ldr.png"></span></p>
<p style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">The API function takes a pointer to a relocation record and information about the old and new base address. First relocation record is stored at the beginning of payload data section.</span></p>
<strong><strong><img src="https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/drome.png?width=640&amp;name=drome.png" alt="drome.png" title="drome.png" style="width: 640px;" width="640" srcset="https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/drome.png?width=320&amp;name=drome.png 320w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/drome.png?width=640&amp;name=drome.png 640w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/drome.png?width=960&amp;name=drome.png 960w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/drome.png?width=1280&amp;name=drome.png 1280w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/drome.png?width=1600&amp;name=drome.png 1600w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/drome.png?width=1920&amp;name=drome.png 1920w" sizes="(max-width: 640px) 100vw, 640px"><br></strong></strong>
<p style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">After processing each relocation record, the LdrProcessRelocationBlock function returns a pointer to the next record. This makes it possible to traverse to the end of relocations (there’s a terminating null, which signals that there’s nothing else to process).</span></p>
<p style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">The last step in the loader part is the API function preparation for the final Andromeda payload. All API functions are represented by the same custom hash form (XOR+ROL) described earlier.</span></p>
<p style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">There is also a little config structure located right after the relocation records. The first value of this structure is a custom hash (DWORD) of the DLL file name. The second value is offset to the final payload (DWORD), where resolved API functions will be stored. &nbsp;The custom hashes (DWORD) of API functions from DLL terminated with 0x0000h are also stored.</span></p>
<p style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-weight: 400;"><img src="https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/payload_api_hash_struct.png?width=640&amp;name=payload_api_hash_struct.png" alt="payload_api_hash_struct.png" title="payload_api_hash_struct.png" style="width: 640px;" width="640" srcset="https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/payload_api_hash_struct.png?width=320&amp;name=payload_api_hash_struct.png 320w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/payload_api_hash_struct.png?width=640&amp;name=payload_api_hash_struct.png 640w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/payload_api_hash_struct.png?width=960&amp;name=payload_api_hash_struct.png 960w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/payload_api_hash_struct.png?width=1280&amp;name=payload_api_hash_struct.png 1280w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/payload_api_hash_struct.png?width=1600&amp;name=payload_api_hash_struct.png 1600w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/payload_api_hash_struct.png?width=1920&amp;name=payload_api_hash_struct.png 1920w" sizes="(max-width: 640px) 100vw, 640px"></span></p>
<p style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">The algorithm for resolving the DLL file name from the hash is similar to resolving API hashes, but it also contains lower-case transformation.</span></p>
<p style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-weight: 400;"><img src="https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/loader_dll_name_algo.png?width=640&amp;name=loader_dll_name_algo.png" alt="loader_dll_name_algo.png" title="loader_dll_name_algo.png" style="width: 640px;" width="640" srcset="https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/loader_dll_name_algo.png?width=320&amp;name=loader_dll_name_algo.png 320w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/loader_dll_name_algo.png?width=640&amp;name=loader_dll_name_algo.png 640w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/loader_dll_name_algo.png?width=960&amp;name=loader_dll_name_algo.png 960w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/loader_dll_name_algo.png?width=1280&amp;name=loader_dll_name_algo.png 1280w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/loader_dll_name_algo.png?width=1600&amp;name=loader_dll_name_algo.png 1600w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/loader_dll_name_algo.png?width=1920&amp;name=loader_dll_name_algo.png 1920w" sizes="(max-width: 640px) 100vw, 640px"></span></p>
<p style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">The loader uses a very uncommon method to search and load resolved DLL files. All steps are made through low-level API and the authors use the same method with PEB_LDR_DATA structure as described above. The loader uses returned UNICODE string from the FullDllName value this time.</span></p>
<p style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-weight: 400;"><img src="https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/loader_peb_trick_full.png?width=640&amp;name=loader_peb_trick_full.png" alt="loader_peb_trick_full.png" title="loader_peb_trick_full.png" style="width: 640px;" width="640" srcset="https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/loader_peb_trick_full.png?width=320&amp;name=loader_peb_trick_full.png 320w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/loader_peb_trick_full.png?width=640&amp;name=loader_peb_trick_full.png 640w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/loader_peb_trick_full.png?width=960&amp;name=loader_peb_trick_full.png 960w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/loader_peb_trick_full.png?width=1280&amp;name=loader_peb_trick_full.png 1280w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/loader_peb_trick_full.png?width=1600&amp;name=loader_peb_trick_full.png 1600w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/loader_peb_trick_full.png?width=1920&amp;name=loader_peb_trick_full.png 1920w" sizes="(max-width: 640px) 100vw, 640px"></span></p>
<p style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">This unicode string with the full DLL path is used as an argument for the RtlDosPathNameToNtPathName_U API function, which transforms the unicode file path string into following unicode format:</span></p>
<p style="text-align: left;"><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">“\??\C:\WINDOWS\system32\ntdll.dll"</span></i></p>
<p style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">This string is used to extract the fully qualified path and the “*.dll” file mask and pass them to the NtQueryDirectoryFile API function, which then enumerates libraries in the system directory. Each library name is hashed and compared with stored custom hashes. If the hashes are equal, the DLL file is directly loaded via the LdrLoadDll API function and the loader continues to resolve API function names from hard-coded hashes.</span></p>
<p style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Finally, the loader writes all the resolved function pointers to the payload. The payload itself uses a more sophisticated API redirection method, which first copies an instruction from the particular API function to the final payload, then executes it and redirects back to the original API function’s second instruction. This technique is known as stolen bytes. The authors use JMP instructions 0xEB and 0xE9 for this trick.</span></p>
<strong><strong><img src="https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/payload_api_algo.png?width=640&amp;name=payload_api_algo.png" alt="payload_api_algo.png" title="payload_api_algo.png" style="width: 640px;" width="640" srcset="https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/payload_api_algo.png?width=320&amp;name=payload_api_algo.png 320w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/payload_api_algo.png?width=640&amp;name=payload_api_algo.png 640w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/payload_api_algo.png?width=960&amp;name=payload_api_algo.png 960w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/payload_api_algo.png?width=1280&amp;name=payload_api_algo.png 1280w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/payload_api_algo.png?width=1600&amp;name=payload_api_algo.png 1600w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/payload_api_algo.png?width=1920&amp;name=payload_api_algo.png 1920w" sizes="(max-width: 640px) 100vw, 640px"><br><br></strong></strong>
<p style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Example of the API redirection:</span></p>
<p style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-weight: 400;"><img src="https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/api_redirect.png?width=640&amp;name=api_redirect.png" alt="api_redirect.png" title="api_redirect.png" style="width: 640px;" width="640" srcset="https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/api_redirect.png?width=320&amp;name=api_redirect.png 320w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/api_redirect.png?width=640&amp;name=api_redirect.png 640w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/api_redirect.png?width=960&amp;name=api_redirect.png 960w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/api_redirect.png?width=1280&amp;name=api_redirect.png 1280w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/api_redirect.png?width=1600&amp;name=api_redirect.png 1600w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/api_redirect.png?width=1920&amp;name=api_redirect.png 1920w" sizes="(max-width: 640px) 100vw, 640px"><br></span></p>

<table style="height: 1078px; float: left; border: 2px solid #f5a300;" width="600">

<h2 style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Persistence</span></h2>
<p style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">The techniques to persist the infection and to camouflage the Andromeda PE binary among regular system binaries are well designed. All communication goes through an injected system application - </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">msiexec.exe</span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;">, which is a part of the standard Windows Installer.</span></p>
<p style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Andromeda copies itself to the </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">%ALLUSERPROFILE%</span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;"> folder and renames the binary to "</span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">ms {random [az] {5}}.exe</span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;">” where the UserID is used as a seed for the RtlRandom API function.</span></p>
<strong><strong><img src="https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/ms_random_name.png?width=525&amp;name=ms_random_name.png" alt="ms_random_name.png" title="ms_random_name.png" style="width: 525px;" data-constrained="true" width="525" srcset="https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/ms_random_name.png?width=263&amp;name=ms_random_name.png 263w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/ms_random_name.png?width=525&amp;name=ms_random_name.png 525w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/ms_random_name.png?width=788&amp;name=ms_random_name.png 788w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/ms_random_name.png?width=1050&amp;name=ms_random_name.png 1050w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/ms_random_name.png?width=1313&amp;name=ms_random_name.png 1313w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/ms_random_name.png?width=1575&amp;name=ms_random_name.png 1575w" sizes="(max-width: 525px) 100vw, 525px"><br></strong></strong>
<p style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Later, the resulting file’s attributes are set to “FILE_ATTRIBUTE_HIDDEN” and “FILE_ATTRIBUTE_SYSTEM” (+h +s) and the file time is set to the file time obtained from the original msiexec.exe file. The well known functions - GetFileTime and SetFileTime are used.</span></p>

<p style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">The entire final payload is injected to a newly created msiexec.exe process and activated via the ResumeThread API function. <h2 style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">NTP traffic</span></h2>
<p style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Andromeda uses hardcoded NTP (Network Time Protocol) domains to obtain the current time, which is &nbsp;received by the “Transmit Timestamp”, if this connection isn’t successful the current time is obtained from infected computer.</span></p>
<p style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-weight: 400;"><img src="https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/pool_check_NTP.png?width=599&amp;height=63&amp;name=pool_check_NTP.png" alt="pool_check_NTP.png" title="pool_check_NTP.png" width="599" height="63" srcset="https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/pool_check_NTP.png?width=300&amp;height=32&amp;name=pool_check_NTP.png 300w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/pool_check_NTP.png?width=599&amp;height=63&amp;name=pool_check_NTP.png 599w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/pool_check_NTP.png?width=899&amp;height=95&amp;name=pool_check_NTP.png 899w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/pool_check_NTP.png?width=1198&amp;height=126&amp;name=pool_check_NTP.png 1198w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/pool_check_NTP.png?width=1498&amp;height=158&amp;name=pool_check_NTP.png 1498w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/pool_check_NTP.png?width=1797&amp;height=189&amp;name=pool_check_NTP.png 1797w" sizes="(max-width: 599px) 100vw, 599px"></span></p>
<p style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">The hardcoded NTP domains are </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">africa.pool.ntp.org, asia.pool.ntp.org, europe.pool.ntp.org, oceania.pool.ntp.org </span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;">and</span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;"> pool.ntp.org </span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;">as the last attempt if the other domains fail</span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">.</span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;"> NTP traffic uses port 123.</span></p>
<p style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">The malware verifies if the size of the received data is 0x30h (48) bytes and first parses DWORD from the “Transmit Timestamp” value. </span></p>
<strong><strong><img src="https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/ntp_luparam.png?width=600&amp;height=399&amp;name=ntp_luparam.png" alt="ntp_luparam.png" title="ntp_luparam.png" width="600" height="399" srcset="https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/ntp_luparam.png?width=300&amp;height=200&amp;name=ntp_luparam.png 300w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/ntp_luparam.png?width=600&amp;height=399&amp;name=ntp_luparam.png 600w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/ntp_luparam.png?width=900&amp;height=599&amp;name=ntp_luparam.png 900w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/ntp_luparam.png?width=1200&amp;height=798&amp;name=ntp_luparam.png 1200w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/ntp_luparam.png?width=1500&amp;height=998&amp;name=ntp_luparam.png 1500w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/ntp_luparam.png?width=1800&amp;height=1197&amp;name=ntp_luparam.png 1800w" sizes="(max-width: 600px) 100vw, 600px"><br></strong></strong>
<p style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">This value is increased by 0x7C558180h and the result is used as an argument of the “aStart” function exported by a plugin.</span></p>
<p style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-weight: 400;"><img src="https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/plugin_ntp_astart.png?width=602&amp;height=475&amp;name=plugin_ntp_astart.png" alt="plugin_ntp_astart.png" title="plugin_ntp_astart.png" width="602" height="475" srcset="https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/plugin_ntp_astart.png?width=301&amp;height=238&amp;name=plugin_ntp_astart.png 301w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/plugin_ntp_astart.png?width=602&amp;height=475&amp;name=plugin_ntp_astart.png 602w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/plugin_ntp_astart.png?width=903&amp;height=713&amp;name=plugin_ntp_astart.png 903w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/plugin_ntp_astart.png?width=1204&amp;height=950&amp;name=plugin_ntp_astart.png 1204w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/plugin_ntp_astart.png?width=1505&amp;height=1188&amp;name=plugin_ntp_astart.png 1505w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/plugin_ntp_astart.png?width=1806&amp;height=1425&amp;name=plugin_ntp_astart.png 1806w" sizes="(max-width: 602px) 100vw, 602px"></span></p>

<h2 style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Obtain local IP via sockaddr struct</span></h2>
<p style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Andromeda uses a very uncommon method to obtain <a href="https://www.avast.com/c-how-to-find-ip-address" rel="noopener">local IP addresses</a> of infected machines.</span></p>
<p style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">The malware tries to connect various legal servers on port 80 with a crafted socket and obtain the infected machine’s IP address from the sockaddr structure via the getsockname API function.</span></p>
<strong><strong><img src="https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/sockaddr.png?width=468&amp;height=104&amp;name=sockaddr.png" alt="sockaddr.png" title="sockaddr.png" width="468" height="104" srcset="https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/sockaddr.png?width=234&amp;height=52&amp;name=sockaddr.png 234w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/sockaddr.png?width=468&amp;height=104&amp;name=sockaddr.png 468w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/sockaddr.png?width=702&amp;height=156&amp;name=sockaddr.png 702w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/sockaddr.png?width=936&amp;height=208&amp;name=sockaddr.png 936w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/sockaddr.png?width=1170&amp;height=260&amp;name=sockaddr.png 1170w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/sockaddr.png?width=1404&amp;height=312&amp;name=sockaddr.png 1404w" sizes="(max-width: 468px) 100vw, 468px"><br><br></strong></strong>
<p style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">The resolved value is used as “la” parameter for C&amp;C requests.</span></p>
<p style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">List of domains that &nbsp;Andromeda tries to connect to in the following order:&nbsp;</span><span style="font-weight: 400;">u</span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">pdate.microsoft.com, microsoft.com, bing.com, google.com, yahoo.com</span></i></p>
<h2 style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">C&amp;C communication</span></h2>
<p style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">All communication is RC4 encrypted and uses HTTP/1.1 in the raw data format “Content-Type: application/octet-stream” with predefined “Mozilla/4.0” User-Agent.</span></p>
<strong><strong><img src="https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/cac_traffic.png?width=589&amp;height=276&amp;name=cac_traffic.png" alt="cac_traffic.png" title="cac_traffic.png" width="589" height="276" srcset="https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/cac_traffic.png?width=295&amp;height=138&amp;name=cac_traffic.png 295w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/cac_traffic.png?width=589&amp;height=276&amp;name=cac_traffic.png 589w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/cac_traffic.png?width=884&amp;height=414&amp;name=cac_traffic.png 884w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/cac_traffic.png?width=1178&amp;height=552&amp;name=cac_traffic.png 1178w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/cac_traffic.png?width=1473&amp;height=690&amp;name=cac_traffic.png 1473w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/cac_traffic.png?width=1767&amp;height=828&amp;name=cac_traffic.png 1767w" sizes="(max-width: 589px) 100vw, 589px"><br></strong></strong>
<p style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Andromeda contains a hard-coded RC4 key, which is used for C&amp;C server communication, for the downloaded plugin decryption and also for decrypting hard-coded C&amp;C URLs where the key is used backwards.</span></p>
<p style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">All values are hardcoded to a structure located in the beginning of payload data. The first value is BID (Botnet/BuildID), which is also used as a parameter for C&amp;C requests. RC4 key is hard-coded between random junk data and is followed by encrypted C&amp;C URLs. The first byte of each encrypted URL is the length of data and it is used as a pointer to the next encrypted URL. Zero byte indicates the end of an encrypted URL data block.</span></p>
<strong><strong><img src="https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/encrypted_urls.png?width=640&amp;name=encrypted_urls.png" alt="encrypted_urls.png" title="encrypted_urls.png" style="width: 640px;" width="640" srcset="https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/encrypted_urls.png?width=320&amp;name=encrypted_urls.png 320w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/encrypted_urls.png?width=640&amp;name=encrypted_urls.png 640w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/encrypted_urls.png?width=960&amp;name=encrypted_urls.png 960w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/encrypted_urls.png?width=1280&amp;name=encrypted_urls.png 1280w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/encrypted_urls.png?width=1600&amp;name=encrypted_urls.png 1600w, https://blog.avast.com/hs-fs/hubfs/avast-blog/Andromeda/encrypted_urls.png?width=1920&amp;name=encrypted_urls.png 1920w" sizes="(max-width: 640px) 100vw, 640px"><br><br></strong></strong>

<h2 style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Plugin encryption</span></h2>
<p style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">The plugin binaries are twice encrypted with RC4 encryption and compressed by Aplib. Each plugin contains 43 bytes of config header, with a hard-coded RC4 key, CRC32 hashes and data length values for validation and a parameter for the case the plugin is stored in the registry.</span></p>

<h2 style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Conclusion</span></h2>
<p style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Andromeda malware has very long history. It’s one of the most prevalent malware families and nothing indicates that it will disappear anytime soon. The authors are skilled programmers and operators, recently updating plugins, maintaining entire systems and looking for new infected domains with Exploit Kits.&nbsp;<span>Analyzing Andromeda's very complex ecosystem is a challenging task, but we're investigating it further. Stay tuned for the next blog post!</span></span></p></span>
