	<h2><span style="font-weight: 400">Executive Summary:</span></h2>
<ul>
<li style="font-weight: 400"><span style="font-weight: 400">On June 25, 2024, the </span><a href="https://sansec.io/research/polyfill-supply-chain-attack"><span style="font-weight: 400">Sansec forensics team</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400"> published a report revealing a </span><b>supply chain attack </b><span style="font-weight: 400">targeting the widely-used </span><b>Polyfill.io</b><span style="font-weight: 400"> JavaScript library. The attack originated in February 2024 when Funnull, a Chinese company, acquired the previously legitimate Polyfill.io domain and GitHub account. Shortly thereafter, the service began redirecting users to malicious sites and deploying sophisticated malware with advanced evasion techniques.</span></li>
<li style="font-weight: 400"><span style="font-weight: 400">On June 27, 2024, Namecheap </span><b>suspended the malicious polyfill.io</b><span style="font-weight: 400"> domain, mitigating the immediate threat for now. However, Censys still detects </span><a href="https://search.censys.io/search?resource=hosts&amp;sort=RELEVANCE&amp;per_page=25&amp;virtual_hosts=INCLUDE&amp;q=services.http.response.body%3A%7B%60https%3A%2F%2Fcdn.polyfill.io%60%2C+%60https%3A%2F%2Fcdn.polyfill.com%60%7D"><b>384,773 hosts</b></a><span style="font-weight: 400"> embedding a polyfill JS script linking to the malicious domain as of July 2,2024, primarily located in Germany.</span>
<ul>
<li style="font-weight: 400"><span style="font-weight: 400">These hosts include websites associated with major platforms like Hulu, Mercedes-Benz, and WarnerBros. Security experts </span><b>strongly advise website owners to remove any references to polyfill.io and its associated domains </b><span style="font-weight: 400">from their codebase as a precautionary measure. Cloudflare and Fastly have offered alternative, secure endpoints for polyfill services as a workaround.</span></li>
</ul>
</li>
<li style="font-weight: 400"><b>Further investigation has uncovered a more extensive network of potentially compromised domains.</b><span style="font-weight: 400"> Researchers identified </span><b>four additional active domains</b><span style="font-weight: 400"> linked to the same account that owned the polyfill.io domain. Censys detected </span><b>1,637,160 hosts</b><span style="font-weight: 400"> referencing one or more of these endpoints. At least one of these domains has been observed engaging in malicious activities dating back to June 2023, but the nature of the other associated domains is currently unknown.</span></li>
<li style="font-weight: 400"><span style="font-weight: 400">This incident highlights the growing threat of supply chain attacks on open-source projects</span></li>

<h2><span style="font-weight: 400">Background:</span></h2>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400">Over the past week, the web development community has been rocked by a supply chain attack targeting the widely-used Polyfill.io JavaScript library. Polyfill.js is designed to provide support for more modern tools and functionality on older web browsers that don’t natively support them, in order to maintain cross-compatibility. </span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400">In February 2024, the Polyfill.io domain and GitHub account were acquired by Funnull, a Chinese CDN company, raising immediate concerns about the service&#8217;s legitimacy. Subsequently, malware injected through cdn.polyfill.io began redirecting users to malicious sites, affecting reports of over <a href="https://publicwww.com/websites/%22cdn.polyfill.io%22/">100,000 websites</a>, including high-profile platforms such as JSTOR, Intuit, and the World Economic Forum. This sophisticated malware employs various evasion techniques, making it particularly challenging to detect and combat.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400">Multiple companies have responded to this situation. </span><a href="https://blog.cloudflare.com/automatically-replacing-polyfill-io-links-with-cloudflares-mirror-for-a-safer-internet"><span style="font-weight: 400">Cloudflare</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400"> and </span><a href="https://polyfill-fastly.io"><span style="font-weight: 400">Fastly</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400"> have offered alternative, secure endpoints for users. Google has blocked ads for e-commerce sites using Polyfill.io. The website blocker uBlock Origin added the domain to its filter list. Andrew Betts, the original creator of Polyfill.io, has <a href="https://x.com/triblondon/status/1761852117579427975">urged website owners</a> to immediately remove the library, emphasizing that it&#8217;s no longer necessary for modern browsers. </span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400">The most important recent development is that as of June 27, Namecheap, the domain registrar for polyfill.io, took down the malicious domain – which mitigates the immediate threat. </span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400">This story is a reminder of the growing threat of supply chain attacks on open-source projects, especially in the web development ecosystem where applications rely on a diverse technology stack of open source packages for functionality. These dependencies can get tenuous. A memorable example is from 2016, when the removal of a single open-source JavaScript package from npm by a developer caused widespread disruption to websites worldwide, prompting npm to reverse the action within just 2 hours. Interconnected dependencies within the open-source ecosystem have interconnected security implications.</span></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<div id="attachment_4174" style="width: 395px" class="wp-caption alignnone"><img aria-describedby="caption-attachment-4174" class="size-full wp-image-4174" src="https://censys.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/07/dependency.png" alt="" width="385" height="489" /><p id="caption-attachment-4174" class="wp-caption-text">(Relevant xkcd: https://www.explainxkcd.com/wiki/index.php/2347:_Dependency)</p></div>
<h2><span style="font-weight: 400">Censys Findings</span></h2>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400">Censys has identified a significant number of web hosts using the polyfill.io CDN. As of the latest data, </span><a href="https://search.censys.io/search?resource=hosts&amp;sort=RELEVANCE&amp;per_page=25&amp;virtual_hosts=INCLUDE&amp;q=services.http.response.body%3A%7B%60https%3A%2F%2Fcdn.polyfill.io%60%2C+%60https%3A%2F%2Fcdn.polyfill.com%60%7D"><span style="font-weight: 400">384,773 hosts</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400"> were found to include references to &#8220;https://cdn.polyfill[.]io&#8221; or &#8220;https://cdn.polyfill[.]com&#8221; in their HTTP responses.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400">A notable concentration of these hosts, approximately 237,700, are located within the Hetzner network (AS24940), primarily in Germany. This is not surprising – Hetzner is a popular web hosting service, and many website developers leverage it. </span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400">Further analysis of the affected hosts reveals domains tied to major companies such as Warner Bros (www.warnerbros.com), Hulu (www.hulu.com), Mercedes-Benz (shop.mercedes-benz.com), and Pearson (digital-library-qa.pearson.com, digital-library-stg.pearson.com) that have large numbers of hosts referencing the malicious polyfill endpoint. Interestingly, the most common hostname associated with hosts presenting the endpoint is </span><span style="font-weight: 400">ns-static-assets.s3.amazonaws.com</span><span style="font-weight: 400">, indicating widespread usage among users of Amazon&#8217;s S3 static website hosting. </span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400">The presence of domains like “</span><span style="font-weight: 400">www.feedthefuture.gov</span><span style="font-weight: 400">” in these top results also highlights the use of polyfill.io across various sectors, including government websites. In total, Censys observed 182 affected hosts displaying a “.gov” domain. </span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400">View the following </span><a href="https://search.censys.io/search/report?resource=hosts&amp;q=services.http.response.body%3A%60https%3A%2F%2Fcdn.polyfill.io%60&amp;virtual_hosts=INCLUDE&amp;field=name&amp;num_buckets=50"><span style="font-weight: 400">report</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400"> breaking down hostnames on sites using this endpoint.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400">While estimates of the scale of affected websites vary widely between sources (Sansec reported 100,000, while Cloudflare suggested </span><a href="https://x.com/eastdakota/status/1806064925670080935"><span style="font-weight: 400">&#8220;tens of millions&#8221;</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400">), it&#8217;s clear that this supply chain attack has had a widespread impact. </span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400">Cloudflare and Fastly have created alternative secure polyfill endpoints for users to mitigate the threat while preventing websites from breaking. Censys has observed </span><a href="https://search.censys.io/search?resource=hosts&amp;sort=RELEVANCE&amp;per_page=25&amp;virtual_hosts=INCLUDE&amp;q=services.http.response.body%3A%7B%22https%3A%2F%2Fpolyfill-fastly.io%22%2C+%22https%3A%2F%2Fcdnjs.cloudflare.com%2Fpolyfill%22%7D"><span style="font-weight: 400">216,504 hosts</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400"> referencing one of these alternative polyfill endpoints: “polyfill-fastly.io” or “cdnjs.cloudflare.com/polyfill”, an increase from the 80,312 we observed last Friday, June 28.</span></p>
<h2><span style="font-weight: 400">Investigating the Malicious Polyfill[.]io Domain</span></h2>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400">We searched historical DNS records for anything that resolved to  </span><span style="font-weight: 400">cdn.polyfill.io</span><span style="font-weight: 400">. Besides cloudflare and fastly domains, the following three were of interest:</span></p>
<p><b>5f52353c.u.fn03.vip.</b></p>
<p><b>cdn.polyfill.io.bsclink.cn.</b></p>
<p><b>wildcard.polyfill.io.bsclink.cn. </b></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400">Of these, we found </span><a href="https://search.censys.io/search?resource=hosts&amp;sort=RELEVANCE&amp;per_page=25&amp;virtual_hosts=INCLUDE&amp;q=wildcard.polyfill.io.bsclink.cn"><span style="font-weight: 400">6 hosts</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400"> presenting the third domain (</span><span style="font-weight: 400">wildcard.polyfill.io.bsclink.cn</span><span style="font-weight: 400">) that were still online as of July 2, 2024:</span></p>
<p><img class="alignnone size-full wp-image-4177" src="https://censys.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/07/Screenshot-2024-06-28-at-11.05.01 AM.png" alt="" width="1034" height="821" /></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400">All are hosted in AS139057, </span><span style="font-weight: 400">LDPL-AS-AP LEGEND DYNASTY PTE. LTD.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400">LEGEND DYNASTY PTE. LTD. is a company based in Singapore. According to </span><a href="https://ipinfo.io/AS149981"><span style="font-weight: 400">IPinfo</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400"> its associated website is edgenext.com, belonging to EdgeNext, a cloud service provider that “specializes in APAC, China, MENA, and global cloud delivery.” (</span><a href="https://www.edgenext.com/"><span style="font-weight: 400">https://www.edgenext.com/</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400">). It’s as of yet unclear how this infrastructure is tied to Funnull, the Chinese CDN that owns the malicious polyfill domain.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400">Another thing to note here is that these hosts are uniformly distributed across different countries: 2 are in the Netherlands, 2 in Spain, and 2 in the U.S.</span></p>
<h2><span style="font-weight: 400">Other Potentially Associated Domains</span></h2>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400">Further investigation has uncovered a more extensive network of potentially related domains. A twitter user </span><a href="https://x.com/zzwudev/status/1806339023092560323"><span style="font-weight: 400">discovered</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400"> that the maintainers of the polyfill GitHub repo leaked their CloudFlare API secrets within the </span><a href="https://github.com/vincent-backend/data.polyfill.com"><span style="font-weight: 400">repo</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400">.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400">It was </span><a href="https://x.com/mdmck10/status/1806349965733544160"><span style="font-weight: 400">thereafter discovered</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400"> that the leaked Cloudflare API key is still active, and shows 4 additional </span><b>active</b><span style="font-weight: 400"> domains linked to the same account:</span></p>
<ul>
<li style="font-weight: 400"><span style="font-weight: 400">bootcdn[.]net</span></li>
<li style="font-weight: 400"><span style="font-weight: 400">bootcss[.]com</span></li>
<li style="font-weight: 400"><span style="font-weight: 400">staticfile[.]net</span></li>
<li style="font-weight: 400"><span style="font-weight: 400">staticfile[.]org</span></li>
</ul>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400">One of these domains, bootcss[.]com, has been observed engaging in malicious activities that are very similar to the polyfill[.]io attack, with evidence dating back to June 2023. </span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400">Analyzing the malicious polyfill JavaScript code, we see a function named </span><span style="font-weight: 400">check_tiaozhuan()</span><span style="font-weight: 400"> whose definition starts as such:</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400">function</span> <span style="font-weight: 400">check_tiaozhuan()</span> <span style="font-weight: 400">{</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400">  </span><span style="font-weight: 400">var</span> <span style="font-weight: 400">_isMobile</span> <span style="font-weight: 400">=</span> <span style="font-weight: 400">navigator.userAgent.match(</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400">    </span><span style="font-weight: 400">/(phone|pad|pod|iPhone|iPod|ios|iPad|Android|Mobile|BlackBerry|IEMobile|MQQBrowser|JUC|Fennec|wOSBrowser|BrowserNG|WebOS|Symbian|Windows</span> <span style="font-weight: 400">Phone)/i</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400">  </span><span style="font-weight: 400">);</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400">  </span><span style="font-weight: 400">if</span> <span style="font-weight: 400">(_isMobile)</span> <span style="font-weight: 400">{</span><span style="font-weight: 400"><br />
</span> <span style="font-weight: 400">&#8230;</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400">Searching for </span><span style="font-weight: 400">check_tiaozhuan()</span><span style="font-weight: 400"> yields a post on the Chinese developer forum V2EX from June 20, 2023. The post warns of </span><span style="font-weight: 400">cdn.bootcss.com</span><span style="font-weight: 400"> being hijacked to spread malware through the highlight.js JavaScript library.</span></p>
<p><img class="alignnone size-full wp-image-4175" src="https://censys.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/07/Screenshot-2024-06-28-at-2.58.07 PM.png" alt="" width="769" height="834" /></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400">The following interesting quotes were translated from this post using Google Translate:</span></p>
<ul>
<li style="font-weight: 400"><span style="font-weight: 400">“The two main malicious domain names involved were both registered at the end of May”</span></li>
<li style="font-weight: 400"><span style="font-weight: 400">“This evil jQuery was introduced by highlight.js hosted on cdn.bootcss.com. When the request for highlight.js has a specific Referer and mobile UA, the server will return highlight.js with malicious code, otherwise it will return normal code, which is highly disguised. Moreover, there are specific conditions for whether malicious jQuery is introduced when this code is executed.”</span></li>
</ul>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400">This tactic seems to closely mirror the tactics used in this attack.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400">The following comment underneath this post references the </span><span style="font-weight: 400">check_tiaozhuan()</span><span style="font-weight: 400"> function:</span></p>
<p><img loading="lazy" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-4178" src="https://censys.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/07/Screenshot-2024-06-28-at-11.47.09 AM.png" alt="" width="765" height="202" /></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400">Translated: “The check_tiaozhuan() function appears to check if the user is using a mobile device by checking the navigator object. If they are using a mobile device, it sets the value of _0x34f873 based on various conditions and then calls the vfed_update(_0x5e3fa1) function with that value. The vfed_update(_0x5e3fa1) function loads a JavaScript file from the URL specified by _0x5e3fa1, which may redirect the user&#8217;s browser to another page.”</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400">This description matches the implementation of the function we observed in the Polyfill[.]io script. </span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400">When we dug in </span><a href="https://search.censys.io/search?resource=hosts&amp;sort=RELEVANCE&amp;per_page=25&amp;virtual_hosts=INCLUDE&amp;q=+services.http.response.body%3A+%7B%60cdn.bootcdn.net%60%2C++%60cdn.bootcss.com%60%2C+%60cdn.staticfile.net%60%2C+%60cdn.staticfile.org%60%7D"><span style="font-weight: 400">Censys Search</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400"> for any exposed hosts referencing one of the additional suspicious domains, we observed</span><span style="font-weight: 400"> a total of</span><span style="font-weight: 400"> 1,637,160 public-facing hosts combined linking to one of these. </span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400">So far, this domain (</span><span style="font-weight: 400">bootcss.com</span><span style="font-weight: 400">) is the only one showing any signs of potential malice. The nature of the other associated endpoints remains unknown, and we avoid speculation. However, it wouldn’t be entirely unreasonable to consider the possibility that the same malicious actor responsible for the polyfill.io attack might exploit these other domains for similar activities in the future.</span></p>
