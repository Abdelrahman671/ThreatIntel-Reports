    <h1 class="text-center">Bisonal: 10 years of play</h1>

    <div class="text-center m-1">
        <div class="post-author">
            <span>By </span>

    <section class="post-content-wrapper mt-5">
        <div class="post-content">
            <p><br> By <a href="https://twitter.com/SecurityBeard/">Warren Mercer</a>, <a href="https://twitter.com/r00tbsd?lang%3Den">Paul Rascagneres</a> and <a href="https://twitter.com/_vventura">Vitor Ventura</a>.</p><p><i>Update 06/03/20: added samples from 2020.</i><br></p><h2 id="executive-summary">Executive summary <br></h2><ul><li>Security researchers detected and exposed the Bisonal malware over the past 10 years. But the Tonto team, the threat actor behind it, didn't stop.</li><li>The victimology didn't change over time, either. Japanese, South Korean and Russian organizations were the prime targets for this threat actor.</li><li>The malware evolved to lower its detection ratio and improve the initial vector success rate.</li></ul><h3 id="what-s-new-bisonal-is-a-remote-access-trojan-rat-that-s-part-of-the-tonto-team-arsenal-the-peculiarity-of-the-rat-is-that-it-s-been-in-use-for-more-than-10-years-this-is-an-uncommon-and-long-period-for-malware-over-the-years-it-has-evolved-and-adapted-mechanisms-to-avoid-detection-while-keeping-the-core-of-its-rat-the-same-we-identified-specific-functions-here-for-more-than-six-years-">What's new? Bisonal is a remote access trojan (RAT) that's part of the <a href="https://malpedia.caad.fkie.fraunhofer.de/actor/tonto_team">Tonto Team</a> arsenal. The peculiarity of the RAT is that it's been in use for more than 10 years â€” this is an uncommon and long period for malware. Over the years, it has evolved and adapted mechanisms to avoid detection while keeping the core of its RAT the same. We identified specific functions here for more than six years.<br></h3><h3 id="how-did-it-work-bisonal-used-multiple-lure-documents-to-entice-their-victims-to-open-and-then-be-infected-with-bisonal-malware-this-group-has-continued-its-operations-for-over-a-decade-and-they-continue-to-evolve-their-malware-to-avoid-detection-bisonal-primarily-used-spear-phishing-to-obtain-a-foothold-within-their-victims-networks-their-campaigns-had-very-specific-targets-which-would-suggest-their-end-game-was-more-around-operational-intelligence-gathering-and-espionage-">How did it work? Bisonal used multiple lure documents to entice their victims to open and then be infected with Bisonal malware. This group has continued its operations for over a decade and they continue to evolve their malware to avoid detection. Bisonal primarily used spear phishing to obtain a foothold within their victims' networks. Their campaigns had very specific targets which would suggest their end game was more around operational intelligence gathering and espionage.<br></h3><h3 id="so-what-this-is-an-extremely-experienced-group-likely-to-keep-their-activities-even-after-exposure-even-if-we-identified-mistakes-and-bad-copy-paste-they-are-doing-this-job-for-more-than-10-years-we-think-that-exposing-this-malware-explaining-the-behavior-and-the-campaigns-where-bisonal-was-used-is-important-to-protect-the-potential-future-targets-the-targets-to-this-point-are-located-in-the-public-and-private-sectors-with-a-focus-on-russia-japan-and-south-korea-we-recommend-the-entities-located-in-this-area-to-prepare-for-this-malware-and-actor-and-implement-detections-based-on-the-technical-details-provided-in-this-article-">So what? This is an extremely experienced group likely to keep their activities even after exposure, even if we identified mistakes and bad copy/paste, they are doing this job for more than 10 years. We think that exposing this malware, explaining the behavior and the campaigns where Bisonal was used is important to protect the potential future targets. The targets to this point are located in the public and private sectors with a focus on Russia, Japan and South Korea. We recommend the entities located in this area to prepare for this malware and actor and implement detections based on the technical details provided in this article.</h3><h2 id="victimology-and-campaigns-from-our-analysis-and-the-intelligence-shared-by-the-community-throughout-the-last-decade-of-activities-of-bisonal-we-can-conclude-that-the-actor-behind-this-malware-is-specifically-targeted-at-the-south-east-asian-region-namely-japan-and-korea-with-another-significant-focus-on-russian-speaking-victims-">Victimology and campaigns <br> From our analysis and the intelligence shared by the community throughout the last decade of activities of Bisonal, we can conclude that the actor behind this malware is specifically targeted at the South East Asian region, namely Japan and Korea with another significant focus on Russian-speaking victims.</h2><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card"><img src="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/-cgCYzh-Sgos/XmCxY45sNyI/AAAAAAAAAso/hmm3XjEmOsUgnmHUY2A9e3rMmX6LN2UYACLcBGAsYHQ/s640/image21.jpg" class="kg-image" alt loading="lazy"></figure><p><br> We identified a couple of decoy documents pointing to the victims. During the Heartbeat campaign documented in 2012 by <a href="https://blog.trendmicro.com/trendlabs-security-intelligence/pulsing-the-heartbeat-apt/">Trend Micro</a>, dating back to 2009, the attacker used Hangul Word Processor (HWP) decoy documents. This file format is mainly used in South Korea. The report mentioned political parties, media outfits, a national policy research institute, a military branch of South Korean armed forces, a small business sector organization and branches of the South Korean government. Later in 2018, <a href="https://unit42.paloaltonetworks.com/unit42-bisonal-malware-used-attacks-russia-south-korea/">Unit 42</a> released a Bisonal paper where we can see a spear-phishing campaign in Russian and a decoy document alleged to be from Rostec, a Russian state-owned holding conglomerate headquartered in Moscow.</p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card"><img src="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/-gzpTiB8HJT4/XmCxr24P3MI/AAAAAAAAAuY/nw2Yl0qniMEJrt7PG4aCgEJ5dLgkEincgCEwYBhgL/s640/image34.png" class="kg-image" alt loading="lazy"></figure><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card"><img src="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/-QwmpMoQI9dk/XmCxkvMqFcI/AAAAAAAAAtU/_AN7_WTeSEQGR58HIKSaPOnYyCY1Iz1zACEwYBhgL/s640/image19.png" class="kg-image" alt loading="lazy"></figure><p><br> Finally, in 2018, Ahnlab released a <a href="https://image.ahnlab.com/file_upload/asecissue_files/ASEC_REPORT_vol.88.pdf">paper </a>about "Operation Bitter Biscuit" where Bisonal was used against Korean and Japanese entities. India is also mentioned, but it was by another malware named "Bioazih" by Ahnlab. In this paper, the editor mentions targets such as manufacturers, defense industry and government.</p><p>Additionally, we can provide additional decoy documents. For example, a Korean document used in September 2014 where the title was "Contact member and counselor of the Agriculture, Forestry, Livestock, Food and Marine Fisheries Committee:"</p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card"><img src="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/-9JpCeLTQ8tM/XmCxizrlbZI/AAAAAAAAAvc/a9CY5s31xzsTCzpimDOLWB0nVHflYgELQCEwYBhgL/s640/image15.png" class="kg-image" alt loading="lazy"></figure><p><br> Or a Russian document about the CIPR Digital conference used in April 2018. Tonto Team was mentioned in the <a href="https://arstechnica.com/information-technology/2017/04/researchers-claim-china-trying-to-hack-south-korea-missile-defense-efforts/">media </a>in 2017 as one of the actors who targeted South Korea, when the country announced it would deploy a Terminal High-Altitude Air Defense (THAAD) in response to North Korean missile tests. At this time, researchers connected the Tonto Team to China.</p><h2 id="10-years-of-evolution">10 years of evolution <br></h2><h3 id="introduction-the-first-variant-of-bisonal-publicly-released-went-by-the-name-of-heartbeat-at-the-end-of-2019-the-actor-changed-their-ttp-and-started-using-the-microsoft-office-extension-wll-to-execute-the-bisonal-payload-based-on-this-recent-change-we-decided-to-dive-into-the-10-years-of-evolution-of-bisonal-to-do-so-we-analysed-more-than-50-different-samples-and-focused-on-the-changes-that-appear-during-the-years-of-usage-">Introduction <br> The first variant of Bisonal publicly released went by the name of "HeartBeat." At the end of 2019, the actor changed their TTP and started using the Microsoft Office extension (.wll) to execute the Bisonal payload. Based on this recent change, we decided to dive into the 10 years of evolution of Bisonal. To do so, we analysed more than 50 different samples and focused on the changes that appear during the years of usage.</h3><h3 id="2010-the-birth-the-oldest-version-of-bisonal-we-identified-was-compiled-on-dec-24-2010-this-version-is-the-simplest-we-identified-the-attacker-created-a-windows-library-dll-designed-as-a-windows-service-servicemain-entry-point-when-executed-the-malware-uses-the-windows-api-to-communicate-with-the-service-control-manager-scm-and-finally-execute-a-thread-this-thread-contains-the-code-of-the-malware-">2010: the birth <br> The oldest version of Bisonal we identified was compiled on Dec. 24, 2010. This version is the simplest we identified. The attacker created a Windows library (.dll) designed as a Windows service (ServiceMain() entry point). When executed, the malware uses the Windows API to communicate with the Service Control Manager (SCM) and finally execute a thread. This thread contains the code of the malware.</h3><p>The C2 server of this first Bisonal variant is young03[.]myfw[.]us (port 8888). We can notice the usage of a dynamic DNS service. This is a Bisonal pattern. Even the newest version we identified used this kind of service. The domain name was not obfuscated:</p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card"><img src="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/-AXWjmHGqPY8/XmCxoSAuF5I/AAAAAAAAAvs/7dlrWnYZEsgNjY3w7PsjpaSWN4XuYpCoACEwYBhgL/s640/image26.png" class="kg-image" alt loading="lazy"></figure><p><br> The IP address is a rollback if the first C2 server is down. In this campaign, the rollback was not used as it is configured to localhost. The communication to the C2 server is performed by using raw sockets:</p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card"><img src="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/-tfKToieAZkM/XmCxhnFS89I/AAAAAAAAAvs/s8J6OXvfH-M-aFjd2agGj6VA7I-nsBzMgCEwYBhgL/s640/image1.png" class="kg-image" alt loading="lazy"></figure><p><br> The first action of the malware is to send the hostname of the infected system and the "kris0315" string. The sent data is not encrypted or obfuscated. We assume the string is an identifier:</p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card"><img src="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/content/images/-KhOYglrF_Nk/XmCxpZeFWHI/AAAAAAAAAvU/kBVW9UpFpRID3AMTBRlodSLxAT0IN8c-QCEwYBhgL/s640/image3.png" class="kg-image" alt loading="lazy"></figure><p><br> The malware supports only three commands:</p><ul><li>Command execution: The execution is performed by the ShellExecuteW() API</li><li>Listing the running processes</li><li>Cleaning the malware: The malware first removes the registry key of the service and removes the library.
