        <h1>EMBERSim: A Large-Scale Databank for Boosting Similarity Search in Malware Analysis</h1>
        
        <div class="publish_info">
            <p>June 06, 2024</p>
            <span>|</span>
            <a href="/en-us/blog/author.drago%C8%99-corl%C4%83tescu---alexandru-dinu---mihaela-g%C4%83man---paul-sumedrea/" title="Posts by Dragoș Corlătescu - Alexandru Dinu - Mihaela Găman - Paul Sumedrea" rel="author">
                Dragoș Corlătescu - Alexandru Dinu - Mihaela Găman - Paul Sumedrea</a>
            <span>|</span>
                
            <a href="/en-us/blog/category.engineering-and-technology/" title="Engineering &amp; Tech">Engineering &amp; Tech</a>
        </div>
        <div class="post_image">
            <img width="1060" height="698" src="https://assets.crowdstrike.com/is/image/crowdstrikeinc/Falcon-Script-Control-Blog-1-1024x674" class="attachment-post-thumbnail size-post-thumbnail wp-post-image" alt="" decoding="async" fetchpriority="high" srcset="/content/dam/crowdstrike/www/en-us/wp/2021/04/Falcon-Script-Control-Blog-1-1024x674.jpg 1024w" sizes="(max-width: 1060px) 100vw, 1060px"/>

    <div id="container-4b5ac66fed" class="cmp-container-wp" data-cmp-is="simple-container" data-is-transparent-header="false">
        
        <div class="text text--blog-content">

  

<div class="cmp-text" id="text-0b5c98ea0f" data-cmp-name="cmp-text-" data-cmp-is="text" data-target-location="false">
    
       <span><ul><li><i>Binary code similarity (BCS) is an important part of training machine learning (ML) models to effectively analyze vast amounts of cybersecurity telemetry. However, BCS has historically focused on finding similarities among malicious examples rather than benign data, which limits its effectiveness.</i></li><li><i>The CrowdStrike research team has released EMBERSim, a BCS dataset that builds on the existing EMBER dataset, with extended data tags and a new leaf similarity co-occurrence algorithm that accounts for both benign and malicious binaries.</i></li><li><i>This innovative approach to qualifying similarity in cybersecurity improves the results of BCS in ML models, showing EMBERSim has the potential to improve malware detection and enable future work in this key research area.</i></li></ul>
<p>CrowdStrike is constantly researching, working and innovating to stay at the cutting edge of threat detection and response. Recently, these efforts include EMBERSim, a large-scale dataset developed to address limitations in binary code similarity (BCS), improve malware detection and facilitate future work in this area.</p>
<p>In recent years, there has been a shift from heuristics to ML-based malware detection. Replicating both malicious and benign code is a common practice that usually results in similar examples, which are analyzed and curated to build new ML-based solutions or improve the performance of existing ML models. Being able to accurately identify similar examples is important in performing bulk analysis over the growing amounts of cybersecurity telemetry. BCS involves comparing two binary files (executables such as the .exe format) for similarities — a technique that is particularly important in cybersecurity because most malware is hidden within such files, making it more challenging to detect.</p>
<p>However, the CrowdStrike research team has identified a scarcity of data for similarity research in malware detection and observed a focus on quantifying similarity only in malicious examples and not in benign data. Given the vast majority of files processed will be benign, having the ability to label them as such would boost the ability to confidently perform a similarity search for an example file — a capability that becomes invaluable in the move toward automated analysis. We propose to address these limitations in BCS research. We are building our research on the existing <a href="https://github.com/elastic/ember" data-link-tracked="true" data-uw-rm-brl="PR" data-uw-original-href="https://github.com/elastic/ember">EMBER</a> dataset of Portable Executable (PE) files, which includes features and tags intended for malware classification.</p>
<p>Our contribution is threefold:</p>
<ol><li>We released <b>EMBERSim</b>, a dataset intended for BCS research that enhances the malware family (FAM) tags metadata (obtained via <a href="https://www.researchgate.net/publication/307853267_AVclass_A_Tool_for_Massive_Malware_Labeling" data-link-tracked="true" data-uw-rm-brl="PR" data-uw-original-href="https://www.researchgate.net/publication/307853267_AVclass_A_Tool_for_Massive_Malware_Labeling">AVClass v1</a>) in the original EMBER dataset with similarity information and malware class (CLASS) and behavior (BEH) tags as well as additional family (FAM) tags. This extended list of tags is determined via a co-occurrence algorithm that we have designed as part of our work.</li><li>Based on the literature to date with applicability in the cybersecurity context, to our knowledge we are the first to repurpose an XGBoost malware classifier to quantify <b>pairwise similarity</b> at the leaf level.</li><li>We are proposing a new extensive <b>evaluation scheme</b> to assess the effectiveness of the proposed leaf similarity technique — i.e., Top-K Selection and Relevance &#64; K. Using this method, we compared ourselves against an established off-the-shelf method for computing similarity in cybersecurity (i.e., ssdeep) and validated leaf similarity as a better alternative.</li></ol>
<p>We documented this work in a paper that was peer-reviewed, accepted and presented in <a href="https://neurips.cc/virtual/2023/poster/73677" data-link-tracked="true" data-uw-rm-brl="PR" data-uw-original-href="https://neurips.cc/virtual/2023/poster/73677">poster</a> format at <a href="https://neurips.cc/Conferences/2023" data-link-tracked="true" data-uw-rm-brl="PR" data-uw-original-href="https://neurips.cc/Conferences/2023">NeurIPS 2023</a>. Through this blog post, we offer an overview of this work and invite readers to research the subject in more detail using our published <a href="https://openreview.net/forum?id&#61;9U8bqr8epr" data-link-tracked="true" data-uw-rm-brl="PR" data-uw-original-href="https://openreview.net/forum?id&#61;9U8bqr8epr">paper</a>, <a href="https://github.com/CrowdStrike/embersim-databank" data-link-tracked="true" data-uw-rm-brl="PR" data-uw-original-href="https://github.com/CrowdStrike/embersim-databank">code</a> and <a href="https://zenodo.org/records/8014709" data-link-tracked="true" data-uw-rm-brl="PR" data-uw-original-href="https://zenodo.org/records/8014709">data</a> as support.</p>
<h2>Data Used for EMBERSim Project</h2>
<p>We built our work on <b>EMBER</b>, a malware classification dataset of binary files in PE format. We used <a href="https://github.com/elastic/ember" data-link-tracked="true" data-uw-rm-brl="PR" data-uw-original-href="https://github.com/elastic/ember">EMBER 2018, feature version 2</a>, which consists of 1M total samples, out of which 800K are labeled (600K train and 200K test) and an extra 200K are unlabeled. The samples in train and test are perfectly split between “benign” and “malicious.” Additionally, the dataset follows a temporal split, wherein all test samples have a more recent timestamp compared to train samples. The EMBER dataset contains the SHA256s of the PEs, assigned ground truth and features.</p>
<p>In <b>EMBERSim</b>, aside from similarity information, we include extended tag information obtained via a co-occurrence-based tag enrichment procedure. We leverage <a href="https://arxiv.org/pdf/2006.10615.pdf" data-link-tracked="true" data-uw-pdf-br="1">AVClass v2</a> and obtain the following tag types:</p>
<ul><li>FAM (family) — e.g., grayware, backdoor, worm</li><li>BEH (behavior) — e.g., XTRAT, zBot, Emotet</li><li>CLASS (class) — e.g., infostealer, DDoS, inject</li><li>FILE (file properties) — e.g., os:Windows, packed, packed:Themida</li></ul>
<p>For each malicious sample in EMBER, we run <a href="https://github.com/malicialab/avclass" data-link-tracked="true" data-uw-rm-brl="PR" data-uw-original-href="https://github.com/malicialab/avclass">AVClass</a> to obtain their associated tags of the types enumerated above. We augment this initial list of tags using the co-occurrence procedure described in the upcoming section. A second set of steps is applied with the end goal of obtaining tag ranking, which we’ll use in the evaluation scheme to assess the performance of the binary code similarity method proposed. See Figure 1.</p>
<div id="attachment_210895" style="	width: 1550.0px;
" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><a href="/content/dam/crowdstrike/www/en-us/wp/2024/06/Figure1-1.png" target="_blank" data-link-tracked="true" data-uw-rm-brl="PR" data-uw-original-href="/content/dam/crowdstrike/www/en-us/wp/2024/06/Figure1-1.png" rel="noopener noreferrer"><img class="wp-image-210895 size-full" src="/content/dam/crowdstrike/www/en-us/wp/2024/06/Figure1-1.png" alt="Figure 1. <h2>Metadata Augmentation</h2>
<h3>Tag Enrichment via Co-Occurrence</h3>
<p>For each sample in EMBER, we use its SHA256 to query <a href="https://www.virustotal.com/" data-link-tracked="true" data-uw-rm-brl="PR" data-uw-original-href="https://www.virustotal.com/">VirusTotal</a> (VT) and then run AVClass v2 to obtain tags with their corresponding confidence scores across multiple vendors. AVClass provides co-occurrence statistics for tag pairs (out of which we can build a co-occurrence matrix). We leverage this information to enrich the set of tags of the malicious samples in EMBER by adding the co-occurring ones above a frequency threshold. Given that we target similarity research, the main goal with this enhancement is being able to find samples with shared characteristics, even when it comes to different families. Algorithm 1 displays the step-by-step tag enrichment procedure followed.</p>
<div id="attachment_210896" style="	width: 590.0px;

<p>Following the procedure described in Algorithm 2, we obtain a tag ranking for each sample in EMBERSim. The end goal is to use the tag ranks in performing the Relevance &#64; K evaluation (i.e., how relevant to the query samples are the top-K most similar retrieved items?).</p>

<h2>Leaf Prediction Similarity Method</h2>
<p>Given a trained tree ensemble with a total of T trees and two samples x1 and x2, we define the similarity of x1 and x2 as the leaf similarity between them in the context of the aforementioned tree ensemble.</p>
<p>Although the method can be applied to any type of tree ensemble, in our experiments we opt for eXtreme Gradient Boosting (XGBoost). Therefore, we train an XGBoost-based malware classifier over the original features released alongside the EMBER dataset. The XGBoost malware detector trained has similar capabilities with the LightGBM baseline described in the original <a href="https://arxiv.org/pdf/1804.04637.pdf" data-link-tracked="true" data-uw-pdf-br="1">EMBER paper</a>. Our model uses the following hyperparameter set: max_depth &#61; 17; η &#61; 0.15; n_estimators &#61; 2048; colsample_bytree &#61; 1. The performance of the malware classifier itself is subject to hyperparameter optimization and of lesser importance for our research.</p>
<p>With a competitive XGBoost-based malware detector, we continue by extracting leaf predictions for the two samples, which are the subject of our similarity assessment. The similarity score represents the fraction of the trees in which both samples fall into the same leaf node. The intuition behind this idea is that if two samples consistently end up in the same leaf node across multiple decision trees, it suggests that they have similar characteristics or share common patterns in their features.</p>
<p><a href="/content/dam/crowdstrike/www/en-us/wp/2024/06/Eq3.png" target="_blank" data-link-tracked="true" data-uw-rm-brl="PR" data-uw-original-href="/content/dam/crowdstrike/www/en-us/wp/2024/06/Eq3.png" rel="noopener noreferrer"><img class="aligncenter wp-image-210905 size-full" src="/content/dam/crowdstrike/www/en-us/wp/2024/06/Eq3.png" alt="" width="614" height="102"/></a></p>

<p>Figure 2 is an example considering three samples (sample 1, sample 2 and sample 3) and an ensemble of three trees (Tree #1, Tree #2 and Tree #3), with the respective paths through the trees highlighted.</p>

<h2>Evaluation</h2>
<p>We employ two different evaluation scenarios, with the end goal of validating the novel similarity method based on leaf predictions. As part of the evaluation procedure, we leverage the tag augmentations and ranking obtained using the algorithms described in the Metadata Augmentation section above. Figure 3 displays the two evaluation paths that were considered — namely, the Top-K selection and Relevance &#64; K evaluation. In the following subsections, we describe the two methods in more detail.</p>
<div id="attachment_210899" style="	width: 897.0px;
" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><a href="/content/dam/crowdstrike/www/en-us/wp/2024/06/Figure3.png" target="_blank" data-link-tracked="true" data-uw-rm-brl="PR" data-uw-original-href="/content/dam/crowdstrike/www/en-us/wp/2024/06/Figure3.png" data-uw-rm-ext-link="na" rel="noopener noreferrer"><img class="wp-image-210899 size-full" src="/content/dam/crowdstrike/www/en-us/wp/2024/06/Figure3.png" alt="" width="887" height="439"/></a><p id="caption-attachment-210899" class="wp-caption-text">Figure 3. Evaluation workflow with two different ramifications for the two scenarios considered, namely Top-K selection and Relevance &#64; K evaluation</p>
</div>

<h3>Relevance &#64; K Evaluation</h3>
<p>Aside from the Top-K selection evaluation, we conducted a second type of evaluation that involves assessing the relevance of the retrieved results in the tags enrichment scenario. To perform the Relevance &#64; K Evaluation, we leverage the tag ranking, which serves as a reference for determining the relevance of the retrieved samples. We then check whether the tag ranking of a query sample is consistent with the Top-K most similar samples retrieved by our model using various scoring mechanisms, which we enumerate and briefly describe below:</p>
<ul><li><b>EM (Exact Match)</b> — outputs 1 if the inputs are equal. This is the strictest comparison method, serving as a lower bound for that performance.</li><li><b>IoU (Intersection over Union) or Jaccard index</b> — disregards element ordering and focuses solely on the presence of the items in the inputs.</li><li><b>NES (Normalized Edit Similarity)</b> — this function, based on <a href="https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/363958.363994" data-link-tracked="true" data-uw-rm-brl="PR" data-uw-original-href="https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/363958.363994">Damerau-Levenshtein distance (DL) for strings</a>, allows us to penalize differences in rank.</li></ul>
<p>The use of these mechanisms allowed us to measure the relevance and accuracy of our approach.</p>
<p>To construct the query and knowledge base subsets, we consider two scenarios: unsupervised labeling and counterfactual analysis. For the counterfactual analysis, we leverage the train-test temporal split (i.e., timestamp(test) &gt; timestamp(train)) in EMBER to simulate a real-world production-like scenario, where we use unseen but labeled data (i.e., the test set) to query the knowledge base. With the Unsupervised labeling scenario, given the unlabelled EMBER data, we aim to identify similar samples within the knowledge base, with the purpose of assigning a provisional label or tag.</p>
<p>In the Unsupervised labeling scenario, we achieved excellent results. Our model demonstrated the ability to accurately match queries with hits based on their tags, further validating the effectiveness of our approach. Similarly, In the counterfactual analysis scenario, we observe that leaf similarity achieves very good results in terms of matching queries and hits according to their tags. This indicates that our approach can successfully identify and retrieve relevant samples based on the leaf predictions.</p>
