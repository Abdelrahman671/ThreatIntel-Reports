<h1><strong>Dark Web Profile: 8Base Ransomware</strong></h1>
<p>In today’s cyber world, while the ransomware scene remains dynamic and active, new actors are emerging with significant numbers of victims. In this article, we will focus on <strong>8Base Ransomware</strong>, which ranked in the <strong>top 5 most active </strong><strong>groups</strong> last month according to Daily Dark Web, with <strong>37</strong> victim announcements in June.</p>
<figure><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" src="https://socradar.io/wp-content/uploads/2023/07/the-10-most-active-ransomware-groups-of-june-2023-724x1024.jpg.webp" alt="The 10 most active ransomware groups of June 2023 " width="680" height="961" /><figcaption><em>Figure 1. The 10 most active ransomware groups of June 2023 (Source: </em><a href="https://twitter.com/DailyDarkWeb/status/1676999451141865479"><em>Daily Dark Web</em></a><em>)</em></figcaption></figure>
</div>
<div class="text-editor-blog !max-w-full main-text-editor">
<h2>Who is 8Base?</h2>
<p>8Base is a ransomware group that has been active<strong> since April 2022</strong>. Despite its relatively recent emergence, the group has rapidly gained notoriety due to its aggressive tactics and the significant number of victims it has claimed. The group primarily targets small and medium-sized businesses (SMBs) across various sectors, including <strong>business services</strong>,<strong> finance</strong>, <a href="https://socradar.io/300-increase-in-the-number-of-dark-web-references-to-manufacturing-organizations/"><strong>manufacturing</strong></a>, and <strong>information technology</strong>.</p>
<figure><img decoding="async" src="https://socradar.io/wp-content/uploads/2023/07/first-attack-claim-shared-by-8base.png.webp" alt="First attack claim shared by 8Base " /><figcaption><em><em><em><em><em>Figure 2. First attack claim shared by 8Base</em></em></em></em></em></figcaption></figure>
<p>The group’s identity, methods, and motivations largely remain a mystery. However, based on its leak site and public accounts, along with the group’s communications, researchers think the group’s verbal style is quite similar to that of <strong>RansomHouse</strong>, a group that typically <strong>purchases already compromised data</strong> or works with data leak sites to extort victims. This has led to speculation that 8Base may be an offshoot of RansomHouse.</p>
<p>Another point of view is that 8Base was built directly with the <strong>leaked </strong><a href="https://socradar.io/what-is-babuk-the-ransomware-gang-you-should-know-about/"><strong>Babuk</strong></a><strong> builder</strong>:</p>
<figure><img decoding="async" src="https://socradar.io/wp-content/uploads/2023/07/8base-meme.png.webp" alt="8base meme" /><figcaption><em><em><em>Figure 3. (Source: </em><a href="https://blog.bushidotoken.net/2023/05/unmasking-ransomware-using-stylometric.html"><em>BushidoToken</em></a><em>)</em></em></em></figcaption></figure>
<p>Putting everything else aside, the group’s rapid rise in activity and the significant number of victims they have claimed have made them a major player in the ransomware landscape. They have been <strong>particularly active i</strong>n recent months, with a significant spike in their activities observed. This has led to them being ranked as one of the <strong>top performing </strong>ransom groups, further highlighting the threat they pose.</p>

<h2>How Does 8Base Ransomware Attack?</h2>
<p>8Base is known for its <strong>double-extortion</strong> tactics. The group threatens to publish the encrypted files unless the ransom is paid, aiming to embarrass the victim by exposing private or confidential information that could <strong>damage their brand or reputation</strong>. The use of the “double-extortion” tactic has become increasingly common among ransomware groups, as it adds an additional layer of pressure on the victims to pay the ransom.</p>
<p>The 8Base ransomware is thought to spread via:</p>
<ul>
<li>Phishing emails,</li>
<li>Exploit kits.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Which tools and vulnerabilities does 8Base Ransomware use?</h2>
<p>8Base uses a variety of ransomware strains, including a variant known as <strong>Phobos</strong>. The group has customized Phobos by appending<strong> ‘.8base’ </strong>to their encrypted files, but the format of the entire appended portion remains the same as Phobos, which includes an ID section, an email address, and then the file extension. This suggests that 8Base is leveraging<a href="https://socradar.io/what-is-ransomware-as-a-service-raas/"><strong> Ransomware-as-a-Service (RaaS)</strong></a> offerings, a common practice among ransomware groups.</p>

<p>From the FAQ text, several aspects can be inferred about the 8Base:</p>
<p><strong>Business-like Approach:</strong> This group operates in a structured, professional manner, with clear policies and procedures. They have dedicated channels for communication and even offer <strong>customer support</strong>, implying they have a well-organized infrastructure.</p>
<p><strong>Cooperation:</strong> The group is open to collaboration with others who share their ethos. They suggest willingness to negotiate ransom deals on behalf of others. However, they clearly state their rejection of any association with ultra-radical groups, indicating an attempt to maintain an image of <strong>ethicality</strong>.</p>
<p><strong>Ethical Line:</strong> The group claims to have ethical boundaries, refusing to engage with extremist groups or release data that violate moral principles. They attempt to legitimize their activities by drawing a line between what they perceive as<strong> ethical hacking </strong>and <strong>malicious activities</strong>.</p>
<p><strong>Respect for Journalism:</strong> They seem to value <strong>journalism </strong>and have special provisions for journalists, indicating their belief in<strong> information accessibility. </strong>This could also be a strategy to <strong>gain media attention</strong> and publicity.</p>
<p><strong>Victim Support:</strong> Surprisingly, they claim to <strong>offer help </strong>to victims of the companies they attack, suggesting that they <strong>remove </strong>personal data before making it public. They also offer data sets to individuals for potential lawsuits. This could be a strategy to frame their actions as <strong>‘just’ </strong>or <strong>‘helpful’</strong> to the public.</p>
<p><strong>Data Disclosure Process:</strong> The group follows a process where they first list a company as <strong>“Evidence”</strong> and later change the status to <strong>“Disclosed”.</strong> They provide the company an opportunity to <strong>prevent data disclosure</strong>, indicating a form of negotiation or potential ransom demand. This suggests they primarily aim at<strong> financial gain.</strong></p>
<figure><img decoding="async" src="https://socradar.io/wp-content/uploads/2023/07/the-remaining-part-of-8base-ransomware-tor-sites-faq-page-1024x435.png.webp" alt="The remaining part of 8Base Ransomware TOR site’s FAQ Page " /><figcaption><em><em><em><em><em><em>Figure 8. <h2>What are the Targets of 8Base Ransomware?</h2>
<p>8Base primarily<strong> targets small and medium-sized businesses (SMBs)</strong> across various sectors. The group seems to have a preference for <strong>certain industries</strong>, with businesses in the business services, finance, manufacturing, and information technology sectors being particularly targeted. This could be due to the perceived ability of businesses in these sectors to pay <strong>larger ransoms</strong>, or it could be due to the <strong>nature of their data</strong>, which may be more sensitive or valuable.</p>
<p>The group’s activities have spiked recently, with the group claiming the <strong>second-largest number of victims</strong> over the past 30 days, second only to the <a href="https://socradar.io/dark-web-profile-lockbit-3-0-ransomware/">LockBit 3.0</a> gang. In May 2023 alone, the group released data from <strong>67 victims</strong> they breached between April 2022 and May 2023. This rapid rise in activity and the significant number of victims they have claimed have made them a major player in the ransomware landscape.</p>

<h2>Conclusion</h2>
<p>8Base represents a <strong>new wave</strong><strong>of </strong>ransomware groups that are highly active, aggressive, and sophisticated. Their rapid rise in activity and the <strong>significant number of victims </strong>they have claimed have made them a <strong>major player</strong> in the ransomware landscape. Their use of <a href="https://socradar.io/evolution-of-ransomware-so-far-and-hereafter/#:~:text=The%20first%20step%20in%20multiple,backup%20and%20data%20recovery%20measures."><strong>double-extortion tactics</strong></a>, where they not only encrypt a victim’s data but also threaten to publish it unless the ransom is paid, adds an <strong>additional layer of pressure</strong> on the victims and makes their attacks even more damaging.</p>
<p>The group’s use of <strong>Ransomware-as-a-Service (RaaS)</strong> offerings and their sophisticated communication and public relations strategies further highlight their capabilities. Their ability to adapt and evolve their tactics and tools, along with their aggressive approach, make them a <strong>significant threat</strong> that businesses need to be aware of.</p>
<p>However, while the threat posed by 8Base is real and significant, it is not insurmountable. Businesses can protect themselves by implementing <strong>robust cybersecurity measures</strong>, including keeping their systems and software updated, training their employees to recognize and<a href="https://socradar.io/solutions/phishing-domain-detection/"> avoid phishing attacks</a>, and using advanced detection tools to identify and respond to threats quickly.</p>
