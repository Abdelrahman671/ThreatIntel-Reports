<h2 id="cyber-operations-and-the-russian-intelligence-services">Cyber operations and the Russian intelligence services</h2>

<p>Russia is one of the world’s most prolific cyber actors and dedicate significant resource into conducting cyber operations around the globe. The UK government has publicly attributed malign cyber activity to parts of three Russian Intelligence services: the <abbr title="Federal Security Service">FSB</abbr>, SVR and GRU, with each having their own remits.</p>

<p>A table of the parts of the Russian Intelligence Services that the UK Government has publicly attributed is below.</p>

<h3 id="russian-intelligence-services-cyber-organogram">Russian Intelligence Services cyber organogram</h3>

<figure class="image embedded"><div class="img"><img src="https://assets.publishing.service.gov.uk/media/6573487b049516000d49bf22/FSB-cyber-structure-organogram.svg" alt=""></div>
<figcaption><p>Organogram showing the Russian Intelligence Services cyber structure.</p></figcaption></figure>

<div class="call-to-action">
  <p>Text alternative for the organogram: the Russin Intelligence Services Cyber Structure includes:</p>

  <p><abbr title="Federal Security Service">FSB</abbr> (federal Security Service)</p>

  <ul>
    <li>
      <p>Centre 16L Centre of the collection of radio-electronic intelligence on communications facilities, post number 71330. Publicly known as Energetic bear, Berserk Bear, Dragonfly, IRON LIBERTY, CASSTLE, CROUCHING YETI, DYMALLOY, TG-4192, Snake, Venomous Bear, Turla, Uroboros</p>
    </li>
    <li>
      <p>Centre 18: Centre for Information Security (TsIB), military unit 64829. Publicly known as:  Star Blizzard, SEABORGIUM, Calisto Group, TA446, COLDRIVER, TAG:53, Blue Charlie</p>
    </li>
  </ul>

  <p>SVR (Foreign Intelligence Service), publicly known as APT29, Cosy Bear, The Dukes</p>

  <p>GRU (Military Intelligence)</p>

  <ul>
    <li>GRU 85th, field post number 26165. Publicly known as APT28, Fancy Bear, Strontium</li>
    <li>GRU GTsST: field post number 74455. Publicly known as: Sandworm</li>
  </ul>
</div>

<h2 id="the-fsb-cyber-programme">The <abbr title="Federal Security Service">FSB</abbr> cyber programme</h2>

<p>The <abbr title="Federal Security Service">FSB</abbr> (Federal Security Service; Russian: (Федеральная служба безопасности (ФСБ)) is Russia’s state security agency and the successor to the KGB. Since its formation in (1995 the <abbr title="Federal Security Service">FSB</abbr> has conducted electronic surveillance of equipment. The UK has exposed the involvement of 2 <abbr title="Federal Security Service">FSB</abbr> Centres in cyber activity directed against the UK.</p>

<ul>
  <li>Centre 16 supports Foreign Intelligence collection, as well as supporting the wider <abbr title="Federal Security Service">FSB</abbr> mission (to include protection of the constitution)</li>
  <li>Centre 18 sits within the Counter-Intelligence Service of the <abbr title="Federal Security Service">FSB</abbr> (Service 1)</li>
</ul>

<h2 id="fsb-centre-16">
<abbr title="Federal Security Service">FSB</abbr> Centre 16</h2>

<p><abbr title="Federal Security Service">FSB</abbr> Centre 16 (16-й Центр) is responsible for cyber operations including the intercepting, decrypting and processing of electronic messages, and the technical penetration of foreign targets. Its full title is the Centre for Radio-Electronic Intelligence by Means of Communication (TsRRSS; Russian: Центр радиоэлектронной разведки на средствах связи (ЦPPCC)) and is also known as “Military Unit 71330” (V/Ch 71330) (Bойсковая часть B/Ч 71330).</p>

<p>When the KGB was disbanded in 1991, the 16th Directorate of the KGB became FAPSI (Russian: ՓАПϹИ) or Federal Agency of Government Communications and Information (FAGCI) (Russian :Փедеральное Агентство Правительственной Ϲвязи и Информации), a Russian government agency, which was responsible for signals intelligence (SIGINT) and security of governmental communications.</p>

<p>In 2003, FAPSI was dissolved, and the 3rd Main Department of FAPSI (responsible for SIGINT) was transferred to the <abbr title="Federal Security Service">FSB</abbr> forming the basis for <abbr title="Federal Security Service">FSB</abbr> Centre 16.</p>

<p>The emblem of <abbr title="Federal Security Service">FSB</abbr> Centre 16 hints at its activities in cyberspace: a satellite dish (signifying SIGINT activity) and a key, broken by lightning, (signifying the breaking of an encryption key) are both present.</p>

<figure class="image embedded"><div class="img"><img src="https://assets.publishing.service.gov.uk/media/62473c53e90e075f06b372ed/emblem.PNG" alt=""></div>
<figcaption><p>Emblem of FSB Centre 16</p></figcaption></figure>

<h3 id="cyber-operations-conducted-by-fsb-centre-16">Cyber operations conducted by <abbr title="Federal Security Service">FSB</abbr> Centre 16</h3>

<p><abbr title="Federal Security Service">FSB</abbr> Centre 16 has been observed conducting cyber operations since at least 2010. They conducted significant campaigns against the energy sector in 2014 and the aviation sector in 2020.</p>

<p><abbr title="Federal Security Service">FSB</abbr> Centre 16 is also responsible for snake malware and its variants which have been a core component in espionage operations conducted by Centre 16 for nearly 2 decades. The implant has been used to collect sensitive information from specific targets, such as government networks, research facilities and journalists, with Snake infrastructure identified in more than 50 countries across the world.</p>

<h3 id="cyber-operations-against-worldwide-critical-national-infrastructure">Cyber operations against worldwide critical national infrastructure</h3>

<p>Centre 16 of the <abbr title="Federal Security Service">FSB</abbr> have targeted/gained unauthorised access systems in countries around the world that are necessary for a country to function and upon which daily life depends. Known as Critical National Infrastructure or CNI, Centre 16 has targeted systems essential for energy, healthcare, finance, education and local/national governments. This has been a concerted campaign over many years and in a wide range of countries across Europe, the Americas and Asia.</p>

<p>The National Cyber Security Centre (<abbr title="National Cyber Security Centre">NCSC</abbr>) and cyber security companies have warned network defenders on multiple occasions of the risks posed by this pattern of activity. While there has been speculation of <abbr title="Federal Security Service">FSB</abbr> involvement, the UK government is confirming this activity was carried out by <abbr title="Federal Security Service">FSB</abbr> Centre 16 and providing further details of specific examples of this activity to increase awareness and transparency around the threat.</p>

<h2 id="fsb-centre-18">
<abbr title="Federal Security Service">FSB</abbr> Centre 18</h2>

<p><abbr title="Federal Security Service">FSB</abbr> Centre 18 is also known as the Centre for Information Security (TsIB) Military Unit 64829.  Centre 18 sits within the <abbr title="Federal Security Service">FSB</abbr> 1st Service (Counter-intelligence Service). <abbr title="National Cyber Security Centre">NCSC</abbr> assesses that Star Blizzard is almost certainly subordinate to the Russian Federal  Security Service (<abbr title="Federal Security Service">FSB</abbr>) Centre 18.</p>

<p>Star Blizzard have conducted cyber espionage operations targeting the UK, including key parts of the democratic and political process. The group have also selectively leaked and amplified the release of information in line with Russian information confrontation priorities, including to undermine trust in politics in the UK and likeminded states.</p>

<p><abbr title="National Cyber Security Centre">NCSC</abbr> has warned of the risks posed by this pattern of activity, including in a technical advisory in January. While there has been speculation of <abbr title="Federal Security Service">FSB</abbr> involvement, the UK government is confirming this activity was carried out by a group subordinate to <abbr title="Federal Security Service">FSB</abbr> Centre 18 and providing further details of specific examples of this activity to increase awareness and transparency around the threat.</p>
