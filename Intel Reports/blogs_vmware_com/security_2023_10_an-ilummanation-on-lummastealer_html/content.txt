<h2><b>Executive Summary </b></h2>
<p>LummaStealer is a Malware-as-a-Service (MaaS) that is available on the dark web. MAAS models have substantially reduced the barrier to entry in the realm of big game hunting.  Instead of executing malspam and phishing campaigns directly, developers offer their platforms to other threat actors through an ecosystem of forums on both the public internet and the Dark Web.  The malware marketplace has evolved in some places &#8211; transitioning from exclusive and highly technical requirements to advertisements that highlight improved user experience, ease of use, and ongoing development to evade antivirus detection.  Emerging threat actors now have access to professionally crafted malware that comes with guaranteed troubleshooting and customer support.</p>
<h2><b>History</b></h2>
<p>Lumma Stealer was initially detected by security researchers in mid-to-late 2022 when it was advertised on Dark Web forums.  Although its promotion began on exclusive underground platforms, we’ve since noticed its advertisements transitioning to Clear Net hacking forums.</p>
<p>Throughout its existence, Lumma Stealer’s affiliates have employed a mix of intensive reconnaissance and targeted spear-phishing campaigns.  In addition, they’ve utilized broader strategies, such as software phishing sites that mimic popular applications and platforms to deceive its users, ultimately leading to the distribution of Lumma Stealer.  Most recently, in 2023, Lumma Stealer has been observed being distributed through drive-by-downloads, masquerading as browser updates, and through deceptive sites offering game downloads and software cracks.</p>

<a href="https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/10/Screenshot-2023-10-17-at-3.04.52-PM.png"><img decoding="async" loading="lazy" class="alignnone size-large wp-image-83633" src="https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/10/Screenshot-2023-10-17-at-3.04.52-PM-1024x614.png" alt="" width="1024" height="614" srcset="https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/10/Screenshot-2023-10-17-at-3.04.52-PM-1024x614.png 1024w, https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/10/Screenshot-2023-10-17-at-3.04.52-PM-300x180.png 300w, https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/10/Screenshot-2023-10-17-at-3.04.52-PM-768x461.png 768w, https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/10/Screenshot-2023-10-17-at-3.04.52-PM-600x360.png 600w, https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/10/Screenshot-2023-10-17-at-3.04.52-PM.png 1034w" sizes="(max-width: 1024px) 100vw, 1024px" /></a></p>
<p>These websites promote “100% working product keys” and serial numbers.  When a victim visits one of these malicious URLs a text file is provided that prompts the user to visit a TINYURL link with a download to the malicious binary.</p>
<p><a href="https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/10/Screenshot-2023-10-17-at-3.04.04-PM.png"><img decoding="async" loading="lazy" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-83632" src="https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/10/Screenshot-2023-10-17-at-3.04.04-PM.png" alt="" width="904" height="418" srcset="https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/10/Screenshot-2023-10-17-at-3.04.04-PM.png 904w, https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/10/Screenshot-2023-10-17-at-3.04.04-PM-300x139.png 300w, https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/10/Screenshot-2023-10-17-at-3.04.04-PM-768x355.png 768w, https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/10/Screenshot-2023-10-17-at-3.04.04-PM-600x277.png 600w" sizes="(max-width: 904px) 100vw, 904px" /></a></p>
<h2><b>Dark Web</b></h2>
<h3><b>Sellers &amp; Origins</b></h3>
<p>LummaStealer is being sold widely across numerous Dark Web and hacker forums.  While researching Lumma, the VMware Carbon Black team discovered that there are several sellers for this malware. While we cannot definitively say how many sellers there are, at the time of research our team found several seller accounts that go by “LummaStealer”, “LummaC2”, “Lumma”, “Lumma Stealer”, “Lummaseller126”, and “lummanowork”.  Some of these accounts may be run by the same person or group.</p>

<h2><b>IDAT Loader</b></h2>
<p>The IDAT loader files are contained in the downloaded <b>DirectX12AdvancedSupport.msi </b>package.  The “IDAT” name originates from the loader’s use of a PNG file format’s IDAT chunk to store its payload.  The loader consists of a DLL, an executable, and a PNG.  These three files can change between instances of the loader – potentially as a method to evade detection – but they appear to always adhere to the following pattern:</p>
<ul>
<li aria-level="1">The executable is a signed, legitimate file that is vulnerable to DLL load order hijacking</li>
<li aria-level="1">The DLL file impersonates a legitimate DLL required by the executable but, in fact, contains malicious code</li>
<li aria-level="1">The PNG file contains an XOR encoded payload within its IDAT chunk</li>
</ul>
<p>In a slight deviation from previously analyzed versions of the IDAT loader, Carbon Black noted a new executable and DLL pairing packaged in one version of the MSI.  Additionally, a <b>.mov</b> file extension was used to disguise the malicious PNG file, a technique that also differed from other attacks.  This particular version of the MSI package contained a legitimate InstallShield Setup Suite Launcher executable file, <b>suiteappxhelper.exe</b>, published by Flexera, a malicious version of the Microsoft Windows <b>xmllite.dll</b>, and a PNG file with a <b>.mov</b> extension &#8211; <b>samarskite.mov</b>.</p>
<p><a href="https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/10/Screenshot-2023-10-17-at-2.50.41-PM.png"><img decoding="async" loading="lazy" class="alignnone wp-image-83621" src="https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/10/Screenshot-2023-10-17-at-2.50.41-PM-1024x350.png" alt="" width="850" height="291" srcset="https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/10/Screenshot-2023-10-17-at-2.50.41-PM-1024x350.png 1024w, https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/10/Screenshot-2023-10-17-at-2.50.41-PM-300x103.png 300w, https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/10/Screenshot-2023-10-17-at-2.50.41-PM-768x263.png 768w, https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/10/Screenshot-2023-10-17-at-2.50.41-PM-600x205.png 600w, https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/10/Screenshot-2023-10-17-at-2.50.41-PM.png 1228w" sizes="(max-width: 850px) 100vw, 850px" /></a></p>
<p>While the contents of this MSI package were unique, the loader’s behavior remained the same.  The IDAT loader behavior Carbon Black observed aligns with <a href="https://www.rapid7.com/blog/post/2023/08/31/fake-update-utilizes-new-idat-loader-to-execute-stealc-and-lumma-infostealers/">Rapid7’s</a> earlier findings.  DLL load order hijacking is used to load the malicious DLL into the legitimate executable (in this case the InstallShield launcher) and co-opt it.  Once the malicious DLL is loaded, it decodes the payload contained in the PNG file and injects the decoded payload into another DLL, <b>mshtml.dll</b>.  The <b>mshtml.dll</b> code is loaded into a second instance of <b>suiteappxhelper.exe</b>, and once it’s executed, it creates and injects into a <b>cmd.exe </b>process.</p>

<h2><b>LummaC2 Payload</b></h2>
<p>The injected <b>cmd.exe</b> creates a file with a randomized name in the <a href="https://dashboard.confer.net/cb/investigate/processes?query=%28filemod_name%3Ac%5C%3A%5C%5Cusers%5C%5Ctbrady%5C%5Cappdata%5C%5Clocal%5C%5Ctemp%5C%5Cywbdapunyjua%29&amp;searchWindow=ALL">c:\users\&lt;user&gt;\appdata\local\temp</a> path.  The cmd.exe process then invokes <b>conhost.exe</b> using “conhost.exe \??\C:\Windows\system32\conhost.exe 0xffffffff -ForceV1” – this technique will run conhost.exe in legacy mode and connect it with the first available console session regardless if attached to a physical console.  Second, <b>cmd.exe</b> injects the LummaC2 payload into <b>explorer.exe</b> using the technique of process doppelgänging.</p>
<p><a href="https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/10/Screenshot-2023-10-17-at-2.48.30-PM.png"><img decoding="async" loading="lazy" class="alignnone size-large wp-image-83619" src="https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/10/Screenshot-2023-10-17-at-2.48.30-PM-1024x219.png" alt="" width="1024" height="219" srcset="https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/10/Screenshot-2023-10-17-at-2.48.30-PM-1024x219.png 1024w, https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/10/Screenshot-2023-10-17-at-2.48.30-PM-300x64.png 300w, https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/10/Screenshot-2023-10-17-at-2.48.30-PM-768x164.png 768w, https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/10/Screenshot-2023-10-17-at-2.48.30-PM-600x129.png 600w, https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/10/Screenshot-2023-10-17-at-2.48.30-PM.png 1354w" sizes="(max-width: 1024px) 100vw, 1024px" /></a></p>
<p>Once injected into, <b>explorer.exe</b> collects environment information, modifies several registry keys, and attempts to reach out to a command and control server to pull down the final payload.</p>
<p><a href="https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/10/Screenshot-2023-10-17-at-2.45.57-PM.png"><img decoding="async" loading="lazy" class="alignnone size-large wp-image-83618" src="https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/10/Screenshot-2023-10-17-at-2.45.57-PM-1024x319.png" alt="" width="1024" height="319" srcset="https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/10/Screenshot-2023-10-17-at-2.45.57-PM-1024x319.png 1024w, https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/10/Screenshot-2023-10-17-at-2.45.57-PM-300x94.png 300w, https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/10/Screenshot-2023-10-17-at-2.45.57-PM-768x239.png 768w, https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/10/Screenshot-2023-10-17-at-2.45.57-PM-600x187.png 600w, https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/10/Screenshot-2023-10-17-at-2.45.57-PM.png 1270w" sizes="(max-width: 1024px) 100vw, 1024px" /></a></p>
<p>We’ve seen the following domains and IP addresses contacted in this final stage:</p>
<ul>
<li aria-level="1">lindodeusercontent[.]com</li>
<li aria-level="1">fibrodoorsbig[.]xyz</li>
<li aria-level="1">doorblu[.]xyz</li>
<li aria-level="1">lpsserversonlene[.]xyz</li>
</ul>
<h2><b>Carbon Black Detection </b></h2>
<p>LummaStealer has been observed by Carbon Black with attacks all at different stages.  We observed a more advanced stage of  the LummaStealer attack, a malicious copy of <b>chromesetup.exe</b> was seen dropping the batch file <b>64.bat</b>.<b>  </b>This copy of <b>chromesetup.exe </b>was observed with the publisher “Paramount Software UK Ltd”.</p>
<p><a href="https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/10/Screenshot-2023-10-17-at-2.44.40-PM.png"><img decoding="async" loading="lazy" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-83617" src="https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/10/Screenshot-2023-10-17-at-2.44.40-PM.png" alt="" width="690" height="290" srcset="https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/10/Screenshot-2023-10-17-at-2.44.40-PM.png 690w, https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/10/Screenshot-2023-10-17-at-2.44.40-PM-300x126.png 300w, https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/10/Screenshot-2023-10-17-at-2.44.40-PM-600x252.png 600w" sizes="(max-width: 690px) 100vw, 690px" /></a></p>
<p><em>Figure 1. Chromesetup.exe creates 64.bat</em></p>
<p>Threat actors commonly use batch files to execute various commands, disable security features, deliver payloads, modify systems, or conduct phishing attacks.  In this instance we see <b>64.bat</b> being dropped to invoke <b>cmd.exe. </b>This in turn invokes <b>powershell.exe. Powershell.exe </b>and <b>cmd.exe </b>are used in Windows environments to run commands or scripts to get programs to run in certain ways or to output certain information (such as OS version or users in an environment).</p>
<p><a href="https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/10/Screenshot-2023-10-17-at-2.43.12-PM.png"><img decoding="async" loading="lazy" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-83616" src="https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/10/Screenshot-2023-10-17-at-2.43.12-PM.png" alt="" width="768" height="230" srcset="https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/10/Screenshot-2023-10-17-at-2.43.12-PM.png 768w, https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/10/Screenshot-2023-10-17-at-2.43.12-PM-300x90.png 300w, https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/10/Screenshot-2023-10-17-at-2.43.12-PM-600x180.png 600w" sizes="(max-width: 768px) 100vw, 768px" /></a></p>
<p><em>Figure 2. 64.bat invokes cmd.exe</em></p>
<p>It is then observed that <b>powershell.exe</b> runs the command that downloads the malicious MSI dropper named <b>DirectX12AdvancedSupport.msi</b> from a remote server, <b>ocmtancmi2c5t[.]life</b>, and saves it as <b>C:\ProgramData\DirectX12AdvancedSupport.msi</b>.</p>
<p><b>DirectX12</b> is a Windows application and is normally updated through Windows Update and should be found in the file path <b>C:\Windows\System32</b>. In another observed event, the PowerShell command connects to the remote server, <b>ocmtancmi2c5t[.]xyz</b>, to download the malicious application which was observed as <b>DirectX12AdvancedSupport.msi</b> as well.</p>
<p><a href="https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/10/Screenshot-2023-10-17-at-2.41.48-PM.png"><img decoding="async" loading="lazy" class="alignnone size-large wp-image-83615" src="https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/10/Screenshot-2023-10-17-at-2.41.48-PM-1024x89.png" alt="" width="1024" height="89" srcset="https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/10/Screenshot-2023-10-17-at-2.41.48-PM-1024x89.png 1024w, https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/10/Screenshot-2023-10-17-at-2.41.48-PM-300x26.png 300w, https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/10/Screenshot-2023-10-17-at-2.41.48-PM-768x67.png 768w, https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/10/Screenshot-2023-10-17-at-2.41.48-PM-600x52.png 600w, https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/10/Screenshot-2023-10-17-at-2.41.48-PM.png 1312w" sizes="(max-width: 1024px) 100vw, 1024px" /></a></p>
<p><em>Figure 3. Alert Triage tree</em></p>
<p><a href="https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/10/Screenshot-2023-10-17-at-2.40.23-PM.png"><img decoding="async" loading="lazy" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-83614" src="https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/10/Screenshot-2023-10-17-at-2.40.23-PM.png" alt="" width="586" height="396" srcset="https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/10/Screenshot-2023-10-17-at-2.40.23-PM.png 586w, https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/10/Screenshot-2023-10-17-at-2.40.23-PM-300x203.png 300w" sizes="(max-width: 586px) 100vw, 586px" /></a></p>
<p><em>Figure 4. Powershell.exe downloads DirectX12AdvancedSupport.msi from ocmtancmi2c5t[.]life</em></p>
<p>The command then checks if the file is fully downloaded and, once verified, silently installs the MSI package on the system using “msiexec.exe /i”.  Keep in mind that the MSI dropper can be named whatever the threat actor wants, but in this instance, it is named <b>DirectX12AdvancedSupport </b>and is not associated with DirectX whatsoever.</p>
<p><b>PowerShell Command:</b></p>
<p><b><a href="https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/10/Screenshot-2023-10-17-at-2.37.28-PM.png"><img decoding="async" loading="lazy" class="alignnone size-large wp-image-83613" src="https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/10/Screenshot-2023-10-17-at-2.37.28-PM-1024x397.png" alt="" width="1024" height="397" srcset="https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/10/Screenshot-2023-10-17-at-2.37.28-PM-1024x397.png 1024w, https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/10/Screenshot-2023-10-17-at-2.37.28-PM-300x116.png 300w, https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/10/Screenshot-2023-10-17-at-2.37.28-PM-768x298.png 768w, https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/10/Screenshot-2023-10-17-at-2.37.28-PM-940x363.png 940w, https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/10/Screenshot-2023-10-17-at-2.37.28-PM-600x232.png 600w, https://blogs.vmware.com/security/files/2023/10/Screenshot-2023-10-17-at-2.37.28-PM.png 1172w" sizes="(max-width: 1024px) 100vw, 1024px" /></a></b></p>
<p>In another attack, it was seen that a C2 server was set up as <b>doorblu[.]xyz</b>.  A C2 server is normally set up by a threat actor in an attempt to create persistence, to launch their attacks, and to even exfiltrate data. In this event, there were no observations of any data being exfiltrated or any other use of the C2 server &#8211; only that it was set up.</p>

<h1><b>Summary</b></h1>
<p>LummaStealer, a malware-as-a-service, varies widely in attack potential and command-and-control domains. A new update allows random generation of LummaStealer builds, enhancing obfuscation, and complicating detection by traditional AV software relying on hashes. Due to its customizable nature, each LummaStealer attack differs leading to unique indicators of compromise including naming conventions, contacted domains, and specific artifacts.</p>
<p>Carbon Black adopts a different approach, focusing on both pre and post-exploit defense. This strategy proves more effective against LummaStealer, ensuring detection, prevention, and containment.</p>
<p>Carbon Black is particularly effective against LummaStealer due to its innovative approach to endpoint security. Unlike traditional antivirus (AV) solutions that rely solely on static signatures or hashes to detect malware, Carbon Black utilizes advanced techniques and behavioral analysis for threat detection.  Here&#8217;s why Carbon Black is a superior endpoint protection to use against LummaStealer:</p>
<ul>
<li aria-level="1"><strong>Dynamic Detection Methods:</strong> Carbon Black employs dynamic detection methods, such as behavioral analysis and machine learning algorithms, to identify malicious behavior patterns.  This proactive approach allows it to detect new and evolving threats such as LummaStealer even when their specific signatures or hashes are unknown.</li>
<li aria-level="1"><strong>Focus on Pre and Post-Exploit Defense:</strong> Carbon Black focuses on both pre-exploit and post-exploit defense.  While traditional AVs primarily concentrate on pre-exploit measures, Carbon Black also monitors activities after a potential breach.  This comprehensive approach enables it to identify and mitigate LummaStealer&#8217;s malicious activities throughout the attack lifecycle.</li>
<li aria-level="1"><strong>Adaptability to Unique Attacks:</strong> LummaStealer, being a malware-as-a-service, allows attackers to customize their attacks resulting in unique configurations for each instance. Carbon Black&#8217;s adaptive and behavioral analysis can recognize these custom configurations and detect LummaStealer variants regardless of the specific parameters set by the attacker.</li>
<li aria-level="1"><strong>Containment Capabilities:</strong> Carbon Black not only detects malware but also offers effective containment measures.  When LummaStealer is detected, Carbon Black can isolate the infected system preventing the malware from spreading further within the network. This containment feature helps prevent widespread damage and data breaches.</li>
<li aria-level="1"><strong>Continuous Updates and Threat Intelligence:</strong> Carbon Black continuously updates its threat intelligence database, incorporating information about emerging threats and attack techniques including those used by LummaStealer.  This up-to-date knowledge enhances its ability to recognize and thwart the latest variants of the malware.</li>
<li aria-level="1"><strong>Adaptive Response:</strong> Carbon Black&#8217;s Managed Detection and Response products provide an adaptive response mechanism. In the event of a LummaStealer attack, it can respond dynamically, adapting its defense strategies based on the evolving threat landscape.  This adaptability is crucial in dealing with constantly changing malware tactics.</li>
<li aria-level="1"><strong>Managed Threat Hunting:</strong> Carbon Black’s newly released Managed Threat Hunting product provides proactive threat hunting on emerging threats. The Managed Threat Hunting product’s unique approach to detection and response allows it to quickly detect and respond to threats, including LummaStealer.</li>
</ul>
<p>In summary, Carbon Black&#8217;s advanced, dynamic, and comprehensive approach to threat detection and response makes it highly effective against LummaStealer and other sophisticated malware threats. Carbon Black’s ability to adapt, analyze behavior, and contain attacks sets it apart as a robust solution in the fight against evolving cyber threats.</p>            </div>
