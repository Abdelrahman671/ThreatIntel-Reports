													<h2 id="introduction">Introduction</h2>
<p>Knowledge is our best weapon in the fight against cybercrime. An understanding of how various gangs operate and what tools they use helps build competent defenses and investigate incidents. This report takes a close look at the history of the Cuba group, and their attack tactics, techniques and procedures. We hope this article will help you to stay one step ahead of threats like this one.</p>
<h2 id="cuba-ransomware-gang">Cuba ransomware gang</h2>
<div id="attachment_110536" style="width: 1034px" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><a href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2023/09/08140902/Cuba_ransomware_01.png" class="magnificImage"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" aria-describedby="caption-attachment-110536" class="size-large wp-image-110536" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2023/09/08140902/Cuba_ransomware_01-1024x628.png" alt="Cuba data leak site" width="1024" height="628" srcset="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2023/09/08140902/Cuba_ransomware_01-1024x628.png 1024w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2023/09/08140902/Cuba_ransomware_01-300x184.png 300w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2023/09/08140902/Cuba_ransomware_01-768x471.png 768w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2023/09/08140902/Cuba_ransomware_01-286x175.png 286w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2023/09/08140902/Cuba_ransomware_01-370x227.png 370w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2023/09/08140902/Cuba_ransomware_01-457x280.png 457w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2023/09/08140902/Cuba_ransomware_01-800x490.png 800w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2023/09/08140902/Cuba_ransomware_01.png 1320w" sizes="(max-width: 1024px) 100vw, 1024px" /></a><p id="caption-attachment-110536" class="wp-caption-text">Cuba data leak site</p></div>
<p>The group&#8217;s offensives first got on our radar in late 2020. Back then, the cybercriminals had not yet adopted the moniker &#8220;Cuba&#8221;; they were known as &#8220;Tropical Scorpius&#8221;.</p>
<p>Cuba mostly targets organizations in the United States, Canada and Europe. The gang has scored a series of resonant attacks on oil companies, <a href="https://www.bleepingcomputer.com/news/security/us-cities-disclose-data-breaches-after-vendors-ransomware-attack/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">financial services</a>, <a href="https://techcrunch.com/2022/08/31/montenegro-ransomware-attack-embassy-warning/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">government agencies</a> and healthcare providers.</p>
<p>As with most cyberextortionists lately, the Cuba gang encrypts victims&#8217; files and demands a ransom in exchange for a decryption key. The gang infamously uses complex tactics and techniques to penetrate victim networks, such as exploitation of software vulnerabilities and social engineering. They have been known to use compromised remote desktop (RDP) connections for initial access.</p>
<p>The Cuba gang&#8217;s exact origins and the identities of its members are unknown, although some researchers believe it might be a successor to another ill-famed extortion gang, Babuk. The Cuba group, like many others of its kind, is a ransomware-as-a-service (RaaS) outfit, letting its partners use the ransomware and associated infrastructure in exchange for a share of any ransom they collect.</p>
<p>The group has changed names several times since its inception. We are currently aware of the following aliases it has used:</p>
<ul>
<li>ColdDraw</li>
<li>Tropical Scorpius</li>
<li>Fidel</li>
<li>Cuba</li>
</ul>
<p>This past February, we came across another name for the gang — &#8220;V Is Vendetta&#8221;, which deviated from the hackers&#8217; favorite Cuban theme. This might have been a moniker used by a sub-group or affiliate.</p>
<p>There is an obvious connection with the Cuba gang: the newly discovered group&#8217;s website is hosted in the Cuba domain:</p>
<p style="font-size: 85%"><em>http[:]//test[.]cuba4ikm4jakjgmkezytyawtdgr2xymvy6nvzgw5cglswg3si76icnqd[.]onion/</em></p>

<p>Cuba remains active as at the time of writing this, and we keep hearing about new extortion victims.</p>
<h2 id="victimology">Victimology</h2>
<p><em>In this section, we used data consensually provided by our users and information about victims from open sources, such as other security vendors&#8217; reports and the data leak site of the ransomware gang itself.</em></p>
<p>The group has attacked numerous companies around the world. Industry affiliation does not seem to be a factor: victims have included retailers, financial and logistical services, government agencies, manufacturers, and others. In terms of geography, most of the attacked companies have been located in the United States, but there have been victims in Canada, Europe, Asia and Australia.</p>

<h2 id="ransomware">Ransomware</h2>
<p>The Cuba ransomware is a single file without additional libraries. Samples often have a forged compilation timestamp: those found in 2020 were stamped with June 4, 2020, and more recent ones, June 19th, 1992.</p>
<h2 id="cuba-extortion-model">Cuba extortion model</h2>
<div id="attachment_110539" style="width: 955px" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><a href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2023/09/08141130/Cuba_ransomware_04.png" class="magnificImage"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" aria-describedby="caption-attachment-110539" class="size-large wp-image-110539" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2023/09/08141130/Cuba_ransomware_04-945x1024.png" alt="Extortion models" width="945" height="1024" srcset="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2023/09/08141130/Cuba_ransomware_04-945x1024.png 945w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2023/09/08141130/Cuba_ransomware_04-277x300.png 277w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2023/09/08141130/Cuba_ransomware_04-768x832.png 768w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2023/09/08141130/Cuba_ransomware_04-161x175.png 161w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2023/09/08141130/Cuba_ransomware_04-370x401.png 370w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2023/09/08141130/Cuba_ransomware_04-258x280.png 258w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2023/09/08141130/Cuba_ransomware_04-800x867.png 800w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2023/09/08141130/Cuba_ransomware_04.png 979w" sizes="(max-width: 945px) 100vw, 945px" /></a><p id="caption-attachment-110539" class="wp-caption-text">Extortion models</p></div>
<p>Four extortion models exist today in terms of tools used for pressuring the victim.</p>
<ul>
<li>Single extortion: encrypting data and demanding a ransom just for decryption.</li>
<li>Double extortion: besides encrypting, attackers steal sensitive information. They threaten to both withhold the encryption key and publish the stolen information online unless the victim pays up. This is the most popular model among ransomware gangs today.</li>
<li>Triple extortion: adding a threat to expose the victim&#8217;s internal infrastructure to DDoS attacks. The model became widespread after the LockBit gang got <a href="https://techcrunch.com/2022/08/22/entrust-lockbit-ddos-ransomware/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">DDoS&#8217;ed</a>, possibly by a victim. After getting targeted, the hackers realized that DDoS was an effective pressure tool, something they <a href="https://www.bleepingcomputer.com/news/security/lockbit-ransomware-gang-gets-aggressive-with-triple-extortion-tactic/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">stated openly</a>, setting an example for others. To be fair, <a href="https://www.bleepingcomputer.com/news/security/ransomware-gangs-add-ddos-attacks-to-their-extortion-arsenal/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">isolated cases of triple extortion</a> predate the LockBit case.</li>
<li>The fourth model is the least common one, as it implies maximum pressure and is thus more costly. It adds spreading news of the breach among the victim&#8217;s investors, shareholders and customers. DDoS attacks in that case are not necessary. This model is exemplified by the recent <a href="https://www.bleepingcomputer.com/news/security/ransomware-gang-hijacks-university-alert-system-to-issue-threats/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">hack of Bluefield University in Virginia</a>, where the AvosLocker ransomware gang hijacked the school&#8217;s emergency broadcast system to send students and staff SMS texts and email alerts that their personal data had been stolen. The hackers urged not to trust the school&#8217;s management, who they said were concealing the true scale of the breach, and to make the situation public knowledge as soon as possible.</li>
</ul>
<p>The Cuba group is using the classic double extortion model, encrypting data with the Xsalsa20 symmetric algorithm, and the encryption key, with the RSA-2048 asymmetric algorithm. This is known as hybrid encryption, a cryptographically secure method that prevents decryption without the key.</p>
<p>Cuba ransomware samples avoid encrypting files with the following name extensions: .exe, .dll, .sys, .ini, .lnk, .vbm and .cuba, and the following folders:</p>
<ul>
<li>\windows\</li>
<li>\program files\microsoft office\</li>
<li>\program files (x86)\microsoft office\</li>
<li>\program files\avs\</li>
<li>\program files (x86)\avs\</li>
<li>\$recycle.bin\</li>
<li>\boot\</li>
<li>\recovery\</li>
<li>\system volume information\</li>
<li>\msocache\</li>
<li>\users\all users\</li>
<li>\users\default user\</li>
<li>\users\default\</li>
<li>\temp\</li>
<li>\inetcache\</li>
<li>\google\</li>
</ul>
<p>The ransomware saves time by searching for, and encrypting, Microsoft Office documents, images, archives and others in the %AppData%\Microsoft\Windows\Recent\ directory, rather than all files on the device. It also terminates all SQL services to encrypt any available databases. It looks for data both locally and inside network shares.</p>

<p>Besides encrypting, the group steals sensitive data that it discovers inside the victim&#8217;s organization. The type of data that the hackers are after depends on the industry that the target company is active in, but in most cases, they exfiltrate the following:</p>
<ul>
<li>Financial documents</li>
<li>Bank statements</li>
<li>Company accounts details</li>
<li>Source code, if the company is a software developer</li>
</ul>

<h2 id="profits">Profits</h2>
<p>The incoming and outgoing payments in the bitcoin wallets whose identifiers the hackers provide in their ransom notes exceed a total of 3,600 BTC, or more than $103,000,000 converted at the rate of $28,624 for 1 BTC. The gang owns numerous wallets, constantly transferring funds between these, and uses bitcoin mixers: services that send bitcoins through a series of anonymous transactions to make the origin of the funds harder to trace.</p>

<div id="attachment_110553" style="width: 1034px" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><a href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2023/09/08142027/Cuba_ransomware_18.png" class="magnificImage"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" aria-describedby="caption-attachment-110553" class="size-large wp-image-110553" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2023/09/08142027/Cuba_ransomware_18-1024x260.png" alt="Malicious process starting " width="1024" height="260" srcset="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2023/09/08142027/Cuba_ransomware_18-1024x260.png 1024w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2023/09/08142027/Cuba_ransomware_18-300x76.png 300w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2023/09/08142027/Cuba_ransomware_18-768x195.png 768w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2023/09/08142027/Cuba_ransomware_18-1536x389.png 1536w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2023/09/08142027/Cuba_ransomware_18-691x175.png 691w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2023/09/08142027/Cuba_ransomware_18-370x94.png 370w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2023/09/08142027/Cuba_ransomware_18-1105x280.png 1105w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2023/09/08142027/Cuba_ransomware_18-800x203.png 800w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2023/09/08142027/Cuba_ransomware_18.png 2048w" sizes="(max-width: 1024px) 100vw, 1024px" /></a><p id="caption-attachment-110553" class="wp-caption-text">Malicious process starting</p></div>
<p><strong>Veeamp.exe </strong>is a custom-built data dumper written in C#, which leverages security flaws in the Veeam backup and recovery service to connect to the VeeamBackup SQL database and grab account credentials.</p>
<div id="attachment_110554" style="width: 1034px" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><a href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2023/09/08142101/Cuba_ransomware_19.png" class="magnificImage"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" aria-describedby="caption-attachment-110554" class="size-large wp-image-110554" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2023/09/08142101/Cuba_ransomware_19-1024x619.png" alt="Analysis of Veeamp" width="1024" height="619" srcset="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2023/09/08142101/Cuba_ransomware_19-1024x619.png 1024w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2023/09/08142101/Cuba_ransomware_19-300x181.png 300w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2023/09/08142101/Cuba_ransomware_19-768x464.png 768w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2023/09/08142101/Cuba_ransomware_19-330x200.png 330w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2023/09/08142101/Cuba_ransomware_19-290x175.png 290w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2023/09/08142101/Cuba_ransomware_19-370x224.png 370w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2023/09/08142101/Cuba_ransomware_19-463x280.png 463w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2023/09/08142101/Cuba_ransomware_19-800x483.png 800w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2023/09/08142101/Cuba_ransomware_19.png 1223w" sizes="(max-width: 1024px) 100vw, 1024px" /></a><p id="caption-attachment-110554" class="wp-caption-text">Analysis of Veeamp</p></div>
<p><strong>Veeamp </strong>exploits the following Veeam vulnerabilities: CVE-2022-26500, CVE-2022-26501, CVE-2022-26504. The first two allow an unauthenticated user to remotely execute arbitrary code, and the third one, lets domain users do the same. After any of the three are exploited, the malware outputs the following in the control panel:</p>
<ul>
<li>User name</li>
<li>Encrypted password</li>
<li>Decrypted password</li>
<li>User description in the Credentials table of Veeam: group membership, permissions and so on</li>
</ul>
<p>The malware is not exclusive to the Cuba gang. We spotted it also in attacks by other groups, such as Conti and <a href="https://securelist.com/how-to-recover-files-encrypted-by-yanluowang/106332/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Yanluowang</a>.</p>

<h4 id="avast-anti-rootkit-driver">Avast Anti-Rootkit driver</h4>
<p>After Bughatch successfully established a connection to its C2, we watched as the group used an increasingly popular technique: Bring Your Own Vulnerable Driver (BYOVD).</p>
<div id="attachment_110556" style="width: 1034px" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><a href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2023/09/08153116/Cuba_ransomware_21.png" class="magnificImage"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" aria-describedby="caption-attachment-110556" class="size-large wp-image-110556" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2023/09/08153116/Cuba_ransomware_21-1024x424.png" alt="Exploiting a vulnerable driver" width="1024" height="424" srcset="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2023/09/08153116/Cuba_ransomware_21-1024x424.png 1024w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2023/09/08153116/Cuba_ransomware_21-300x124.png 300w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2023/09/08153116/Cuba_ransomware_21-768x318.png 768w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2023/09/08153116/Cuba_ransomware_21-1536x636.png 1536w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2023/09/08153116/Cuba_ransomware_21-423x175.png 423w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2023/09/08153116/Cuba_ransomware_21-370x153.png 370w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2023/09/08153116/Cuba_ransomware_21-676x280.png 676w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2023/09/08153116/Cuba_ransomware_21-800x331.png 800w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2023/09/08153116/Cuba_ransomware_21.png 2048w" sizes="(max-width: 1024px) 100vw, 1024px" /></a><p id="caption-attachment-110556" class="wp-caption-text">Exploiting a vulnerable driver</p></div>
<p>The malicious actors install the vulnerable driver in the system and subsequently use it to various ends, such as terminating processes or evading defenses through privilege escalation to kernel level.</p>
<p>Hackers are drawn to vulnerable drivers because they all run in kernel mode, with a high level of system access. Besides, a legitimate driver with a digital signature will not raise any red flags with security systems, helping the attackers to stay undetected for longer.</p>
<p>During the attack, the malware created three files in the temp folder:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>aswarpot.sys</strong>: a legitimate anti-rootkit driver by Avast that has two vulnerabilities: <a href="https://cve.mitre.org/cgi-bin/cvename.cgi?name=CVE-2022-26522" target="_blank" rel="noopener">CVE-2022-26522</a> and <a href="https://cve.mitre.org/cgi-bin/cvename.cgi?name=CVE-2022-26523" target="_blank" rel="noopener">CVE-2022-26523</a>, which allow a user with limited permissions to run code at kernel level.</li>
<li><strong>KK.exe</strong>: malware known as Burntcigar. The file we found was a new variety that used the flawed driver to terminate processes.</li>
<li><strong>av.bat</strong> batch script: a stager that helps the kernel service to run the Avast driver and executes Burntcigar.</li>
</ul>
<p>Analysis of the BAT file and telemetry data suggests that av.bat uses the sc.exe utility to create a service named &#8220;aswSP_ArPot2&#8221;, specifying the path to the driver in the С\windows\temp\ directory and the service type as kernel service. The BAT file then starts the service with the help of the same sc.exe utility and runs KK.exe, which connects to the vulnerable driver.</p>

<h3 id="srv_mail-host-exchange-server">SRV_MAIL host (Exchange server)</h3>
<p>On December 20, the customer granted our request to add the Exchange server to the scope of monitoring. The host must have been used as an entry point to the customer network, as the server was missing critical updates, and it was susceptible to most of the group&#8217;s initial access vectors. <h2 id="byovd-bring-your-own-vulnerable-driver">BYOVD (Bring Your Own Vulnerable Driver)</h2>
<p>We will now take a closer look at an attack that uses insecure drivers, which we observed as we investigated the incident and which is currently growing in popularity as various APT and ransomware gangs add it to their arsenals.</p>
<p>Bring Your Own Vulnerable Driver (BYOVD) is a type of attack where the bad actor uses legitimate signed drivers that are known to contain a security hole to execute malicious actions inside the system. If successful, the attacker will be able to exploit the vulnerabilities in the driver code to run any malicious actions at kernel level!</p>
<p>Understanding why this is one of the most dangerous kinds of attacks takes a quick refresher on what drivers are. A driver is a type of software that acts as an intermediary between the operating system and the device. The driver converts OS instructions into commands that the device can interpret and execute. A further use of drivers is supporting applications or features that the operating system originally lacks. As you can see from the image below, the driver is a layer of sorts between user mode and kernel mode.</p>
<div id="attachment_110574" style="width: 522px" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><a href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2023/09/08154422/Cuba_ransomware_39.png" class="magnificImage"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" aria-describedby="caption-attachment-110574" class="size-full wp-image-110574" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2023/09/08154422/Cuba_ransomware_39.png" alt="User mode and kernel mode interaction diagram" width="512" height="426" srcset="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2023/09/08154422/Cuba_ransomware_39.png 512w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2023/09/08154422/Cuba_ransomware_39-300x250.png 300w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2023/09/08154422/Cuba_ransomware_39-210x175.png 210w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2023/09/08154422/Cuba_ransomware_39-370x308.png 370w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2023/09/08154422/Cuba_ransomware_39-337x280.png 337w" sizes="(max-width: 512px) 100vw, 512px" /></a><p id="caption-attachment-110574" class="wp-caption-text">User mode and kernel mode interaction diagram. Source:<br /><a href="https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/windows-hardware/drivers/gettingstarted/user-mode-and-kernel-mode" target="_blank" rel="noopener">https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/windows-hardware/drivers/gettingstarted/user-mode-and-kernel-mode</a></p></div>
<p>Applications running in user mode have fewer privileges to control the system. All they can get access to is a virtualized memory area that is isolated and protected from the rest of the system. The driver runs inside the kernel memory, and it can execute any operations just like the kernel itself. The driver can get access to critical security structures and modify those. Modifications like that make the system liable to attacks that use privilege escalation, disabling of OS security services, and arbitrary reading and writing.</p>
<p>The <a href="https://www.bleepingcomputer.com/news/security/lazarus-hackers-abuse-dell-driver-bug-using-new-fudmodule-rootkit/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Lazarus</a> gang made use of that technique in 2021 as they gained write access to kernel memory and disabled Windows security features by abusing a Dell driver that contained the <a href="https://cve.mitre.org/cgi-bin/cvename.cgi?name=CVE-2021-21551" target="_blank" rel="noopener">CVE-2021-21551</a> vulnerability.</p>
<p>There is no sure-fire defense from legitimate drivers, because any driver could prove to have a security flaw. Microsoft has published a list of recommendations to protect against this type of techniques:</p>
<ul>
<li>Enable Hypervisor-Protected Code Integrity.</li>
<li>Enable Memory Integrity.</li>
<li>Enable validation of driver digital signatures.</li>
<li>Use the <a href="https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/windows/security/threat-protection/windows-defender-application-control/microsoft-recommended-driver-block-rules" target="_blank" rel="noopener">vulnerable driver blocklist</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p>However, <a href="https://habr.com/ru/companies/kaspersky/articles/693840/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">studies</a> suggest that the recommendations are irrelevant even with every Windows protection feature enabled, and attacks like these go through anyway.</p>
<p>To counter this technique, many security vendors started adding a self-defense module into their products that prevents malware from terminating processes and blocks every attempt at exploiting vulnerable drivers. Our <a href="https://www.kaspersky.com/small-to-medium-business-security/endpoint-select" target="_blank" rel="noopener">products</a> have that feature too, and it proved effective during the incident.</p>
<h2 id="conclusion">Conclusion</h2>
<p>The Cuba cybercrime gang employs an extensive arsenal of both publicly available and custom-made tools, which it keeps up to date, and various techniques and methods including fairly dangerous ones, such as BYOVD. Combating attacks at this level of complexity calls for sophisticated technology capable of detecting advanced threats and protecting security features from being disabled, and a massive, continuously updated threat knowledge base that helps to detect malicious artifacts manually.</p>
<p>The incident detailed in this article shows that investigation of real-life cyberattacks and incident response, such as Managed Detection and Response (MDR), are sources of the latest information about malicious tactics, techniques and procedures. In particular, during this investigation, we discovered new and previously undetected samples of the Cuba malware, and artifacts suggesting that at least some of the gang members spoke Russian.</p>
<p>That said, effective investigation and response begin with knowledge of current cyberthreats, which is available from Threat Intelligence services. At Kaspersky, the Threat Intelligence and MDR teams work closely while exchanging data and enhancing their services all the time.</p>
