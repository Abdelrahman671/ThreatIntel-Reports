              <span id="hs_cos_wrapper_post_body" class="hs_cos_wrapper hs_cos_wrapper_meta_field hs_cos_wrapper_type_rich_text" style="" data-hs-cos-general-type="meta_field" data-hs-cos-type="rich_text"><p><span style="color: #cafa3c;">Authors: Tejaswini Sandapolla, Shilpesh Trivedi</span></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>The 8220 Gang, a notorious Chinese-based threat actor group, has once again surfaced in the spotlight with a renewed assault on cloud based infrastructure. This latest campaign, unfolding from May 2023 through February 2024, showcases the gang's strategic pivot towards more sophisticated tactics and techniques, targeting both Linux and Windows platforms. Through a meticulously orchestrated operation, the group has been exploiting well-known vulnerabilities, including CVE-2021-44228 and CVE-2022-26134, underscoring a persistent threat to cloud environments worldwide.</p>
<!--more-->
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>The significance of this development cannot be overstated. The shift in the 8220 Gang's approach marks a critical evolution in cyber threats facing cloud infrastructure today. By leveraging internet scans for vulnerable applications, the group identifies potential entry points into cloud systems, exploiting unpatched vulnerabilities to gain unauthorized access. Once inside, they deploy a series of advanced evasion techniques, demonstrating a profound understanding of how to navigate and manipulate cloud environments to their advantage. This includes disabling security enforcement, modifying firewall rules, and removing cloud security services, thereby ensuring their malicious activities remain undetected.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>The implications of these attacks are far-reaching, affecting countless organizations relying on cloud infrastructure for their operations. The change in tactics and methods employed by the 8220 Gang signifies an alarming advancement in cybercriminal capabilities, posing an increased risk to cloud security and emphasizing the need for heightened vigilance and robust security measures.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>Uptycs’ threat research team reveals the intricate details of the 8220 Gang's latest campaign, offering an in-depth analysis of their attack methodologies, the vulnerabilities exploited, and the defensive evasion tactics used. By understanding the nature of these attacks and the changes in the group's approach, organizations can better prepare themselves to defend against such sophisticated threats, ensuring the security and integrity of their cloud based infrastructure.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h2>Overview of 8220 Gang’s latest cryptomining campaign and historical timeline</h2>
<p>In this latest campaign, the utilization of Windows PowerShell for fileless execution is noted, which leads to the deployment of a cryptominer. What sets this campaign apart from its predecessors is the adoption of distinctive techniques, including DLL sideloading, User Account Control (UAC) bypass, and the modification of AMSIscanBuffer and ETWEventWrite. These specific tactics represent a novel approach, showcasing the group's ingenuity in optimizing stealth and evasion measures, which distinguish it from previous instances. In the Linux campaign, there were no major changes found.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><a href="https://www.uptycs.com/hubfs/Figure1-2.png" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><img src="https://www.uptycs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Figure1-2.png?width=1999&amp;height=675&amp;name=Figure1-2.png" width="1999" height="675" loading="lazy" alt="Figure1-2" style="height: auto; max-width: 100%; width: 1999px;" srcset="https://www.uptycs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Figure1-2.png?width=1000&amp;height=338&amp;name=Figure1-2.png 1000w, https://www.uptycs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Figure1-2.png?width=1999&amp;height=675&amp;name=Figure1-2.png 1999w, https://www.uptycs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Figure1-2.png?width=2999&amp;height=1013&amp;name=Figure1-2.png 2999w, https://www.uptycs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Figure1-2.png?width=3998&amp;height=1350&amp;name=Figure1-2.png 3998w, https://www.uptycs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Figure1-2.png?width=4998&amp;height=1688&amp;name=Figure1-2.png 4998w, https://www.uptycs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Figure1-2.png?width=5997&amp;height=2025&amp;name=Figure1-2.png 5997w" sizes="(max-width: 1999px) 100vw, 1999px"></a></p>

<h2>Windows</h2>
<p>The group extends its focus to Windows systems, employing novel file and command-and-control (C&amp;C) servers to circumvent prior detection methods. Their tactics involve employing diverse techniques to bypass antivirus (AV) and endpoint detection and response (EDR) systems, coupled with the utilization of fileless attacks, UAC Bypass and DLL sideloading strategies.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h3>Stage 1: Bypass.ps1</h3>
<p>1. In the first stage using the same URL as the Linux sample, a powershell script is used in the first step to download and execute the stage 2 payload.<img src="https://www.uptycs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Figure13-1.png?width=1422&amp;height=317&amp;name=Figure13-1.png" width="1422" height="317" loading="lazy" alt="Figure13-1" style="height: auto; max-width: 100%; width: 1422px;" srcset="https://www.uptycs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Figure13-1.png?width=711&amp;height=159&amp;name=Figure13-1.png 711w, https://www.uptycs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Figure13-1.png?width=1422&amp;height=317&amp;name=Figure13-1.png 1422w, https://www.uptycs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Figure13-1.png?width=2133&amp;height=476&amp;name=Figure13-1.png 2133w, https://www.uptycs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Figure13-1.png?width=2844&amp;height=634&amp;name=Figure13-1.png 2844w, https://www.uptycs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Figure13-1.png?width=3555&amp;height=793&amp;name=Figure13-1.png 3555w, https://www.uptycs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Figure13-1.png?width=4266&amp;height=951&amp;name=Figure13-1.png 4266w" sizes="(max-width: 1422px) 100vw, 1422px"></p>

3.<span style="font-size: 16px;"> Other known cryptocurrency miners such as network0, kthreaddi, sysrv etc are killed.</span>
<p>4. Processes listening on ports 3333, 4444, 5555, 7777, and 9000 are also stopped, as these ports are used by crypto mining processes.</p>
<p>5. Downloads stage2(deliver.cmd) and then executes it.</p>

<h3>Stage 2: deliver.cmd</h3>
<p>1. This is an obfuscated batch script. On deobfuscation we can see the following content.</p>
<p><img src="https://www.uptycs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Figure14-1.png?width=1835&amp;height=847&amp;name=Figure14-1.png" width="1835" height="847" loading="lazy" alt="Figure14-1" style="height: auto; max-width: 100%; width: 1835px;" srcset="https://www.uptycs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Figure14-1.png?width=918&amp;height=424&amp;name=Figure14-1.png 918w, https://www.uptycs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Figure14-1.png?width=1835&amp;height=847&amp;name=Figure14-1.png 1835w, https://www.uptycs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Figure14-1.png?width=2753&amp;height=1271&amp;name=Figure14-1.png 2753w, https://www.uptycs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Figure14-1.png?width=3670&amp;height=1694&amp;name=Figure14-1.png 3670w, https://www.uptycs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Figure14-1.png?width=4588&amp;height=2118&amp;name=Figure14-1.png 4588w, https://www.uptycs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Figure14-1.png?width=5505&amp;height=2541&amp;name=Figure14-1.png 5505w" sizes="(max-width: 1835px) 100vw, 1835px"></p>

<p>2. The script has two encrypted payloads(starting with ::). which are base64 decoded, AES decrypted and GZIP decompression.</p>
<p><img src="https://www.uptycs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Figure15.png?width=1653&amp;height=217&amp;name=Figure15.png" width="1653" height="217" loading="lazy" alt="Figure15" style="height: auto; max-width: 100%; width: 1653px;" srcset="https://www.uptycs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Figure15.png?width=827&amp;height=109&amp;name=Figure15.png 827w, https://www.uptycs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Figure15.png?width=1653&amp;height=217&amp;name=Figure15.png 1653w, https://www.uptycs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Figure15.png?width=2480&amp;height=326&amp;name=Figure15.png 2480w, https://www.uptycs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Figure15.png?width=3306&amp;height=434&amp;name=Figure15.png 3306w, https://www.uptycs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Figure15.png?width=4133&amp;height=543&amp;name=Figure15.png 4133w, https://www.uptycs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Figure15.png?width=4959&amp;height=651&amp;name=Figure15.png 4959w" sizes="(max-width: 1653px) 100vw, 1653px"></p>

<p>3. After decompression, it gives two PE files.</p>
<p>4. The small PE file which is about 11kb (name: YCWNEP) used for amsiscanbuffer and etweventwrite bypass. This executable aims to circumvent AMSI and evade Microsoft's event tracking mechanisms by patching with the EtwEventWrite and AmsiScanBuffer APIs, thereby making it difficult for security systems to detect and track its activities through ETW and AMSI.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img src="https://www.uptycs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Figure16-1.png?width=1118&amp;height=544&amp;name=Figure16-1.png" width="1118" height="544" loading="lazy" alt="Figure16-1" style="height: auto; max-width: 100%; width: 1118px;" srcset="https://www.uptycs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Figure16-1.png?width=559&amp;height=272&amp;name=Figure16-1.png 559w, https://www.uptycs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Figure16-1.png?width=1118&amp;height=544&amp;name=Figure16-1.png 1118w, https://www.uptycs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Figure16-1.png?width=1677&amp;height=816&amp;name=Figure16-1.png 1677w, https://www.uptycs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Figure16-1.png?width=2236&amp;height=1088&amp;name=Figure16-1.png 2236w, https://www.uptycs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Figure16-1.png?width=2795&amp;height=1360&amp;name=Figure16-1.png 2795w, https://www.uptycs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Figure16-1.png?width=3354&amp;height=1632&amp;name=Figure16-1.png 3354w" sizes="(max-width: 1118px) 100vw, 1118px"></p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><em>Figure 5 - Uptycs Alert: Powershell execution detected to bypass defender detection</em></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>The patching of EtwEventWrite and AmsiScanBuffer functions is typically associated with evading detection mechanisms, specifically those employed by security software and systems. Let's break down the potential reasons for patching these functions:</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>EtwEventWrite Patching: Event Tracing for Windows (ETW): EtwEventWrite is a function related to ETW, a Windows feature for collecting and tracing events. By patching this function, attackers may attempt to suppress or manipulate the generation of event logs, making their activities less visible to system administrators and security analysts. This can be crucial for maintaining stealth during an attack.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>AmsiScanBuffer Patching: Anti-Malware Scan Interface (AMSI): AmsiScanBuffer is part of the AMSI interface, which allows security applications to integrate with scripting engines and scan script content for malicious behavior. By patching this function, attackers seek to bypass or disable the scanning capabilities provided by AMSI, enabling them to execute malicious scripts without triggering alerts or interventions from security software.In summary, the patching of EtwEventWrite and AmsiScanBuffer functions is indicative of efforts to avoid detection and hinder the logging and scanning capabilities of Windows security features. This is a common strategy employed by attackers to operate stealthily and increase the likelihood of their malicious activities going undetected.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>The bigger decrypted PE file serves several purposes (Stage 3).</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img src="https://www.uptycs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Figure17.png?width=1481&amp;height=685&amp;name=Figure17.png" width="1481" height="685" loading="lazy" alt="Figure17" style="height: auto; max-width: 100%; width: 1481px;" srcset="https://www.uptycs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Figure17.png?width=741&amp;height=343&amp;name=Figure17.png 741w, https://www.uptycs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Figure17.png?width=1481&amp;height=685&amp;name=Figure17.png 1481w, https://www.uptycs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Figure17.png?width=2222&amp;height=1028&amp;name=Figure17.png 2222w, https://www.uptycs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Figure17.png?width=2962&amp;height=1370&amp;name=Figure17.png 2962w, https://www.uptycs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Figure17.png?width=3703&amp;height=1713&amp;name=Figure17.png 3703w, https://www.uptycs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Figure17.png?width=4443&amp;height=2055&amp;name=Figure17.png 4443w" sizes="(max-width: 1481px) 100vw, 1481px"></p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><em>Figure 6 - The patching of EtwEventWrite and AmsiScanBuffer functions to evade detection</em></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h3>Stage 3</h3>
<p>The bigger decrypted PE (from stage2) file serves several purposes:</p>
<p>a. Checks for the presence of a debugger.</p>
<p>b. Installs startup entry of itself as batch script (Network99717Man.cmd).</p>

<h3>What changed as compared to previous campaigns in Windows</h3>
<p>The comprehensive strategy in the above detailed scenario revolves around the utilization of PowerShell for fileless execution, leading to the deployment of a cryptominer. What sets this campaign apart from its predecessors is the adoption of distinctive techniques, including DLL sideloading, User Account Control (UAC) bypass, and the modification of AMSIscanBuffer and ETWEventWrite. These specific tactics represent a novel approach, showcasing the campaign's ingenuity in optimizing stealth and evasion measures, which distinguish it from previous instances.</p>

It looks like he has used an open source github project: <a href="https://github.com/theaog/spirit/tree/master" rel="noopener" target="_blank">https://github.com/theaog/spirit/tree/master</a> for masscan and spirit tools.</p>

<p><img src="https://www.uptycs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Figure10.png?width=1783&amp;height=453&amp;name=Figure10.png" width="1783" height="453" loading="lazy" alt="Figure10" style="height: auto; max-width: 100%; width: 1783px;" srcset="https://www.uptycs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Figure10.png?width=892&amp;height=227&amp;name=Figure10.png 892w, https://www.uptycs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Figure10.png?width=1783&amp;height=453&amp;name=Figure10.png 1783w, https://www.uptycs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Figure10.png?width=2675&amp;height=680&amp;name=Figure10.png 2675w, https://www.uptycs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Figure10.png?width=3566&amp;height=906&amp;name=Figure10.png 3566w, https://www.uptycs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Figure10.png?width=4458&amp;height=1133&amp;name=Figure10.png 4458w, https://www.uptycs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Figure10.png?width=5349&amp;height=1359&amp;name=Figure10.png 5349w" sizes="(max-width: 1783px) 100vw, 1783px"></p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><em>Figure 20 - Uses massscan and zgrab hacktools for discovery and lateral movement</em></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>4. It also automates SSH connections to various hosts using multiple keys and users, with the purpose of downloading and executing a remote script on each host. It parses command history files (~/.bash_history, /home/*/.bash_history, /root/.bash_history) to find previously used SSH connections.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img src="https://www.uptycs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Figure11.png?width=1817&amp;height=521&amp;name=Figure11.png" width="1817" height="521" loading="lazy" alt="Figure11" style="height: auto; max-width: 100%; width: 1817px;" srcset="https://www.uptycs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Figure11.png?width=909&amp;height=261&amp;name=Figure11.png 909w, https://www.uptycs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Figure11.png?width=1817&amp;height=521&amp;name=Figure11.png 1817w, https://www.uptycs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Figure11.png?width=2726&amp;height=782&amp;name=Figure11.png 2726w, https://www.uptycs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Figure11.png?width=3634&amp;height=1042&amp;name=Figure11.png 3634w, https://www.uptycs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Figure11.png?width=4543&amp;height=1303&amp;name=Figure11.png 4543w, https://www.uptycs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Figure11.png?width=5451&amp;height=1563&amp;name=Figure11.png 5451w" sizes="(max-width: 1817px) 100vw, 1817px"></p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><em>Figure 21 - Lateral movement via local ssh credentials</em></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h3>Downloader</h3>
<p>1. The main purpose of this shell script is to download payloads which includes “Tsunami IRCBot” and “Coinminer”.</p>
<p>2. Verifies the existence of established connections to the IP addresses 51[.]255[.]171[.]23, 217[.]182.205.238, 89[.]185[.]85[.]102, 178[.]62[.]234[.]229, and 159[.]223[.]201[.]180. These IP addresses are associated with miner and IRCbot frameworks, and this process is undertaken to assess the presence of any active malware.</p>
<p>3. If they are not connected to above IPS it downloads miner as $DIR/bash and executes with parameters “-c -p 80 -p 443 -tls -dp 80 -dp 443 -tls -d”</p>
<p>4. It downloads Tsunami malware as "$DIR/python3" and executes it.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img src="https://www.uptycs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Figure12-1.png?width=1748&amp;height=432&amp;name=Figure12-1.png" width="1748" height="432" loading="lazy" alt="Figure12-1" style="height: auto; max-width: 100%; width: 1748px;" srcset="https://www.uptycs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Figure12-1.png?width=874&amp;height=216&amp;name=Figure12-1.png 874w, https://www.uptycs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Figure12-1.png?width=1748&amp;height=432&amp;name=Figure12-1.png 1748w, https://www.uptycs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Figure12-1.png?width=2622&amp;height=648&amp;name=Figure12-1.png 2622w, https://www.uptycs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Figure12-1.png?width=3496&amp;height=864&amp;name=Figure12-1.png 3496w, https://www.uptycs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Figure12-1.png?width=4370&amp;height=1080&amp;name=Figure12-1.png 4370w, https://www.uptycs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Figure12-1.png?width=5244&amp;height=1296&amp;name=Figure12-1.png 5244w" sizes="(max-width: 1748px) 100vw, 1748px"></p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><em>Figure 22 - Downloading and executing Tsunami and miner.</em></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>5. It also uses the $(uname -m) command to download files related to the specific architecture. The Tsunami malware(md5: &nbsp;63a86932a5bad5da32ebd1689aa814b3) and miner (md5: 915aec68a5b53aa7681a461a122594d9) haven't changed over last 2 years of the campaign.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h2>Uptycs CNAPP coverage</h2>
<p>Uptycs CNAPP is flagging a growing number of suspicious alerts, encompassing activities such as system startup, potential information theft, attempts to gain high-level access, termination of running services, executing processes from temporary locations, and the discovery of dropped files within the AppData folder. These alerts collectively contribute to an escalating level of suspicion.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<img src="https://www.uptycs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Figure23.png?width=1796&amp;height=877&amp;name=Figure23.png" width="1796" height="877" loading="lazy" alt="Figure23" style="height: auto; max-width: 100%; width: 1796px;" srcset="https://www.uptycs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Figure23.png?width=898&amp;height=439&amp;name=Figure23.png 898w, https://www.uptycs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Figure23.png?width=1796&amp;height=877&amp;name=Figure23.png 1796w, https://www.uptycs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Figure23.png?width=2694&amp;height=1316&amp;name=Figure23.png 2694w, https://www.uptycs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Figure23.png?width=3592&amp;height=1754&amp;name=Figure23.png 3592w, https://www.uptycs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Figure23.png?width=4490&amp;height=2193&amp;name=Figure23.png 4490w, https://www.uptycs.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Figure23.png?width=5388&amp;height=2631&amp;name=Figure23.png 5388w" sizes="(max-width: 1796px) 100vw, 1796px">

<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h2>Conclusion: 8220 Gang</h2>
<p>The 8220 Gang has proven to be a substantial threat, challenging the characterization by some researchers who initially labeled them as mere "script kiddies." While their Linux campaign saw minimal changes, the group significantly enhanced and altered their tactics in the Windows campaign.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>Organizations are now tasked with the continuous improvement and updating of their security systems to match the group's evolving strategies. In the early stages, the group employed straightforward and easily detectable scripts in their deployments. Maintaining a watchful eye on the 8220 Gang and their deployments is crucial for ongoing analysis, detection, and the effective implementation of blocking measures.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h2>Precautions</h2>
<ul>
<li>Utilize trustworthy antivirus and anti-malware solutions, ensuring they are regularly updated.</li>
<li>Maintain current security patches for operating systems and software to stay protected.</li>
<li>Inform users/employees about the risks associated with clicking on unfamiliar links or downloading questionable attachments.</li>
<li>Enforce robust email filtering to prevent malicious attachments and links from infiltrating your system.</li>
<li>Consistently observe network traffic for any abnormal or questionable behaviors.</li>
<li>Frequently back up essential data and store it in an offline location to safeguard against ransomware encryption.</li>
</ul>
