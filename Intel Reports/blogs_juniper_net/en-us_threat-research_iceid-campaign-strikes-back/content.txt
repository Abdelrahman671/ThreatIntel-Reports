			<div class="dfd-blog-title">IcedID Campaign Strikes Back</div>
							<div class="entry-meta meta-bottom">
	<span class="entry-date">August 12, 2020</span>	<div class="meta-author">
	<span class="before-author">by</span>

	<span class="byline author vcard">
	<a href="https://blogs.juniper.net/author/paulkim" title="Posts by Paul Kimayong" class="author url fn" rel="author">Paul Kimayong</a>	</span>
	</div>

	
	</div>
			</div>
	<div class="entry-content">

		<p><span style="font-weight: 400;"><img loading="lazy" class="aligncenter wp-image-22824 size-full" src="https://blogs.juniper.net/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/SEC-200675_DIGITAL_Add-logo-to-Threat-Labs-blog-images.gif" alt="IcedID Trojan - Juniper Threat Labs Blogs" width="830" height="463" /></span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">In our</span><a href="https://blogs.juniper.net/en-us/threat-research/covid-19-and-fmla-campaigns-used-to-install-new-icedid-banking-malware"> <span style="font-weight: 400;">previous blog</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> about IcedID, weÂ  explored some of the changes in the malware and how it tries to evade detection. We also detailed how threat actors took advantage of the COVID-19 pandemic to phish their target victims. Recently, we discovered an evolution in their phishing methods, particularly how they attempt to evade detection by implementing a password protected attachment, keyword obfuscation and minimalist macro code in their trojanized documents. This time, they also use a DLL for the second stage downloader, which shows a new maturity level of this threat actor.</span></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h1><span style="font-weight: 400;">Phishing Victims</span></h1>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">In the current campaign discovered in July 2020, an email phishing campaign is performed using compromised business accounts where the recipients are customers of the same businesses. This makes the phish that much more likely to succeed, given the sender and the recipient have an established business relationship. One example we are going to highlight is from a compromise of PrepNow.com, a private, nationwide student tutoring company with business presence in many states.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">The phishing emails are sent to potential victims from the accounting department and purported to include an invoice. The attachment is a password protected zip file named request.zip. The password protection is to prevent anti-malware analysis solutions from decrypting and inspecting the attachment. The password is included in the email message body, in the hopes that the victim would read the email, locate the password and use it to open the attached file.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">An interesting characteristic of these messages is the word âattachedâ is obfuscated in multiple ways. This may be an attempt for this phish to bypass spam filters or phishing detection systems that could be looking for such keywords. However, this is useless because there is no need for any security solution to rely on the word âattachedâ to figure out there is an attachment. If anything, we expected the obfuscation to obfuscate the word âpasswordâ because thatâs a tell-tale sign of something phishy going on. Then again, modifying the body of the email ever so slightly may change some fuzzy hashes email security solutions calculate to identify bulk email campaigns.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Additionally, the campaign has rotated the file name used for the attachment inside the zip file. Again, this seems futile, since the password protection should prevent most security solutions from opening and inspecting the content.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Nonetheless, this technique proved successful against Googleâs Gmail security, which did not block this email.</span></p>
<figure id="attachment_22245" class="thumbnail wp-caption alignnone" style="width: 592px"><img loading="lazy" class="wp-image-22245 size-full" src="https://blogs.juniper.net/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/phishing1.png" alt="" width="592" height="421" srcset="https://blogs.juniper.net/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/phishing1.png 592w, https://blogs.juniper.net/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/phishing1-300x213.png 300w" sizes="(max-width: 592px) 100vw, 592px" /><figcaption class="caption wp-caption-text">Sample email containing the password protected Request.zip sha256: 2beadfb91e794860aad159dcca1c94855a99b9bc908d03d10cea005dad652422 MS Word Document inside zip file: legal paper_07.23.2020.doc: Sha 256: 9b0ff58ddedd7a78e3b8f28c9c5a4934ea9f4dc530d57cc7715bdca6687590fc</figcaption></figure>

<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Letâs take a look at the malicious documents in the attachments.</span></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h1><span style="font-weight: 400;">First Stage: MS Office Documents Macro Downloader</span></h1>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">In short, once the zip file is expanded, the user finds a Microsoft Word document that contains a macro that executes upon opening the document. There is the usual social engineering attempt to get victims to enable macros, which claims the document was created with a previous version of MS Word, in this case. Once macros are enabled, the VB script will download a DLL, save it as a PDF and install it as a service using regsvr32 to guarantee persistence.</span></p>
<p><img loading="lazy" class="alignnone wp-image-22242" src="https://blogs.juniper.net/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/document_macro-1024x282.png" alt="" width="694" height="191" srcset="https://blogs.juniper.net/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/document_macro-1024x282.png 1024w, https://blogs.juniper.net/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/document_macro-300x83.png 300w, https://blogs.juniper.net/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/document_macro-768x211.png 768w, https://blogs.juniper.net/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/document_macro.png 1341w" sizes="(max-width: 694px) 100vw, 694px" /></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">The authors have resorted to being âminimalistâ in this recent campaign. The âmacroâ code is very simple and straightforward but they managed to add a few tricks to evade detection. For instance, all strings and function calls in the macro are obfuscated.Â </span></p>
<p><img loading="lazy" class="alignnone wp-image-22241" src="https://blogs.juniper.net/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/macro_code.png" alt="" width="598" height="589" srcset="https://blogs.juniper.net/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/macro_code.png 902w, https://blogs.juniper.net/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/macro_code-300x295.png 300w, https://blogs.juniper.net/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/macro_code-768x756.png 768w" sizes="(max-width: 598px) 100vw, 598px" /></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">There are also instances where the URL is saved as an XML file inside the document.</span></p>
<p><img loading="lazy" class="alignnone wp-image-22240" src="https://blogs.juniper.net/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/macros_code2-1024x408.png" alt="" width="645" height="257" srcset="https://blogs.juniper.net/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/macros_code2-1024x408.png 1024w, https://blogs.juniper.net/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/macros_code2-300x120.png 300w, https://blogs.juniper.net/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/macros_code2-768x306.png 768w, https://blogs.juniper.net/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/macros_code2.png 1039w" sizes="(max-width: 645px) 100vw, 645px" /></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">To some extent, these few tricks worked. Virustotal hits were low at first submission on the samples from July 20.</span></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<figure id="attachment_22239" class="thumbnail wp-caption alignnone" style="width: 727px"><img loading="lazy" class=" wp-image-22239" src="https://blogs.juniper.net/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/VT1.png" alt="" width="727" height="107" srcset="https://blogs.juniper.net/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/VT1.png 768w, https://blogs.juniper.net/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/VT1-300x44.png 300w" sizes="(max-width: 727px) 100vw, 727px" /><figcaption class="caption wp-caption-text">Sha256: 469fc41ba6d15f2af6bcf369e39c5c06b8bb5d991c008efadbfd409d096e911b<br />Source: virustotal.com</figcaption></figure>

<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h1><span style="font-weight: 400;">Second Stage: DLL Trojan</span></h1>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">In our observation, the second stage payload consists of a DLL that is downloaded from 3wuk8wv[.]com or 185.43.4[.]241, which is hosted on aÂ </span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">hosting provider in Russian Siberia</span><a href="https://ispserver.com/"> <span style="font-weight: 400;">https://ispserver.com/</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">Â </span></p>
<p><img loading="lazy" class="alignnone wp-image-22249" src="https://blogs.juniper.net/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/domain.png" alt="" width="477" height="336" srcset="https://blogs.juniper.net/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/domain.png 908w, https://blogs.juniper.net/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/domain-300x211.png 300w, https://blogs.juniper.net/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/domain-768x541.png 768w" sizes="(max-width: 477px) 100vw, 477px" /></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Once downloaded, the malicious DLL is saved as a pdf file, then the macro executes it via a call to regsvr32.exe.</span></p>
<figure id="attachment_22237" class="thumbnail wp-caption alignnone" style="width: 748px"><img loading="lazy" class="wp-image-22237 " src="https://blogs.juniper.net/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/pcap-1024x643.png" alt="" width="748" height="470" srcset="https://blogs.juniper.net/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/pcap-1024x643.png 1024w, https://blogs.juniper.net/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/pcap-300x188.png 300w, https://blogs.juniper.net/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/pcap-768x482.png 768w, https://blogs.juniper.net/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/pcap.png 1206w" sizes="(max-width: 748px) 100vw, 748px" /><figcaption class="caption wp-caption-text">Pcap capture of downloading of DLL</figcaption></figure>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Our sample has very few detection on Virustotal, upon initial submission.</span></p>
<p><img loading="lazy" class="alignnone wp-image-22236" src="https://blogs.juniper.net/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/VT3.png" alt="" width="700" height="170" srcset="https://blogs.juniper.net/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/VT3.png 860w, https://blogs.juniper.net/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/VT3-300x73.png 300w, https://blogs.juniper.net/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/VT3-768x187.png 768w" sizes="(max-width: 700px) 100vw, 700px" /></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h1><span style="font-weight: 400;">Third Stage: Malicious Payload Downloader</span></h1>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Once launched, the DLL will download the next stage from the domain loadhnichar[.]co as a PNG file and decrypt it. Similar to the second stage loader we analyzed in our previous blog, this loader </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">blends its traffic with requests to benign domains, such as apple.com, twitter.com, microsoft.com, etc. to look more benign to sandboxes trying to analyze it.</span></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img loading="lazy" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-22235" src="https://blogs.juniper.net/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/CheckPNG.png" alt="" width="570" height="277" srcset="https://blogs.juniper.net/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/CheckPNG.png 570w, https://blogs.juniper.net/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/CheckPNG-300x146.png 300w" sizes="(max-width: 570px) 100vw, 570px" /></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Unfortunately, at the time of our testing, the download domain, loadhnichar[.]co for the next stage is already down.</span></p>
<p><img loading="lazy" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-22234" src="https://blogs.juniper.net/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/DNS_queries.png" alt="" width="510" height="180" srcset="https://blogs.juniper.net/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/DNS_queries.png 510w, https://blogs.juniper.net/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/DNS_queries-300x106.png 300w" sizes="(max-width: 510px) 100vw, 510px" /></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Using a similar sample from malware-traffic analysis, </span><a href="https://malware-traffic-analysis.net/2020/07/20/index.html"><span style="font-weight: 400;">https://malware-traffic-analysis.net/2020/07/20/index.html</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">, we analyzed the next stages.Â </span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">We have not found any changes from this stage, compared to our previous analysis.Â </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">The second stage will download the third stage as a PNG file, decrypt it and run it. It will be saved as {random}.exe and will create a scheduled task for persistence.Â </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">The third stage will download the IcedID main module as a PNG file, spawn a msiexec.exe process and inject the IcedID main module into it.</span></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Juniper Advanced Threat Prevention (ATP) detects this file as malware.</span></p>
