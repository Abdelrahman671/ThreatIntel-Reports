											<div class="text-block bullets-listing">
											<p>By Securonix Threat Labs, Threat Research: D. Iuzvyk, T. Peck, O. Kolesnikov</p>
<h2>TL;DR</h2>
<p>An unusual attack/phishing campaign delivering malware while using meme-filled code and complex obfuscation methods continues dropping Xworm payloads for the last few months and is still ongoing today.</p>
<p><a href="https://www.securonix.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/05/Picture16.png" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="alignnone wp-image-20763 size-full" src="https://www.securonix.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/05/Picture16.png" alt="" width="2048" height="1224" /></a></p>
<h2>Intro</h2>
<p>For the last few months, an interesting and ongoing attack campaign was identified and tracked by the Securonix Threat Research team. The attack campaign (tracked by Securonix as MEME#4CHAN) was leveraging rather unusual meme-filled PowerShell code, followed by a heavily obfuscated XWorm payload to infect its victims. Today, we’ll dive into this campaign by taking an in-depth technical analysis of the entire attack chain starting with the phishing email samples, covering obfuscation methods, and ending with an analysis of the final binary payload that gets decoded from .NET assemblies in PowerShell.</p>
<p>The attack chain leveraging XWorm payloads was <a href="https://www.elastic.co/security-labs/attack-chain-leads-to-xworm-and-agenttesla">first reported</a> by threat researchers at Elastic. Today, we will take a look at some new unique payloads along with new obfuscation methods used by the attackers that were not covered before. We will also provide new samples, IoCs and detections which we’ve been tracking since the start of the campaign a few months ago.</p>
<p>The attack begins with a malicious Microsoft Office Word document and appears to be targeting various businesses, including Germany / .de email addresses. Microsoft Word attachments have fallen out of favor since Microsoft decided to disable macro execution by default, however today as we dive into one of the samples collected, attempted code execution from a macro-less document is still very much in use today.</p>
<h2>Attack chain overview</h2>
<p>The [MEME#4CHAN] campaign follows a rather unique attack chain consisting of both PowerShell and JavaScript execution originating from a malicious Word document file.</p>
<p>CSharp code execution contained within the main PowerShell script is used to deliver the final payload which ends with XWorm v3.1 execution. Below is a diagram of the overall attack chain. We will dive into each portion of the attack chain in depth as we continue.</p>
<p><a href="https://www.securonix.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/05/Picture2.png" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="alignnone wp-image-20711 size-full" src="https://www.securonix.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/05/Picture2.png" alt="" width="1136" height="669" /></a></p>
<p>Figure 1: [MEME#4CHAN] attack chain</p>
<h2>Initial infection</h2>
<p>As with many modern attacks we see these days, the MEME#4CHAN typically begins with a phishing email. The attacks appear to be patterned after a known <a href="http://web.archive.org/web/20230128125509/https://blackcell.io/hackers-target-hotel-and-travel-companies-with-fake-reservations/">fake hotel reservation</a> phishing scheme. The goal is to get the company employee to open the attached phishing document which will kick off the initial code execution portion of the attack. Typically the subject, body and even contents of the lure attachment are designed to create a sense of urgency to mask any potentially unusual requests.</p>
<h3>Phishing email details</h3>
<p>In one example, the phishing email contained the subject “Reservation For Room” with a brief message in the body containing a generic text containing room and booking requests. The email appears to be sent from “zoe[at]kbowlingslaw.com”, however after examining the header, the actual sender came from a Gmail address: “panelnew12[at]gmail.com”.</p>
<p>What makes this particular phishing campaign especially interesting is the fact that the target email belonged to a German company involved in manufacturing. This could indicate that the attackers are not only specifically targeting hotels, but blasting out phishing emails using a generic corporate email list and hoping for the best. To bolster this theory, another phishing email our team intercepted was from the same Gmail address and contained the subject “Urgent booking for Honeymoon” and was targeted to a small German hospital clinic.</p>
<p>Most of the phishing emails analyzed by the team followed the same pattern, so we’ll dive into one of these in depth.</p>
<h3>Stage 1: Email attachment analysis: Details for booking.docx</h3>
<p>Throughout the rest of this article, we’ll follow the attack chain of one document, though others were overall similar and produced the same final result. In this example, the email attachment is a single Microsoft Word document file named “Details for booking.docx”. When opened, a prompt to the user appears before any content is displayed asking the user if they want to update the document with externally linked files.</p>
<p><a href="https://www.securonix.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/05/Picture3.png" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="alignnone wp-image-20715 size-full" src="https://www.securonix.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/05/Picture3.png" alt="" width="1253" height="271" /></a></p>
<p>Figure 2: Details for booking.docx linked files prompt</p>
<p>If either of the prompts are clicked, the pop-up closes and we’re presented with what looks like stolen images of a bank debit card as well as a driver&#8217;s license. Both cards appear to be from two unique French citizens.</p>
<p>The document contains no macros or discernable p-code which means that macro execution is not the attack vector for the phishing document.</p>
<p><a href="https://www.securonix.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/05/Picture5.png" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="alignnone wp-image-20719 size-full" src="https://www.securonix.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/05/Picture5.png" alt="" width="1187" height="710" /></a></p>
<p>Figure 3: Details for booking.docx file contents</p>
<p>Rather than using macros to execute malicious VBscript, this document uses a known vulnerability from last year (CVE-2022-30190). In summary, this vulnerability works by embedding external objects contained in a relationship file within the .docx word file. These relationship files can reference external objects included inside the doc.</p>
<p>In this example, the document footer contained a shape object that was used maliciously. The shape object used the footer relationship file “<span style="color: #ff0000;">footer2.xml.rels</span>” to fetch the external objects.</p>
<p><a href="https://www.securonix.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/05/Picture6.png" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="alignnone wp-image-20723 size-full" src="https://www.securonix.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/05/Picture6.png" alt="" width="2048" height="1224" /></a></p>
<p>Figure 4: Details for booking.docx external references</p>
<p>Notice on the right side of the figure above that the relationship file contains two links to external resources.</p>
<p><span style="color: #ff0000;">hxxps://huskidkifklaoksikfkfijsju.blogspot[.]com/atom.xml</span></p>
<p><span style="color: #ff0000;">hxxps://73cceb63-7ecd-45e2-9eab-f8d98aab177f.usrfiles.com/ugd/73cceb_b5b6005e2aa74cf48cd55dca1a2ff093[.]docx</span></p>
<p>The referenced file 73cceb_b5b6005e2aa74cf48cd55dca1a2ff093.docx appears to be an empty MS document file containing a single picture with a small white square. The same code execution tactic happens again with the same referenced Blogspot URL as the original.</p>
<p>The other URL contained in the original phishing document references the <span style="color: #ff0000;">atom.xml</span> file hosted on Usrfiles, a public file sharing service. This file redirects to another URL which downloads and executes a PowerShell script hosted at:</p>
<p><span style="color: #ff0000;">hxxps://73cceb63-7ecd-45e2-9eab-f8d98aab177f.usrfiles[.]com/ugd/73cceb_e5a698286daf43ac87b4544a35b1a482.txt</span></p>
<p>Some documents we observed executed PowerShell code directly contained within the atom.xml file, while others, as with this case referenced a separate URL containing the malicious PowerShell code.</p>
<h3>Stage 2: PowerShell execution</h3>
<p>The PowerShell that gets executed at this stage is semi-obfuscated and contains quite a few functions which we’ll dive into further on. The code is rather interesting and contains memes, crass variable names and comments throughout, so you’ll have to pardon the use of the blur tool throughout the next series of figures as we go through it and subsequent files.</p>
<p><a href="https://www.securonix.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/05/Picture7.png" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="alignnone wp-image-20727 size-full" src="https://www.securonix.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/05/Picture7.png" alt="" width="2048" height="1224" /></a></p>
<p>Figure 5: 73cceb_e5a698286daf43ac87b4544a35b1a482.txt &#8211; CSharp and initial execution</p>
<p>The script begins by stopping the RegSvcs and Msbuild processes. We’ll get into why this is important further down. After that, a directory inside C:\ProgramData\ is created called <span style="color: #ff0000;">MEMEMAN</span> which is where most of the malware staging from this point will happen.</p>
<p>The<span style="color: #ff0000;"> $NuclearDefusion</span> variable contains a CSharp script that accomplishes a few tasks. First, at the beginning, there is a deobfuscation function named <span style="color: #ff0000;">Â£Â£Â£</span> which is used to hex decode some of the other variables later on.</p>
<p>Two additional variables, each with their own PowerShell code are contained inside the <span style="color: #ff0000;">$amsii</span> variable contained in the <span style="color: #ff0000;">JJJI</span> function:</p>
<p><span style="color: #ff0000;">$AMI</span>: This variable contains a long hexadecimal string that is triple hex encoded. Once decoded, we’re presented with a simple obfuscated AMSI bypass technique which uses <a href="https://github.com/S3cur3Th1sSh1t/Amsi-Bypass-Powershell">Matt Graeber’s reflection method</a> to crash the AMSI (Anti-malware Scan Interface) instance for the current PowerShell session. This will prevent subsequent code from being scanned for malicious content.</p>
<p><a href="https://www.securonix.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/05/Picture8.png" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="alignnone wp-image-20731 size-full" src="https://www.securonix.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/05/Picture8.png" alt="" width="2048" height="458" /></a></p>
<p>Figure 6: $AMI &#8211; AMSI bypass techniques</p>
<p>Next, a registry key is created under “<span style="color: #ff0000;">HKCU:\\Software\Classes\CLSID\{fdb00e52-a214-4aa1-8fba-4357bb0072ec}\InProcServer32</span>”  with a default value set to &#8220;<span style="color: #ff0000;">C:\IDontExist.dll</span>&#8221; This registry change also <a href="https://enigma0x3.net/2017/07/19/bypassing-amsi-via-com-server-hijacking/">assists in disabling AMSI</a> by overriding the Microsoft Defender COM object for AMSI and points it to a DLL that doesn’t exist.</p>
<p><span style="color: #ff0000;">$DEF</span>: This variable contains PowerShell code to once again disable AMSI using the same two methods as before, just obfuscated differently. Additionally, defender exclusions are created for pretty much everything on the host using the “<span style="color: #ff0000;">Add-MpPreference</span>” PowerShell module.</p>
<p><a href="https://www.securonix.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/05/Picture9.png" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="alignnone wp-image-20735 size-full" src="https://www.securonix.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/05/Picture9.png" alt="" width="2048" height="1224" /></a></p>
<p>Figure 7: $DEF &#8211; Code samples: Disable AMSI, Defender exclusions, and new local user</p>
<p>Towards the end of the script, a new local user named “System32” with a password of 123 is created. The new local user is then added to the administrators and Remote Desktop Users groups. And then lastly, Windows Firewall is disabled.</p>
<p>Moving through the Csharp script, we run into another obfuscated variable called <span style="color: #ff0000;">$CHOTAbheem</span> which contains a long encoded string. Further down we see it leveraging the <span style="color: #ff0000;">£££</span> function to decode it.</p>
<p><a href="https://www.securonix.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/05/Picture10.png" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="alignnone wp-image-20739 size-full" src="https://www.securonix.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/05/Picture10.png" alt="" width="2048" height="510" /></a></p>
<p>Figure 8: Deobfuscated code behind $CHOTAbheemdecoded</p>
<p>The script above leverages the WScript.shell COM object “<span style="color: #ff0000;">{40FC6ED5-2438-11CF-A3DB-080036F12502}</span>” to execute the <span style="color: #ff0000;">$NuclearDefusion</span> variable which we’ll see written to disk later on. This takes us through the first half of the original PowerShell script.</p>
<p><a href="https://www.securonix.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/05/Picture11.png" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="alignnone wp-image-20743 size-full" src="https://www.securonix.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/05/Picture11.png" alt="" width="2048" height="1224" /></a></p>
<p>Figure 9: 73cceb_e5a698286daf43ac87b4544a35b1a482.txt &#8211; PowerShell code</p>
<p>The code referenced in the first half of the script is written to disk and saved in the  “<span style="color: #ff0000;">C:\ProgramData\MEMEMAN<span style="color: #000000;">” directory as “</span>CypherDeptography.~+~</span>” using the PowerShell <a href="https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/dotnet/api/system.io.file.writealltext?view=net-7.0">[IO.File]::WriteAllText</a> method.</p>
<p>Next, let’s take a look at what’s hidden away behind the <span style="color: #ff0000;">$allsave</span> variable. Once decoded using the same method we used to decode the <span style="color: #ff0000;">$CHOTAbheem</span> variable, we’re faced with a hugely obfuscated JScript one-liner as seen in the figure below.</p>
<p><a href="https://www.securonix.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/05/Picture12.png" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="alignnone wp-image-20747 size-full" src="https://www.securonix.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/05/Picture12.png" alt="" width="2048" height="1224" /></a></p>
<p>Figure 10: $allsave JScript and deobfuscated PowerShell code</p>
<p>Once decoded, the script behind <span style="color: #ff0000;">$allsave</span> is similar to that of <span style="color: #ff0000;">$CHOTAbheem</span> . However this appears to invoke a variable that is referenced in the main script which points to:</p>
<p><span style="color: #ff0000;">hxxps://backuphotelall.blogspot[.]com/atom.xml</span></p>
<p>Unfortunately, we were not about to pull this script as the Blogspot URL was taken offline at some point. However, we can speculate that it is similar to that of the original atom.xml script.</p>
<p>Next down the list, we’ve got a repeating function (probably for redundancy) where the <span style="color: #ff0000;">$shakalakaboomboom</span> variable is decoded. In the end, carving through the layers of obfuscation once again, it retrieves yet another atom.xml file from the following URL:</p>
<p><span style="color: #ff0000;">hxxps://3000allfitheyito.blogspot[.]com/atom.xml</span></p>
<p>Persistence is established by writing the obfuscated JScript code to disk and saving it as <span style="color: #ff0000;">\ZeeNEWsTV\UpdateEscan.js</span> using the WriteAllText method. A scheduled task is then created called EscanDissldo referencing the newly created file that runs once every 200 minutes.</p>
<p><span style="color: #ff0000;">schtasks /create /sc MINUTE /mo 200 /tn EscanDissldo /F /tr &#8220;wscript.exe //b //e:jscript  C:\\ProgramData\\MEMEMAN\\UpdateEscan.js&#8221;</span></p>
<p>The next level of persistence happens during the last 4 lines of code where all of the content from the staging directory (&#8220;<span style="color: #ff0000;">C:\ProgramData\MEMEMAN\</span>&#8220;) is copied to the user’s startup directory. This is defined using “<span style="color: #ff0000;">[environment]::getfolderpath(&#8220;Startup&#8221;)</span>”</p>
<p>Lastly, the script deletes the CSharp decode script, or any file ending in <span style="color: #ff0000;">*.~+~</span> from the startup directory.</p>
<h2>Stage 3: Binary file execution</h2>
<p>Circling back to the C# script portion of the original PowerShell script, there are two variables which contain binary file data. Both are heavily obfuscated .NET binaries which leverage the unlicenced version of .NET Reactor to hide the original source code.</p>
<p>Both binary files are injected into the RegSvcs.exe or Msbuild.exe process using in-memory execution using .NET assemblies via reflection. For reference, the <span style="color: #ff0000;">$Ripple</span> variable contains the hexadecimal binary data for<span style="color: #ff0000;"> sssss.exe</span> which we’ll be going over further down.</p>
<p><a href="https://www.securonix.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/05/Picture13.png" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="alignnone wp-image-20751 size-full" src="https://www.securonix.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/05/Picture13.png" alt="" width="2048" height="1224" /></a></p>
<p>Figure 11: In-memory assembly execution</p>

<p><span style="color: #ff0000;">[Reflection.Assembly]::Load(&#8220;Salmankhan($pp&#8221;).GetType(&#8220;A.B&#8221;).GetMethod(C).Invoke($null,{[OBJECT[]]}, (&#8220;C:\Windows\Microsoft.NET\Framework\v4.0.30319\RegSvcs.exe&#8221;, $Ripple)</span></p>

<h2>Some Possible Attribution Insights</h2>
<p>The attack methodology is similar to that of TA558 where phishing emails were delivered targeting the hospitality industry. TA558 also typically uses a wide range of C2 campaign <a href="https://www.proofpoint.com/us/blog/threat-insight/reservations-requested-ta558-targets-hospitality-and-travel">artifacts and payloads similar</a>, but not positively in line with what we witnessed through the MEME#4CHAN campaign.</p>
<p>Based on the English meme-themed code and 4chan references, it’s likely that the malicious threat actor originates from a group of English-speaking origin, such as the UK or US. Some of the malicious attack activity appears to be targeting victims in Germany.</p>

<h2>Conclusion</h2>
<p>The [MEME#4CHAN] campaign provided us with some interesting insights. Though phishing emails rarely use Microsoft Office documents since Microsoft made the decision to disable macros by default, today we’re seeing proof that it is still important to be vigilant about malicious document files, especially in this case where there was no VBscript execution from macros.</p>
<p>It’s likely that since several C2 domains are still active that this campaign is ongoing. Also, given the fact that XWorm v3.1 was recently cracked and released, it’s likely that activity surrounding this particular strain will only increase.</p>
<h2>Securonix recommendations and mitigations</h2>
<ul>
<li>Avoid opening any attachments especially from those that are unexpected or are from outside the organization. Be extra vigilant with Microsoft document files, even if there are no macros present.</li>
<li>Implement an application whitelisting policy to restrict the execution of unknown binaries.</li>
<li>Deploy additional process-level logging such as <a href="https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/sysinternals/downloads/sysmon">Sysmon</a> and <a href="https://www.crowdstrike.com/blog/investigating-powershell-command-and-script-logging/">PowerShell logging</a> for additional log detection coverage.</li>
<li>Monitor for the usage of potentially malicious file hosting websites such as mediafire and usrfiles.</li>
<li>Securonix customers can scan endpoints using the Securonix Seeder Hunting Queries below.</li>
</ul>
