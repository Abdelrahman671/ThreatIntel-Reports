					<h1 class="entry-title">Ransomware Group Turns to Facebook Ads</h1>
				</div>
							
			
			</header><!-- .entry-header -->

	<div id="primary" class="site-content">
		<div id="content" role="main">

			
					<article id="post-53550" class="post-53550 post type-post status-publish format-standard has-post-thumbnail hentry category-ransomware category-comingstorm tag-chris-hodson tag-emsisoft tag-fabian-wosar tag-ragnar-locker tag-ransomware">
				<header class="entry-header">
					<div class="clear"></div>
	<div class="btm-wrap">
	<div class="below-title-meta">
		<div class="adt">
			
			
				
				<span class="date updated">November 10, 2020</span>
					
        </div>
		<div class="adt-comment">
		<span><a class="link-comments" href="https://krebsonsecurity.com/2020/11/ransomware-group-turns-to-facebook-ads/#comments">41 Comments</a></span> 
        </div>    <div class="clear"></div>	   
    </div><!-- below title meta end -->
	</div>
		
						</header><!-- .entry-header -->
		
		
							<div class="entry-content">
												<p>It&#8217;s bad enough that many ransomware gangs now have blogs where they publish data stolen from companies that refuse to make an extortion payment. Now, one crime group has started using hacked <strong>Facebook</strong> accounts to run ads publicly pressuring their ransomware victims into paying up.</p>
<p><a class="lightbox" href="https://krebsonsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/11/campari-fb.png"><img decoding="async" class="aligncenter wp-image-53552" src="https://krebsonsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/11/campari-fb.png" alt="" width="598" height="391" srcset="https://krebsonsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/11/campari-fb.png 1332w, https://krebsonsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/11/campari-fb-768x502.png 768w" sizes="(max-width: 598px) 100vw, 598px" /></a></p>
<p>On the evening of Monday, Nov. 9, an ad campaign apparently taken out by the <a href="https://resources.infosecinstitute.com/topic/ragnar-locker-malware-what-it-is-how-it-works-and-how-to-prevent-it-malware-spotlight/" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Ragnar Locker Team</a> began appearing on Facebook. The ad was designed to turn the screws to the Italian beverage vendor <strong>Campari Group</strong>, which <a href="https://ftaonline.com/news/campari-group-vittima-di-un-attacco-malware" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">acknowledged on Nov. 3</a> that its computer systems had been <a href="https://www.zdnet.com/article/italian-beverage-vendor-campari-knocked-offline-after-ransomware-attack/" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">sidelined by a malware attack</a>.</p>
<p>On Nov. 6, Campari issued a follow-up statement saying &#8220;at this stage, we cannot completely exclude that some personal and business data has been taken.&#8221;</p>
<p>&#8220;This is ridiculous and looks like a big fat lie,&#8221; reads the Facebook ad campaign from the Ragnar crime group. &#8220;We can confirm that confidential data was stolen and we talking about huge volume of data.&#8221;</p>
<p>The ad went on to say Ragnar Locker Team had offloaded two terabytes of information and would give the Italian firm until 6 p.m. EST today (Nov. 10) to negotiate an extortion payment in exchange for a promise not to publish the stolen files.</p>
<p>The Facebook ad blitz was paid for by <strong>Hodson Event Entertainment</strong>, an account tied to <a href="http://www.djchrishodson.com" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Chris Hodson</a>, a deejay based in Chicago. Contacted by KrebsOnSecurity, Hodson said his Facebook account indeed was hacked, and that the attackers had budgeted $500 for the entire campaign.</p>
<p>&#8220;I thought I had two-step verification turned on for all my accounts, but now it looks like the only one I didn&#8217;t have it set for was Facebook,&#8221; Hodson said.</p>
<p>Hodson said a review of his account shows the unauthorized campaign reached approximately 7,150 Facebook users, and generated 770 clicks, with a cost-per-result of 21 cents. Of course, it didn&#8217;t cost the ransomware group anything. Hodson said Facebook billed him $35 for the first part of the campaign, but apparently detected the ads as fraudulent sometime this morning before his account could be billed another $159 for the campaign.<span id="more-53550"></span></p>
<div id="attachment_53555" style="width: 608px" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><a class="lightbox" href="https://krebsonsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/11/hodsoncampaign.png"><img aria-describedby="caption-attachment-53555" decoding="async" loading="lazy" class="wp-image-53555" src="https://krebsonsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/11/hodsoncampaign.png" alt="" width="598" height="178" srcset="https://krebsonsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/11/hodsoncampaign.png 1321w, https://krebsonsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/11/hodsoncampaign-768x228.png 768w" sizes="(max-width: 598px) 100vw, 598px" /></a><p id="caption-attachment-53555" class="wp-caption-text">The results of the unauthorized Facebook ad campaign. Image: Chris Hodson.</p></div>
<p>It&#8217;s not clear whether this was an isolated incident, or whether the fraudsters also ran ads using other hacked Facebook accounts. A spokesperson for Facebook said the company is still investigating the incident. A request for comment sent via email to Campari&#8217;s media relations team was returned as undeliverable.</p>
<p>But it seems likely we will continue to see more of this and other mainstream advertising efforts by ransomware groups going forward, even if victims really <a href="https://krebsonsecurity.com/2020/11/why-paying-to-delete-stolen-data-is-bonkers/" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">have no expectation that paying an extortion demand will result in criminals actually deleting or not otherwise using stolen data</a>.</p>
<p><strong>Fabian Wosar</strong>, chief technology officer at computer security firm <a href="https://www.emsisoft.com" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Emsisoft</a>, said some ransomware groups have become especially aggressive of late in pressuring their victims to pay up.</p>
<p>&#8220;They have also started to call victims,&#8221; Wosar said. &#8220;They&#8217;re outsourcing to Indian call centers, who call victims asking when they are going to pay or have their data leaked.&#8221;</p>
											</div><!-- .entry-content -->

While religion is not an unlimited umbrella for ideas, it&#8217;s pretty clear that people and the law have considered it somewhere between douchey and criminal to discriminate based on a difference of opinion.</p>
<p>Secondly, common carrier protections &#8211; Remember that big debate about &#8220;Net Neutrality&#8221;?  The gist of that was codifying in law the non-discrimination aspect of the common carrier doctrine as it applies to communications.   The Left was adamant about getting more government regulation involved in the peering arrangements of private ISPs so that evil corporations like Comcast couldn&#8217;t charge more &#8211; e.g. to Netflix and Amazon for the privilege of access to Comcast&#8217;s customers, or block access to types of traffic e.g. torrents, etc.  While it specifically applies to ISPs,  the principles where clear &#8211; zero discrimination based on user, content, website, platform, application, type of equipment, source address, destination address, or method of communication.      The common carrier concept derives from the carriage of letters, cargo people, etc.   A common carrier provides service to the public _without discrimination_, as opposed to a contract carrier who can discriminate, but also accepts more liability for what their customer is asking them to carry.   Common law does not favor the censors.</p>
<p>Section 230 of the CDA of &#8217;96 protects providers and users from being treated as the publisher or speaker of information provided by another content provider. Now if Yelp were to start curating content &#8211; e.g. annotating comments with links to their own opinions about the mater, or blocking content that is not otherwise illegal or objectionable, but on the basis of their own viewpoints, it starts to become less clear that Yelp isn&#8217;t at least a partial contributor / provider of that content, and as such accepting liability for publishing or speaking that content.   I don&#8217;t know why they would risk giving up their section 230 protection by getting involved at all with modifying or curating user content.
