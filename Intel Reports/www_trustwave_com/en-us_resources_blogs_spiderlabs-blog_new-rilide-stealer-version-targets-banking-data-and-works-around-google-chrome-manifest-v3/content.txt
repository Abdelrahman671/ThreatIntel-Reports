          <h1>New Rilide Stealer Version Targets Banking Data and Works Around Google Chrome Manifest V3</h1>          
          
        </div>
        <div class="blog-details-header-right">
          
            
            <a href="#" class="theme-changer">Change theme to light</a>
            
            
            <img src="https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Web/Spiders/2024/spider_01-mob.png?width=450&amp;height=250&amp;name=spider_01-mob.png" loading="eager" alt="New Rilide Stealer Version Targets Banking Data and Works Around Google Chrome Manifest V3" class="hero-spider">
            
          
        </div>
      </div>
      <div class="toc-content content-wrapper">
        
        <div class="wrapper-main_content">
          
          <div class="blog-details-metadata">
            <span class="date-time">
              <time datetime="2023-08-03 10:38:00">August 03, 2023</time>
            </span>
            <span class="minread">10 Minute Read</span>
            
              <span class="blog-author">by Pawel Knapczyk, Wojciech Cieslak</span>
            
          </div> 
          
          
          <div class="wrapper-blog-content">
            <span id="hs_cos_wrapper_post_body" class="hs_cos_wrapper hs_cos_wrapper_meta_field hs_cos_wrapper_type_rich_text" style="" data-hs-cos-general-type="meta_field" data-hs-cos-type="rich_text"><p>Trustwave SpiderLabs discovered a new version of the Rilide Stealer extension targeting Chromium-based browsers such as Google Chrome, Microsoft Edge, Brave, and Opera. This malware uses a creative way to work around the Chrome Extension Manifest V3 from Google which is aimed at blocking the installation of malicious extensions for<span>&nbsp;</span><span>chromium browsers</span>.</p>
<!--more--><p>While sharing similarities with its predecessor, which was discovered by SpiderLabs and<span>&nbsp;</span><a href="https://www.trustwave.com/en-us/resources/blogs/spiderlabs-blog/rilide-a-new-malicious-browser-extension-for-stealing-cryptocurrencies/">described in our previous blog</a>, it exhibits a higher level of sophistication through modular design, code obfuscation, adaptation to the Chrome Extension Manifest V3, and additional features such as the ability to exfiltrate stolen data to a Telegram channel or interval-based screenshot captures.&nbsp;</p>
<p>&nbsp;We have identified campaigns in the wild which we will examine in detail:</p>
<ul>
<li>The first Rilide campaign seems to target corporate users through the use of a PowerPoint phishing lure and a fake Palo Alto GlobalProtect plugin.</li>
<li>The second campaign advertises fake P2E (Play To Earn) games using Twitter. A beta installer was found dropping Rilide and Redline Stealer.</li>
<li>A third campaign from the last few days focuses on banking data of users in Australia and the UK, employing a unique method for loading extensions. Interestingly, we found that crypto token phishing sites from that campaign exclusively employed AngelDrainer scripts to steal cryptocurrencies from unsuspecting users' wallets. Further analysis revealed Twitter as a prominent distribution channel for these malicious activities.</li>
</ul>
<p>During the investigation of Rilide's related domains and associated IP addresses, we discovered over 1,300 phishing websites impersonating various entities, including banks, government services, software companies, delivery services, and crypto token airdrops. Among these websites, several were found to be distributing harmful malware like BumbleBee, IceID, or Phorpiex.</p>
<h3>Updates in the New Version of Rilide</h3>
<p>As does its predecessor, the new Rilide stealer enables threat actors to carry out a broad spectrum of malicious activities, including enabling or disabling other browser extensions, retrieving browsing history and cookies, stealing login credentials, taking on demand screenshots, and injecting malicious scripts to withdraw funds from various cryptocurrency exchanges.</p>
<p>The biggest change is adoption to the Chrome Extension Manifest V3, described in the next section. This change required the complete refactor of the Rilide stealer’s main capabilities. The updated version of Rilide stealer malware was first observed by Trellix and tracked as ‘CookieGenesis’ in their<span>&nbsp;</span><a href="https://www.trellix.com/en-hk/about/newsroom/stories/research/genesis-market-no-longer-feeds-the-evil-cookie-monster.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener">report</a>.</p>
<p>The commands configured in the Rilide extension packages observed are similar to those identified by Trellix, but the functionality has been extended with a new command called ‘screenshot_rules’ . This lets the attacker capture active tab screenshots for every time interval, according to predefined URL rules, allowing recording of sensitive data, such as credit card details entered during online transactions. Another interesting feature is the ability to exfiltrate stolen data to a Telegram channel.</p>

<h3>Rilide’s Adaptation to Manifest V3</h3>
<p>As per the Chrome<span>&nbsp;</span><a href="https://developer.chrome.com/docs/extensions/mv3/intro/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">documentation</a><span>&nbsp;</span>‘an extension manifest gives the browser information about the extension, such as the most important files and the capabilities the extension might use‘. This information is stored in the JSON-formatted file called manifest.json and located in the extension’s root directory.</p>
<p><a href="https://21158977.fs1.hubspotusercontent-na1.net/hubfs/21158977/Web/Blogs/SpiderLab/BSL_20329_fig2.webp" rel="noopener" target="_blank" linktext=""><img loading="lazy" src="https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Web/Blogs/SpiderLab/BSL_20329_fig2.webp?width=1376&amp;height=852&amp;name=BSL_20329_fig2.webp" alt="BSL_20329_fig2" width="1376" height="852" style="margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto; display: block; height: auto; max-width: 100%; width: 1376px;" srcset="https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Web/Blogs/SpiderLab/BSL_20329_fig2.webp?width=688&amp;height=426&amp;name=BSL_20329_fig2.webp 688w, https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Web/Blogs/SpiderLab/BSL_20329_fig2.webp?width=1376&amp;height=852&amp;name=BSL_20329_fig2.webp 1376w, https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Web/Blogs/SpiderLab/BSL_20329_fig2.webp?width=2064&amp;height=1278&amp;name=BSL_20329_fig2.webp 2064w, https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Web/Blogs/SpiderLab/BSL_20329_fig2.webp?width=2752&amp;height=1704&amp;name=BSL_20329_fig2.webp 2752w, https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Web/Blogs/SpiderLab/BSL_20329_fig2.webp?width=3440&amp;height=2130&amp;name=BSL_20329_fig2.webp 3440w, https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Web/Blogs/SpiderLab/BSL_20329_fig2.webp?width=4128&amp;height=2556&amp;name=BSL_20329_fig2.webp 4128w" sizes="(max-width: 1376px) 100vw, 1376px"></a></p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><em>Figure 2. Comparison of Rilide stealer manifest version V2 and V3</em></p>
<p>With the introduction of a Manifest V3 there are several features and functional changes for extensions compared to the previous version called Manifest V2.&nbsp; With security in mind, one of the new major improvements is that extensions<span>&nbsp;</span><a href="https://developer.chrome.com/docs/extensions/mv3/intro/mv3-overview/#remotely-hosted-code" target="_blank" data-anchor="#remotely-hosted-code" rel="noopener">can’t load remote JavaScript</a><span>&nbsp;</span>code and execute arbitrary strings. Specifically, all logic must be included in the extension package thus allowing the more reliable and effective review process for the extensions submitted to the Chrome Web Store.</p>
<p>This is a big hit for the core functionality of Rilide, that relied on the injection of the remotely hosted scripts. The old approach leveraged in the previous version of Rilide does not work because it violates the minimum Content Security Policy applied for the extensions. It ensures that the extension will not run in-line JavaScript or be able to evaluate strings as executable<span>&nbsp;</span><a href="https://developer.chrome.com/docs/extensions/mv3/manifest/content_security_policy/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">code</a>.</p>
<p><a href="https://21158977.fs1.hubspotusercontent-na1.net/hubfs/21158977/Web/Blogs/SpiderLab/BSL_20330_fig3.webp" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><img loading="lazy" src="https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Web/Blogs/SpiderLab/BSL_20330_fig3.webp?width=684&amp;height=306&amp;name=BSL_20330_fig3.webp" alt="BSL_20330_fig3" width="684" height="306" style="height: auto; max-width: 100%; width: 684px;" srcset="https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Web/Blogs/SpiderLab/BSL_20330_fig3.webp?width=342&amp;height=153&amp;name=BSL_20330_fig3.webp 342w, https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Web/Blogs/SpiderLab/BSL_20330_fig3.webp?width=684&amp;height=306&amp;name=BSL_20330_fig3.webp 684w, https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Web/Blogs/SpiderLab/BSL_20330_fig3.webp?width=1026&amp;height=459&amp;name=BSL_20330_fig3.webp 1026w, https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Web/Blogs/SpiderLab/BSL_20330_fig3.webp?width=1368&amp;height=612&amp;name=BSL_20330_fig3.webp 1368w, https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Web/Blogs/SpiderLab/BSL_20330_fig3.webp?width=1710&amp;height=765&amp;name=BSL_20330_fig3.webp 1710w, https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Web/Blogs/SpiderLab/BSL_20330_fig3.webp?width=2052&amp;height=918&amp;name=BSL_20330_fig3.webp 2052w" sizes="(max-width: 684px) 100vw, 684px"></a></p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><em>Figure 3. Violation of Content Security Policy, while loading script using the approach used in old version of Rilide.</em></p>
<p>However as described in the<span>&nbsp;</span><a href="https://chromium.googlesource.com/chromium/src/+/main/extensions/docs/security_faq.md" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Extensions Security FAQ</a>, execution of a remote code in extensions is a policy change for the developers distributing through the Chrome Web Store and not enforced by the Chromium browser. While there were additional restrictions implemented with the release of Manifest V3, there are known ways to execute remotely hosted code that are not considered a security bug. Extensions leveraging such workarounds should be rejected during the review process when submitted to the Chrome Web Store.</p>
<p><a href="https://www.trustwave.com/media/20331/fig4.png"><img loading="lazy" src="https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Web/Blogs/SpiderLab/BSL_20331_fig4.webp?width=1688&amp;height=399&amp;name=BSL_20331_fig4.webp" alt="BSL_20331_fig4" width="1688" height="399" style="height: auto; max-width: 100%; width: 1688px;" srcset="https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Web/Blogs/SpiderLab/BSL_20331_fig4.webp?width=844&amp;height=200&amp;name=BSL_20331_fig4.webp 844w, https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Web/Blogs/SpiderLab/BSL_20331_fig4.webp?width=1688&amp;height=399&amp;name=BSL_20331_fig4.webp 1688w, https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Web/Blogs/SpiderLab/BSL_20331_fig4.webp?width=2532&amp;height=599&amp;name=BSL_20331_fig4.webp 2532w, https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Web/Blogs/SpiderLab/BSL_20331_fig4.webp?width=3376&amp;height=798&amp;name=BSL_20331_fig4.webp 3376w, https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Web/Blogs/SpiderLab/BSL_20331_fig4.webp?width=4220&amp;height=998&amp;name=BSL_20331_fig4.webp 4220w, https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Web/Blogs/SpiderLab/BSL_20331_fig4.webp?width=5064&amp;height=1197&amp;name=BSL_20331_fig4.webp 5064w" sizes="(max-width: 1688px) 100vw, 1688px"></a></p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><em>Figure 4. Paragraph from the Chromium Extensions Security FAQ confirming remotely hosted code execution in manifest V3.</em></p>
<p>The review processes are never 100% fault proof and Google is constantly<span>&nbsp;</span><a href="https://www.bleepingcomputer.com/news/security/malicious-chrome-extensions-with-75m-installs-removed-from-web-store/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">removing malicious extensions</a><span>&nbsp;</span>from the Chrome Web Store. While threat actors may try to hide the parts of code responsible for the execution of remotely hosted code to pass the review process, this is not really an issue for Rilide, which is distributed via local loaders executed by unsuspecting users.</p>
<p>Developers of the Rilide malware used combination of few publicly disclosed techniques to achieve injection of a remotely hosted script.</p>
<p>The core of the functionality relies on use of inline events to execute malicious JavaScript code. This technique was described in a popular<span>&nbsp;</span><a href="https://stackoverflow.com/a/9517879" target="_blank" rel="noopener">answer</a><span>&nbsp;</span>to the Stack Overflow topic around Chrome extensions content scripts.&nbsp; Threat Actors used the exact code pattern shared in the answer.</p>
<p><a href="https://21158977.fs1.hubspotusercontent-na1.net/hubfs/21158977/Web/Blogs/SpiderLab/BSL_20332_fig5.webp" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><img loading="lazy" src="https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Web/Blogs/SpiderLab/BSL_20332_fig5.webp?width=684&amp;height=460&amp;name=BSL_20332_fig5.webp" alt="BSL_20332_fig5" width="684" height="460" style="height: auto; max-width: 100%; width: 684px;" srcset="https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Web/Blogs/SpiderLab/BSL_20332_fig5.webp?width=342&amp;height=230&amp;name=BSL_20332_fig5.webp 342w, https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Web/Blogs/SpiderLab/BSL_20332_fig5.webp?width=684&amp;height=460&amp;name=BSL_20332_fig5.webp 684w, https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Web/Blogs/SpiderLab/BSL_20332_fig5.webp?width=1026&amp;height=690&amp;name=BSL_20332_fig5.webp 1026w, https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Web/Blogs/SpiderLab/BSL_20332_fig5.webp?width=1368&amp;height=920&amp;name=BSL_20332_fig5.webp 1368w, https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Web/Blogs/SpiderLab/BSL_20332_fig5.webp?width=1710&amp;height=1150&amp;name=BSL_20332_fig5.webp 1710w, https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Web/Blogs/SpiderLab/BSL_20332_fig5.webp?width=2052&amp;height=1380&amp;name=BSL_20332_fig5.webp 2052w" sizes="(max-width: 684px) 100vw, 684px"></a></p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><em>Figure 5 Stack Overflow answer detailing the code execution via inline events, as observed in the new Rilide version.</em></p>
<p>Source: https://stackoverflow.com/a/9517879</p>
<p>To overcome the Content Security Policy, the developers used the Declarative Net Requests rules to remove the CSP headers. It’s worth noting that the headers are still displayed in the Network tab of the Developer Tools in Chrome, which may mislead the user trying to analyze suspicious behavior.</p>
<h3>Code Obfuscation</h3>
<p>With the refactor of the code, developers of Rilide malware applied modular code structure with the core capabilities in the /src/functions folder. However, we can see the similarities in naming conventions and C2 endpoints used by the malware.</p>
<p><a href="https://21158977.fs1.hubspotusercontent-na1.net/hubfs/21158977/Web/Blogs/SpiderLab/BSL_20333_fig6.webp" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><img loading="lazy" src="https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Web/Blogs/SpiderLab/BSL_20333_fig6.webp?width=684&amp;height=285&amp;name=BSL_20333_fig6.webp" alt="BSL_20333_fig6" width="684" height="285" style="height: auto; max-width: 100%; width: 684px;" srcset="https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Web/Blogs/SpiderLab/BSL_20333_fig6.webp?width=342&amp;height=143&amp;name=BSL_20333_fig6.webp 342w, https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Web/Blogs/SpiderLab/BSL_20333_fig6.webp?width=684&amp;height=285&amp;name=BSL_20333_fig6.webp 684w, https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Web/Blogs/SpiderLab/BSL_20333_fig6.webp?width=1026&amp;height=428&amp;name=BSL_20333_fig6.webp 1026w, https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Web/Blogs/SpiderLab/BSL_20333_fig6.webp?width=1368&amp;height=570&amp;name=BSL_20333_fig6.webp 1368w, https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Web/Blogs/SpiderLab/BSL_20333_fig6.webp?width=1710&amp;height=713&amp;name=BSL_20333_fig6.webp 1710w, https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Web/Blogs/SpiderLab/BSL_20333_fig6.webp?width=2052&amp;height=855&amp;name=BSL_20333_fig6.webp 2052w" sizes="(max-width: 684px) 100vw, 684px"></a></p>

<p>Threat Actors applied obfuscation of code to evade detection and make analysis more difficult. They used custom strings obfuscation algorithms to make the code harder to understand. In version 1.8.7 they used a simple list with actual strings that was later subjected to a custom algorithm performing shift operations to get the elements in the list in the right order. In the code instead of using plaintext strings, a call to the function returning a string from the list is used.</p>

<h3>Rilide Targeting Twitter Users using Fake P2E (Play To Earn) Games</h3>
<p>In this campaign threat actors advertise fake P2E (Play To Earn) games.&nbsp; In such games players earn money by collecting NFTs and selling them for real money. Threat actors lured unsuspecting users also with airdrops of a small number of cryptocurrencies for the new users. The games are advertised on Twitter, with additional rewards for the users bringing in new players and retweeting the posts. To make the games more credible, the attackers created dedicated websites and Discord servers. Interestingly the “beta” access is not granted to everyone, but just to the selected individuals based on the Google Form answers. Users must share their Twitter handle and SOL wallets; it is unclear if this is done to add credibility or to pick the more prosperous targets. Upon execution the downloaded installer will drop Rilide and also Redline Stealer.</p>

<p>Interestingly we identified a PowerShell<span>&nbsp;</span><a href="https://www.virustotal.com/gui/file/aa76e48528f473b171b98bfc4d4e4d839a98c255e78382dc6f020e36ed00ea5b" target="_blank" rel="noopener">loader</a><span>&nbsp;</span>installing extensions from this campaign with a new approach. In our previous blogpost we described a well-known technique of loading an extension via a –load-extension flag in Chromium based browsers. However, this technique does not install an extension permanently, when a browser is relaunched without this parameter the extension is not available. That's why the threat actors had to modify the shortcut files for the browsers, to ensure that user will open it with necessary parameter. In the new approach threat actors install the extension permanently, the result is as if the user installed the unpacked extension from the browser GUI.</p>
<p>The new techniques leverage modification of a JSON structured file holding part of Chrome’s settings, Secure Preferences.&nbsp; In particular, this file contains information on the installed extensions, like the extension path on disk and its permissions. To ensure that external applications other than the browser cannot modify this file, Chromium introduced a security mechanism based on HMAC hashing. This mechanism is undocumented, however it was already reversed and explained by researchers from Chalmers University of Technology in their<span>&nbsp;</span><a href="https://www.cse.chalmers.se/~andrei/cans20.pdf" target="_blank" rel="noopener">paper</a>. To generate a valid HMAC hash, the attackers require two additional values other than the settings itself, seed stored in the resources.apk file and SID identifier of the current.</p>
<p><a href="https://21158977.fs1.hubspotusercontent-na1.net/hubfs/21158977/Web/Blogs/SpiderLab/BSL_20343_fig17.webp" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><img loading="lazy" src="https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Web/Blogs/SpiderLab/BSL_20343_fig17.webp?width=684&amp;height=353&amp;name=BSL_20343_fig17.webp" alt="BSL_20343_fig17" width="684" height="353" style="height: auto; max-width: 100%; width: 684px;" srcset="https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Web/Blogs/SpiderLab/BSL_20343_fig17.webp?width=342&amp;height=177&amp;name=BSL_20343_fig17.webp 342w, https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Web/Blogs/SpiderLab/BSL_20343_fig17.webp?width=684&amp;height=353&amp;name=BSL_20343_fig17.webp 684w, https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Web/Blogs/SpiderLab/BSL_20343_fig17.webp?width=1026&amp;height=530&amp;name=BSL_20343_fig17.webp 1026w, https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Web/Blogs/SpiderLab/BSL_20343_fig17.webp?width=1368&amp;height=706&amp;name=BSL_20343_fig17.webp 1368w, https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Web/Blogs/SpiderLab/BSL_20343_fig17.webp?width=1710&amp;height=883&amp;name=BSL_20343_fig17.webp 1710w, https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Web/Blogs/SpiderLab/BSL_20343_fig17.webp?width=2052&amp;height=1059&amp;name=BSL_20343_fig17.webp 2052w" sizes="(max-width: 684px) 100vw, 684px"></a></p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><em>Figure 17. HMAC implementation in Chromium based browsers.</em></p>
<p>The hash values generated for single setting entries, like configuration of an extension, are called macs and stored in the Secure Preferences file to ensure their integrity. There is also a value called super_mac that is generated based on all other mac values to ensure the integrity of the entire file.</p>

<h3>Rilide sold on the Underground Forums</h3>
<p>The Trustwave SpiderLabs team identified an actor with the nickname ‘friezer’ selling the Rilide extension and control panel on the XSS and Exploit forums for $5000 (price was dropped to the $3000 during promotion period). The first listings for that product appeared on the forums at the end of January 2023. The actor stated in one of the comments that no one uploads the extension to the Chrome Store and all his clients use self-written droppers. Lack of a dropper bundled together with the extension explains the number and variety of droppers observed in the wild.</p>
<p>The threat actor got into trouble in April, having arbitrage cases opened against him (part of an underground court like system) on both XSS and Exploit forums. One of such cases effectively led to his ban on the Exploit forum. Notably in one of such cases opened by a dissatisfied customer, he made a comment in his defense. He stated that the product got burned by the antivirus solutions on April 5th and he is making fixes for it to work again. That explains the new versions of Rilide with obfuscation of increasing complexity.</p>
<p>In our previous blog we mentioned that source code of the Rilide extension was leaked on the underground forum. <h3>Hunting for Rilide with Permhash</h3>
<p>With an effort to better hunt, cluster, and pivot on malicious APKs and browser extensions, the team at Mandiant created a new framework called Permhash. It creates a hash value based on permissions assigned to the browser extension or an APK. Permhash has been added to the Virustotal platform. In the IOCs section we’ve identified Permhashes for Rilide malware. The malware is under continuous development and the permissions vary between the samples; however, we found the Permhash to correctly cluster different versions of Rilide and help in finding additional samples.</p>
