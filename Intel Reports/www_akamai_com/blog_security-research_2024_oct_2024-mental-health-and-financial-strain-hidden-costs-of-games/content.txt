    <p>To do this, we have to understand why we created the separation in the first place. Our brains like compartmentalization because it’s easy to do. “Mental” is literally in the middle of the word, and we use the term “no-brainer” with glee. Here’s where the negativity lies in wait: <b>In order to categorize something, a judgment has to be applied</b>.</p>
<p>Judgment usually has a negative connotation, and <a href="https://copepsychology.com/why-judging-others-is-bad-for-you/" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noreferrer">science</a> supports that. More often than not, that&#39;s when we’re not doing well mentally. That negativity follows us into the game world, both as a player and as an architect. Those of us who are responsible for these “textured [digital] spaces” (Voll) need not only to be aware of these concerns for ourselves as players but also how we approach designing and securing them.&lt;/p>\r\n&lt;p>&lt;a href=\&#34;https://explodingtopics.com/blog/number-of-gamers\&#34; target=\&#34;_blank\&#34; rel=\&#34;nofollow noreferrer\&#34;>Half the population&lt;/a> are gamers, so the effects of this widespread negativity is rampant among our community. It affects our ability to critically think, grow, and feel a &lt;a href=\&#34;https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/earth-and-planetary-sciences/sense-of-place\&#34; target=\&#34;_blank\&#34; rel=\&#34;nofollow noreferrer\&#34;>sense of place&lt;/a> within a space, which can be exacerbated in online forums (where nuance is lacking) or in highly emotional situations (like losing a game).&amp;nbsp;&lt;/p>\r\n&lt;p>&lt;b>This dark cloud affects how we show up everywhere: to work, in relationships, and especially in our games — a place that can provide us with a sense of identity when we can’t find one within ourselves&lt;/b>.&lt;/p>\r\n&#34;}}">
    <p>We seek games out the most when we crave escapism. More often than not, that's when we’re not doing well mentally. That negativity follows us into the game world, both as a player and as an architect. Those of us who are responsible for these “textured [digital] spaces” (Voll) need not only to be aware of these concerns for ourselves as players but also how we approach designing and securing them.</p>
<p><a href="https://explodingtopics.com/blog/number-of-gamers" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noreferrer">Half the population</a> are gamers, so the effects of this widespread negativity is rampant among our community. It affects our ability to critically think, grow, and feel a <a href="https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/earth-and-planetary-sciences/sense-of-place" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noreferrer">sense of place</a> within a space, which can be exacerbated in online forums (where nuance is lacking) or in highly emotional situations (like losing a game). </p>
<p><b>This dark cloud affects how we show up everywhere: to work, in relationships, and especially in our games — a place that can provide us with a sense of identity when we can’t find one within ourselves</b>.</p>

    <p>We’re all gamers here — no one likes lag, especially when the stakes are survival (in game and out). Computers are just really fast versions of our brains, cache included.     <p>The trauma cache can enhance an “us vs. them” undercurrent that our brain will then use to create an oversimplified and exaggerated belief that “people = unsafe, therefore people = bad.” This is what the 2017 League Honors update was combatting: instead of “us/me vs. them” it’s “us vs. the challenge.” (Thanks to u/yearsofpractice for your comment on a Reddit thread with this idea.)</p>
<p><b>When we start generalizing at the expense of individuality, we subconsciously reinforce stereotypes and choose actions based on judgments for or against those stereotypes rather than on reality</b>.</p>
<p>If the binary is as simple as “good” and “bad” we don’t actually have to expend the effort to think — we can just react because there’s a very clear “right” and “wrong” answer.</p>

<div class="text aem-GridColumn aem-GridColumn--default--12">
<div data-cmp-is="text" class="cmp-text" data-cmp-data-layer="{&#34;text-d521bacd70&#34;:{&#34;@type&#34;:&#34;akamai/components/text&#34;,&#34;repo:modifyDate&#34;:&#34;2024-11-26T14:28:38Z&#34;,&#34;xdm:text&#34;:&#34;&lt;p>Since we slant toward pessimism naturally, those of us raised without a feeling of safety experience a particularly bleak version of reality. <p>You may believe that if someone is a “bad” person, they deserve to be treated poorly. If you believe every person is bad, then everyone is an enemy. Someone who is literally opposing you (such as in-game) will seem even further removed from you. <p>If you feel like your teammate is actually your enemy, you’ll function completely differently than you would if you trusted them. If you grow up never learning how to trust, this can be a huge mental point of contention that could drive a wedge between you and your other social connections, including partners and bosses. </p>
<p>Mix all this up with heightened emotions and a success-based self-worth, and you can get some nasty sportsmanship. That inability to play nicely with friends, of course, may then lead to more solo playing, which just removes any further opportunities to foster collaboration over victory. This isolation intensifies the mental conundrum, as well. We may believe that we are helping everyone by isolating (saving people from being around us and saving ourselves from pain), but we’re actually making it worse. We are social creatures by nature, and actively working against that will <a href="https://www.cdc.gov/social-connectedness/risk-factors/index.html" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noreferrer">worsen</a> preexisting mental conditions. </p>
<p><b>The bottom line: The trauma cache can’t discern a helper from a hurter</b>, so we ultimately end up hurting ourselves even more in the long run via our faulty programming.</p>

<div data-cmp-is="text" class="cmp-text" data-cmp-data-layer="{&#34;text-fdea74786e&#34;:{&#34;@type&#34;:&#34;akamai/components/text&#34;,&#34;repo:modifyDate&#34;:&#34;2024-11-26T16:03:39Z&#34;,&#34;xdm:text&#34;:&#34;&lt;p>Toxic behaviors are often a projection of how we feel we should be treated ourselves. Social media and streamers can exacerbate this issue because they are seen as sources of authority, and they often highlight heightened emotions. A comedian’s performance is that person at 150%. An influencer posts their moments of greatest success.&lt;/p>\r\n&lt;p>Dr. Voll reminds us that “we have a tendency to assume people are just like us” and this is a good example of how that thought can be weaponized. If you believe the norm is 150%, you may unnecessarily judge yourself and others (and thus act) negatively.&lt;/p>\r\n&#34;}}">
    <p>Toxic behaviors are often a projection of how we feel we should be treated ourselves. Social media and streamers can exacerbate this issue because they are seen as sources of authority, and they often highlight heightened emotions. A comedian’s performance is that person at 150%. An influencer posts their moments of greatest success.</p>
<p>Dr. Voll reminds us that “we have a tendency to assume people are just like us” and this is a good example of how that thought can be weaponized. If you believe the norm is 150%, you may unnecessarily judge yourself and others (and thus act) negatively.</p>

      <h2 class="cmp-title__text">The echo chamber of survival<br />
</h2>
    
</div>

    


</div>
<div class="text aem-GridColumn aem-GridColumn--default--12">
<div data-cmp-is="text" class="cmp-text" data-cmp-data-layer="{&#34;text-26b45f319d&#34;:{&#34;@type&#34;:&#34;akamai/components/text&#34;,&#34;repo:modifyDate&#34;:&#34;2024-11-26T14:30:24Z&#34;,&#34;xdm:text&#34;:&#34;&lt;p>Personalities that develop around gaming successes and failures as a means to escape an unsafe situation create an unwavering desperation for success because &lt;b>that is their route to survival&lt;/b>. Becoming a successful e-athlete or streamer could mean getting out of an abusive household. This could make a victory literally feel like the stakes are life or death. Even if our consciousness doesn’t know something is wrong, our brain’s survival mechanism does.&lt;/p>\r\n&lt;p>Because this untreated mental illness is so rampant, these extreme behaviors feel even more relatable to many of us, which creates an echo chamber. Exposure to differences in culture, communication style — even hair color — provides us more with context and nuance, which can give us that extra second to think rather than act. This gained information can greatly affect how we show up in the next similar situation, and allow us to (hopefully) lean more toward the positive the second time.&lt;/p>\r\n&#34;}}">
    <p>Personalities that develop around gaming successes and failures as a means to escape an unsafe situation create an unwavering desperation for success because <b>that is their route to survival</b>. Becoming a successful e-athlete or streamer could mean getting out of an abusive household. This could make a victory literally feel like the stakes are life or death. Even if our consciousness doesn’t know something is wrong, our brain’s survival mechanism does.</p>
<p>Because this untreated mental illness is so rampant, these extreme behaviors feel even more relatable to many of us, which creates an echo chamber. Exposure to differences in culture, communication style — even hair color — provides us more with context and nuance, which can give us that extra second to think rather than act. This gained information can greatly affect how we show up in the next similar situation, and allow us to (hopefully) lean more toward the positive the second time.</p>

Working together allows you to understand how the machine works as a whole and find your place within said machine.&lt;/p>\r\n&#34;}}">
    <p>There may not be an I in team but there is one in collaboration. <b>Individuality within the collective should be celebrated, not suppressed — there is a difference between individuals working together for a common cause and getting lost in the thoughtless hoard</b>.</p>
<p>The 2017 League Honors update by Voll and her team is a great case study on how collaboration doesn’t have to mean putting oneself down for the sake of the many. Working together allows you to understand how the machine works as a whole and find your place within said machine.</p>
