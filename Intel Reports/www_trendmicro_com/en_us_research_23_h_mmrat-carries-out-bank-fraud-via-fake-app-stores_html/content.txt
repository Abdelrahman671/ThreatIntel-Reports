            <div class="richText">
	
    


	
		<div>
			<p>The Trend Micro Mobile Application Reputation Service (MARS) team discovered a new, fully undetected Android banking trojan, dubbed MMRat (detected by TrendMicro as AndroidOS_MMRat.HRX), that has been targeting mobile users in Southeast Asia since late June 2023. The malware, named after its distinctive package name <i>com.mm.user</i>, can capture user input and screen content, and can also remotely control victim devices through various techniques, enabling its operators to carry out bank fraud on the victim’s device.</p>
<p>Furthermore, MMRat uses a special customized command-and-control (C&amp;C) protocol based on protocol buffers (aka Protobuf), an open-source data format used for serializing structured data. This feature, which is rarely seen in Android banking trojans, enhances its performance during the transfer of large volumes of data.</p>
<h1><span class="main-subtitle-black"><span class="body-subhead-title">Distribution analysis</span></span></h1>
<p>Our analysis reveals that most MMRat samples are downloaded from a series of similar phishing websites disguised as official app stores. These websites primarily differ in language, which indicates the target victims of MMRat’s operators. However, the exact method by which these phishing links reach the victim’s devices remains unclear.</p>

		</div>
	

</div>

		<div>
			<p>At the time of writing, the malware has managed to remain entirely undetected on VirusTotal, demonstrating that the techniques used to allow it to remain under the radar have been successful. Similar malware, such as <a href="https://cyble.com/blog/gigabud-rat-new-android-rat-masquerading-as-government-agencies/">GigabudRat</a> and <a href="https://www.threatfabric.com/blogs/vultur-v-for-vnc">Vultur</a>, which also exploit similar techniques such as keylogging and screen capturing, achieve notable anti-evasion results during their attack stages</p>
<h1><span class="body-subhead-title">How MMRat is used in bank fraud</span></h1>
<ol>
<li>The MMRat attack sequence:</li>
<li>The victim downloads and installs MMRat.</li>
<li>The victim grants MMRat the necessary permissions.</li>
<li>MMRat starts to communicate with the remote server and sends a large amount of data that includes device status, personal data, and keylogging data.</li>
<li>When the target device isn’t being used, the threat actor can wake up the device remotely, unlock the screen, and perform bank fraud. Concurrently, the threat actor can also initiate screen capturing for server-side visualization of the device screen.</li>
<li>In the final step, MMRat uninstalls itself, removing all traces of the malware from the system.</li>
</ol>

		</div>
	

</div>

		<div>
			<h1><span class="body-subhead-title">Analysis of MMRat</span></h1>
<p>As previously mentioned, MMRat is capable of capturing user input, screen content and remotely controlling the devices of its victims. It relies heavily on Android Accessibility service and MediaProjection API to function properly.</p>
<h2><span class="body-subhead-title">Impersonation and persistence routine</span></h2>
<p>To avoid suspicion, MMRat often masquerades as an official government or dating app, then presents a phishing website to victims upon being launched. Subsequently, it registers a receiver that can receive system events, including the ability to detect when the system switches on and off, and reboots, among others. Upon the receipt of these events, the malware launches a 1x1-sized pixel activity to ensure its persistence.</p>

		</div>
	

</div>

<h2><span class="body-subhead-title">Hiding tracks</span></h2>
<p>The MMRat malware possesses the capability to delete itself upon receiving the C&amp;C command <i>UNINSTALL_APP</i>. This behavior usually takes place after the execution of bank fraud, making it more difficult to trace its activities.</p>

			<h1><span class="body-subhead-title">Conclusion and recommendation</span></h1>
<p>MMRat is a potent Android banking trojan that poses a considerable threat to mobile users, particularly in Southeast Asia. Its key capabilities, which includ keylogging, screen recording, and <a name="_Int_Aadci8xG" id="_Int_Aadci8xG"></a>remote control access, <a name="_Int_DMoN4GTF" id="_Int_DMoN4GTF"></a>enables it to execute bank fraud effectively and efficiently.</p>
<p>To protect against this malware, users are advised to:</p>
<ol>
<li>Only download apps from official sources. MMRat is often downloaded from phishing websites posing as official app stores. Always use trusted platforms such as Google Play Store or Apple App Store.</li>
<li>Regularly update device software. Updates often include security enhancements that protect against new threats like MMRat.</li>
<li>Be cautious of granting accessibility permissions. MMRat exploits Android's Accessibility service to carry out its malicious activities. Always scrutinize the permissions requested by apps.</li>
<li>Install a reputable security solution on your device. This can help detect and remove threats before they can cause harm.</li>
<li>Be vigilant with your personal and banking information. MMRat's goal is to commit bank fraud, so be cautious about the information you share online and the data you provide to your personal apps.</li>
</ol>

As part of this alliance, Trend, in partnership with Google, helps protect users from malicious actors, keeping the world safer for exchanging digital information.</p>
