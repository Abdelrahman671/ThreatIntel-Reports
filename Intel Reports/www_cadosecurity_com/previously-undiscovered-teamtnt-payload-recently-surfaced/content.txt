<!--more--><h2>Introduction</h2> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:heading --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>Sysdig’s threat research team recently reported on a fascinating client engagement they conducted, in response to a sophisticated cloud attack resulting in the loss of proprietary data. This multi-stage attack piqued the interest of those working in the cloud security industry, as it involved a complex exploitation chain - including credential theft and lateral movement between AWS services.</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>Part of the attack lifecycle involved the deployment of a miner, which Sysdig analysts suspect may have been deployed as a distraction or decoy to aid data exfiltration. After understanding the details of this attack, Cado threat researchers performed a quick VirusTotal triage of files uploaded around the time of the report. Some interesting IoCs were found as a result.</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>One such IoC was the name of an XMRig configuration file: config_background.json. A quick search in VirusTotal led us to this file, uploaded on the 25th of February 2023. Three days prior to the publication of Sysdig’s blog.</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:image {"align":"center","id":7080,"sizeSlug":"full","linkDestination":"none"} --></p> 
<figure class="wp-block-image aligncenter size-full"> 
 <img src="https://14518100.fs1.hubspotusercontent-na1.net/hubfs/14518100/Imported_Blog_Media/image6-21.png" alt="" class="wp-image-7080"> 
</figure> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:image --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>From the XMRig configuration file, we could see an obvious pivot to the parent <a href="https://www.virustotal.com/gui/file/61fdad6d9b149e8d4fc54a848a25219eb9f1364a58073c27eadde8f8298a9573/content">executable</a>, which is where things start to get really interesting - potentially involving a well-known cloud threat actor: TeamTNT.</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:heading --></p> 
<h2>An Old Friend?</h2> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:heading --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>At Cado we know TeamTNT well, having been the first organisation to identify their <a href="https://www.cadosecurity.com/team-tnt-the-first-crypto-mining-worm-to-steal-aws-credentials/">original AWS-specific attacks</a>.</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>There has been some <a href="https://www.trendmicro.com/en_us/research/22/j/teamtnt-returns-or-does-it.html">speculation</a> about the return of TeamTNT for some time now, since their public announcement of retirement in late 2021.&nbsp;</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:image {"align":"center","id":7081,"sizeSlug":"full","linkDestination":"none"} --></p> 
<figure class="wp-block-image aligncenter size-full"> 
 <img src="https://14518100.fs1.hubspotusercontent-na1.net/hubfs/14518100/Imported_Blog_Media/image13-8.png" alt="" class="wp-image-7081"> 
</figure> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:image --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>In that time, several cloud-focused threat actors have appropriated TTPs attributed to TeamTNT, in an attempt to foil attribution of their own activities. Since much of the TeamTNT tooling is delivered as shell scripts, it’s very easy to copy the functionality.</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>That makes the question - “Is this TeamTNT? And if so, is this new activity or just newly uncovered older activity?” harder to answer.</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:heading --></p> 
<h2>Malware Analysis</h2> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:heading --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:image {"align":"center","id":7082,"sizeSlug":"full","linkDestination":"none"} --></p> 
<figure class="wp-block-image aligncenter size-full"> 
 <img src="https://14518100.fs1.hubspotusercontent-na1.net/hubfs/14518100/Imported_Blog_Media/image3-25.png" alt="" class="wp-image-7082"> 
</figure> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:image --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>The script begins with the header from the screenshot above and includes strings like “hilde”, known to be linked to TeamTNT. The domain DonaldTrump[.]cc was last updated on the 2nd of May 2021 and hasn’t previously been attributed to a malware campaign (TeamTNT or otherwise).&nbsp;</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:heading {"level":3} --></p> 
<h3>System Preparation</h3> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:heading --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>A number of system weakening and preparatory techniques to facilitate mining can be observed at the start of the script. These techniques are common and aren’t worth elaborating on, but they include:</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:list --></p> 
<ul> 
 <!-- wp:list-item --> 
 <li>Configuration of resource limits via the <em>ulimit</em> command,</li> 
 <p> 
  <!-- /wp:list-item --></p> 
 <p> 
  <!-- wp:list-item --></p> 
 <li>setting the $HISTFILE envar to /dev/null to prevent command history logging and</li> 
 <p> 
  <!-- /wp:list-item --></p> 
 <p> 
  <!-- wp:list-item --></p> 
 <li>configuring iptables to accept all ingress or egress traffic.</li> 
 <p> 
  <!-- /wp:list-item --></p> 
</ul> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:list --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:image {"align":"center","id":7083,"sizeSlug":"full","linkDestination":"none"} --></p> 
<figure class="wp-block-image aligncenter size-full">

  <em>Example of system weakening commands</em> 
 </figcaption> 
</figure> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:image --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>Similarly, enumeration of hardware resources in preparation for the miner can be seen further down the script.</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:image {"align":"center","id":7084,"sizeSlug":"full","linkDestination":"none"} --></p> 
<figure class="wp-block-image aligncenter size-full">

  <em>Example of Hashrate calculation and thread number enumeration</em> 
  <br> 
 </figcaption> 
</figure> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:image --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:heading {"level":3} --></p> 
<h3>System Cleaning and Dynamic Linker Hijacking</h3> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:heading --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>Typical for TeamTNT, two functions have been dedicated to cleaning the system of artifacts left by prior compromises - both by themselves and competing miner groups.</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>The first such function - named <em>CLEANUP_BY_TRUMP()</em> (references to Donald Trump are found throughout) - includes an additional base64-encoded payload entitled LD.PRELOAD.CLEANER. At the time of writing, this payload was unavailable on VirusTotal and other public repositories. Making it more likely that these payloads are from an as yet undiscovered TeamTNT campaign.</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>As the name suggests, this script ensures that the file /etc/ld.so.preload exists and is writable. It also unsets envars related to the dynamic linker, preparing the system for <a href="https://www.cadosecurity.com/linux-attack-techniques-dynamic-linker-hijacking-with-ld-preload/">dynamic linker hijacking</a> (T1574.006). This is another technique typically leveraged by TeamTNT, normally it is used to register a process hider which is able to cloak the XMRig miner process.</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:image {"align":"center","id":7085,"sizeSlug":"full","linkDestination":"none"} --></p> 
<figure class="wp-block-image aligncenter size-full"> 
 <img src="https://14518100.fs1.hubspotusercontent-na1.net/hubfs/14518100/Imported_Blog_Media/image12-8.png" alt="" class="wp-image-7085"> 
 <br> 
 <figcaption class="wp-element-caption"> 
  <em>Snippet of LD.PRELOAD.CLEANER</em> 
 </figcaption> 
</figure> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:image --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>A final note on the LD.PRELOAD.CLEANER script: log statements found throughout the script are written in German, consistent with prior TeamTNT campaigns and <a href="https://documents.trendmicro.com/assets/white_papers/wp-tracking-the-activities-of-teamTNT.pdf">knowledge</a> about this threat actor.&nbsp;</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>The rest of the <em>CLEANUP_BY_TRUMP() function </em>handles removing miner domains from /etc/hosts, reverting a renamed version of the ps command (a technique WatchDog have leveraged in the <a href="https://www.cadosecurity.com/tales-from-the-honeypot-watchdog-evolves-with-a-new-multi-stage-cryptojacking-attack/">past</a>) and cleaning evidence of prior compromise from the crontab.</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:heading {"level":3} --></p> 
<h3>Wallet Configuration</h3> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:heading --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>XMRig ships with a default configuration file, within which mining parameters can be set. Most cryptojacking scripts set these parameters dynamically and this script is no different. In a function named <em>WalletConfigSet(),</em> parameters - such as the mining pool address, username and password - are written to disk. The config file is then renamed to config_background.json, which happens to be the same configuration filename referenced in the Sysdig blog.</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:image {"align":"center","id":7087,"sizeSlug":"full","linkDestination":"none"} --></p> 
<figure class="wp-block-image aligncenter size-full"> 
 <img src="https://14518100.fs1.hubspotusercontent-na1.net/hubfs/14518100/Imported_Blog_Media/image15-3.png" alt="" class="wp-image-7087"> 
 <br> 
 <figcaption class="wp-element-caption"> 
  <em>Snippet of WalletConfigSet() function</em> 
 </figcaption> 
</figure> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:image --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>The user parameter (wallet address) seen in the above has been observed in prior campaigns <a href="https://cloud-tencent-com.translate.goog/developer/article/1891498?_x_tr_sl=zh-CN&amp;_x_tr_tl=en&amp;_x_tr_hl=en&amp;_x_tr_pto=sc">attributed</a> to TeamTNT. A full list of IoCs is available at the end of this blog.</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:heading {"level":3} --></p> 
<h3>Registering a Process Hider</h3> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:heading --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>Cryptojacking groups frequently make use of <a href="https://github.com/gianlucaborello/libprocesshider">libprocesshider</a>, a Linux shared object executable used in conjunction with the LD Preload dynamic linker hijacking technique mentioned earlier to hide their malicious processes.&nbsp;</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>In this script we can see the shared object file being retrieved from the URL hxxps://park74110[.]github[.]io/virus/processhider[.]so, which hasn’t previously been seen in cryptojacking campaigns. The file was uploaded to <a href="https://www.virustotal.com/gui/file/c8d05df48e059da25ff3786470f52a4c4918851db742a02887fa592c5b38d101/detection">VirusTotal</a> on the same date as the shell script in question and has a high detection ratio, with most vendors detecting it as “libprocesshider” or similar.</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>As expected, the path to the process hider, renamed as “p.so”, is then inserted into the /etc/ld.so.preload file.</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:image {"align":"center","id":7088,"sizeSlug":"full","linkDestination":"none"} --></p> 
<figure class="wp-block-image aligncenter size-full">

 <br>

 </figcaption> 
</figure>

 <!-- /wp:image --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p>

<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:heading {"level":3} --></p> 
<h3>Persistence</h3> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:heading --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>A number of techniques are used to persist the miner across reboots.</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>First, a simple shell script, named ping.sh, is written to the working directory. This script checks whether XMRig is running via the <em>pidof</em> command. If it’s not, XMRig is executed in the background using the <em>nice</em> command.&nbsp;</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>This script is then written to the file .profile in the user’s home directory. The .profile file is a BASH configuration file typically including commands run at login. Naturally, this is an effective persistence mechanism, as these commands are executed when the user activates a shell session (i.e. logging in after a reboot).</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:image {"align":"center","id":7090,"width":800,"height":409,"sizeSlug":"full","linkDestination":"none"} --></p> 
<figure class="wp-block-image aligncenter size-full is-resized">

 <!-- /wp:image --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>After determining whether systemctl is available on the system, the script then goes on to register an additional persistence mechanism in the form of a systemd service unit. The service itself is straightforward, simply running the downloaded version of XMRig via the <em>nice</em> command and a priority of 10. The service is then saved as SystemRaid.service and added to the systemd directory.&nbsp;</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p>

<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>The script checks to see whether the <em>systemctl </em>command used to start the service was successful and if not, it uses <em>nohup</em> to manually execute the miner in the background.</p>

  <em>Using nohup to execute the miner</em> 
  <br> 
 </figcaption> 
</figure> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:image --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>Finally, a file existence check is performed to determine whether the ping.sh script mentioned previously exists, if so a cron job is written to execute it at an interval of every minute.</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:image {"align":"center","id":7095,"sizeSlug":"full","linkDestination":"none"} --></p> 
<figure class="wp-block-image aligncenter size-full"> 
 <img src="https://14518100.fs1.hubspotusercontent-na1.net/hubfs/14518100/Imported_Blog_Media/image7-11.png" alt="" class="wp-image-7095"> 
 <br> 
 <figcaption class="wp-element-caption"> 
  <em>Registering a cron job for ping.sh</em> 
  <br> 
 </figcaption> 
</figure> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:image --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>The above procedure is repeated for a file named trump.sh. This file was not retrieved during this analysis but it seems likely that it could be a copy of the script that forms the basis of this blog.&nbsp;</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:image {"align":"center","id":7097,"sizeSlug":"full","linkDestination":"none"} --></p> 
<figure class="wp-block-image aligncenter size-full"> 
 <img src="https://14518100.fs1.hubspotusercontent-na1.net/hubfs/14518100/Imported_Blog_Media/image16-4.png" alt="" class="wp-image-7097"> 
 <br> 
 <figcaption class="wp-element-caption"> 
  <em>trump.sh cron registration</em> 
 </figcaption> 
</figure> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:image --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:heading --></p> 
<h2>Conclusion</h2> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:heading --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>Without more information, it’s impossible to conclusively link the sample analysed in this blog to the attack that Sysdig reported, but it’s interesting that these files surfaced around the same time. Perhaps this was the “decoy” miner that they referred to in their original blog.</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>This malware does bear several syntactic and semantic similarities to prior TeamTNT payloads, and includes a wallet ID that has previously been attributed to them. As a result, it can be determined that this script is indeed a TeamTNT payload.&nbsp;</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>New infrastructure, in the form of a previously-unattributed C2 domain, suggests that this sample is part of a new campaign.&nbsp;</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p> 
 <!-- wp:paragraph --></p> 
<p>However, Passive DNS results show that the domain was last updated on the 2nd of May 2021. It seems more likely that this is an old sample that’s never been reported on. Either way, it’s interesting to unearth a previously-undiscovered payload from a threat actor well-known to Cado researchers.</p> 
<p> 
 <!-- /wp:paragraph --></p>
