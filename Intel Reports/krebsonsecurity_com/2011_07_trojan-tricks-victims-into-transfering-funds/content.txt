					<h1 class="entry-title">Trojan Tricks Victims Into Transferring Funds</h1>
				</div>
							
			
			</header><!-- .entry-header -->

	<div id="primary" class="site-content">
		<div id="content" role="main">

			
					<article id="post-10881" class="post-10881 post type-post status-publish format-standard hentry category-sunshine category-latest-warnings category-smallbizvictims category-web-fraud-2-0 tag-bka tag-bundeskriminalant tag-url-zone-trojan tag-zeus">
				<header class="entry-header">
					<div class="clear"></div>
	<div class="btm-wrap">
	<div class="below-title-meta">
		<div class="adt">
			
			
				
				<span class="date updated">July 28, 2011</span>
					
        </div>
		<div class="adt-comment">
		<span><a class="link-comments" href="https://krebsonsecurity.com/2011/07/trojan-tricks-victims-into-transfering-funds/#comments">60 Comments</a></span> 
        </div>    <div class="clear"></div>	   
    </div><!-- below title meta end -->
	</div>
		
						</header><!-- .entry-header -->
		
		
							<div class="entry-content">
												<p>It&#8217;s horrifying enough when a computer crook breaks into your PC, steals your passwords and empties your bank account. Now, a new malware variant uses a devilish scheme to trick people into voluntarily transferring money from their accounts to a cyber thief&#8217;s account.</p>
<p><a href="https://krebsonsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/07/scamkeys.jpg"><img decoding="async" class="alignright size-medium wp-image-10884" title="scamkeys" src="https://krebsonsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/07/scamkeys-300x199.jpg" alt="" width="300" height="199" srcset="https://krebsonsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/07/scamkeys-300x199.jpg 300w, https://krebsonsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/07/scamkeys.jpg 425w" sizes="(max-width: 300px) 100vw, 300px" /></a>The<strong> German Federal Criminal Police</strong> (the &#8220;Bundeskriminalamt&#8221; or BKA for short) recently warned consumers about a new Windows malware strain that waits until the victim logs in to his bank account. The malware then presents the customer with a message stating that a credit has been made to his account by mistake, and that the account has been frozen until the errant payment is transferred back.</p>
<p>When the unwitting user views his account balance, the malware modifies the amounts displayed in his browser; it appears that he has recently received a large transfer into his account. The victim is told to immediately make a transfer to return the funds and unlock his account. The malicious software presents an already filled-in online transfer form &#8212; with the account and routing numbers for a bank account the attacker controls.</p>
<p><span id="more-10881"></span></p>
<p>The BKA&#8217;s <a href="http://www.bka.de/pressemitteilungen/2011/pm110715.html" target="_blank">advisory</a> isn&#8217;t specific about the responsible strain of malware, but it is becoming increasingly common for banking Trojans to incorporate &#8220;Web injects,&#8221; custom designed plug-ins that manipulate what victims see in their Web browsers.</p>
<p>This attack is an insidious extension of the tactic that was pioneered by the <a href="http://news.cnet.com/8301-27080_3-10363836-245.html" target="_blank">URL Zone Trojan</a>, which specializes in manipulating the balance that victims see when they log into their (cleaned-out) bank accounts.</p>
<p>If you log in to your bank account and see something odd, such as a &#8220;down for maintenance&#8221; page or an alert about a wayward transfer, your best option is to pick up the phone and call your bank. Make sure you are using the bank&#8217;s real phone number: Malware like the ZeuS Trojan has been known to present newly-fleeced victims with messages about problems with the bank&#8217;s Web site, along with <a title="Crooks Crank Up Volume of eBanking Attacks" href="http://krebsonsecurity.com/2010/03/crooks-crank-up-volume-of-e-banking-attacks/" target="_blank">a bogus customer support phone number</a>.</p>

				<p>@Neej: &#8220;Well actually you do sort of come across as a hater&#8230;.&#8221;  </p>
<p>When it is somehow unreasonable to point out that the vast majority of online banking losses have occurred due to machines running Microsoft Windows, then it is not us but Truth which &#8220;hates&#8221; Windows.  Things are not going to get better by reflexively loving the system which causes most of our trouble.  </p>
<p>Every modern OS is a large, complex system with exploitable faults.  But there are more Windows systems with faults than there are other systems with faults.  And that is the reason Windows will be attacked more than similar systems.  </p>
<p>&#8220;&#8230;many infections are a result of social engineering in which case no OS that allows functions such as users installing applications is any defense.&#8221; </p>
<p>Contradiction please!  Users *can* install applications in some Live DVD forms, for example Puppy Linux.  An OS which boots from DVD makes it almost impossible for malware to infect the OS across reboots.  While a mere DVD boot does not stop new malware from running, it does clean the system every time it is rebooted, thus reducing the bot problem by orders of magnitude.  </p>
<p>If we had a cut-down version of Microsoft Windows for online use which would boot quickly from DVD, we could get a clean system by rebooting before going online.  But reasonable usage also  requires supporting user configuration and updates across boots, as Puppy Linux does now.  </p>
<p>Or, if Microsoft would release a tool which would certify their own installations as not being infected, users could just run that tool before online banking.  Ironically, banks seem to want customer computer owners to be more capable in practice than Microsoft itself.  </p>
<p>&#8220;As OSX, iOS and Android have become more widely used there has been a correspoding rise in attacks on these platforms – see where I’m going?&#8221;</p>
<p>The vast, vast majority of malware is written to function under Windows.  We can expect that to continue exactly as long as the Windows bots produce the best returns.  </p>
<p>We should not expect attacks to be proportional to target size.  For example, if some OS gets to 15 percent of the installed base, an attacker would then have to choose between running under Microsoft Windows, a 75 percent chance, or running under the new up-and-comer, a 15 percent chance.  So each successful up-and-comer bot install has to be 5 times as profitable as a Windows bot install just to compete.  </p>
<p>Of course, not all attackers are interested in living with Windows, and may be satisfied with what they can get on their favorite platform.  But the business of malware will employ the expertise needed to get the best returns.</p>
