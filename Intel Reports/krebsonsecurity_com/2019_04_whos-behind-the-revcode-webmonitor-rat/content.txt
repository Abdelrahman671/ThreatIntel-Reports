					<h1 class="entry-title">Who&#8217;s Behind the RevCode WebMonitor RAT?</h1>
				</div>
							
			
			</header><!-- .entry-header -->

	<div id="primary" class="site-content">
		<div id="content" role="main">

			
					<article id="post-47508" class="post-47508 post type-post status-publish format-standard hentry category-sunshine category-breadcrumbs category-neer-do-well-news tag-alex-yucel tag-blackshades-rat tag-krabsonsecurity tag-ratsit-ab tag-revcode tag-webmonitor tag-webmonitor-rat">
				<header class="entry-header">
					<div class="clear"></div>
	<div class="btm-wrap">
	<div class="below-title-meta">
		<div class="adt">
			
			
				
				<span class="date updated">April 22, 2019</span>
					
        </div>
		<div class="adt-comment">
		<span><a class="link-comments" href="https://krebsonsecurity.com/2019/04/whos-behind-the-revcode-webmonitor-rat/#comments">65 Comments</a></span> 
        </div>    <div class="clear"></div>	   
    </div><!-- below title meta end -->
	</div>
		
						</header><!-- .entry-header -->
		
		
							<div class="entry-content">
												<p>The owner of a Swedish company behind a popular remote administration tool (RAT) implicated in thousands of malware attacks shares the same name as a Swedish man who pleaded guilty in 2015 to co-creating the <strong>Blackshades RAT</strong>, a similar product that was used to infect more than half a million computers with malware, KrebsOnSecurity has learned.</p>
<div id="attachment_47511" style="width: 605px" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><a class="lightbox" href="https://krebsonsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2019/04/revcode.jpg"><img aria-describedby="caption-attachment-47511" decoding="async" class="wp-image-47511" src="https://krebsonsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2019/04/revcode.jpg" alt="" width="595" height="427" /></a><p id="caption-attachment-47511" class="wp-caption-text">An advertisement for RevCode WebMonitor.</p></div>
<p>At issue is a program called &#8220;<strong>WebMonitor</strong>,&#8221; which was designed to allow users to remotely control a computer (or multiple machines) via a Web browser. The makers of WebMonitor, a company in Sweden called &#8220;<strong>RevCode</strong>,&#8221; say their product is legal and legitimate software &#8220;that helps firms and personal users handle the security of owned devices.&#8221;</p>
<p>But critics say WebMonitor is far more likely to be deployed on &#8220;pwned&#8221; devices, or those that are surreptitiously hacked. The software is broadly classified as malware by most antivirus companies, likely thanks to an advertised feature list that includes dumping the remote computer&#8217;s temporary memory; retrieving passwords from dozens of email programs; snarfing the target&#8217;s Wi-Fi credentials; and viewing the target&#8217;s Webcam.</p>
<p>In <a href="https://unit42.paloaltonetworks.com/unit42-say-cheese-webmonitor-rat-comes-c2-service-c2aas/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">a writeup on WebMonitor published in April 2018</a>, researchers from security firm <strong>Palo Alto Networks</strong> noted that the product has been primarily advertised on underground hacking forums, and that its developers promoted several qualities of the software likely to appeal to cybercriminals looking to secretly compromise PCs.</p>
<p>For example, RevCode&#8217;s website touted the software&#8217;s compatibility with all &#8220;<a href="https://www.trendmicro.com/vinfo/us/security/definition/crypter" target="_blank" rel="noopener">crypters</a>,&#8221; software that can encrypt, obfuscate and manipulate malware to make it harder to detect by antivirus programs. Palo Alto also noted WebMonitor includes the option to suppress any notification boxes that may pop up when the RAT is being installed on a computer.</p>
<div id="attachment_47512" style="width: 609px" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><img aria-describedby="caption-attachment-47512" decoding="async" loading="lazy" class=" wp-image-47512" src="https://krebsonsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2019/04/webmonitor.jpg" alt="" width="599" height="240" /><p id="caption-attachment-47512" class="wp-caption-text">A screenshot of the WebMonitor builder panel.</p></div>
<p>RevCode maintains it is a legitimate company officially registered in Sweden that obeys all applicable Swedish laws. A few hours of searching online turned up <a href="https://translate.googleusercontent.com/translate_c?depth=1&amp;hl=en&amp;prev=search&amp;rurl=translate.google.com&amp;sl=sv&amp;sp=nmt4&amp;u=https://www.ratsit.se/900511ALKO00001-REVCODE&amp;xid=17259,15700002,15700022,15700186,15700190,15700253,15700256,15700259&amp;usg=ALkJrhho5bMKFXsDXE9sFGF1xQhyLBx1SQ" target="_blank" rel="noopener">an interesting record</a> at <a href="https://sv.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ratsit" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Ratsit AB</a>, a credit information service based in Sweden. That record indicates RevCode is owned by 28-year-old Swedish resident <strong>Alex Yücel</strong>.</p>
<p>In February 2015, a then 24-year-old Alex Yücel <a href="https://www.fbi.gov/contact-us/field-offices/newyork/news/press-releases/swedish-co-creator-of-blackshades-malware-that-enabled-users-around-the-world-to-secretly-and-remotely-control-victims-computers-sentenced-to-57-months-in-prison" target="_blank" rel="noopener">pleaded guilty in a U.S. court</a> to computer hacking and to creating, marketing and selling <a href="https://krebsonsecurity.com/2014/05/blackshades-trojan-users-had-it-coming/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Blackshades</a>, a RAT that was used to compromise and spy on hundreds of thousands of computers. Arrested in Moldova in 2013 as part of a large-scale, international takedown against Blackshades and hundreds of customers, Yücel became the first person ever to be extradited from Moldova to the United States.<span id="more-47508"></span></p>
<p>Yücel was sentenced to 57 months in prison, but according to a record for Yücel at the <a href="https://www.bop.gov/inmateloc/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">U.S. Federal Bureau of Prisons</a>, he was released on Nov. 1, 2016. The first advertisements in hacker forums for the sale of WebMonitor began in mid-2017. RevCode was registered as an official Swedish company in 2018, according to Ratsit.</p>
<p>Until recently, RevCode published on its Web site a <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Value-added_tax" target="_blank" rel="noopener">value added tax</a> (VAT) number, an identifier used in many European countries for value added tax purposes. That VAT number &#8212; first noted by the blog <a href="https://krabsonsecurity.com/2019/03/10/some-interesting-notes-on-revcode/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Krabsonsecurity.com</a> (which borrows heavily from this site&#8217;s design and banner but otherwise bears no relation to KrebsOnSecurity.com) &#8212; has since been removed from the RevCode Web site and from <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/2/https://revcode.se/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">historic records at The Internet Archive</a>. The <a href="http://www.vatnumber.net/?vat=SE900511095301&amp;submit=Check" target="_blank" rel="noopener">VAT number cited in that report</a> is registered to Alex Yücel, and matches the number listed for RevCode by Ratsit AB.</p>
<p>Yücel could not be immediately reached for comment. But an unnamed person responded to an email sent to the customer support address listed at RevCode&#8217;s site. Presented with the information and links referenced above, the person responding wrote, &#8220;nobody working for/with RevCode is in any way related to BlackShades. Anything else suggesting otherwise is nothing but rumors and attempts to degrade our company by means of defamation.&#8221;</p>
<p>The person responding from the RevCode support email address contended that the Alex Yücel listed as owner of the company was not the same Alex Yücel convicted of co-authoring Blackshades. However, unless the Ratsit record is completely wrong, this seems unlikely to be true.</p>
<p>According to the Ratsit listing, the Alex Yücel who heads RevCode currently lives in a suburb of Stockholm, Sweden with his parents Can and Rita Yücel. Both Can and Rita Yücel co-signed <a href="https://krebsonsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2019/04/49-1.pdf" target="_blank" rel="noopener">a letter</a> (PDF) in June 2015 testifying to a New York federal court regarding their son&#8217;s upstanding moral character prior to Yücel the younger&#8217;s sentencing for the Blackshades conviction, according to court records.</p>
<div id="attachment_47522" style="width: 608px" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><img aria-describedby="caption-attachment-47522" decoding="async" loading="lazy" class=" wp-image-47522" src="https://krebsonsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2019/04/alex-letter-parents.jpg" alt="" width="598" height="544" /><p id="caption-attachment-47522" class="wp-caption-text">A letter from Alex Yücel&#8217;s parents to the court in June 2016.</p></div>

    the defendant’s age at the time of the conviction<br />
    whether the defendant’s credibility is central to the current case<br />
    the importance of the defendant’s testimony in the current case (the defendant may refuse to testify if evidence of the prior conviction will come in), and<br />
    the defendant’s conduct since the prior conviction.<br />
&#8221;</p>
<p>You make the mistake of thinking in black/white terms.  Prosecutors WILL try to make the case regardless of whether the defendant, the defense, or those sympathetic think it is &#8220;fair&#8221;.</p>
<p>&#8220;What if the author behind Revcode only invested and registered the company in their name..&#8221;<br />
Good point.  Which is why investigations happen.  </p>
<p>I don&#8217;t suggest that prosecutors &#8220;need&#8221; articles like this for evidence.  But rather that the evidence that prosecutors will use, is the same as what Krebs has found.  At most, law enforcement is often alerted to easier cases to prosecute.  Why would the US go after some random malware provider, when they can get Yücel handed on a plate for another conviction with half the effort.  They are busy and do like the lower hanging fruit.</p>

			<section class="comment-content comment">
				<p>Actually, I have found that *very* few cybercriminals are good at operational security. What&#8217;s more, I have yet to find one that couldn&#8217;t be tracked down in real life with enough effort and time spent on the research. </p>
<p>The truth is most cybercriminals do not grow up thinking they want to be cybercrooks. They get into it gradually, and during that transition period almost always fail to fully airgap who their are in real life from who they are online. I have yet to find a single example I&#8217;ve pursued to suggest otherwise. </p>
<p>By the way, this includes lazy and sloppy criminals as well as some of the most accomplished crooks out there. Sometimes, it takes days of research. In other cases, it&#8217;s taken many months. </p>
<p>Nonetheless, in part due to the prolific nature of their activities and the frequency with which they must establish new identities, domains and other forms of identity online that leave trails, it&#8217;s rare to encounter someone who is extremely good at opsec. The rare few that fit this profile are almost without exception sociopaths.</p>
