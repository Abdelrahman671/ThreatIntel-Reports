    <div class="article-tags mb-2 dark big"><p class="article-tag text-capitalize">ESET Research</p></div>    <h1 class="page-headline">WinorDLL64: A backdoor from the vast Lazarus arsenal?</h1>    <p class="sub-title">The targeted region, and overlap in behavior and code, suggest the tool is used by the infamous North Korea-aligned APT group</p>
    
    <div class="article-authors d-flex flex-wrap"><div class="article-author d-flex"><a href="/en/our-experts/vladislav-hrcka/" title="Vladislav Hrčka"><picture><source srcset="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/tn/-x45/wls/2019/11/vladislav-hrcka.jpg" media="(max-width: 768px)" /><img class="author-image me-3"  src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/tn/-x45/wls/2019/11/vladislav-hrcka.jpg" alt="Vladislav Hrčka"  /></picture></a><div class="author-text"><p><a href="/en/our-experts/vladislav-hrcka/" title="Vladislav Hrčka"><b>Vladislav Hrčka</b></a></p></div></div></div>

                    <div class="article-body">
    <p>ESET researchers have discovered one of the payloads of the <a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/2021/10/27/wslink-unique-undocumented-malicious-loader-runs-server/">Wslink downloader</a> that we uncovered back in 2021. We named this payload WinorDLL64 based on its filename <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">WinorDLL64.dll</span>. Wslink, which had the filename <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">WinorLoaderDLL64.dll</span>, is a loader for Windows binaries that, unlike other such loaders, runs as a server and executes received modules in memory. As the wording suggests, a loader serves as a tool to load a payload, or the actual malware, onto the already compromised system. The initial Wslink compromise vector has not been identified.</p>
<p>The initially unknown Wslink payload was uploaded to VirusTotal from South Korea shortly after the publication of our blogpost, and hit one of our YARA rules based on Wslink’s unique name <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">WinorDLL64</span>. Regarding Wslink, ESET telemetry has seen only a few detections - in Central Europe, North America, and the Middle East.</p>
<p>The <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">WinorDLL64</span> payload serves as a backdoor that most notably acquires extensive system information, provides means for file manipulation, such as exfiltrating, overwriting, and removing files, and executes additional commands. Interestingly, it communicates over a connection that was already established by the Wslink loader.</p>
<p>In 2021, we did not find any data that would suggest Wslink is a tool from a known threat actor. However, after an extensive analysis of the payload, we have attributed <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">WinorDLL64</span> to the Lazarus APT group with low confidence based on the targeted region and an overlap in both behavior and code with known Lazarus samples.</p>
<p>Active since at least 2009, this infamous North-Korea aligned group is responsible for high-profile incidents such as both the <a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/2014/12/08/sony-pictures-hacking-traced-thai-hotel-north-korea-denies-involvement/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Sony Pictures Entertainment hack</a> and tens-of-millions-of-dollar <a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/2016/03/15/bangladesh-central-bank-boss-quits-100m-cyberheist/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">cyberheists in 2016</a>, the <a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/2017/05/15/wannacryptor-key-questions-answered/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">WannaCryptor</a> (aka WannaCry) outbreak in 2017, and a long history of disruptive attacks against <a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/2020/11/16/lazarus-supply-chain-attack-south-korea/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">South Korean public and critical infrastructure</a> since at least 2011. US-CERT and the FBI call this group <a href="https://www.cisa.gov/uscert/northkorea" target="_blank" rel="noopener">HIDDEN COBRA</a>.</p>
<p>Based on our <a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/?s=lazarus" target="_blank" rel="noopener">extensive knowledge</a> of the activities and operations of this group, we believe that Lazarus consists of a large team that is systematically organized, well prepared, and is made up of several subgroups that utilize a large toolset. Last year, we <a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/2022/09/30/amazon-themed-campaigns-lazarus-netherlands-belgium/">discovered a Lazarus tool</a> that took advantage of the <a href="https://cve.mitre.org/cgi-bin/cvename.cgi?name=CVE-2021-21551" target="_blank" rel="noopener">CVE‑2021‑21551</a> vulnerability to target an employee of an aerospace company in the Netherlands, and a political journalist in Belgium. It was the first recorded abuse of the vulnerability; in combination, the tool and the vulnerability led to the blinding of the monitoring of all security solutions on compromised machines. We also provided an extensive description of the <a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/2022/03/28/under-hood-wslink-multilayered-virtual-machine/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">structure of the virtual machine</a> used in samples of Wslink.</p>
<p>This blogpost explains the attribution of WinorDLL64 to Lazarus and provides an analysis of the payload.</p>
<h2>Links to Lazarus</h2>
<p>We have discovered overlaps in both behavior and code with Lazarus samples from <a href="https://www.mcafee.com/blogs/other-blogs/mcafee-labs/analyzing-operation-ghostsecret-attack-seeks-to-steal-data-worldwide/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Operation GhostSecret</a> and the <a href="https://www.mcafee.com/blogs/other-blogs/mcafee-labs/hidden-cobra-targets-turkish-financial-sector-new-bankshot-implant/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Bankshot implant</a> described by McAfee. The description of the implants in both GhostSecret and Bankshot articles contains overlaps in the functionality with WinorDLL64 and we found some code overlap in the samples. In this blogpost we will only use the <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">FE887FCAB66D7D7F79F05E0266C0649F0114BA7C</span> sample from GhostSecret for comparison against WinorDLL64 (<span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">1BA443FDE984CEE85EBD4D4FA7EB1263A6F1257F</span>), unless specified otherwise.</p>
<p>The following details summarize the supporting facts for our low confidence attribution to Lazarus:</p>
<h3>1. Victimology</h3>
<ul>
<li><span style="font-size: large;">Fellow researchers from AhnLab confirmed South Korean victims of Wslink in their telemetry, which is a relevant indicator considering the traditional Lazarus targets and that we have observed only a few hits.</span></li>
</ul>

Hence, these samples were spotted a relatively short period of time apart.</span></li>
<li><span style="font-size: large;">The <a href="https://www.virusbulletin.com/virusbulletin/2020/01/vb2019-paper-rich-headers-leveraging-mysterious-artifact-pe-format/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">PE rich headers</a> indicate that the same development environment and projects of similar size were used in several other known Lazarus samples (e.g., <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">70DE783E5D48C6FBB576BC494BAF0634BC304FD6</span>; <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">8EC9219303953396E1CB7105CDB18ED6C568E962</span>). <h2>Technical analysis</h2>
<p>WinorDLL64 serves as a backdoor that most notably acquires extensive system information, provides means for file manipulation, and executes additional commands. Interestingly, it communicates over a TCP connection that was already established by its loader and uses some of the loader’s functions.</p>
<p><div class="caption center"><a href="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2023/02/Winor64DLL_communication_diagram.png"><img class="wp-image-169790 size-full" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2023/02/Winor64DLL_communication_diagram.png" alt="" width="2000" height="1255" /></a><p class="caption-text"><em>Figure 4. Visualization of Wslink's communication</em></p></div></p>
<p>The backdoor is a DLL with a single unnamed export that accepts one parameter – a structure for communication that was already described in our <a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/2021/10/27/wslink-unique-undocumented-malicious-loader-runs-server/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">previous blogpost</a>. The structure contains a TLS-context – socket, key, IV – and callbacks for sending and receiving messages encrypted with 256-bit AES-CBC that enable WinorDLL64 to exchange data securely with the operator over an already established connection.</p>
<p>The following facts lead us to believe with high confidence that the library is indeed part of Wslink:</p>
<ul>
<li>The unique structure is used everywhere in the expected way, e.g., the TLS-context and other meaningful parameters are supplied in the anticipated order to the correct callbacks.</li>
<li>The name of the DLL is <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">WinorDLL64.dll</span> and Wslink’s name was <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">WinorLoaderDLL64.dll</span>.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Conclusion</h2>
<p>Wslink’s payload is dedicated to providing means for file manipulation, execution of further code, and obtaining extensive information about the underlying system that possibly can be leveraged later for lateral movement, due to specific interest in network sessions. The Wslink loader listens on a port specified in the configuration and can serve additional connecting clients, and even load various payloads.</p>
<p>WinorDLL64 contains an overlap in the development environment, behavior, and code with several Lazarus samples, which indicates that it might be a tool from the vast arsenal of this North-Korea aligned APT group.</p>
