<p>It seems like the rumors of FIN7’s decline have been hasty. Just a few months after the <a href="https://www.justice.gov/opa/pr/three-members-notorious-international-cybercrime-group-fin7-custody-role-attacking-over-100" rel="noopener" target="_blank">well-publicized indictment</a> of three high-ranking members in August, Morphisec has identified a new FIN7 campaign that appears to be targeting the restaurant industry.</p>
<p><a href="//blog.morphisec.com/fileless-attack-framework-discovery"><!--more--></a></p>
<p><img src="https://blog.morphisec.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Imported_Blog_Media/FIN7-11-18-header.png?width=750&amp;height=425&amp;name=FIN7-11-18-header.png" width="750" height="425" loading="lazy" alt="FIN7-11-18-header" style="height: auto; max-width: 100%; width: 750px;" srcset="https://blog.morphisec.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Imported_Blog_Media/FIN7-11-18-header.png?width=375&amp;height=213&amp;name=FIN7-11-18-header.png 375w, https://blog.morphisec.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Imported_Blog_Media/FIN7-11-18-header.png?width=750&amp;height=425&amp;name=FIN7-11-18-header.png 750w, https://blog.morphisec.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Imported_Blog_Media/FIN7-11-18-header.png?width=1125&amp;height=638&amp;name=FIN7-11-18-header.png 1125w, https://blog.morphisec.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Imported_Blog_Media/FIN7-11-18-header.png?width=1500&amp;height=850&amp;name=FIN7-11-18-header.png 1500w, https://blog.morphisec.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Imported_Blog_Media/FIN7-11-18-header.png?width=1875&amp;height=1063&amp;name=FIN7-11-18-header.png 1875w, https://blog.morphisec.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Imported_Blog_Media/FIN7-11-18-header.png?width=2250&amp;height=1275&amp;name=FIN7-11-18-header.png 2250w" sizes="(max-width: 750px) 100vw, 750px"></p>
<p><a href="//blog.morphisec.com/fileless-attack-framework-discovery">FIN7</a>, also known as Carbanak, is one of the major threat groups <a href="/fin7-attacks-restaurant-industry" rel="noopener nofollow" target="_blank">tracked by Morphisec</a> and numerous other security entities, and among the top three criminal computer intrusion cases that the FBI is currently working. FIN7 is composed of a very sophisticated network of developers and hackers and brings in an <a href="https://www.wired.com/story/fin7-carbanak-hacking-group-behind-a-string-of-big-breaches/" rel="noopener" target="_blank">estimated $50 million a month</a>. They target very specific industries, hospitality – hotels and restaurants – being one of them, and are behind a string of high-profile breaches including Red Robin, Chili’s, Arby’s, Burgerville, Omni Hotels and Saks Fifth Avenue, among many others.</p>
<p>FIN7 is known for its stealth techniques and ability to continuously evade security systems. In the case of Burgerville, malware sat on the company’s network collecting payment data for nearly a year before it was discovered. And that was only due to an FBI investigation.</p>
<p>In this blog post, we present our findings on two campaigns, which occurred in the first and second weeks of November. These campaigns follow patterns similar to those presented by <a href="https://cloud.google.com/blog/topics/threat-intelligence/fin7-pursuing-an-enigmatic-and-evasive-global-criminal-operation/" rel="noopener" target="_blank">FireEye</a> in August&nbsp;<a href="https://www.fireeye.com/blog/threat-research/2018/08/fin7-pursuing-an-enigmatic-and-evasive-global-criminal-operation.html"></a>but with just enough variations to bypass many security vendors.</p>
<h2>Technical Description</h2>
<p>The initial document was probably sent within the Baltic region (or tested there). It was submitted to VirusTotal from Latvia. The name of the document translated from Russian is <em>“new questioner”. </em>It is password-protected with the password: <em>“goodmorning”.</em></p>
<p><em>Oprosnik_new.doc 6e1230088a34678726102353c622445e1f8b8b8c9ce1f025d11bfffd5017ca82)</em></p>
<p><img src="https://blog.morphisec.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog_images/18-Q4/FIN7-1.png?width=500&amp;name=FIN7-1.png" alt="FIN7-1" width="500" style="width: 500px; margin: 0px auto; display: block;" srcset="https://blog.morphisec.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog_images/18-Q4/FIN7-1.png?width=250&amp;name=FIN7-1.png 250w, https://blog.morphisec.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog_images/18-Q4/FIN7-1.png?width=500&amp;name=FIN7-1.png 500w, https://blog.morphisec.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog_images/18-Q4/FIN7-1.png?width=750&amp;name=FIN7-1.png 750w, https://blog.morphisec.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog_images/18-Q4/FIN7-1.png?width=1000&amp;name=FIN7-1.png 1000w, https://blog.morphisec.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog_images/18-Q4/FIN7-1.png?width=1250&amp;name=FIN7-1.png 1250w, https://blog.morphisec.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Blog_images/18-Q4/FIN7-1.png?width=1500&amp;name=FIN7-1.png 1500w" sizes="(max-width: 500px) 100vw, 500px"></p>

<p>If the <em>“enable macro” </em>button is activated, the following obfuscated Macro runs and the next stage obfuscated JavaScript is extracted from the form caption, similar to the last several FIN7 campaigns.</p>

<p>Following deobfuscation of the macro, we notice known FIN7 patterns of executing JavaScript from VBScript with the slight modification of copying the wscript.exe file and renaming it to mses.exe. This may allow it to bypass some EDR solutions that are tracing WScript by name.</p>

<h3>Deobfuscated JavaScript</h3>
<p>The deobfuscated JavaScript is actually a backdoor component that directly communicates to the C2 server (in this case hxxps://bing-cdn[.]com). It executes the response which is yet another JavaScript command, which can be evaluated by <em>eval. </em>Although there have been slight modifications in the Macro delivery in the last couple of campaigns, the JavaScript backdoor stays the same, including its communication protocol.</p>
<p>During the first request, the MAC address and the computer domain are also delivered to the target C2. We believe that the next stage is only delivered to specific targets based on domains as the data that is delivered in the first request is very limited.</p>

<h2>Conclusion</h2>
<p>Like the Hydra, cutting off one, or even three, heads of FIN7 barely slows it down. With the holiday rush nearly upon us, we expect the threat group to step up its activities to take advantage of increased email traffic flow and seasonal staff that may be less security conscious. Workers in any industry should stay vigilant against social engineering methods – although with today’s highly targeted campaigns this can sometimes be tough to spot. And never enable macros unless you are 100 percent certain that the file is safe.<span style="background-color: transparent;">&nbsp;</span></p>
