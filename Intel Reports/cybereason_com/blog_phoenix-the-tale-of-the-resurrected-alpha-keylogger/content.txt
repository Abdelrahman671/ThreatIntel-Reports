      <h1><span id="hs_cos_wrapper_name" class="hs_cos_wrapper hs_cos_wrapper_meta_field hs_cos_wrapper_type_text" style="" data-hs-cos-general-type="meta_field" data-hs-cos-type="text">Phoenix: The Tale of the Resurrected Keylogger</span></h1>
      <div class="cr-mln__post-author-share">
      <div id="hubspot-author_data" class="hubspot-editable cr-mln__post-meta" data-hubspot-form-id="author_data" data-hubspot-name="Blog Author">

          <span id="hs_cos_wrapper_post_body" class="hs_cos_wrapper hs_cos_wrapper_meta_field hs_cos_wrapper_type_rich_text" style="" data-hs-cos-general-type="meta_field" data-hs-cos-type="rich_text"><h4 style="font-weight: normal;">Research by: Assaf Dahan</h4>
<h2>Introduction: Keylogger Malware</h2>
<p>Cybereason’s Nocturnus team is tracking a new keylogger gaining traction among cybercriminals called <em>Phoenix</em>. The keylogger first emerged in July 2019 packed with a myriad of information-stealing features. These features extend beyond solely logging keystrokes, to the point where we are inclined to classify it as an infostealer.</p>
<!--more-->
<p>This research explains several aspects of the Phoenix keylogger, including:</p>
<ol>
<li><strong>A Look Into the Underground Community</strong>: The underground, ongoing marketing efforts to promote Phoenix and its reception in the underground community.</li>
<li><strong>A Technical Breakdown: </strong>A technical breakdown of the Phoenix keylogger, including info stealing capabilities, communication through Telegram, and potential persistence.</li>
<li><strong>The Connection to a Previous Keylogger</strong>: The discovery of the Phoenix keylogger’s connection to the “orphaned” Alpha keylogger.</li>
</ol>
<h2>Key Findings</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>The Phoenix Keylogger</strong>: The Cybereason Nocturnus team is investigating multiple incidents of a new, emerging keylogger called <em>Phoenix</em>, and is now able to provide details into the keylogger’s operations and its creator.</li>
<li><strong>Steals Data From Multiple Sources</strong>: Phoenix operates under a malware-as-a-service model and steals personal data from almost 20 different browsers, four different mail clients, FTP clients, and chat clients.</li>
<li><strong>Tries to Stop over 80 Security Products</strong>: On top of its information stealing features, Phoenix has several defensive and evasive mechanisms to avoid analysis and detection, including an Anti-AV module that tries to kill the processes of over 80 different security products and analysis tools.</li>
<li><strong>Targets Across Continents</strong>: Despite Phoenix having been released in July 2019, it has already targeted victims across North America, the United Kingdom, France, Germany and other parts of Europe and the Middle East. We expect more regions to be affected as it gains popularity.</li>
<li><strong>Exfiltrates Data through Telegram</strong>: Phoenix offers common SMTP and FTP exfiltration protocols, but also supports data exfiltration over Telegram. <a href="https://telegram.org/">Telegram</a>, a popular chat application worldwide, is leveraged by cybercriminals for its legitimacy and end-to-end encryption.</li>
<li><strong>Has the Same Author as the Alpha Keylogger</strong>: Phoenix was clearly authored by the same team behind the Alpha keylogger, which disappeared earlier this year.</li>
<li><strong>“Malware for the People”</strong>: This research showcases the ever-growing popularity of the Malware-as-a-Service model in the cybercrime ecosystem. Malware authors are developing malware that is easy for any user to operate and comes bundled with customer support and a competitive price point. As we move into 2020, we expect to see many less-technical cybercriminals leverage MaaS to commit cybercrime, especially as MaaS authors start to compete for the most impressive offering.</li>
</ul>
<p><span style="font-style: italic; font-weight: bold;">Advanced endpoint protection platforms address these kinds of attacks. Learn about</span><a href="/building-an-all-encompassing-epp-platform-in-todays-market" rel=" noopener" style="font-style: italic; font-weight: bold;"> the future of EPPs during our webinar.&nbsp;</a></p>
<h2>Background:&nbsp;Phoenix Keylogger</h2>
<p>At the end of July 2019, the Cybereason platform detected a malware sample that was classified by some antivirus vendors as <a href="https://krebsonsecurity.com/2018/10/who-is-agent-tesla/">Agent Tesla</a>. Upon further review, however, it became clear that this was not Agent Tesla. We were able to determine this malware was a completely new and previously undocumented malware known as the <em>Phoenix keylogger</em>.</p>
<p><img src="https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Phoenix%20Keylogger-9.png?width=604&amp;name=Phoenix%20Keylogger-9.png" alt="" width="604" style="width: 604px; display: block; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto;" srcset="https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Phoenix%20Keylogger-9.png?width=302&amp;name=Phoenix%20Keylogger-9.png 302w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Phoenix%20Keylogger-9.png?width=604&amp;name=Phoenix%20Keylogger-9.png 604w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Phoenix%20Keylogger-9.png?width=906&amp;name=Phoenix%20Keylogger-9.png 906w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Phoenix%20Keylogger-9.png?width=1208&amp;name=Phoenix%20Keylogger-9.png 1208w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Phoenix%20Keylogger-9.png?width=1510&amp;name=Phoenix%20Keylogger-9.png 1510w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Phoenix%20Keylogger-9.png?width=1812&amp;name=Phoenix%20Keylogger-9.png 1812w" sizes="(max-width: 604px) 100vw, 604px"></p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><em>Phoenix MaaS Model Pricing</em></p>
<p><img src="https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/image-139.png?width=587&amp;name=image-139.png" width="587" style="width: 587px; display: block; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto;" srcset="https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/image-139.png?width=294&amp;name=image-139.png 294w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/image-139.png?width=587&amp;name=image-139.png 587w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/image-139.png?width=881&amp;name=image-139.png 881w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/image-139.png?width=1174&amp;name=image-139.png 1174w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/image-139.png?width=1468&amp;name=image-139.png 1468w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/image-139.png?width=1761&amp;name=image-139.png 1761w" sizes="(max-width: 587px) 100vw, 587px"></p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><em>Phoenix updated MaaS model pricing.</em></p>
<p>In searching underground communities, we learned that Phoenix first emerged at the end of July in 2019. This keylogger follows the malware as a service (MaaS) model and is sold for $14.99-$25.00 per month by a community member with the handle <em>Illusion</em>.</p>

<h2>Malware Analysis</h2>
<h3>Malware Capabilities</h3>
<p>The Phoenix keylogger is written in VB.NET.</p>
<p>Phoenix has a host of features that extend far beyond keylogging, including:</p>
<ul>
<li>Keylogger + Clipboard Stealer</li>
<li>Screen Capture</li>
<li>Password Stealing (Browsers, Mail Clients, FTP clients, Chat Clients)</li>
<li>Data exfiltration via SMTP, FTP or Telegram</li>
<li>Downloader (to download additional malware)</li>
<li>Alleged AV-Killer Module</li>
<li>Anti-debugging and Anti-VM Features</li>
</ul>
<h3>Delivery Method</h3>
<p>By default, <em>Illusion</em> supplies the Phoenix keylogger to their buyers as a stub. The buyer must use their own methods to deliver the stub to the target machine. The majority of Phoenix infections we observe originate from phishing attempts that leverage a weaponized rich text file (RTF) or Microsoft Office document. These deliveries do not use the more popular malicious macro technique, but instead use known exploits. Most commonly, they exploit the Equation Editor vulnerability (<a href="https://cve.mitre.org/cgi-bin/cvename.cgi?name=CVE-2017-11882">CVE-2017-11882</a>).</p>

<h3>Infected System Profiling</h3>
<p>Once Phoenix successfully infects the target machine, it profiles the machine to gather information on the operating system, hardware, running processes, users, and its external IP. Phoenix stores the information in memory and sends it back to the attackers directly, without writing it to disk. Attackers commonly do this to be more stealthy, since it is harder to know what was exfiltrated if it is not written to disk.</p>

<h4>Anti-Analysis &amp; Anti-Detection Features</h4>
<p>It’s clear <em>Illusion</em> invested time and effort into protecting Phoenix, as the stub uses a few different methods to protect itself from inspection.</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>String Encryption</strong>: Most critical strings used by the malware are encrypted and only decrypted in memory.</li>
<li><span style="font-weight: bold;">Obfuscation:</span>The stub is obfuscated by what appears to be an implementation of the open source <a href="https://github.com/yck1509/ConfuserEx/releases">ConfuserEx .NET obfuscator</a> to hinder correct decompilation and code inspection.</li>
</ul>
<p>Illusion recommends using an additional third-party crypter to “make it FUD”, or fully undetectable. It is worth noting that most Phoenix samples caught in the wild are packed with a crypter, but are still prevented by the majority of antivirus vendors.</p>
<p>After obtaining basic system information, Phoenix checks to see if it is running in a “hostile” environment. A hostile environment can take different forms: if Phoenix is deployed in a virtual machine, debugger, or on a machine with analysis tools or antivirus products installed. Phoenix has a set of features to disable different Windows tools within the admin panel, like disabling CMD, the registry, task manager, system restore, and others.</p>
<p>It is interesting to note that even though the user interface used by Phoenix’s operators seems to have support for a persistence feature, most samples analyzed by Cybereason did not exhibit persistence behavior following a successful infection. A possible explanation to this can lie in the attackers’ wish to minimize the risk of over exposure. Once Phoenix obtained the necessary data, there is no need for it to increase the risk of exposure by persisting longer than needed.</p>

<h3>Additional Communication with the C2 Server</h3>
<p>At its current stage of development, Phoenix does not seem to use a standard, interactive C2 model. Specifically, it doesn’t expect to receive commands back from the C2 server. Phoenix’s various tasks like infostealing, downloading additional malware, and spreading via USB are predefined by the operators in the configuration file before compilation. Phoenix uses a predefined exfiltration method from the configuration file to steal any collected data on execution.</p>
<h2>Connecting to Alpha Keylogger</h2>
<p>During our investigation, we discovered the Phoenix keylogger is actually an evolution of an earlier project, <em>Alpha keylogger</em>. We believe the Alpha keylogger was authored by the same team behind the Phoenix keylogger.</p>
<h3>Code Similarity Between Alpha and Phoenix Keylogger</h3>
<p>In order to investigate deeper, we used YARA rules and other methods to retrieve additional samples of Phoenix. One of the samples we retrieved was almost identical to Phoenix, with some parts copy-pasted with the same naming conventions, parameter names, and more. However, the name of the malware as it appeared in logs and in code, was consistently Alpha keylogger.</p>

<h3>Alpha Keylogger Overview</h3>
<p>In searching the underground communities, we found references to the Alpha keylogger beginning as early as April of 2019. At that time, membe<em>r Alpha_Coder</em> and later, member <em>AK_Generation, </em>began marketing the keylogger to the underground community.</p>

<p>We believe the Phoenix keylogger is not just an evolution of the Alpha keylogger, but also an attempt to rebrand and give the author a clean slate in the underground community.</p>
<h2>Conclusion</h2>
<p>This research breaks down the Phoenix keylogger, an information stealer operating under a malware-as-a-service model, currently under active development. Since its emergence in late July 2019, it has gained popularity in the underground community because of its ease of use, competitive pricing, and personal customer support.</p>
<p>Phoenix is more than just a keylogger, with broad information-stealing capabilities, self-defense mechanisms, which include an anti-AV module that attempts to stop over 80 security products, and the ability to exfiltrate data through Telegram. The majority of samples we identified in the wild do not implement a persistence mechanism, nor do they interact bidirectionally with the C2 server. Instead, the stolen data is posted to a pre-configured exfiltration method, which suggests Phoenix is being used mostly as a “set it and forget it” type of malware.</p>
<p>Based on our analysis, Phoenix’s malware-as-a-service model appeals to a broad range of cybercriminals, particularly the less sophisticated who do not possess the technical know-how to develop their own successful malware infrastructure. This signals a continued trend of cybercriminals following the malware-as-a-service model to make malware accessible for any level user. Malware authors are starting to use many of the same methodologies as legitimate software-as-a-service businesses, including marketing their software, personalized customer support, and an easy user interface to continuously profit off of other, less technical cybercriminals.</p>
<p>Moving into 2020, we expect a proliferation of less-technical cybercriminals to leverage MaaS to target, steal, and harm individuals, particularly as MaaS authors add additional features to their offerings.</p>

  var window_top = $(window).scrollTop();
  var div_top = $('#sticky-anchor').offset().top;
  if (window_top > div_top) {
    $('#sticky').addClass('stick');
  } else {
    $('#sticky').removeClass('stick');
  }
}

$(function() {
  $(window).scroll(sticky_relocate);
  sticky_relocate();
});
</script>
