                <div class="yoast-breadcrumbs"><span><span><a href="https://blog.sekoia.io/">Accueil</a></span> » <span><a href="https://blog.sekoia.io/category/research-threat-intelligence/">Research &amp; Threat Intelligence</a></span> » <span class="breadcrumb_last" aria-current="page">Raccoon Stealer v2 &#8211; Part 1: The return of the dead</span></span></div>


<p class="has-medium-font-size"><strong>Raccoon Stealer</strong> was one of the most prolific information stealers in 2021, being used by multiple cybercriminal actors. Due to its wide stealing capabilities, the customizability of the malware and its ease of use, Raccoon Stealer was highly <strong>popular among threat actors</strong>. The malware was mainly distributed using fake installers, or as cracked versions of popular software.</p>



<span id="more-1556"></span>



<p class="has-medium-font-size">Previously sold as a <strong>malware-as-a-service</strong> on <a href="https://blog.sekoia.io/eternityteam-a-new-prominent-threat-group-on-underground-forums/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">underground forums</a> since early 2019, its operations suddenly stopped on March 25, 2022. This abrupt shutdown was purportedly due to the loss of a developer of the project Raccoon Stealer during the &#8220;special operation&#8221;, likely in reference to the Russian conflict in Ukraine. At the time, the <em>raccoonstealer</em> profile stated on several forums they &#8220;don&#8217;t say goodbye forever&#8221;, and that they were already working on a second version.</p>

<p class="has-medium-font-size">Sekoia.io kept a close eye on activities related to Raccoon Stealer as it is assessed to make a <strong>strong comeback in the information stealer market</strong>.</p>



<p><em>We have reverse-engineered the new version of Raccoon Stealer and our in-depth analysis is available in part 2 at: <a href="https://blog.sekoia.io/raccoon-stealer-v2-part-2-in-depth-analysis/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">https://blog.sekoia.io/raccoon-stealer-v2-part-2-in-depth-analysis/</a></em>.</p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Samples in the wild</h2>



<p class="has-medium-font-size">On June 16, 2022, S2W published a comprehensive analysis<sup><a href="https://medium.com/s2wblog/raccoon-stealer-is-back-with-a-new-version-5f436e04b20d" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">1</a></sup> of the new version of Raccoon Stealer. Based on a file created by the malware (<em>System Info.txt</em>), they <strong>attributed payloads</strong> distributed in the wild to the <strong>Raccoon Stealer V2</strong>. This file contains information about the victim&#8217;s system.</p>



<p class="has-medium-font-size">The sample analysed by S2W matches a newly discovered malware family discussed on Twitter by cybersecurity researchers, which was later named RecordBreaker by @James_inthe_box (<a href="https://twitter.com/James_inthe_box/status/1534593736356663296">related tweet</a>). <strong>Raccoon Stealer v2</strong> and <strong>RecordBreaker</strong> could be two different names for the <strong>same malware family</strong>.</p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Technical analysis</h2>



<p class="has-medium-font-size">In the <em>raccoonstealer</em>&#8216;s Telegram channel, the new version of the malware has been advertised with an improved software, back-end and front-end. Raccoon Stealer&#8217;s developers rewrote the malware and the administration panel from scratch, with a focus on performance and efficiency. In the next part, SEKOIA.IO analysed the malware and its communication in depth.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Malware capabilities</h3>



<p class="has-medium-font-size">Raccoon Stealer&#8217;s capabilities are those of a classic stealer, with a focus on cryptocurrency wallets. The malware is also advertised as a loader and a file grabber.</p>



<p class="has-medium-font-size">Here is an overview of its capabilities:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Targeting of popular browsers (to steal passwords, cookies, autoforms and credit cards);</li>



<li>Targeting of almost all desktop cryptocurrency wallets and extension for cryptocurrency wallets (<em>MetaMask, TronLink, BinanceChain, Ronin, Exodus, Atomic, JaxxLiberty, Binance, Coinomi, Electrum, Electrum-LTC, ElectronCash</em>, <em>etc.</em>);</li>



<li>File downloading;</li>



<li>File loading (cmd, dll, exe);</li>



<li>File grabbing in all disks;</li>



<li>Screenshot capturing;</li>



<li>System fingerprinting;</li>



<li>Installed applications listing.</li>
</ul>



<p class="has-medium-font-size">The capabilities advertised on Telegram match those identified during our analysis.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading">In-depth analysis</h3>



<p class="has-medium-font-size">Raccoon Stealer v2 is written in C/C++ using WinApi. Sample size is around 56KB, working on both 32 and 64-bit systems without any dependencies. The malware downloads legitimate third-party DLLs from its C2 server(s). The C2 configuration and strings are encrypted using RC4 and Base64 encoding.</p>



<p class="has-medium-font-size">SEKOIA.IO reverse engineered the malware and will soon publish an in-depth analysis to share further details.</p>



<p class="has-medium-font-size">In the meantime, here is a description of the step-by-step execution of Raccoon Stealer v2:</p>



<ol class="wp-block-list">
<li>Dynamic Loading of DLLs;</li>



<li>Run-Time Dynamic Linking of WinApi functions;</li>



<li>Strings deobfuscation (base64 decoding and RC4 decryption);</li>



<li>C2 server(s) deobfuscation;</li>



<li>Checks (mutex, user privileges);</li>



<li>Host fingerprint (MachineGuid, Username) and data exfiltration;</li>



<li>Retrieving its configuration from its C2;</li>



<li>Downloading, then loading the legitimate third-party DLLs;</li>



<li>Fingerprint the infected host (CPU, RAM, OS version, Display info) and send this data to the C2;</li>



<li>Collecting personal information and exfiltrating it (system information, browsers, crypto wallets);</li>



<li>Capturing a screenshot and exfiltrating it;</li>



<li>Removal of files created by the malware.</li>
</ol>



<p class="has-medium-font-size">Interestingly, during the collection stage, the malware collects the data and sends it directly in a file via a POST request to the C2 server. It repeats this step for each new type of data (system information, cookies, screenshot, etc.).</p>



<p class="has-medium-font-size">It is worth noting that the malware implements almost no defense evasion techniques, such as anti-analysis, obfuscation, or impair defenses.</p>

<li>URLs hosting the legitimate third-party DLLs;</li>



<li>Token used for data extraction (corresponds to the C2’s endpoint);</li>

<p class="has-medium-font-size">To conclude, we expect a <strong><a href="https://blog.sekoia.io/fr/raccoon-stealer-v2-partie-1-de-retour-des-morts/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">resurgence of Raccoon Stealer</a> v2</strong>, as developers implemented a version tailored to the needs of cybercriminals (efficiency, performance, stealing capabilities, etc.) and scaled their backbone servers to handle large loads. In addition, the malware benefits of the threat actors&#8217; popularity gained in recent years.</p>



<p class="has-medium-font-size">We can assess with high confidence that possible future updates will implement more anti-analysis techniques to avoid detection by antiviruses.</p>
