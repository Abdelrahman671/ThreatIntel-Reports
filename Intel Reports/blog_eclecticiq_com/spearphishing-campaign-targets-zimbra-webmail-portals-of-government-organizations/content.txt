                <h1>Spearphishing Campaign Targets Zimbra Webmail Portals of Government Organizations </h1>
                
                    <p class="c-intro mt-5">EclecticIQ researchers identified a spearphishing campaign that leverages exploited Zimbra and Roundcube email servers to target government organizations. <h2 style="text-align: left;">Executive Summary</h2> 
<p style="text-align: left;">EclecticIQ researchers identified a spearphishing campaign that leverages exploited Zimbra and Roundcube email servers to target government organizations.&nbsp;<br><br>Analysts assess with high confidence that the threat actor probably compromised government owned Zimbra and Roundcube email servers and used these to send spearphishing emails to other government entities.&nbsp;<br><br>The malicious emails contained Zimbra maintenance phishing lures to deceive recipients and steal email credentials.&nbsp;<br><br>The campaign had been underway since as early as January 2023. Most of the analyzed phishing emails target government entities in Ukraine. Analysts also identified lures aimed at government bodies in Spain, Indonesia, and France.&nbsp;</p> 
<!--more--> 
<h2 style="text-align: left;">Key Findings&nbsp;</h2> 
<ul> 
 <li style="text-align: left;">The actor probably exploited vulnerabilities in the Zimbra and Roundcube webmail portals. The portals are publicly accessible and at the time of reporting are running outdated versions.</li> 
 <li style="text-align: left;">The webmail portals were used to distribute spearphishing emails targeting other government organizations.</li> 
 <li style="text-align: left;">Analysis of email headers indicates that the threat actor successfully circumvented anti-spam filters of targeted government organizations. These findings suggest that the threat actor employed evasive tactics throughout the operation.</li> 
 <li style="text-align: left;">According to “Originating-IP” email header section in the observed emails, the threat actor very likely used VPN services to hide its real identity.</li> 
 <li style="text-align: left;">The threat actor abused legitimate web services like Google Firebase, MailChimp, chilipepper.io, and webflow.io to collect email credentials.&nbsp;<br><br>&nbsp;</li> 
</ul> 
<h2 style="text-align: left; font-size: 18px;"><span style="background-color: transparent;"><span data-contrast="none" xml:lang="EN-US" lang="EN-US"><span data-ccp-parastyle="heading 1">Zimbra Maintenance Phishing Lure</span><span data-ccp-parastyle="heading 1">s &amp; </span><span data-ccp-parastyle="heading 1">Legitimate Web Services Abused for Credential Collection</span></span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;134245418&quot;:true,&quot;134245529&quot;:true,&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559738&quot;:240,&quot;335559739&quot;:0,&quot;335559740&quot;:259}">&nbsp;</span></span></h2> 
<p style="text-align: left; font-size: 15px;"><span style="background-color: transparent;">Analysts assess with moderate confidence that a threat actor gained access to Zimbra and Roundcube email servers of several organizations and used the systems to send phishing lures mostly to other government organizations.&nbsp;<br><br>EclecticIQ analysts discovered 12 phishing emails that contained Zimbra lures. The emails were sent from email accounts of various government organizations. Analysis of the email headers revealed that the sender address was not spoofed. Analysts also discovered a commonality among the sender organizations - they either use Zimbra or Roundcube as the email server.&nbsp;<br><br>Figure 1 provides a visual representation of the organizations and countries affected by this campaign.&nbsp;</span></p>

<h2 style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-size: 0.9375rem;"><span style="font-size: 18px;">Email Address in Reply-To Field Suggests Link to Russian Hacking Forum</span>&nbsp;<br><br></span></h2> 
<p style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-size: 0.9375rem;">One of the emails (figure 7) also contains a "reply-to” address - ctousey29[@]gmail.com - that is very likely owned or controlled by the threat actor. If a victim clicks on the reply button, the email client would use this address instead of the legitimate sender addresses.&nbsp;<br><br>It is almost certain that the actor used the “reply-to” field to avoid a target’s reply being sent to the sender address, which could have raised suspicion.&nbsp;<br><br>The redirect to an external address also indicates that the actor may not have had full control of the Zimbra Email servers. Exploit.in is a popular hacking forum that is widely used by Russian and Eastern European actors. <h2><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559739&quot;:160,&quot;335559740&quot;:259}"><br></span><span data-contrast="none" style="font-size: 1.125rem; font-weight: bold; background-color: transparent;">Threat Actor Using CyberGhost VPN for Anonymity</span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;134245418&quot;:true,&quot;134245529&quot;:true,&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559738&quot;:240,&quot;335559739&quot;:0,&quot;335559740&quot;:259}" style="font-size: 1.125rem; font-weight: bold; background-color: transparent;">&nbsp;</span></h2> 
<p><span data-contrast="auto">EclecticIQ analysts assess with high confidence that the threat actor used VPN service CyberGhost for anonymity. Extracted IP addresses (188[.]241[.]177[.]108 and 154[.]16[.]192[.]141) from the “X-Originating-IP" email header are linked to CyberGhost VPN service.</span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559739&quot;:160,&quot;335559740&quot;:259}">&nbsp;</span></p> 
<p><span data-contrast="auto">“The X-Originating-IP" email header field is a standard for identifying the originating IP address of a client connecting to a mail service's HTTP frontend. When clients (threat actor) connect directly to a mail server, its address is already known to the server, but web frontends act as a proxy which internally connects to the mail server. This header can therefore serve to identify the original sender address despite the frontend.</span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335559739&quot;:160,&quot;335559740&quot;:259}">&nbsp;</span></p>

<h2><span><span style="font-size: 13px;"><span style="font-size: 18px;">Mitigation and Prevention&nbsp;</span></span></span></h2> 
<p>According to Zimbra, mitigating the vulnerability against in the wild exploitation of Zimbra webmail version 8.8.15 requires admins to go through the following steps:&nbsp;<br><br>1. Take a backup of the file /opt/zimbra/jetty/webapps/zimbra/m/momoveto&nbsp;<br><br>2. Edit this file and go to line number 40&nbsp;<br><br>3. Update the parameter value to &lt;input name="st" type="hidden" value="${fn:escapeXml(param.st)}"/&gt;&nbsp;<br><br>4. Before the update, the line appeared as &lt;input name="st" type="hidden" value="${param.st}"/&gt;&nbsp;<br><br>The inclusion of the escapeXml() function will sanitize the user-input data by escaping special characters used in XML markup to prevent XSS flaws. &nbsp;</p> 
<ul> 
 <li>Patch Management: Ensure all software and systems, including Zimbra and Roundcube, are patched and updated to the latest versions. This is critical to close off known vulnerabilities.&nbsp;</li> 
 <li>Two-Factor Authentication (2FA): Implementing 2FA on all email and web services can greatly reduce the risk of credential theft. Even if an attacker is able to obtain a user's password, they will also need to bypass this additional layer of security.</li> 
</ul>
