<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Key points</h2>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Behavioural fingerprinting and content similarity scans used to reveal 150+ new Lumma C2 IOCs.</li>



<li>Administrator TTPs and new malware iterations used to reveal an new pre-attack landscape.</li>



<li>New Lumma domain administrator email addresses discovered.</li>
</ul>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Background</h2>



<p>Lumma (also known as LummaC2) is an information stealer with strong links to Russian threat activity, that’s been available on the dark web as a MaaS platform since 2022.</p>



<p>The malware &#8211; available for between <a href="https://outpost24.com/blog/everything-you-need-to-know-lummac2-stealer/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">$250-$1,000 per month</a> &#8211; steals system data and sensitive information from infected machines, including browser data, stored credentials, and cryptocurrency data.</p>



<figure class="wp-block-image size-full"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" width="1684" height="828" src="https://www.silentpush.com/wp-content/uploads/2-2.png" alt="" class="wp-image-1676"/></figure>



<p class="has-text-align-center">Lumma advertisement on a dark web forum (Source: cyble[.]com)</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Previous Russian research</h2>



<p>In December 2022 we published <a href="https://www.silentpush.com/blog/silent-push-uncovers-a-russian-ursnifgozi-banking-trojan-operation-targeting-global-anydesk-users">research on the Ursnif/Gozi banking trojan</a> &#8211; an early C2 progenitor with its roots in the Russian criminal underworld, distributed via AnyDesk phishing pages.</p>



<p>We followed this up in February 2023 with a blog on how we uncovered <a href="https://www.silentpush.com/blog/infostealing-killed-the-video-star-youtube-targeted-in-expansive-russian-c2-malware-operation">a Russian infostealing campaign</a> that distributed malware (including the Burmilia infostealer) via hijacked YouTube channels.</p>



<p>Despite a renewed focus on Russian APT activity that came as a result of private sector research &#8211; itself a validation of the <a href="https://www.infosecurity-magazine.com/news/mwise-fbi-private-public/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">FBI’s vision for private and public sector collaboration</a> announced at mWise this month &#8211; Lumma has remained a persistent threat. Our Analysts <a href="https://darktrace.com/blog/the-rise-of-the-lumma-info-stealer" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">remained on the trail of Russian C2 infrastructure throughout</a> the summer, and kept abreast of emerging threats into the fall.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Investigation summary</h2>



<p>In this blog, we’ll take a look at Lumma’s delivery methods, before showing how we used a single HTTP header to map over 150 previously unknown Lumma C2 servers, pinpointing new attack vectors and using the attackers&#8217; own TTPs against them to reveal a previously unexplored threat landscape… all with the help of a Russian poet that’s been dead for nearly a century.</p>



<p>If that sounds weird, we can explain…</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Delivery method</h2>



<p>Before we delve into how we tracked the new infrastructure, let’s take a brief look at thin end of the wedge &#8211; the malware itself.</p>



<p>How Lumma ends up on a user’s machine depends entirely on the motives and tactics of the operator who purchased the infostealer from its administrators, but generally speaking the payload is distributed via two methods:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Spear phishing campaigns featuring malicious email attachments, and social engineering tactics that lure victims into executing the payload.</li>



<li>Malvertisement campaigns and cracked software links &#8211; particularly within YouTube video descriptions, and gaming content.</li>
</ul>



<p>As we’ll go on to discuss, the new version of Lumma also features steganographic PNGs that hide malicious content within embedded image files.</p>

This delivery method remains popular, especially among the gaming community. <h2 class="wp-block-heading">POST request</h2>



<p>Once user data has been collected, the malware attempts to establish a connection with associated C2 domains, and sends information via unencrypted archive files.</p>



<p>The malware also retrieves a config list from the C2 server, which means that extracted data is not embedded within the infostealer itself.</p>



<p>Here’s a HTTP POST request to a Lumma C2 server that we’ve identified &#8211; erorblackday[.]xyz &#8211; registered by <a href="/cdn-cgi/l/email-protection#fe95978e8e94918d9b8e969dbe99939f9792d09d9193"><span class="__cf_email__" data-cfemail="a1cac8d1d1cbced2c4d1c9c2e1c6ccc0c8cd8fc2cecc">[email&#160;protected]</span></a>, an email address associated with multiple C2 servers that we’ll talk about a little later on.</p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Discovering new infrastructure</h2>



<p>Early reports from security researchers matched Lumma C2 activity to the HTML title &#8220;LummaC2 | Вход&#8221;.</p>



<p>Silent Push scans the Internet’s entire IPv4 range and collects granular data on every observable we come across, including HTML data, allowing us to form a top down picture of attacker TTPs, rather than relying on isolated IOCs.</p>



<p>We took the above HTML title and executed a content similarity scan, which unearthed 6 Lumma C2 IPs active sometime between January-May 2023, with identical HTML titles:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>144.76.173[.]247</li>



<li>195.123.226[.]91</li>



<li>45.9.74[.]78</li>



<li>77.73.134[.]68</li>



<li>82.117.255[.]127</li>



<li>82.118.23[.]50</li>
</ul>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">The hunt begins…</h2>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading">The curious case of Sergei Yesenin’s body data</h2>



<p>It’s not just human cadavers that offer up forensic clues to intrepid investigators. Body data fom a webpage can be just as enlightening (and a lot easier to extract!).</p>



<p>Sergei Yesenin was a Russian poet born in 1895. Yesenin spent his days wandering the streets of Moscow and Leningrad, romanticising a rural Russia that was utterly at odds with the relentless march of industrial life in the early 20th-century.</p>



<p>Why is any of this relevant? Yesenin is a poster boy for modern-day Russians that harbour anti-Western sentiments, and yearn for a return to the “good old days” of Russian imperialism. America, according to Yesenin, was “a stinking place where not just art is being murdered, but with it, all the loftiest aspirations of humankind.”</p>



<p><em>Get off the fence, Sergei!</em></p>



<p>We scanned the new domains we’d gathered and noticed that, rather than containing Lumma control panels, some of them hosted a page with a poem from Yesenin, complete with a picture of the dapper young poet in his Sunday best:</p>


<div class="wp-block-image">
<figure class="aligncenter size-full"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" width="1658" height="1226" src="https://www.silentpush.com/wp-content/uploads/10-1.png" alt="" class="wp-image-1691"/></figure></div>


<p class="has-text-align-center">Russian-language Sergei Yesenin content on 2flowers-my[.]xyz</p>


<div class="wp-block-image">
<figure class="aligncenter size-full"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" width="1668" height="1240" src="https://www.silentpush.com/wp-content/uploads/12-1.png" alt="" class="wp-image-1692"/></figure></div>


<p class="has-text-align-center">Translated Sergei Yesenin content on 2flowers-my[.]xyz</p>



<p>We initially hypothesized that that Lumma’s administrators had recently amended their C2 infrastructure to point at the Yesenin content to evade detection.</p>



<p>This piece of analysis, however, wasn’t entirely correct. Further investigation showed that some domains had actually shifted back from the Yesenin page to a Lumma control panel.</p>



<p>We used Sergei Yesenin’s “body” (OK, the page content, but it’s close enough!) to scan our database for Lumma C2 domains and IPs displaying the same content, leading us to <strong>71 IOCs, 15 of which were still active</strong>.</p>



<p>We were able to confirm the link between these new domains and Lumma by cross referencing various pieces of DNS and WHOIS data with what we already knew about confirmed IOCs.</p>



<p>Most of the domains were proxied to Cloudflare, and had been registered on PDR in the past month, with a Gmail address as the registrant, but this time with a different prefix to levisrogers13 &#8211; <a href="/cdn-cgi/l/email-protection" class="__cf_email__" data-cfemail="c9a2a0b9b9a3a6baacb9a1aa89aea4a8a0a5e7aaa6a4">[email&#160;protected]</a>:</p>


<div class="wp-block-image">
<figure class="aligncenter size-full"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" width="2500" height="952" src="https://www.silentpush.com/wp-content/uploads/14.png" alt="" class="wp-image-1693"/></figure></div>


<p class="has-text-align-center">WHOIS information for Lumma C2 domain</p>



<p>Using a domain search query to pivot on this new email address, we uncovered 20 domains (none active) linked to Lumma, all of which had all been registered on since 19 August 2023 &#8211; as with the <a href="/cdn-cgi/l/email-protection" class="__cf_email__" data-cfemail="d1bdb4a7b8a2a3beb6b4a3a2e0e291b6bcb0b8bdffb2bebc">[email&#160;protected]</a> domains, all of them appear to be proxied to Cloudflare.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Obtaining non-proxied domain data</h2>



<p>During our investigation, we noticed something odd about a group of 4 domains that shared a similair naming convention.</p>



<p>Contrary to the majority of Lumma C2 IOCs, this subset isn’t proxied to Cloudflare, which gave us all kinds of pivoting options that we weren’t able to use with the Cloudflare domains:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>lazagrc2cnk[.]xyz</li>



<li>lazagrc3cnk[.]xyz</li>



<li>ocmtancmi2c4t[.]life</li>



<li>ocmtancmi2c5t.life</li>
</ul>



<p>Either the attackers got sloppy, or deliberately left them unproxied for technical reasons. Either way, our old friend Mr Yesenin was able to confirm their involvement:</p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Conclusion</h2>



<p>In this blog, we’ve shown how we took a single piece of data &#8211; a HTML header &#8211; and used our platform to extrapolate 150+ new C2 IPs, domains and control panel URLs, by focusing on <strong>how attackers deploy their infrastructure</strong>.</p>



<p>Malicious domains and IP addresses offer minimal insight to SOC teams and threat hunters when taken in isolation. Just like the punch that a boxer doesn’t see, the most injurious attacks originate from an unknown source.</p>



<p>Lumma feed data ingested into a SOAR/SIEM that relies on bare lists of C2 servers without any consideration of the underlying TTPs involved is borderline redundant once published, and it has to be said, significantly overvalued by the industry as a whole.</p>



<p>Lumma’s operators and administrators aren’t stupid. Immoral, but not stupid. They know full well when their infrastructure has been compromised, and they adjust their attack vectors accordingly to render most threat data relatively useless upon discovery &#8211; unless it’s part of a broader analysis that turns their own methods against them, and tracks their movement across the Internet.</p>



<p>That’s where we come in…</p>



<p>Silent Push maps attacker TTPs by building a behavioural fingerprint that’s aggregated, clustered, enriched, and scored to provide the most complete view of Lumma C2 activity available anywhere in the world.</p>
