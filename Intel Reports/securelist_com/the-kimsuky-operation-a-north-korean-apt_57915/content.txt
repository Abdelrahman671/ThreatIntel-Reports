<p>For several months, we have been monitoring an ongoing cyber-espionage campaign against South Korean think-tanks. There are multiple reasons why this campaign is extraordinary in its execution and logistics. It all started one day when we encountered a somewhat unsophisticated spy program that communicated with its &#8220;master&#8221; via a public e-mail server. This approach is rather inherent to many amateur virus-writers and these malware attacks are mostly ignored.</p>
<p>However, there were a few things that attracted our attention:</p>
<ul>
<li>The public e-mail server in question was Bulgarian &#8211; <em>mail.bg</em>.</li>
<li>The compilation path string contained Korean hieroglyphs.</li>
</ul>
<p>These two facts compelled us take a closer look at this malware &#8212; Korean compilers alongside Bulgarian e-mail command-and-control communications.</p>
<p>The complete path found in the malware presents some Korean strings:</p>
<p>D:rsh공격UAC_dll(완성)Releasetest.pdb</p>
<p>The &#8220;rsh&#8221; word, by all appearances, means a shortening of &#8220;Remote Shell&#8221; and the Korean words can be translated in English as &#8220;attack&#8221; and &#8220;completion&#8221;, i.e.:</p>
<p>D:rshATTACKUAC_dll(COMPLETION)Releasetest.pdb</p>
<p>Although the full list of victims remains unknown, we managed to identify several targets of this campaign. According to our technical analysis, the attackers were interested in targeting following organizations&#8221;.</p>
<p>The Sejong Institute</p>
<p><em>The Sejong Institute is a non-profit private organization for public interest and a leading think tank in South Korea, conducting research on national security strategy, unification strategy, regional issues, and international political economy.</em></p>
<p>Korea Institute For Defense Analyses (KIDA)</p>
<p><em>KIDA is a comprehensive defense research institution that covers a wide range of defense-related issues. KIDA is organized into seven research centers: the Center for Security and Strategy; the Center for Military Planning; the Center for Human Resource Development; the Center for Resource Management; the Center for Weapon Systems Studies; the Center for Information System Studies; and the Center for Modeling and Simulation. KIDA also has an IT Consulting Group and various supporting departments. KIDA&#8217;s mission is to contribute to rational defense policy-making through intensive and systematic research and analysis of defense issues. </em></p>
<p>Ministry of Unification</p>
<p><em>The Ministry of Unification is an executive department of the South Korean government responsible for working towards the reunification of Korea. Its major duties are: establishing North Korea Policy, coordinating inter-Korean dialogue, pursuing inter-Korean cooperation and educating the public on unification. </em></p>
<p>Hyundai Merchant Marine</p>
<p><em>Hyundai Merchant Marine is a South Korean logistics company providing worldwide container shipping services. </em></p>
<p>Some clues also suggest that computers belonging to <em> &#8220;The supporters of Korean Unification&#8221; </em> (http://www.unihope.kr/) were also targeted. Among the organizations we counted, 11 are based in South Korea and two entities reside in China.</p>
<p>Partly because this campaign is very limited and highly targeted, we have not yet been able to identify how this malware is being distributed. The malicious samples we found are the early stage malware most often delivered by spear-phishing e-mails.</p>
<h2 id="infecting-a-system">Infecting a system</h2>
<p>The initial Trojan dropper is a Dynamic Link Library functioning as a loader for further malware. It does not maintain exports and simply delivers another encrypted library maintained in its resource section. This second library performs all the espionage functionality.</p>
<p>When running on Windows 7, the malicious library uses the Metasploit Framework&#8217;s open-source code Win7Elevate to inject malicious code into <em>explorer.exe</em>. In any case, be it Windows 7 or not, this malicious code decrypts its spying library from resources, saves it to disk with an apparently random but hardcoded name, for example, <em>~DFE8B437DD7C417A6D.TMP</em>, in the user&#8217;s temporary folder and loads this file as library.</p>
<p>This next stage library copies itself into the <em>System32</em> directory of the Windows folder after the hardcoded file name &#8212; either <em>KBDLV2.DLL or AUTO.DLL</em>, depending on the malware sample. Then the service is created for the service dll. Service names also can differ from version to version; we discovered the following names &#8212;<em> DriverManage, WebService</em> and <em>WebClientManager</em>. These functions assure malware persistence in a compromised OS between system reboots.</p>
<p>At this stage, the malware gathers information about the infected computer. This includes an output of the <em>systeminfo</em> command saved in the file <em>oledvbs.inc</em> by following the hardcoded path: <em>C:Program FilesCommon FilesSystemOle DBoledvbs.inc</em>. There is another function called &#8211; the malware creates a string containing computer and user names but this isn&#8217;t used anywhere. By all appearances, this is a mistake by the malware author. Later on, we will come to a function where such a string could be pertinent but the malware is not able to find this data in the place where it should be. These steps are taken only if it&#8217;s running on an infected system for the first time. At system startup, the malicious library performs spying activities when it confirms that it is loaded by the generic <em>svchost.exe</em> process.</p>

<h2 id="regular-reporting">Regular reporting</h2>
<p>To report infection status, the malware reads from <em>C:Program FilesCommon FilesSystemOle DBoledvbs.inc</em> which contains the <em>systeminfo</em> command output. If the file exists, it is deleted after reading.</p>
<p>Then, it reads user-related info from the file <em>sqlxmlx.inc</em> in the same folder (we can see strings referencing to &#8220;UserID&#8221; commentary in this part of the code). But this file was never created. As you recall, there is a function that should have collected this data and should have saved it into this <em>sqlxmlx.inc</em> file. However, on the first launch, the collected user information is saved into &#8220;<em>xmlrwbin.inc&#8221;</em>. This effectively means that the malware writer mistakenly coded the bot to save user information into the wrong file. There is a chance for the mistaken code to still work &#8212; user information could be copied into the send information heap. But not in this case &#8211; at the time of writing, the gathered user information variable which should point to the <em>xmlrwbin.inc</em> filename has not yet been initialized, causing the file write to fail. We see that <em>sqlxmlx.inc</em> is not created to store user information.</p>
<p>Next, the intercepted keystrokes are read from the file and sent to the master. Keystrokes are logged and kept in an ordinary and consistent format in this file &#8211; both the names of windows in which keys were typed and the actual sequence of keyboard entry. This data is found in the file <em>C:Program FilesCommon FilesSystemOle DBmsolui80.inc</em> created by the external key logger module.</p>
<p>All this data is merged in one file <em>xmlrwbin.inc,</em> which is then encrypted with RC4. The RC4 key is generated as an MD5 hash of a randomly generated 117-bytes buffer. To be able to decipher the data, the attacker should certainly know either the MD5 hash or the whole buffer content. This data is also sent, but RSA encrypted. The malware constructs a 1120 bit public key, uses it to encrypt the 117-bytes buffer. The malware then concatenates all the data to be sent as a 128-bytes block. The resulting data is saved in <em>C:Program FilesCommon FilesSystemOle DB </em>to a file named according to the following format:</p>
<p>&#8220;&lt;system time&gt;_&lt;account at Bulgarian email server&gt;.txt&#8221;, for example, &#8220;08191757_beautifl@mail.bg.txt&#8221;.</p>
<p>The file is then attached to an e-mail and sent to the master&#8217;s e-mail account. Following transmission, it is immediately deleted from the victim system.</p>
<h2 id="getting-the-masters-data">Getting the master&#8217;s data</h2>
<p>The malware also retrieves instructions from the mail server. It checks for mails in its Bulgarian e-mail account with a particular subject tag. We have identified several &#8220;subject tags&#8221; in the network communication: <em>Down_0</em>, <em>Down_1, Happy_0, Happy_2</em> and <em>ddd_3</em>. When found and the e-mail maintains an attachment, the malware downloads this attachment and saves it with filename &#8220;<em>msdaipp.cnt&#8221;</em> in <em>C:Program FilesCommon FilesSystemOle DB</em>. The attacker can send additional executables in this way. The executables are RC4 encrypted and then attached. The key for decryption is hardcoded in the malicious samples. It&#8217;s interesting that the same &#8220;<em>rsh!@!#</em>&#8221; string is maintained across all known samples and is used to generate RC4 keys. As described earlier, the malware computes the MD5 of this string and uses the hash as its RC4 key to decrypt the executable. Then, the plain executable is dropped onto disk as &#8220;<em>sqlsoldb.exe&#8221;</em> and run, and then moved to the <em>C:Windows</em> folder with the file name <em>&#8220;taskmgr.exe&#8221;</em>. The original e-mail and its attachment are then deleted from the Bulgarian e-mail inbox.</p>
<h2 id="key-logger">Key logger</h2>
<p>The additional key logger module is not very complex &#8212; it simply intercepts keystrokes and writes typed keys into <em>C:Program FilesCommon FilesSystemOle DBmsolui80.inc, </em>and also records the active window name where the user pressed keys. We saw this same format in the <a href="http://www.kaspersky.com/about/news/virus/2012/kaspersky_lab_and_seculert_announce_madi_a_newly_discovered_cyber_espionage_campaign_in_the_middle_east">Madi malware</a>. There is also one key logger variant that logs keystrokes into <em>C:WINDOWSsetup.log</em>.</p>
<h2 id="directory-listing-collector">Directory listing collector</h2>
<p>The next program sent to victims enumerates all the drives on the infected system and executes the following command on them:</p>
<p>dir &lt;drive letter&gt;: /a /s /t /-c</p>
<p>In practice, this command is written to <em>C:WINDOWSmsdatt.bat</em> and executed with output redirected to <em>C:WINDOWSmsdatl3.inc</em>. As a result, the latter maintains a listing of all files in all the folders on the drive. The malware later reads that data and appends it to content of the file <em>C:Program FilesCommon FilesSystemOle DBoledvbs.inc</em>. At this point, &#8220;<em>oledvbs.inc</em> &#8220;already stores <em>systeminfo</em> output.</p>
<p>It&#8217;s interesting that one sample of the directory listing collector was infected with the infamous &#8220;Viking&#8221; virus of Chinese origin. Some of this virus&#8217; modifications were wandering in the wild for years and its authors or operators would never expect to see it end up in a clandestine APT-related spying tool. For the attackers, this is certainly a big failure. Not only does the original spying program have marks of well-known malware that can be detected by anti-malware products; moreover the attackers are revealing their secret activities to cyber-criminal gangs.  However, by all appearances, the attackers noticed the unwanted addition to their malware and got rid of the infection. This was the only sample bearing the Viking virus.</p>
<p>Due to expensive work of malware with variety of additional files, it&#8217;s not out of place to show these &#8220;relationships&#8221; in a diagram:</p>
<p><img decoding="async" class="aligncenter" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2013/09/08100213/tarakanov_pic2.jpg" alt="" border="0" /></p>
<h2 id="hwp-document-stealer">HWP document stealer</h2>
<p>This module intercepts HWP documents on an infected computer. The HWP file format is similar to Microsoft Word documents, but supported by Hangul, a South Korean word processing application from the Hancom Office bundle. Hancom Office is widely used in South Korea. This malware module works independently of the others and maintains its own Bulgarian e-mail account. The account is hardcoded in the module along with the master&#8217;s e-mail to which it sends intercepted documents. It is interesting that the module does not search for all the HWP files on infected computer, but reacts only to those that are opened by the user and steals them. <h2 id="remote-control-module-downloader">Remote control module downloader</h2>
<p>An extra program is dedicated exclusively to download attachments out of incoming e-mails with a particular subject tag. This program is similar to the pivot module but with reduced functionality: it maintains the hardcoded Bulgarian e-mail account, logs in, reads incoming e-mails and searches for the special subject tag &#8220;<em>Team</em>&#8220;. When found, it loads the related attachment, drops it onto the hard drive as <em>C:Program FilesCommon FilesSystemOle DBtaskmgr.exe</em> and executes. This particular executable arrives without any encryption.</p>

<h2 id="whos-kim">Who&#8217;s Kim?</h2>
<p>It&#8217;s interesting that the drop box mail accounts <em>iop110112@hotmail.com </em>and<em> rsh1213@hotmail.com</em> are registered with the following &#8220;kim&#8221; names: <em>kimsukyang</em> and <em>&#8220;Kim asdfa&#8221;</em>.</p>
<p>Of course, we can&#8217;t be certain that these are the real names of the attackers. However, the selection isn&#8217;t frequently seen. Perhaps it also points to the suspected North Korean origin of attack. Taking into account the profiles of the targeted organizations &#8212; South Korean universities that conduct researches on international affairs, produce defense policies for government, national shipping company, supporting groups for Korean unification &#8212; one might easily suspect that the attackers might be from North Korea.</p>
<p>The targets almost perfectly fall into their sphere of interest. On the other hand, it is not that hard to enter arbitrary registration information and misdirect investigators to an obvious North Korean origin. It does not cost anything to concoct fake registration data and enter <em>kimsukyang</em> during a Hotmail registration. We concede that this registration data does not provide concrete, indisputable information about the attackers.</p>
<p>However, the attackers&#8217; IP-addresses do provide some additional clues. During our analysis, we observed ten IP-addresses used by the Kimsuky operators. All of them lie in ranges of the Jilin Province Network and Liaoning Province Network, in China.</p>
<p><img decoding="async" class="aligncenter" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2013/09/08100208/tarakanov_pic3.jpg" alt="" border="0" /></p>
<p>No other IP-addresses have been uncovered that would point to the attackers&#8217; activity and belong to other IP-ranges. Interestingly, the ISPs providing internet access in these provinces are also believed to maintain lines into North Korea. Finally, this geo-location supports the likely theory that the attackers behind Kimsuky are based in North Korea.</p>
