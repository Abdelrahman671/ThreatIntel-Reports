Nowadays, many successful phishing campaigns leverage tools called &quot;phishing kits.&quot; These kits are pre-packaged sets of malicious tools and resources meticulously crafted by TAs to streamline and amplify their fraudulent activities.</p><p>A phishing kit is a collection of malicious assets and scripts designed to replicate legitimate websites, often mimicking the login pages of banks, financial institutions, or other trusted platforms. These kits are constructed to deceive unsuspecting victims into divulging confidential information such as usernames, passwords, and phone numbers.<br/>The deployment of phishing kits serves several purposes for cybercriminals engaged in fraudulent activities, especially those centered around banking fraud:</p><ul role="list"><li>Ease of Use: Phishing kits simplify setting up malicious websites, allowing even less technically skilled TAs to conduct sophisticated cyber attacks.</li><li>Speed and Efficiency: With pre-configured templates and scripts, TAs can rapidly deploy phishing campaigns, maximising the volume of potential victims.</li><li>Concealment: By mimicking legitimate sites, phishing kits help TAs avoid detection by blending into the vast sea of genuine online traffic.</li><li>Customisation: TAs can tailor phishing kits to target specific organisations or demographics, enhancing the chances of success in their fraudulent endeavours.</li></ul></div></section><section id="5" class="bigmarginbottom"><h3 class="titlesectionclab">Introducing Mr. Robot: a C2 Framework for phishing campaigns</h3><div class="blog w-richtext"><p>Our investigations unveiled an additional C2 framework dubbed &quot;Mr. Robot&quot;. On the other hand, TAs put effort into developing evasion techniques to avoid a quick detection of a newly registered phishing domain. </p><p>Typically, modern phishing kits adopt multiple anti-detection techniques, including:</p><ul role="list"><li>Geofencing checks </li><li>Device fingerprinting</li><li>Blackisting specific ASN and/or network ranges </li><li>Abuse of legitimate services, such as CDN and reverse-proxy, for masking the actual location of the web server</li><li>Dynamic content generation</li></ul><p>The following Figure summarises all the primary anti-evasion techniques adopted, starting when a potential new victim follows a malicious link set up by TAs.</p><figure style="max-width:1412pxpx" class="w-richtext-align-fullwidth w-richtext-figure-type-image"><div><img src="https://cdn.prod.website-files.com/60201cc2b6249b0358f70f8a/65e1d2ea140fcce0145046d3_f6.png" loading="lazy" alt="Figure 6 â Mr. Robot - Anti-evasion techniques"/></div><figcaption>Figure 6 â Mr. Robot - Anti-evasion techniques</figcaption></figure><p>Since the primary focus of this fraudulent campaign is the clientele of retail banking institutions from specific geographical areas (Italy, Spain, and the UK), filtering out all the connections except the ones coming from a mobile device is a pretty standard technique adopted by various TAs. These panels are pivotal components in the infrastructure of botnets, offering attackers a range of functionalities to execute and oversee their malicious activities. </p><p>Furthermore, web panels facilitate data collection from compromised devices, including system information and credentials, enabling analysis and exploitation for further malicious activities. Lastly, botnet controllers leverage web control panels to monitor the health and performance of their botnet, tracking the number of active bots and their geographic distribution.</p><p>Understanding the capabilities and functionalities of web control panels is crucial for comprehending the threat landscape posed by botnet operations. Analysts can gain deeper insights into attacker tactics, techniques, and procedures (TTPs) by dissecting these components, enhancing their ability to effectively mitigate and counteract such threats.</p><h4>Overview of C2 panel and functionalities</h4><p>Copybara leverages a C2 panel named âJOKER RATâ. Starting from its dashboard, the panel displays the list of all the infected devices and their geographical distribution over a map, with the feature called âLive MAPâ, as shown in the following Figure 11.</p><figure style="max-width:1378pxpx" class="w-richtext-align-fullwidth w-richtext-figure-type-image"><div><img src="https://cdn.prod.website-files.com/60201cc2b6249b0358f70f8a/65e1d49e2dc733c253d3b498_f11.png" loading="lazy" alt="Figure 11 â Copybara Dashboard (Live MAP)"/></div><figcaption>Figure 11 â Copybara Dashboard (Live MAP)</figcaption></figure><p>This dashboard can also retrieve basic information about the infected devices, such as the device name, OS version, and IP address. With this data, TAs can easily âtriageâ the infected devices by country or determine which victim is online/offline. A ânotification mechanismâ is also triggered whenever a new device is infected. </p><p>As shown in the following Figure 12, for each infected device inside the panel, TAs can perform different actions, in particular:</p><ul role="list"><li><strong>Silent Connect</strong>: This is the main feature of the panel that allows the TAs to control remotely and interact in real-time with the victim&#x27;s infected device (VNC). </li><li><strong>Inject</strong>: injecting the overlay page to steal the banking/crypto credentials (Overlay attacks).</li><li><strong>Notes</strong>: This feature is usually used by TAs to note down some information about the victim to be defrauded.</li><li><strong>Delete</strong>: delete the infected device from the dashboard.</li></ul><figure style="max-width:1298pxpx" class="w-richtext-align-fullwidth w-richtext-figure-type-image"><div><img src="https://cdn.prod.website-files.com/60201cc2b6249b0358f70f8a/65e1d4cd0e196f45da26eb6b_f12.png" loading="lazy" alt="Figure 12 â C2 panel with infected device"/></div><figcaption>Figure 12 â C2 panel with infected device</figcaption></figure><p>When the âSilent Connectâ button is clicked, a new page is opened, and TAs can collect additional data and perform fraudulent actions on the infected device.</p><p>In the Android banking trojan context, this feature is also known as VNC, which enables the attacker to view and manipulate the screen of the compromised device remotely. This level of access allows them to carry out various fraudulent activities, including on-device frauds (ODF).</p><figure style="max-width:1344pxpx" class="w-richtext-align-fullwidth w-richtext-figure-type-image"><div><img src="https://cdn.prod.website-files.com/60201cc2b6249b0358f70f8a/65e1d4eb7448fac644d13fb6_f13.png" loading="lazy" alt="Figure 13 â Copybara - âSilent connectâ feature "/></div><figcaption>Figure 13 â Copybara - âSilent connectâ feature</figcaption></figure><h4>Real-time keylogging</h4><p>Once the user accepts the Accessibility Service popup during the installation phases, the malware can record every activity done by the user on the compromised device.
