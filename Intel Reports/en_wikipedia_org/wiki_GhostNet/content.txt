					<div id="mw-content-text" class="mw-body-content"><div class="mw-content-ltr mw-parser-output" lang="en" dir="ltr"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Electronic spy operation</div>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1236090951">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}@media print{body.ns-0 .mw-parser-output .hatnote{display:none!important}}</style><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">For the fishing net, see <a href="/wiki/Ghost_net" title="Ghost net">Ghost net</a>.</div>
<p class="mw-empty-elt">
</p><p><b>GhostNet</b> (<a href="/wiki/Simplified_Chinese_characters" title="Simplified Chinese characters">simplified Chinese</a>&#58; <span lang="zh-Hans">幽灵网</span>; <a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_characters" title="Traditional Chinese characters">traditional Chinese</a>&#58; <span lang="zh-Hant">幽靈網</span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>&#58; <i><span lang="zh-Latn">YōuLíngWǎng</span></i>) is the name given by researchers at the <a href="/wiki/Information_Warfare_Monitor" title="Information Warfare Monitor">Information Warfare Monitor</a> to a large-scale <a href="/wiki/Cyber_spying" title="Cyber spying">cyber spying</a><sup id="cite_ref-bbc_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-guardian_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-guardian-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> operation discovered in March 2009. The operation is likely associated with an <a href="/wiki/Advanced_persistent_threat" title="Advanced persistent threat">advanced persistent threat</a>, or a network actor that spies undetected.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Its command and control infrastructure is based mainly in the <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">People's Republic of China</a> and GhostNet has infiltrated high-value political, economic and media locations<sup id="cite_ref-nato_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nato-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> in 103 countries. Computer systems belonging to <a href="/wiki/Embassy" class="mw-redirect" title="Embassy">embassies</a>, foreign ministries and other government offices, and the <a href="/wiki/14th_Dalai_Lama" title="14th Dalai Lama">Dalai Lama</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Tibet" title="Tibet">Tibetan</a> exile centers in India, London and New York City were compromised.
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Discovery">Discovery</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=GhostNet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Discovery"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>GhostNet was discovered and named following a 10-month investigation by the <a href="/wiki/Infowar_Monitor" class="mw-redirect" title="Infowar Monitor">Infowar Monitor</a> (IWM), carried out after IWM researchers approached the <a href="/wiki/14th_Dalai_Lama" title="14th Dalai Lama">Dalai Lama</a>'s representative in Geneva<sup id="cite_ref-Tracking_Ghostnet_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tracking_Ghostnet-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> suspecting that their computer network had been infiltrated.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC_3003_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_3003-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The IWM is composed of researchers from The SecDev Group and Canadian consultancy and the <a href="/wiki/Citizen_Lab" title="Citizen Lab">Citizen Lab</a>, <a href="/wiki/Munk_Centre_for_International_Studies" class="mw-redirect" title="Munk Centre for International Studies">Munk School of Global Affairs</a> at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Toronto" title="University of Toronto">University of Toronto</a>; the research findings were published in the <i>Infowar Monitor</i>, an affiliated publication.<sup id="cite_ref-NY-TIMES_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NY-TIMES-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Researchers from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cambridge" title="University of Cambridge">University of Cambridge</a>'s <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cambridge_Computer_Laboratory" class="mw-redirect" title="University of Cambridge Computer Laboratory">Computer Laboratory</a>, supported by the <a href="/wiki/Institute_for_Information_Infrastructure_Protection" title="Institute for Information Infrastructure Protection">Institute for Information Infrastructure Protection</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Cambridge_p2_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cambridge_p2-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> also contributed to the investigation at one of the three locations in <a href="/wiki/Dharamshala" title="Dharamshala">Dharamshala</a>, where the Tibetan government-in-exile is located.  The discovery of the 'GhostNet', and details of its operations, were reported by <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> on March 29, 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-NY-TIMES_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NY-TIMES-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Investigators focused initially on allegations of Chinese cyber-espionage against the <a href="/wiki/Tibetan_exile" class="mw-redirect" title="Tibetan exile">Tibetan exile</a> community, such as instances where email correspondence and other data were extracted.<sup id="cite_ref-bp_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bp-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Compromised systems were discovered in the <a href="/wiki/Embassy" class="mw-redirect" title="Embassy">embassies</a> of <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cyprus" title="Cyprus">Cyprus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malta" title="Malta">Malta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a>, <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a> and the office of the Prime Minister of <a href="/wiki/Laos" title="Laos">Laos</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Foreign_Affairs" class="mw-redirect" title="Ministry of Foreign Affairs">foreign ministries</a> of <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh" title="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Latvia" title="Latvia">Latvia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brunei" title="Brunei">Brunei</a>, <a href="/wiki/Barbados" title="Barbados">Barbados</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bhutan" title="Bhutan">Bhutan</a> were also targeted.<sup id="cite_ref-bbc_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Reuters_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reuters-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> No evidence was found that <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">U.S.</a> or <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">U.K.</a> government offices were infiltrated, although a <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a> computer was monitored for half a day and the computers of the <a href="/wiki/Embassy_of_India_in_Washington,_D.C." class="mw-redirect" title="Embassy of India in Washington, D.C.">Indian embassy</a> in <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a>, were infiltrated.<sup id="cite_ref-nato_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nato-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Reuters_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reuters-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Since its discovery, GhostNet has attacked other government networks, for example Canadian official financial departments in early 2011, forcing them off-line. Governments commonly do not admit such attacks, which must be verified by official but anonymous sources.<sup id="cite_ref-cbc_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cbc-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Technical_functionality">Technical functionality</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=GhostNet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Technical functionality"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Emails are sent to target organizations that contain contextually relevant information. These emails contain malicious attachments, that when opened, enable a <a href="/wiki/Trojan_horse_(computing)" title="Trojan horse (computing)">Trojan horse</a> to access the system.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> This Trojan connects back to a control server, usually located in China, to receive commands. The infected computer will then execute the command specified by the control server. Occasionally, the command specified by the control server will cause the infected computer to download and install a Trojan known as <a href="/wiki/Gh0st_Rat" class="mw-redirect" title="Gh0st Rat">Gh0st Rat</a> that allows attackers to gain complete, real-time control of computers running <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Windows" title="Microsoft Windows">Microsoft Windows</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-nato_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nato-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Such a computer can be controlled or inspected by attackers, and the software even has the ability to turn on camera and audio-recording functions of infected computers, enabling attackers to perform surveillance.<sup id="cite_ref-NY-TIMES_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NY-TIMES-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Origin">Origin</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=GhostNet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Origin"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The researchers from the IWM stated they could not conclude that the Chinese government was responsible for the spy network.<sup id="cite_ref-uoft_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uoft-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> However, a report from researchers at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cambridge" title="University of Cambridge">University of Cambridge</a> says they believe that the Chinese government is behind the intrusions they analyzed at the Office of the Dalai Lama.<sup id="cite_ref-snoop_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-snoop-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Researchers have also noted the possibility that GhostNet was an operation run by private citizens in China for profit or for patriotic reasons, or created by intelligence agencies from other countries such as Russia or the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-NY-TIMES_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NY-TIMES-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The Chinese government has stated that China "strictly forbids any cyber crime."<sup id="cite_ref-bbc_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bp_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bp-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>The "Ghostnet Report" documents several unrelated infections at Tibetan-related organizations in addition to the Ghostnet infections. By using the email addresses provided by the IWM report, Scott J. Henderson had managed to trace one of the operators of one of the infections (non-Ghostnet) to <a href="/wiki/Chengdu" title="Chengdu">Chengdu</a>. He identifies the hacker as a 27-year-old man who had attended the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Electronic_Science_and_Technology_of_China" title="University of Electronic Science and Technology of China">University of Electronic Science and Technology of China</a>, and currently connected with the Chinese hacker <a href="/wiki/Subculture" title="Subculture">underground</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Despite the lack of evidence to pinpoint the Chinese government as responsible for intrusions against Tibetan-related targets, researchers at Cambridge have found actions taken by Chinese government officials that corresponded with the information obtained via computer intrusions. One such incident involved a diplomat who was pressured by Beijing after receiving an email invitation to a visit with the <a href="/wiki/14th_Dalai_Lama" title="14th Dalai Lama">Dalai Lama</a> from his representatives.<sup id="cite_ref-snoop_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-snoop-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Another incident involved a Tibetan woman who was interrogated by Chinese intelligence officers and was shown transcripts of her online conversations.<sup id="cite_ref-uoft_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uoft-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> However, there are other possible explanations for this event. Drelwa uses <a href="/wiki/Tencent_QQ" title="Tencent QQ">QQ</a> and other instant messengers to communicate with Chinese Internet users. In 2008, IWM found that TOM-Skype, the Chinese version of Skype, was logging and storing text messages exchanged between users. It is possible that the Chinese authorities acquired the chat transcripts through these means.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>18<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>IWM researchers have also found that when detected, GhostNet is consistently controlled from IP addresses located on the island of <a href="/wiki/Hainan" title="Hainan">Hainan</a>, China, and have pointed out that Hainan is home to the Lingshui signals intelligence facility and the Third Technical Department of the People's Liberation Army.<sup id="cite_ref-nato_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nato-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Furthermore, one of GhostNet's four control servers has been revealed to be a <span class="clarify-content" style="padding-left:0.1em; padding-right:0.1em; color:var(--color-subtle, #54595d); border:1px solid var(--border-color-subtle, #c8ccd1);">government server.</span><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Clarify" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="This passage needs to be better explained. (July 2020)">clarify</span></a></i>&#93;</sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>19<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
