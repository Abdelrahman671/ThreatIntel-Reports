      <h1><span id="hs_cos_wrapper_name" class="hs_cos_wrapper hs_cos_wrapper_meta_field hs_cos_wrapper_type_text" style="" data-hs-cos-general-type="meta_field" data-hs-cos-type="text">Cybereason vs. DarkSide Ransomware</span></h1>
      <div class="cr-mln__post-author-share">
      <div id="hubspot-author_data" class="hubspot-editable cr-mln__post-meta" data-hubspot-form-id="author_data" data-hubspot-name="Blog Author">
        
          <span class="descriptor">Written By</span>
          <p><span class="author">Cybereason Nocturnus</span></p>
         
      </div>
    </div>
     
    </div>
    
    <!-- Sticky Author and Social Box -->
    
    <!-- END Sticky Author and Social Box -->
      <div class="container-is-blog columns is-multiline page-center cr-mln__blog-post--body">
        
        <div class="column is-7-fullhd is-7-desktop is-10-tablet is-10-mobile is-offset-1-fullhd is-offset-1-desktop is-offset-1-tablet is-offset-1-mobile">
          <span id="hs_cos_wrapper_post_body" class="hs_cos_wrapper hs_cos_wrapper_meta_field hs_cos_wrapper_type_rich_text" style="" data-hs-cos-general-type="meta_field" data-hs-cos-type="rich_text"><p>DarkSide is a relatively new ransomware strain that made its first appearance in August 2020. DarkSide follows the RaaS (ransomware-as-a-service) model, and, according to Hack Forums, the DarkSide team recently made an announcement that DarkSide 2.0 has been released. According to the group, it is equipped with the <a href="https://www.israeldefense.co.il/en/node/48908" rel="noopener" target="_blank">fastest encryption speed on the market</a>, and even includes Windows and Linux versions.</p>
<!--more-->
<p>The team is very active on hack forums and keeps its customers updated with news related to the ransomware. In an effort to grow and expand their operations, the group has started an <a href="https://twitter.com/3xp0rtblog/status/1369727242562134017" rel="noopener" target="_blank">affiliates program</a> for potential users.</p>
<p>Like many other ransomware variants, DarkSide follows the <a href="/blog/ransomware-attacks-remain-persistent-and-pervasive" rel="noopener" target="_blank">double extortion</a><a href="/blog/ransomware-attacks-remain-persistent-and-pervasive" rel="noopener" target="_blank"> trend</a>, which means the threat actors not only encrypt the user’s data, but first exfiltrate the data and threaten to make it public if the ransom demand is not paid. This technique effectively renders the strategy of backing up data as a precaution against a ransomware attack moot.<br><br>DarkSide is observed being used against targets in English-speaking countries, and appears to avoid targets in countries associated with former Soviet Bloc nations. The ransom demand ranges between US$200,000 to $2,000,000, and according to their website, the group has published stolen data from more than 40 victims, which is estimated to be just a fraction of the overall number of victims:</p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><img src="https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/image%201.png?width=1474&amp;name=image%201.png" alt="image 1" width="1474" loading="lazy" style="width: 1474px;" srcset="https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/image%201.png?width=737&amp;name=image%201.png 737w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/image%201.png?width=1474&amp;name=image%201.png 1474w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/image%201.png?width=2211&amp;name=image%201.png 2211w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/image%201.png?width=2948&amp;name=image%201.png 2948w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/image%201.png?width=3685&amp;name=image%201.png 3685w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/image%201.png?width=4422&amp;name=image%201.png 4422w" sizes="(max-width: 1474px) 100vw, 1474px"><em>DarkSide Leaks website</em></p>
<p>Unlike many ransomware variants such as Maze, which was employed to successfully attack <a href="https://statescoop.com/maze-ransomware-attackers-leak-data-stolen-from-suburban-washington-schools/" rel="noopener" target="_blank">suburban Washington schools</a>, the group behind DarkSide appears to have a code of conduct that prohibits attacks against hospitals, hospices, schools, universities, non-profit organizations, and government agencies:</p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><img src="https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/image2-1.png?width=1600&amp;name=image2-1.png" alt="image2-1" width="1600" loading="lazy" style="width: 1600px;" srcset="https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/image2-1.png?width=800&amp;name=image2-1.png 800w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/image2-1.png?width=1600&amp;name=image2-1.png 1600w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/image2-1.png?width=2400&amp;name=image2-1.png 2400w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/image2-1.png?width=3200&amp;name=image2-1.png 3200w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/image2-1.png?width=4000&amp;name=image2-1.png 4000w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/image2-1.png?width=4800&amp;name=image2-1.png 4800w" sizes="(max-width: 1600px) 100vw, 1600px"><em>One of the rules of the affiliates program - prohibited sectors to attack</em></p>
<h3 style="text-align: left; font-size: 36px; line-height: 1.75; font-weight: normal;">Key details</h3>
<p style="padding-left: 40px;"><strong>• Emerging Threat: </strong><span style="font-weight: normal;">In a short amount of time, the DarkSide group has established a reputation for being a very “professional” and “organized” group that has potentially generated millions of dollars in profits from the ransomware.</span>&nbsp;</p>
<p style="padding-left: 40px;"><strong>• High Severity: </strong>The Cybereason Nocturnus Team assesses the threat level as HIGH given the destructive potential of the attacks.</p>
<p style="padding-left: 40px;"><strong>• Human Operated Attack: </strong>Prior to the deployment of the ransomware, the attackers attempt to infiltrate and move laterally throughout the organization, carrying out a fully-developed attack operation.</p>
<p style="padding-left: 40px;"><strong>• Aiming Towards the DC: </strong><span style="font-weight: normal;">The DarkSide group is targeting domain controllers (DCs), which puts targets and the whole network environment at great risk.</span></p>
<p style="padding-left: 40px;"><strong>• Detected and Prevented:</strong> <a href="/platform" rel="noopener" target="_blank">The Cybereason Defense Platform</a> fully detects and prevents the DarkSide ransomware.</p>
<div class="hs-embed-wrapper" data-service="wistia" data-script-embed="true" style="position: relative; overflow: hidden; width: 100%; height: auto; padding: 0px; max-width: 791px; max-height: 445px; min-width: 256px; display: block; margin: auto;"><div class="hs-embed-content-wrapper"><script src="https://fast.wistia.net/embed/iframe/186xxdwuxz.jsonp" async>&nbsp;</script><script src="https://fast.wistia.com/assets/external/E-v1.js" async></script><div class="wistia_responsive_padding" style="padding:56.25% 0 0 0;position:relative;"><div class="wistia_responsive_wrapper" style="height:100%;left:0;position:absolute;top:0;width:100%;"><span class="wistia_embed wistia_async_186xxdwuxz popover=true popoverAnimateThumbnail=true videoFoam=true" style="display:inline-block;height:100%;position:relative;width:100%">&nbsp;</span></div></div></div></div>
<p style="text-align: center;"><em>Cybereason Blocks DarkSide Ransomware</em></p>
<p style="text-align: left;">The DarkSide group is a relatively new player in the game of ransomware. Despite being a new group, though, the DarkSide team has already built itself quite a reputation for making their operations more professional and organized. The group has a phone number and even a help desk to facilitate negotiations with victims, and they are making a great effort at collecting information about their victims - not just technical information about their environment, but more general information about the company itself, like the organization’s size and estimated revenue. <br><br>By collecting information about the victims, the group is making sure the ransomware is only used against the “right targets.” The group claims they only target large, profitable companies in their ransomware attacks, and claim to have extorted millions of dollars from companies in an effort to "make the world a better place." <a href="https://www.bbc.com/news/technology-54591761" rel="noopener" target="_blank">The group even wrote</a> in a forum that "some of the money the companies have paid will go to charity… No matter how bad you think our work is, we are pleased to know that we helped change someone's life. Today we sended (sic) the first donations."</p>

<h2 style="text-align: left; font-size: 36px;">Breaking Down the Attack</h2>
<h3 style="text-align: left; font-size: 30px; font-weight: normal;">Downloading the Ransomware</h3>
<p style="text-align: left;">After gaining an initial foothold in the network, the attackers start to collect information about the environment and the company. If it turns out that the potential target is on the attacker’s list of prohibited organizations to attack (ie: hospitals, hospices, schools, universities, non-profit organizations, or government agencies), they don’t move forward with the attack.<br><br>If not on the prohibited list, the attackers continue to carry out the operation:</p>
<p style="padding-left: 40px;"><strong>• </strong><span style="font-weight: normal;">The attackers begins to collect files, credentials and other sensitive information, and exfilitrate it.</span></p>
<p style="padding-left: 40px;"><strong>• </strong><span style="font-weight: normal;">The attackers use PowerShell to download the DarkSide binary as “update.exe” using the “DownloadFile” command, <a href="https://lolbas-project.github.io/lolbas/Binaries/Certutil/" rel="noopener" target="_blank">abusing Certutil.exe</a> and <a href="https://lolbas-project.github.io/lolbas/Binaries/Bitsadmin/" rel="noopener" target="_blank">Bitsadmin.exe</a> in the process:</span></p>

<p style="text-align: center;"><em><img src="https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/image%2015.png?width=557&amp;name=image%2015.png" alt="image 15" width="557" loading="lazy" style="width: 557px; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto; display: block;" srcset="https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/image%2015.png?width=279&amp;name=image%2015.png 279w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/image%2015.png?width=557&amp;name=image%2015.png 557w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/image%2015.png?width=836&amp;name=image%2015.png 836w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/image%2015.png?width=1114&amp;name=image%2015.png 1114w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/image%2015.png?width=1393&amp;name=image%2015.png 1393w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/image%2015.png?width=1671&amp;name=image%2015.png 1671w" sizes="(max-width: 557px) 100vw, 557px">The PowerShell commands as shown in the Cybereason defence platform</em></p>
<p style="text-align: left;">The de-obfuscated PowerShell script: <br>Get-WmiObject Win32_Shadowcopy | ForEach-Object {$_.Delete();}<br><br>The malware then enumerates the running processes and terminates different processes to unlock their files so it can both steal related information stored in the files and encrypt them.<br><br>DarkSide creates a unique User_ID string for the victim, and adds it to the encrypted files extension as follows:<br>&lt;File_name&gt;.{userid}. In addition, the malware also changes the icons for the encrypted files and changes the background of the desktop:</p>
