      <h1><span id="hs_cos_wrapper_name" class="hs_cos_wrapper hs_cos_wrapper_meta_field hs_cos_wrapper_type_text" style="" data-hs-cos-general-type="meta_field" data-hs-cos-type="text">Cybereason vs. Cl0p Ransomware</span></h1>
      <div class="cr-mln__post-author-share">
      <div id="hubspot-author_data" class="hubspot-editable cr-mln__post-meta" data-hubspot-form-id="author_data" data-hubspot-name="Blog Author">
        
          <span class="descriptor">Written By</span>
          <p><span class="author">Cybereason Nocturnus</span></p>
         
      </div>
    </div>
     
    </div>

    <!-- END Sticky Author and Social Box -->

        <div class="column is-7-fullhd is-7-desktop is-10-tablet is-10-mobile is-offset-1-fullhd is-offset-1-desktop is-offset-1-tablet is-offset-1-mobile">
          <span id="hs_cos_wrapper_post_body" class="hs_cos_wrapper hs_cos_wrapper_meta_field hs_cos_wrapper_type_rich_text" style="" data-hs-cos-general-type="meta_field" data-hs-cos-type="rich_text"><p><span style="color: #000000;">In the past months, the Cybereason Nocturnus team has been tracking the activity of the </span><a href="https://malpedia.caad.fkie.fraunhofer.de/details/win.clop" rel="noopener" target="_blank">Cl0p</a><span style="color: #000000;"> ransomware, a variant of </span><a href="https://malpedia.caad.fkie.fraunhofer.de/details/win.cryptomix"><span>CryptoMix</span></a><span style="color: #000000;"> ransomware. The name “clop” comes from Russian or Bulgarian, and means “bug”.</span></p>
<!--more-->
<h3 style="font-size: 36px; line-height: 1.75;"><span style="color: #000000; font-weight: normal;">Key Findings</span><span style="color: #000000;"></span></h3>
<p><strong><span style="color: #1f2e33;">Evolving Threat</span></strong><span style="color: #1f2e33;">: TA505 have evolved their attack tactics, delivering Cl0p ransomware as the final payload on as many systems as possible in order to pressure the victim to pay the ransom - non-paying Cl0p victims’ data is being published on the Cl0p leaks site</span></p>
<p><strong><span style="color: #1f2e33;">Multi-Staged Attack: </span></strong><span style="color: #1f2e33;">Before deploying Cl0p, two prior payloads are deployed to allow the attackers to move laterally within the compromised network before downloading and deploying the Clop ransomware.</span></p>
<p><strong><span style="color: #1f2e33;">High Severity</span></strong><span style="color: #1f2e33;">: The Cybereason Nocturnus Team assesses the threat level as HIGH given the destructive potential of the attacks.</span></p>
<p><strong><span style="color: #1f2e33;">Detected and Prevented</span></strong><span style="color: #1f2e33;">: </span><a href="https://www.cybereason.com/platform"><span>The Cybereason Defense Platform</span></a><span style="color: #1f2e33;"> fully detects and prevents the Cl0p ransomware.</span></p>
<div class="hs-embed-wrapper" data-service="wistia, inc." data-script-embed="true" style="position: relative; overflow: hidden; width: 100%; height: auto; padding: 0px; max-width: 791px; max-height: 544px; min-width: 256px; display: block; margin: auto;"><div class="hs-embed-content-wrapper"><iframe src="https://fast.wistia.net/embed/iframe/8bbk62ddie" title="Cybereason vs. Clop Ransomware Video" allow="autoplay; fullscreen" allowtransparency="true" frameborder="0" scrolling="no" class="wistia_embed" name="wistia_embed" allowfullscreen msallowfullscreen="" width="960" height="540"></iframe>
<script src="https://fast.wistia.net/assets/external/E-v1.js" async></script></div></div>
<h3 style="font-size: 36px; font-weight: normal;"><span style="color: #000000;">Background</span></h3>
<p><span style="color: #000000;">In 2019, the TA505 threat actor started delivering Cl0p as their final payload. </span><a href="https://malpedia.caad.fkie.fraunhofer.de/actor/ta505"><span>TA505</span></a><span style="color: #000000;"> is a well known </span><a href="https://blog.fox-it.com/2020/11/16/ta505-a-brief-history-of-their-time/"><span>sophisticated</span></a><span style="color: #000000;"> cybercrime threat actor, attacking various sectors for financial gain.</span></p>
<p><span style="color: #000000;">In 2019, the TA505 group changed their main strategy into encrypting assets in a corporate network and demanding a Bitcoin ransom for the decryption key.</span></p>
<p><span style="color: #000000;">A more recent Cl0p attack was against </span><a href="https://www.zdnet.com/article/german-tech-giant-software-ag-down-after-ransomware-attack/"><span>AG</span></a><span style="color: #000000;">, a large German software company. Their internal network was breached, and the attackers demanded more than $20 million ransom. In </span><a href="https://twitter.com/darktracer_int/status/1330933879398559744"><span>another case</span></a><span style="color: #000000;">, the group attacked a South Korean retailer, demanding $40 million ransom this time, and threatening to leak 2 million cards in case the negotiation fails.</span></p>
<p><span style="color: #000000;">Moreover, the group maintains a site where they leak data of victims who did not pay the ransomware:</span></p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><span style="color: #000000;"><img src="https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/dam/images/images-web/blog-images/cr-vs-clop-ransomware-blog-8.png?width=713&amp;name=cr-vs-clop-ransomware-blog-8.png" alt="cr-vs-clop-ransomware-blog-8" width="713" style="width: 713px; margin: 0px auto;" srcset="https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/dam/images/images-web/blog-images/cr-vs-clop-ransomware-blog-8.png?width=357&amp;name=cr-vs-clop-ransomware-blog-8.png 357w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/dam/images/images-web/blog-images/cr-vs-clop-ransomware-blog-8.png?width=713&amp;name=cr-vs-clop-ransomware-blog-8.png 713w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/dam/images/images-web/blog-images/cr-vs-clop-ransomware-blog-8.png?width=1070&amp;name=cr-vs-clop-ransomware-blog-8.png 1070w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/dam/images/images-web/blog-images/cr-vs-clop-ransomware-blog-8.png?width=1426&amp;name=cr-vs-clop-ransomware-blog-8.png 1426w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/dam/images/images-web/blog-images/cr-vs-clop-ransomware-blog-8.png?width=1783&amp;name=cr-vs-clop-ransomware-blog-8.png 1783w, https://www.cybereason.com/hs-fs/hubfs/dam/images/images-web/blog-images/cr-vs-clop-ransomware-blog-8.png?width=2139&amp;name=cr-vs-clop-ransomware-blog-8.png 2139w" sizes="(max-width: 713px) 100vw, 713px"></span></p>
<p style="text-align: center; font-size: 16px;"><em><span style="color: #999999;">A Screenshot from the Cl0p leaks website</span></em></p>
<p><span style="color: #000000;">The </span><a href="https://www.hornetsecurity.com/en/security-information/clop-clop-ta505-html-malspam-analysis/"><span>infection chain</span></a><span style="color: #000000;"> is as follows, and depicted </span><span>below</span><span style="color: #000000;">: First of all, when a malspam campaign is launched, emails are sent to victims from compromised accounts, thus increasing their credibility. The emails contain an HTML attachment that redirects to a compromised website.&nbsp;</span></p>
<p><span style="color: #000000;">It then delivers a document containing a malicious macro that drops the Get2 loader. Get2 downloads and executes </span><a href="https://malpedia.caad.fkie.fraunhofer.de/details/win.sdbbot"><span>SDBbot</span></a><span style="color: #000000;">, </span><a href="https://malpedia.caad.fkie.fraunhofer.de/details/win.flawedgrace"><span>FlawedGrace</span></a><span style="color: #000000;"> or </span><a href="https://malpedia.caad.fkie.fraunhofer.de/details/win.flawedammyy"><span>FlawedAmmy</span></a><span style="color: #000000;">.
