<div class="blog-text">
<h2><span style="font-weight: 300;">Context</span></h2>
<p>The Metabase Q Security Operations Center (SOC) triages millions of alerts a day but a recent attempt to infect a customer&#8217;s network caught our attention. Although the customers&#8217; endpoint detection and response security tools properly blocked the initial payload, the use of fake certificates to try to evade detection was unusual and warranted additional investigation by Metabase Q&#8217;s Threat Intelligence Team.</p>
<p>During the analysis of the artifacts provided by the SOC team, 20 different spam campaigns were identified, which targeted <strong>Chile, Mexico, Peru</strong>, and <strong>Portugal</strong>. These campaigns focused on credential stealing, specifically online banking, schools, government services, social media, gaming, ecommerce, public repositories, and Outlook email.</p>
<p>In several cases, the cyber criminals created fake webpages for the victim, such as online banking windows. For the initial infection, the attackers tried to lure the victims into opening different types of fake bills via HTML pages or PDF password-protected files. An example of a PDF file targeting Mexican users is below:</p>
<p><img decoding="async" class="alignnone size-medium wp-image-7567 aligncenter" src="https://www.metabaseq.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/03/Mispadu-Banking-Trojan-Fig-01.png" alt="" width="500" srcset="https://www.metabaseq.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/03/Mispadu-Banking-Trojan-Fig-01.png 791w, https://www.metabaseq.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/03/Mispadu-Banking-Trojan-Fig-01-300x211.png 300w, https://www.metabaseq.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/03/Mispadu-Banking-Trojan-Fig-01-768x541.png 768w" sizes="(max-width: 791px) 100vw, 791px" /></p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Figure 1: Fake Bill sent via PDF</span></p>
<p>The campaigns started around <strong>August 2022,</strong> and were still running at the time of this writing, <strong>early March 2023</strong>.</p>
<h3><b>What is the size of the impact? </b></h3>
<p>Due to a misconfiguration in the attackers’ network, Metabase Q was able to access 8 of the 20 Command and Control Servers (C2s). Most of these were compromised websites. In the files containing the stolen credentials, we discovered a total of <strong>90,518 </strong>credentials coming from <strong>17,595 </strong>unique websites across all industry sectors. We estimate the total infection numbers to be substantially larger given this only represents close to half of the C2 servers across a single period of time. More information can be found in the table below:</p>

<h3><b>Who is the actor behind these attacks?</b></h3>
<p>The techniques, tactics and procedures (TTPs) used during these campaigns resembles the banking trojan <strong>Mispadu</strong>, however contains new components that have not been seen before. We will go into further detail in this blog.</p>
<p><a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/2019/11/19/mispadu-advertisement-discounted-unhappy-meal/"><strong>Mispadu</strong></a> was discovered around 2019 by ESET and is known to target Latin American countries via spamming and malvertising campaigns. It is an important group to monitor given it’s high level of activity in the region along with its malware-as-a-service modus operandi. As a result, the group has constantly launched new types of campaigns, containing different layers of obfuscation and new techniques, making it difficult to fully protect systems against it.</p>
<p>One of their main strategies is to compromise legitimate websites and use them as Command &amp; Control Servers to further spread malware. They accomplish this by searching for and compromising websites with vulnerable versions of Content Management Systems, like WordPress. From there, they leverage these websites to spread malware in a customized manner, such as filtering out countries that they do not wish to infect, dropping different types of malware based on the country being infected and even deploying a unique malicious RAT (Remote Administration Tool) when they detect an interesting device, like a computer from a bank’s employee.</p>
<p>During these campaigns, the group discard any victim with the following system languages:</p>
<ul>
<li style="font-weight: 300;" aria-level="1"><span style="font-weight: 300;">Spanish Spain</span></li>
<li style="font-weight: 300;" aria-level="1"><span style="font-weight: 300;">English United States</span></li>
<li style="font-weight: 300;" aria-level="1"><span style="font-weight: 300;">Portuguese &#8211; Brazil</span></li>
</ul>
<p>Because the cyber criminals have a payload building process automated, they can deliver new variants of malware rapidly and thus scale and support multiple campaigns in parallel.</p>
<p>Based on our analysis of the malware, it is clear that the group is very familiar with the main banks and institutions in the targeted Latin American countries. Multiple Spanish words found in their malware suggest that several of the programmers may be Latinos, and specifically may be from Chile based on the slang utilized in the comments.</p>
<p>Metabase Q also discovered several new techniques that were added in this massive infection that enabled the cyber criminals to access thousands of credentials:</p>
<ul>
<li style="font-weight: 300;" aria-level="1"><span style="font-weight: 300;">Fake certificates to obfuscate initial stage malware</span></li>
<li style="font-weight: 300;" aria-level="1"><span style="font-weight: 300;">A new .NET-based backdoor able to take screenshots or even send fake Windows to the victim</span></li>
<li style="font-weight: 300;" aria-level="1"><span style="font-weight: 300;">A new RUST-Based Backdoor, this programming language is still not well handled by endpoint protection</span></li>
</ul>

<p>If the file does not exist yet, the steps to decode the certificate is the following:</p>

<li>Creates a directory in %appdata%\herman\exe\vayala\.</li>

<p>MD5: 618A60899AAE66EA55E5DC8374C7B828</p>

<p><img decoding="async" class="alignnone size-medium wp-image-7567 aligncenter" src="https://www.metabaseq.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/03/Mispadu-Banking-Trojan-Fig-31.png" alt="" width="500" /></p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><span style="font-weight: 300;">Figure 30. IDA requesting transacted_hollowing_rust.pdb .</span></p>
<p>But, what is transacted hollowing? Well, this technique is a hybrid between process hollowing and process doppelganging injection techniques:</p>
<p><img decoding="async" class="alignnone size-medium wp-image-7567 aligncenter" src="https://www.metabaseq.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/03/Mispadu-Banking-Trojan-Fig-32.png" alt="" width="500" /></p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><span style="font-weight: 300;">Figure 31. Transacted Hollowing Process Injection</span></p>
<p>Basically, the loader will run a legitimate windows program, in this case ARP.exe, replace it in memory with malicious binary using NTFS transactions, meaning, while the file is created inside a transaction, no other process can access it, this includes anti-virus and therefore allows to hide from detection, finally the injected process resumes execution to perform malicious actions. Below the Windows APIs resolved at runtime related to his process injection technique:</p>
<p>NtCreateTransaction, CreateFileTransactedA, NtCreateSection, NtRollbackTransaction, NtMapViewOfSection.</p>
<p>If you are not familiar with this technique, the colleagues from Malwarebytes described it in detail <a href="https://www.malwarebytes.com/blog/news/2018/08/process-doppelganging-meets-process-hollowing_osiris">here</a>.</p>
<p>In the Figure 32, the newly replaced ARP.exe process can be seen executing PowerShell code:</p>

<p style="text-align: center;"><span style="font-weight: 300;">Figure 32. ARP replaced via Transacted hollowing</span></p>

<h2><span style="font-weight: 300;">Conclusions</span></h2>
