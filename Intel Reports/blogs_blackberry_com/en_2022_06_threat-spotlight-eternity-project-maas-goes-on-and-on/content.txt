    <div class="text">    
    
    <p>The Eternity Project sounds like some kind of glitzy new album promoted by a pop star who has a symbol for a name. Instead, it’s a malware toolkit sold as a malware-as-a-service (MaaS) opportunity. The cheapest subscription costs less per year than a Netflix subscription. Threat actors running the service distribute it through the anonymous Tor marketplace and Telegram channels as the “Eternity Group.”</p>
<p>The group markets its product in both English and Russian and appears to have links to the Russian “Jester Group,” which has been active since July 2021. The Eternity Stealer, sold as a component of Eternity Group’s MaaS platform, appears to be a rebrand of the Jester Stealer malware, which was seen targeting Ukraine in May 2022.</p>
<p>The threat actors utilize the Telegram messaging platform to communicate feature updates. In addition, a Telegram bot enables the purchaser to customize the build of the malware and choose desired features. The malware is sold as annual subscriptions or perpetual licenses, with prices ranging from US$90 to almost US$500 for separate parts of the toolkit.</p>
<h3><b>Operating System</b></h3>

    
    
</div>

    <div class="text">    
    
    <h3><b>Risk and Impact</b><br>
</h3>

    
    
</div>


    
    
    <div class="image">
  <div data-cmp-is="image" data-cmp-src="/en/2022/06/threat-spotlight-eternity-project-maas-goes-on-and-on/_jcr_content/main/par/sectionblog/par/image_640240523.coreimg{.width}.png/1656562190186/eternity-table002.png" data-asset="/content/dam/blogs-blackberry-com/images/blogs/2022/06/eternity-table002.png" data-asset-id="e760a461-f25a-4d11-83b7-3cb7f256c89e" class="cmp-image" itemscope itemtype="http://schema.org/ImageObject">
 
     
         
         
          
         <img src="/content/dam/blogs-blackberry-com/images/blogs/2022/06/eternity-table002.png" class="cmp-image__image" itemprop="contentUrl" data-cmp-hook-image="image" alt/>

    <div class="text">    
    
    <h3><b>Eternity Project Malware-as-a-Service Pricing</b></h3>
<p>The malware toolkit is hosted on the Eternity Projects TOR webpage, where an overview of each malware component is presented and listed for sale, as seen in Figure 1. Each malware item in the toolkit is individually priced.</p>
<ul>
<li>Eternity Stealer - $260 annual subscription</li>
<li>Eternity Miner - $90 annual subscription</li>
<li>Eternity Worm - $390</li>
<li>Eternity Ransomware - $490</li>
<li>Eternity Clipper - $110</li>
<li>Eternity DDoS Bot&nbsp;- (Still in development)</li>
</ul>

    
    
</div>


    
    
    <div class="image">
  <div data-cmp-is="image" data-cmp-src="/en/2022/06/threat-spotlight-eternity-project-maas-goes-on-and-on/_jcr_content/main/par/sectionblog/par/image_365060870.coreimg{.width}.png/1656562190209/eternity-fig01.png" data-asset="/content/dam/blogs-blackberry-com/images/blogs/2022/06/eternity-fig01.png" data-asset-id="c0a77c53-fd60-4daa-8ce3-84feba4df4e3" class="cmp-image" itemscope itemtype="http://schema.org/ImageObject">
 
     
         
         
          
         <img src="/content/dam/blogs-blackberry-com/images/blogs/2022/06/eternity-fig01.png" class="cmp-image__image" itemprop="contentUrl" data-cmp-hook-image="image" alt/>
         
         <figcaption class="figure-caption" styles="display:table-caption;caption-side:bottom;"></figcaption>
          
     
 
 
 
</div>

    
</div>


    
    
    <div class="text">    
    
    <p><i>Figure 1 - Eternity TOR webpage displaying a variety of malware for sale</i></p>
<p>Eternity was originally brought to light by a Cyble <a href="https://blog.cyble.com/2022/05/12/a-closer-look-at-eternity-malware/" target="_blank">report</a> that mentioned the group’s Telegram channel had over 500 subscribers. This channel has since been banned and a new one has been created, as seen in Figure 2. At the time of writing, the new channel has 150 subscribers. </p>

    
    
</div>


    
    
    <div class="image">
  <div data-cmp-is="image" data-cmp-src="/en/2022/06/threat-spotlight-eternity-project-maas-goes-on-and-on/_jcr_content/main/par/sectionblog/par/image_1465185196.coreimg{.width}.png/1656562190232/eternity-fig02.png" data-asset="/content/dam/blogs-blackberry-com/images/blogs/2022/06/eternity-fig02.png" data-asset-id="d71cd555-8b30-4f01-aaa6-7a587eb18384" class="cmp-image" itemscope itemtype="http://schema.org/ImageObject">
 
     
         
         
          
         <img src="/content/dam/blogs-blackberry-com/images/blogs/2022/06/eternity-fig02.png" class="cmp-image__image" itemprop="contentUrl" data-cmp-hook-image="image" alt/>
         
         <figcaption class="figure-caption" styles="display:table-caption;caption-side:bottom;"></figcaption>
          
     
 
 
 
</div>

    
</div>


    
    
    <div class="text">    
    
    <p><i>Figure 2 - New Telegram channel used for communicating product updates</i></p>
<h3><b>How Eternity Ransomware Operates</b><br>
</h3>
<p>The ransomware offered by Eternity is a .NET executable that uses the name “Microsoft.exe” to try and lull the user into a false sense of safety. The file contains a lot of similarities to the open-source, proof of concept (PoC) ransomware “<a href="https://github.com/0xFenrik/Povlsomware" target="_blank">Povlsomware;</a>” it even includes the name of this ransomware in its strings. It appears that the developer of Eternity ransomware is utilizing the source code from the open-source GitHub page of Povlsomware, and modifying it to create its own bespoke ransomware.</p>
<p>Eternity ransomware achieves persistence on the target machine by modifying the startup registry to launch itself on system startup, as seen in Figure 3.</p>

    
    
</div>


    
    
    <div class="image">
  <div data-cmp-is="image" data-cmp-src="/en/2022/06/threat-spotlight-eternity-project-maas-goes-on-and-on/_jcr_content/main/par/sectionblog/par/image_83404497.coreimg{.width}.png/1656562190254/eternity-fig03.png" data-asset="/content/dam/blogs-blackberry-com/images/blogs/2022/06/eternity-fig03.png" data-asset-id="1f088654-a704-4ff3-9691-7f2abdc196c6" class="cmp-image" itemscope itemtype="http://schema.org/ImageObject">
 
     
         
         
          
         <img src="/content/dam/blogs-blackberry-com/images/blogs/2022/06/eternity-fig03.png" class="cmp-image__image" itemprop="contentUrl" data-cmp-hook-image="image" alt/>
         
         <figcaption class="figure-caption" styles="display:table-caption;caption-side:bottom;"></figcaption>
          
     
 
 
 
</div>

    
</div>


    
    
    <div class="text">    
    
    <p><i>Figure 3 – Eternity modifies Startup registry keys to achieve persistence</i></p>
<p>The malware targets personal files, with a list of 788 different file extensions that it seeks. It then performs AES256 encryption on the files it identifies.</p>
<p>Eternity differs from most ransomware we have observed by performing extensionless encryption. Typically, ransomware will append a unique extension to encrypted files; however, in this instance, the original file extension is kept. But because the contents of the files have been modified, the encrypted files no longer work as intended.</p>
<p>The malware will avoid encrypting files within the following directories, to decrease the chance of making crucial system files inoperable:</p>
<ul>
<li>All Users\\Microsoft\\</li>
<li>AppData\\</li>
<li>C:\\Program Files</li>
<li>\\Eternity\\</li>
<li>C:\\ProgramData\\</li>
<li>$Recycle.Bin</li>
<li>\\source\\</li>
<li>Temporary Internet Files</li>
<li>C:\\Windows</li>
</ul>
<p>Once encryption has been carried out, the malware will attempt to destroy shadow copies using Windows Management Instrumentation (WMI). Removing these backups prevents victims from easily reverting back to their machine’s previous pre-encryption state.</p>

    
    
</div>


    
    
    <div class="image">
  <div data-cmp-is="image" data-cmp-src="/en/2022/06/threat-spotlight-eternity-project-maas-goes-on-and-on/_jcr_content/main/par/sectionblog/par/image_891713172.coreimg{.width}.png/1656562190277/eternity-fig04.png" data-asset="/content/dam/blogs-blackberry-com/images/blogs/2022/06/eternity-fig04.png" data-asset-id="27671b49-0485-483b-95b8-6ce06d7fac7a" class="cmp-image" itemscope itemtype="http://schema.org/ImageObject">
 
     
         
         
          
         <img src="/content/dam/blogs-blackberry-com/images/blogs/2022/06/eternity-fig04.png" class="cmp-image__image" itemprop="contentUrl" data-cmp-hook-image="image" alt/>
         
         <figcaption class="figure-caption" styles="display:table-caption;caption-side:bottom;"></figcaption>
          
     
 
 
 
</div>

    
</div>


    
    
    <div class="text">    
    
    <p><i>Figure 4 - Eternity deletes backups to prevent the victim from rolling their computer back to a pre-encryption state</i></p>
<p>Post encryption, the victim is presented with a ransom note which is dropped to the desktop. The note states that their computer’s files have been encrypted with “military-grade” encryption. It also gives instructions on how the victim can go about decrypting their files for the cost of $800 in Monero cryptocurrency.</p>

    
    
</div>


    
    
    <div class="image">
  <div data-cmp-is="image" data-cmp-src="/en/2022/06/threat-spotlight-eternity-project-maas-goes-on-and-on/_jcr_content/main/par/sectionblog/par/image_260282138.coreimg{.width}.png/1656562190300/eternity-fig05.png" data-asset="/content/dam/blogs-blackberry-com/images/blogs/2022/06/eternity-fig05.png" data-asset-id="b34d6fb7-bd45-47d5-9993-02598886ec4d" class="cmp-image" itemscope itemtype="http://schema.org/ImageObject">
 
     
         
         
          
         <img src="/content/dam/blogs-blackberry-com/images/blogs/2022/06/eternity-fig05.png" class="cmp-image__image" itemprop="contentUrl" data-cmp-hook-image="image" alt/>
         
         <figcaption class="figure-caption" styles="display:table-caption;caption-side:bottom;"></figcaption>
          
     
 
 
 
</div>

    
</div>


    
    
    <div class="text">    
    
    <p><i>Figure 5 - Ransom note dropped by Eternity</i></p>
<h3><b>Eternity Stealer Malware Functionality</b><br>
</h3>
<p>As is the case with the other malware offered by Eternity, the Eternity Stealer is a .NET executable file. The file is highly obfuscated to make analyzing the file more difficult.</p>
<p>The threat actors allow purchasers of this stealer to utilize a Telegram bot to build the malware to meet their exact specifications. To accomplish this, it prompts them with questions about which features they would like to include in the build.</p>
<p>As seen in Figure 6, available options include enabling AntiVM (Virtual Machine), enabling Startup persistence, choosing the file extension of the build, and enabling AntiRepeat. AntiRepeat is used to prevent any repeat infections on the target machines, to avoid duplication of exfiltrated data.<br>
&nbsp;</p>

    
    
</div>


    
    
    <div class="image">
  <div data-cmp-is="image" data-cmp-src="/en/2022/06/threat-spotlight-eternity-project-maas-goes-on-and-on/_jcr_content/main/par/sectionblog/par/image_995241505.coreimg{.width}.png/1656562190323/eternity-fig06.png" data-asset="/content/dam/blogs-blackberry-com/images/blogs/2022/06/eternity-fig06.png" data-asset-id="9054a049-2faf-43fc-be46-acc4e408612f" class="cmp-image" itemscope itemtype="http://schema.org/ImageObject">
 
     
         
         
          
         <img src="/content/dam/blogs-blackberry-com/images/blogs/2022/06/eternity-fig06.png" class="cmp-image__image" itemprop="contentUrl" data-cmp-hook-image="image" alt/>
         
         <figcaption class="figure-caption" styles="display:table-caption;caption-side:bottom;"></figcaption>
          
     
 
 
 
</div>

    
</div>


    
    
    <div class="text">    
    
    <p><i>Figure 6 - Telegram bot builder options for stealer</i></p>
<p>The strings that are visible in the samples used in this analysis contain a lot of references to the online sandbox video game “<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Growtopia" target="_blank">Growtopia.</a>” The malware looks like it is related to an old Eternity tool (shown in Figure 7) that was initially designed to steal credentials of Growtopia accounts. This type of credential-stealing tool is commonly distributed via YouTube videos and online forums, where they advertise this functionality. These accounts can be of great monetary value to attackers, as they often contain in-game items that are sold on underground marketplaces.</p>

    
    
</div>

<h3><b>Eternity Worm Malware<br>
</b></h3>
<p>The Telegram bot builder for Eternity Worm offers the budding cybercriminal customer a wide range of customizability when purchasing the malware. As shown in Figure 9, this includes:</p>

<li>A Telegram spreader message</li>

The malware will then remove the original copy of itself from wherever it was initially located on the disk. The worm will also create a scheduled task to ensure it launches at system start up.</p>
<p>One of the malicious features of Eternity Worm is that it creates Discord and Telegram spam in an attempt to spread itself further.</p>
<p>In creating the Discord spam shown in Figure 10, the worm retrieves a list of target usernames by issuing a GET request to “http://discord[.]com[.]/api/users/@me.” This will return a JSON file with a list of usernames. The worm then issues a GET request to “http://discord[.]com[.]/api/users/@me/channels,” which similarly returns a JSON file with a list of Discord channel IDs. </p>

Very good stuff. [URL]” with the [URL] portion being replaced by the URL where the worm payload itself is hosted. <p>An additional feature of the worm is its ability to infect the Python interpreter. The malware enumerates the “%AppData%/Local/Programs/Python” directory, and then (as seen in Figure 11) it injects the worm loader into all compiled Python projects on the infected machine.</p>

<h3><b>How Eternity Miner Behaves</b><br>
</h3>
<p>Cryptocurrency miners are used by attackers to steal the host’s computing power to mine for crypto coins; this process is also known as <a href="https://blogs.blackberry.com/en/2019/05/outlaw-monero-cryptojacking-in-the-wild" target="_blank">cryptojacking</a>. The miner used by Eternity is a .NET <a href="https://blogs.blackberry.com/en/2020/12/lessons-from-the-front-line-threat-hunting" target="_blank">XMRig</a> miner that targets the Monero cryptocurrency. The following screenshot shown in Figure 14 is the output screen of a sample Telegram bot build for the miner. </p>

<p>When launched, the miner will achieve persistence by placing a copy of itself in the Windows® startup folder. The XMRig miner is then injected into the “explorer.exe” process, where cryptocurrency mining operations begin. The miner will periodically send beacon updates back to the C2 server.</p>
<h3><b>Conclusion</b><br>

<p>The threat actors behind the Eternity Project have shown great determination in remaining prevalent in the current threat landscape. At the time of writing this report, a new update has just been posted to the malware’s Telegram page stating, “Police have confiscated some servers and devices, but a new domain has already been setup and services will be resuming as normal shortly.”</p>

<p>The Eternity project contains a wide range of customizable and updateable pieces of malware to suit the needs of any attacker. A threat actor who decides to purchase any of the available Eternity malware can customize it in such a way that each piece of malware will have unique IoCs. This customizability allows the evolution of the malware to continue, while making threat hunting more difficult.</p>
<p>Continued growth of the Eternity Project and its malware offerings is expected in the coming months. This will possibly include a DDoS bot, reportedly in development by the threat actor. From the Telegram pages used by the threat actors and their malware customers, it appears that the primary distribution methods will continue to be through YouTube videos, Discord links and email attachments. To guard against Eternity-based attacks, it is important that users stay extra vigilant when interacting with any of these services.</p>
<h3><b>Mitigation Tips</b></h3>
<p>Some steps that can be put in place to mitigate the effects of the attacks mentioned in this report include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Put into place a recovery plan to safely maintain multiple copies of sensitive or proprietary information.</li>
<li>Avoid downloading cracked software, or software from unknown/unverified links, and create rules that prevent employees from doing this.</li>
<li>Make sure corporate login credentials and personal passwords are not saved in your browser.</li>
<li>Application Configuration Hardening: Modify application configurations to reduce its attack surface (MITRE D3FEND™ technique <a href="https://d3fend.mitre.org/technique/d3f:ApplicationConfigurationHardening" target="_blank">D3-ACH</a>)</li>
<li>Implement multi-factor authentication (MITRE D3FEND technique <a href="https://d3fend.mitre.org/technique/d3f:Multi-factorAuthentication" target="_blank">D3-MFA</a>)</li>
<li>DNS Traffic Analysis: Analyze domain name metadata, including name and DNS records, to determine whether the domain is likely to resolve to an undesirable host (MITRE D3FEND technique <a href="https://d3fend.mitre.org/technique/d3f:DNSTrafficAnalysis" target="_blank">D3-DNSTA</a>)</li>
<li>User Data Transfer Analysis: Analyze the amount of data transferred by a user (MITRE D3FEND technique <a href="https://d3fend.mitre.org/technique/d3f:UserDataTransferAnalysis" target="_blank">D3-UDTA</a>)</li>
</ul>
