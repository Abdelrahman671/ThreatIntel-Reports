												<div class="c-wysiwyg">
													<p>In July 2019, Dr. Web <a href="https://news.drweb.com/show/?i=13349&amp;c=0&amp;p=0">reported</a> about a backdoor trojan in Google Play, which appeared to be sophisticated and unlike common malware often uploaded for stealing victims&#8217; money or displaying ads. So, we conducted an inquiry of our own, discovering a long-term campaign, which we dubbed &#8220;PhantomLance&#8221;, its earliest registered domain dating back to December 2015. We found dozens of related samples that had been appearing in the wild since 2016 and had been deployed in various application marketplaces including Google Play. One of the latest samples was published on the official Android market on November 6, 2019. We informed Google of the malware, and it was removed from the market shortly after.</p>
<p><a href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2020/04/27141031/sl_phantomlance_01.png" class="magnificImage"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-96773" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2020/04/27141031/sl_phantomlance_01.png" alt="" width="701" height="823" srcset="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2020/04/27141031/sl_phantomlance_01.png 701w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2020/04/27141031/sl_phantomlance_01-256x300.png 256w" sizes="(max-width: 701px) 100vw, 701px" /></a></p>
<p style="text-align: center"><strong><em>The latest example of spyware in Google Play disguised as a browser cleaner</em></strong></p>
<p>During our investigation, we discovered various overlaps with reported OceanLotus APT campaigns. Thus, we found multiple code similarities with the previous Android campaign, as well as macOS backdoors, infrastructure overlaps with Windows backdoors and a few cross-platform resemblances.</p>
<p>Besides the attribution details, this document describes the actors&#8217; spreading strategy, their techniques for bypassing app market filters, malware version diversity and the latest sample deployed in 2020, which uses Firebase to decrypt the malicious payload. We also found out that Blackberry Cylance research team <a href="https://threatvector.cylance.com/en_us/home/mobile-malware-and-apt-espionage-prolific-pervasive-and-cross-platform.html" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">investigated</a> this activity.</p>
<p>Our report is broken down into several sections.</p>
<ol>
<li><a href="#malware-versions">Malware versions</a> – technical description of versions found, their features and relationships between them.</li>
<li><a href="#spread">Spread</a> – information on specific tactics used by the threat actors for distributing their malware.</li>

<h2 id="malware-versions">Malware versions</h2>
<p>For the purposes of the research, we divided samples we found into a series of &#8220;versions&#8221; based on technical complexity: from the basic Version 1 to the highly sophisticated Version 3. Note that they do not fully correlate with the chronological order of their appearance ITW: for example, we observed Version 1 samples in late 2019 and in 2017, the year that we also saw Version 3.</p>
<p>Functionality of all samples are similar – the main purpose of spyware was to gather sensitive information. While the basic functionality was not very broad, and included geolocation, call logs, contact access and SMS access, the application could also gather a list of installed applications, as well as device information, such as model and OS version. Furthermore, the threat actor was able to download and execute various malicious payloads, thus, adapting the payload that would be suitable to the specific device environment, such as Android version and installed apps. This way the actor is able to avoid overloading the application with unnecessary features and at the same time gather information needed.</p>
<h3 id="version-1">Version 1</h3>
<p>We attribute the latest Google Play sample (MD5: 2e06bbc26611305b28b40349a600f95c) to this version. This is a clear payload, and unlike the other versions, it does not drop an additional executable file. Our main theory about the reasons for all these versioning maneuvers is that the attackers are trying to use diverse techniques to achieve their key goal, to bypass the official Google marketplace filters. And achieve it they did, as even this version passed Google&#8217;s filters and was uploaded to Google Play Store in 2019 (see Spreading for details).</p>
<p>No suspicious permissions are mentioned in the manifest file; instead, they are requested dynamically and hidden inside the dex executable. This seems to be a further attempt at circumventing security filtering. In addition to that, there is a feature that we have not seen before: if the root privileges are accessible on the device, the malware can use a reflection call to the undocumented API function &#8220;setUidMode&#8221; to get permissions it needs without user involvement.</p>
<p><a href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2020/04/27141037/sl_phantomlance_02.png" class="magnificImage"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-96774" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2020/04/27141037/sl_phantomlance_02.png" alt="" width="903" height="658" srcset="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2020/04/27141037/sl_phantomlance_02.png 903w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2020/04/27141037/sl_phantomlance_02-300x219.png 300w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2020/04/27141037/sl_phantomlance_02-768x560.png 768w" sizes="(max-width: 903px) 100vw, 903px" /></a></p>
<p>Note that this trick only works with Android SDK version 19 or higher.</p>
<p>Most of the aforementioned operations naturally require root access, but we believe that the root exploit may be delivered as payload in a server response to collected device info. Also, some of the applications that the malware mimics will have notified the user that they only work on rooted devices. For instance, Browser Cleaner can only clean up the browser cache if it is given root permissions.</p>
<h3 id="version-2">Version 2</h3>
<p>Specimens of this version were also detected in 2019 and earlier. One of the samples was located in Google Play Store in November 2019 and <a href="https://vms.drweb.ru/virus/?i=18042508&amp;lng=en">described</a> in the Dr. Web blog. Based on our detection statistics and spotted version stamps, we believe that this version is a replacement for Version 3, which we did not observe in 2019.</p>
<p>Below are the most valuable points and main differences from the Version 1.</p>
<p>The malicious payload APK is now packed in an encrypted file in the assets directory and is decrypted by the first stage using an AES algorithm. A decryption key and initialization vector (IV) are located in the first 32 + 16 bytes of the encrypted payload.</p>
<p><a href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2020/04/27141042/sl_phantomlance_03.png" class="magnificImage"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-96775" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2020/04/27141042/sl_phantomlance_03.png" alt="" width="700" height="184" srcset="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2020/04/27141042/sl_phantomlance_03.png 700w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2020/04/27141042/sl_phantomlance_03-300x79.png 300w" sizes="(max-width: 700px) 100vw, 700px" /></a></p>
<p>After decryption, the asset file will look like this.</p>
<p><a href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2020/04/27141046/sl_phantomlance_04.png" class="magnificImage"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-96776" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2020/04/27141046/sl_phantomlance_04.png" alt="" width="684" height="324" srcset="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2020/04/27141046/sl_phantomlance_04.png 684w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2020/04/27141046/sl_phantomlance_04-300x142.png 300w" sizes="(max-width: 684px) 100vw, 684px" /></a></p>

<h3 id="version-2-1">Version 2.1</h3>
<p>The latest samples of PhantomLance discovered in the early 2020 introduced a new technique for decrypting payloads: the malicious payload was shipped with its dropper, encrypted with AES. The key is not stored anywhere in the dropper itself but sent to the device using <a href="https://firebase.google.com/">Google&#8217;s Firebase</a> remote config system. The other technical features are very similar to the ones we observed in Version 2, so we tagged this generation as Version 2.1.</p>
<p>We were able to make a valid request to PhantomLance&#8217;s Firebase API. The response consisted of a JSON struct containing the AES decryption key, where the &#8220;code_disable&#8221; value is the decryption key for payload.</p>

<p>What is important, the dropper expects that the AES decryption key will be stored in a parameter named &#8220;code&#8221;, so this specific variant should not function properly. Besides, we noticed that Firebase previously returned one more field, named &#8220;conf_disable&#8221;, which has the same value as the &#8220;code_disable&#8221;, so we assume that the actors are still tinkering with this new feature.</p>
<p>Another interesting technique that the actors are trying to implement is a third-stage payload implant. The second-stage payload (MD5: 83cd59e3ed1ba15f7a8cadfe9183e156) contains an APK file named &#8220;data&#8221; (MD5: 7048d56d923e049ca7f3d97fb5ba9812) with a corrupted header in the assets path.</p>

<p>This results in an APK file (MD5: c399d93146f3d12feb32da23b75304ba) that appears to be a typical PhantomLance payload configured with already known C2 servers (cloud.anofrio[.]com, video.viodger[.]com, api.anaehler[.]com). This third-stage APK is deployed with a custom native library named &#8220;data.raw&#8221;, also stored at the assets path. This library is used for achieving persistence on the infected device and appears to be a custom daemonized ELF executable based on the open-source &#8220;<a href="https://github.com/Miracle963/root/blob/5c8356dec3e175ab6b506344bcbfb570f51b02d5/Superuser-master/Superuser/jni/su/daemon.c">daemon.c</a>&#8221; Superuser tool component, while in previous samples, we saw <a href="https://github.com/Marswin/MarsDaemon">MarsDaemon</a> used for this purpose.</p>
<p><a href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2020/04/27141106/sl_phantomlance_08.png" class="magnificImage"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-96780" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2020/04/27141106/sl_phantomlance_08.png" alt="" width="792" height="408" srcset="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2020/04/27141106/sl_phantomlance_08.png 792w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2020/04/27141106/sl_phantomlance_08-300x155.png 300w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2020/04/27141106/sl_phantomlance_08-768x396.png 768w" sizes="(max-width: 792px) 100vw, 792px" /></a></p>
<p style="text-align: center"><strong><em>Code comparison of the library used to daemonize the third stage payload with daemon.c source code hosted on Github</em></strong></p>
<h3 id="version-3">Version 3</h3>
<p>While we have found that Version 2 has been used as a replacement for this one, as we have not observed any new deployments of Version 3 in 2019, it still looks more advanced in terms of technical details than Version 2. According to our detection statistics and deployment dates on application markets, Version 3 was active at least from 2016 to 2018.</p>
<p>Below are the most valuable points and main differences between Version 3 and Version 2.</p>
<p>The first-stage dropper appears even more obfuscated than that in Version 2; it uses a similar way of decrypting the payload, but it has minor differences. The encrypted content is split into multiple asset files under 10256 bytes in size plus an encrypted config file, and contains payload decryption details.</p>
<p><a href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2020/04/27141111/sl_phantomlance_09.png" class="magnificImage"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-96781" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2020/04/27141111/sl_phantomlance_09.png" alt="" width="413" height="159" srcset="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2020/04/27141111/sl_phantomlance_09.png 413w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2020/04/27141111/sl_phantomlance_09-300x115.png 300w" sizes="(max-width: 413px) 100vw, 413px" /></a></p>
<p>Below is the payload decryption sequence.</p>
<ol>
<li>Decrypt the payload config file from the assets with both a hardcoded name and AES key.</li>
<li>Read the following values from the decrypted payload config file in this order:
<ul>
<li>AES key for APK payload decryption</li>
<li>Class and method names for reflection calls to the payload</li>
<li>MD5 for APK payload integrity check</li>
<li>Number and names of the split APK payload parts</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>Decrypt the APK payload header hardcoded in the first stage with the AES key from the payload config. Write it to the APK payload file.</li>
<li>Using decrypted names of the split payload parts, decrypt their content and append them to the APK payload file one by one.</li>
<li>Check the integrity of the resulting APK payload file by comparing with the MD5 value decrypted from the payload config.</li>
<li>Load and run the APK payload.</li>
</ol>
<p>The following reversed code fragment represents the actual payload decryption process.</p>
<p><a href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2020/04/27141115/sl_phantomlance_10.png" class="magnificImage"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-96782" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2020/04/27141115/sl_phantomlance_10.png" alt="" width="886" height="743" srcset="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2020/04/27141115/sl_phantomlance_10.png 886w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2020/04/27141115/sl_phantomlance_10-300x252.png 300w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2020/04/27141115/sl_phantomlance_10-768x644.png 768w" sizes="(max-width: 886px) 100vw, 886px" /></a></p>
<p>Each Version 3 payload has the same package name, &#8220;com.android.process.gpsp&#8221;, and is signed with the same certificate (CN=Ventoplex), used to sign some of the Version 2 payloads.</p>
<p>The only developer version stamp that we have found in Version 3 payloads is &#8220;10.2.98&#8221;.</p>
<p>Another notable finding is the 243e2c6433815f2ecc204ada4821e7d6 sample, which we believe belongs to a Version 3 payload. However, no related dropper has been spotted in the wild, and unlike the other payloads, it is signed with a debug certificate and not obfuscated at all, revealing all variable/class/method names and even BuildConfig values. Our guess that this is a debug developer version that somehow got leaked.</p>
<p>As a conclusion to this technical review, it is worth saying that all payloads across the different versions, even Version 1, which is in fact a clear payload without a dropper, share a code structure and locations where sensitive strings, such as С2 addresses, are stored.</p>

<h3 id="oceanlotus-macos-backdoors">OceanLotus macOS backdoors</h3>
<p>There are multiple <a href="https://blog.trendmicro.com/trendlabs-security-intelligence/new-macos-backdoor-linked-to-oceanlotus-found/">public</a> <a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/2019/04/09/oceanlotus-macos-malware-update/">reports</a> of macOS backdoors linked by the industry to OceanLotus. We examined these in order to find possible overlaps, with the caveat that it was really difficult to compare malware implemented for two completely different platforms, since two different programming languages were obviously used for the implementation process. However, during the analysis of the macOS payload (MD5: 306d3ed0a7c899b5ef9d0e3c91f05193) dated early 2018, we were able to catch a few minor tracks of the code pattern used in the Android malware implementation described above. In particular, three out of seven main classes had the same names and similar functionality: &#8220;Converter&#8221;, &#8220;Packet&#8221; and &#8220;Parser&#8221;.</p>
<p><a href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2020/04/27141213/sl_phantomlance_22.png" class="magnificImage"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-96794" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2020/04/27141213/sl_phantomlance_22.png" alt="" width="458" height="364" srcset="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2020/04/27141213/sl_phantomlance_22.png 458w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2020/04/27141213/sl_phantomlance_22-300x238.png 300w" sizes="(max-width: 458px) 100vw, 458px" /></a></p>

<p>Based on the complete analysis of previous campaigns, with the actors&#8217; interests in victims located in Vietnam, infrastructure overlaps between PhantomLance and OceanLotus for Windows, multiple code similarities between an old Android campaign and MacOS backdoors, we attribute the set of the Android activity (campaign 2014-2017 and PhantomLance) to OceanLotus with medium confidence.</p>
<p><a href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2020/04/27141217/sl_phantomlance_23.png" class="magnificImage"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-96795" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2020/04/27141217/sl_phantomlance_23.png" alt="" width="769" height="607" srcset="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2020/04/27141217/sl_phantomlance_23.png 769w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2020/04/27141217/sl_phantomlance_23-300x237.png 300w" sizes="(max-width: 769px) 100vw, 769px" /></a></p>
<p>Considering the timeline of the Android campaigns, we believe that the activity reported by Antiy Labs is a previous campaign that was conducted by OceanLotus until 2017, and PhantomLance is a successor, active since 2016.</p>
<p>In summarizing the results of this research, we are able to assess the scope and evolution of the actors&#8217; Android set of activity, operating for almost six years.</p>
