    <h1 class="text-center">Transparent Tribe APT expands its Windows malware arsenal</h1>

    <div class="text-center m-1">
        <div class="post-author">
            <span>By </span>
                <a href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/author/asheer-malhotra/">Asheer Malhotra</a>, 
                <a href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/author/kendall/">Kendall McKay</a>
        </div>

        <br/>
        <time class="post-datetime" datetime="May 13, 2021 08:08">
            Thursday, May 13, 2021 08:08
        </time>

        <div class="m-3">
                        <a href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/category/threat-spotlight/" class="category primary-category">
                            Threat Spotlight
                        </a>
                                            <a href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/category/securex-3/" class="category primary-category">
                            SecureX
                        </a>
                            </div>
    </div>

    <section class="post-content-wrapper mt-5">
        <div class="post-content">
            <p>Transparent Tribe, also known as APT36 and Mythic Leopard, continues to create fake domains mimicking legitimate military and defense organizations as a core component of their operations. Cisco Talos’ previous research has mainly linked this group to CrimsonRAT, but new campaigns show they are expanding their Windows malware arsenal with <a href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/2021/02/obliquerat-new-campaign.html">ObliqueRAT</a>.</p><p>While military and defense personnel continue to be the group’s primary targets, Transparent Tribe is increasingly targeting diplomatic entities, defense contractors, research organizations and conference attendees, indicating that the group is expanding its targeting.</p><p>Our recent research into Transparent Tribe uncovered two types of domains the group uses in their various campaigns: fake domains masquerading as legitimate Indian defense and government-related websites, and malicious domains posing as content-hosting sites. These domains work in conjunction with each other to deliver maldocs distributing <a href="https://malpedia.caad.fkie.fraunhofer.de/details/win.crimson">CrimsonRAT</a> and <a href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/2021/02/obliquerat-new-campaign.html">ObliqueRAT</a>.</p><p>Based on our findings, Transparent Tribe’s tactics, techniques, and procedures (TTPs) have remained largely unchanged since 2020, but the group continues to implement new lures into its operational toolkit. The variety of maldoc lures Transparent Tribe employs indicates the group still relies on social engineering as a core component of its operations.</p><h2 id="hosting-infrastructure">Hosting infrastructure</h2><p>Transparent Tribe uses a two-pronged approach for registering malicious domains: Fake domains masquerading as legitimate sites belonging to government, defense, or research entities, and malicious domains that resemble file-sharing websites.</p><h3 id="fake-domains">Fake domains<br></h3><p>Our latest Transparent Tribe research confirms that the group continues to create malicious domains mimicking defense-related entities as a core component of their operations. During our most recent investigation, we discovered a fake domain, <em>clawsindia[.]com,</em> registered by the attackers. This domain masquerades as the website for the <a href="https://www.claws.in/">Center For Land Warfare Studies (CLAWS)</a>, an India-based think tank covering national security and military issues. (The legitimate domain for CLAWS is <em>claws[.]in.</em>) The malicious clawsindia[.]com domain was previously hosted on 164[.]68[.]101[.]194, a known command and control (C2) for CrimsonRAT, Transparent Tribe’s custom .NET remote access trojan (RAT). At this point, we cannot confirm how the attackers are using or intend to use this domain as part of their broader operations. However, we also identified a subdomain, mail[.]clawsindia[.]com, hosted on the same IP, suggesting that the attackers are using it as part of a malspam campaign. <br></p><p>Below is one of the attackers’ maldocs they used to target individuals applying for the CLAWS “Chair of Excellence,” an honorary title for those making exceptional research contributions to strategic studies, according to the think tank’s official <a href="https://www.claws.in/static/CoAS-CoE-2021-PROPOSAL.pdf">documentation</a>. The victim is encouraged to click on an embedded URL hosted on sharingmymedia[.]com, which then downloads  <a href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/2021/02/obliquerat-new-campaign.html">ObliqueRAT</a>, the trojan discovered by Talos in 2020 associated with threat activity targeting entities in South Asia. <br><br>&lt;iframe src="https://open.spotify.com/embed-podcast/episode/7DqBSPHYlnhsNb2Kr1WXI5" width="100%" height="232" frameborder="0" allowtransparency="true" allow="encrypted-media"&gt;&lt;/iframe&gt;<br><br>We cannot confirm how the maldocs were delivered to victims, but we suspect they were probably sent as attachments to phishing emails based on previous threat actor behavior and the targeted nature of this particular lure. Security researchers previously discovered Transparent Tribe using sharingmymedia[.]com to host Android malware targeting Indian military and defense personnel.</p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card"><img src="https://lh3.googleusercontent.com/BOKGEnLu3EnaiY83yPHsWfHkieTLO-wfEO_JwOomdy3C36Xdxis3TF1YHj8fTHhM5RUjyk4ejSPlvC5pYKp6EOTkdMsmH64tng34Ulj08tA1MVNNFSoVC_I8IwuVvR__4E0p-_NukWT07una0Zx-0DxRmkVxb_lh3vcnMLAzjGaB8cDMLgeSg3XW" class="kg-image" alt loading="lazy" width="624" height="457"></figure><p>Figure 1: Maldoc masquerading as a congratulatory notice from CLAWS.</p><p>Although we could not confirm the initial infection vector of ObliqueRAT maldocs, earlier campaigns had the same infection chain as those seen in previous CrimsonRAT operations. In such cases, adversaries would deliver phishing maldocs to targets containing a malicious VBA macro that extracted either the CrimsonRAT executable or a ZIP archive embedded in the maldoc. The macro dropped the implant to the disk, setting up persistence mechanisms and eventually executing the payload on the infected endpoint. </p><p>The actors recently deviated from the CrimsonRAT infection chains to make their ObliqueRAT phishing maldocs appear more legitimate. For example, attackers leveraging ObliqueRAT started hosting their malicious payloads on compromised websites instead of embedding the malware in the maldoc. In one such case in early 2021, the adversaries used <em>iiaonline[.]in</em>, the Indian Industries Association’s legitimate website, to host ObliqueRAT artifacts. The attackers then moved to hosting fake websites resembling those of legitimate organizations in the Indian subcontinent. Figure 2 shows the attackers’ use of HTTrack, a free website copier program, to duplicate a legitimate website to use for their own malicious purposes. The attackers then used this fake website, which they hosted on a domain that was nearly identical to its legitimate counterpart, to distribute ObliqueRAT. These examples highlight Transparent Tribe’s heavy reliance on social engineering as a core TTP and the group’s efforts to make their operations appear as legitimate as possible.</p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card"><img src="https://lh4.googleusercontent.com/zprcvV4LGWNBuO4qbhhZPxiSNDWpDHhzLzud4stCv8tLoMBz0Nn4hexVsOMen6CgFn0F_CTyRYoskDs-RFLxRvV7vy0vvxoYhLLhAbOnJ38kXuxFV8BtIOpywnKR7-L9WS6bXdgQyC6QiEj5kauCE8liPWwaMdzdHOnK0wzICaTCpf_iICTIHnJf" class="kg-image" alt loading="lazy" width="624" height="107"></figure><p>Figure 2: Fake website cloned using HTTrack on May 29, 2020.</p><p>Another fake domain the group uses to serve CrimsonRAT is 7thcpcupdates[.]info. This domain masquerades as an information portal for <a href="https://www.finmin.nic.in/seven-cpc">The 7th Central Pay Commission (CPC)</a> of India, which provides payment information and updates  for government employees. The malicious domain prompts the victim to enter their name and email address to sign up and download a seemingly important “guide on pay and allowance.”</p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card"><img src="https://lh3.googleusercontent.com/2Oswh7uMG72fnOKUK-rqT-lWvhlAm-HEpzndcjwViQMSvH7o-7XpW9ysSRuTUT7UHMOynE9NGP_VCYXy4MsoYCnOmijShTzrVM6aPH6cWBj-CL-dnzogc5r-9lipUpE-np9kLBYCaqjDJURyeV1pfnW2pEDJjJJNrbwCpjuhDsU4T4U-OLxcTW5y" class="kg-image" alt loading="lazy" width="624" height="569"></figure><p>Figure 3: 7thcpcupdates[.]info landing page.</p><p>Once the victim enters their information, the portal prompts them to download the guide. Upon clicking “Download Now,” a malicious XLS file is downloaded onto the victim’s computer. After enabling macros, the file executes CrimsonRAT on the endpoint.</p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card"><img src="https://lh5.googleusercontent.com/DHkWrHadz6JUHHzHuB76LNSdUS5dzwz69jv71eswwRDSRneq4toe8vTFn4A56MCnnpjWq6foMNGIzu3t9MvV2K-r7MeIppRAXqhK0dm8BxC6fYyPg8BROWJvsYkfnA8hsWTjfc8dYXqm0y3H3ZUGgRaB3jy2ps7kh_xyWJfPGWWGaQxm1SYQOM3U" class="kg-image" alt loading="lazy" width="624" height="552"></figure><p>Figure 4: The “Download Now” button contains a link to a malicious XLS with CrimsonRAT embedded in it.<br></p><h3 id="malicious-file-sharing-domains">Malicious file-sharing domains</h3><p>Transparent Tribe also regularly registers domains that appear to be legitimate file- and media-sharing services. For example, the group has used drivestransfer[.]com, file-attachment[.]com, mediaclouds[.]live, and emailhost[.]network during their operations. In the CLAWS example above, the adversaries used another such malicious domain, sharingmymedia[.]com, to host ObliqueRAT. (Additional domains are listed in the IOCs section.) The infection chain involving these domains is similar to the one described above in which the threat actors use social engineering to convince the victim to download and open the malware hosted on these sites.</p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card"><img src="https://lh4.googleusercontent.com/ndBJDlISJlOeGAu_fv2WGvHOzyivBLWdnh_R6zMomf51iYplhWboaHJge-rl1Mp670lWqSx4AJoGq4F3u78p79QdSUm9k9m2SE7MR8LY9IK4jfxhCai1D1EDSkdp7Xc_Mni-wuc7UjisAwwCJJVAYHAprbCMnZpM13pw--R4w_XmvUjDEE7x39Np" class="kg-image" alt loading="lazy" width="624" height="699"></figure><p>Figure 5: A sample XLS maldoc containing a malicious macro hosted on <em>emailhost[.]network.</em><br><br></p><h2 id="lures-and-targeting">Lures and targeting</h2><p>Transparent Tribe uses a variety of themes in their lures that evolved over time. The group has leveraged generic themes, such as resumes and CVs, since early 2019. From 2019 and continuing into 2020, the attackers started using honeytrap-themed lures to trick targets into opening ZIP archives and maldocs that posed as pictures of women. By mid-2020, the attackers reverted to primarily distributing military-themed maldocs. These maldocs did not contain popular news topics, as seen in older campaigns, but instead masqueraded as logistical and operational documents for the Indian Armed Forces.<br></p><p>But Transparent Tribe’s attacks are not limited to only India. In one campaign, the attackers used an Iranian Ministry of Foreign Affairs (MOFA)-themed maldoc to distribute CrimsonRAT in mid-2019. Then, in mid- to late-2020, the attackers targeted diplomatic entities with RAR archives pretending to be related to the British High Commision in Islamabad, Pakistan. In mid-2020, we observed the first instance of conference attendees being targeted in the form of a CrimsonRAT maldoc masquerading as the agenda for an Afghani conference. However, since the start of this year, the group has increasingly used lures disguised as content from Indian government-sponsored conferences.</p><h3 id="defense-themed-lures">Defense-themed lures</h3><p>Transparent Tribe has historically used military and defense-themes in their phishing emails and maldocs to target Indian military and government personnel.
