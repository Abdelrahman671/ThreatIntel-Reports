class="entry-container"><div class="entry-content-wrap"><div class="entry-content"><p>Starting this past Friday we have seen a number of websites showing a fake ransomware infection. Google search results for &#8220;<em><strong>FOR RESTORE SEND 0.1 BITCOIN</strong></em>&#8221; were sitting at 6 last week and increased to 291 at the time of writing this. Upon visiting their website webmasters have been met with an alarming message:</p><p><img decoding="async" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-28068" src="https://blog.sucuri.net/wp-content/uploads/2021/11/siteencrypted.png" alt="" width="1391" height="510" srcset="https://blog.sucuri.net/wp-content/uploads/2021/11/siteencrypted.png 1391w, https://blog.sucuri.net/wp-content/uploads/2021/11/siteencrypted-600x220.png 600w, https://blog.sucuri.net/wp-content/uploads/2021/11/siteencrypted-650x238.png 650w, https://blog.sucuri.net/wp-content/uploads/2021/11/siteencrypted-768x282.png 768w, https://blog.sucuri.net/wp-content/uploads/2021/11/siteencrypted-390x143.png 390w, https://blog.sucuri.net/wp-content/uploads/2021/11/siteencrypted-820x301.png 820w, https://blog.sucuri.net/wp-content/uploads/2021/11/siteencrypted-1180x433.png 1180w, https://blog.sucuri.net/wp-content/uploads/2021/11/siteencrypted-560x205.png 560w, https://blog.sucuri.net/wp-content/uploads/2021/11/siteencrypted-80x29.png 80w" sizes="(max-width: 1391px) 100vw, 1391px" /></p><pre>SITE ENCRYPTED

FOR RESTORE SEND 0.1 BITCOIN: 3BkiGYFh6QtjtNCPNNjGwszoqqCka2SDEc

(create file on site /unlock .txt with transaction key inside)</pre><p>The warning indicated that the website was hit with a ransomware attack. The files were reported to be encrypted and the attackers demanded a ransom payment of 0.1 Bitcoin. While the price of Bitcoin is extremely volatile, at the time of writing this article that would clock in at a whopping $6,000+ USD</p><p><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-28069" src="https://blog.sucuri.net/wp-content/uploads/2021/11/bitcoinexchange.png" alt="" width="902" height="184" srcset="https://blog.sucuri.net/wp-content/uploads/2021/11/bitcoinexchange.png 902w, https://blog.sucuri.net/wp-content/uploads/2021/11/bitcoinexchange-600x122.png 600w, https://blog.sucuri.net/wp-content/uploads/2021/11/bitcoinexchange-650x133.png 650w, https://blog.sucuri.net/wp-content/uploads/2021/11/bitcoinexchange-768x157.png 768w, https://blog.sucuri.net/wp-content/uploads/2021/11/bitcoinexchange-390x80.png 390w, https://blog.sucuri.net/wp-content/uploads/2021/11/bitcoinexchange-820x167.png 820w, https://blog.sucuri.net/wp-content/uploads/2021/11/bitcoinexchange-560x114.png 560w, https://blog.sucuri.net/wp-content/uploads/2021/11/bitcoinexchange-80x16.png 80w" sizes="auto, (max-width: 902px) 100vw, 902px" /></p><p>Not a negligible sum of money for an average website owner, to say the least! Before panicking and paying the ransom (or completely re-building their website from scratch) thankfully some website owners hired us to take a look.</p><h2>Past Cases of Website Ransomware</h2><p>This would certainly not be our first time seeing websites impacted by ransomware. Typically ransomware affects endpoint devices, sometimes entire businesses and institutions. In fact, as I type this the entire provincial health care system of Newfoundland has been brought to its knees with thousands of delayed surgeries due to a crippling suspected ransomware <a href="https://www.cbc.ca/news/canada/newfoundland-labrador/health-services-it-outage-update-nov-1-1.6232426">attack</a>.</p><p>Starting at the beginning of 2016 we saw some examples of website files themselves being encrypted by attackers with a ransom being demanded, which we wrote about on our <a href="https://blog.sucuri.net/2016/01/ransomware-strikes-websites.html">blog</a> at the time. This was pretty short lived, however. Attackers always go after the money to maximise their profits. We can only presume that targeting websites wasn&#8217;t terribly profitable and was best for them to go after endpoints, businesses and organisations. It&#8217;s also much more common for website owners to have backups handy, rendering the entire attack moot.</p><h2>More than Meets the Eye</h2><p>The clock was ticking on the ransom notification: Seven days, ten hours, 21 minutes and 9 seconds to pay the ransom before the files would be encrypted and irretrievable forever. Tick, tock, tick, tock &#x23f0;</p><p>However, when we began our investigation into the website it turned out that nothing was encrypted at all! Normally when ransomware attacks website files the extension is changed to <i>.lock</i> or something similar, and the files have been rendered as unreadable, encrypted rubbish. Not so in this example!</p><p>So what was causing the frightening warning?</p><h2>Fake Ransomware Warning Generated by Plugin</h2><p>The solution to the source was actually quite simple: All we had to do was to query the file structure for the BitCoin account number. That led us to the following file:</p><pre>./wp-content/plugins/directorist/directorist-base.php</pre><p>Sure enough, the ransom warning was completely bogus. Nothing was encrypted at all! It was a simple HTML page generated by this bogus plugin and nothing more. Let&#8217;s take a look at this code and see what exactly it was doing.</p><p>Near the end of that file we can see that the malicious bit is just using some very basic HTML to generate the ransom message:</p><p><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-28070" src="https://blog.sucuri.net/wp-content/uploads/2021/11/bitcoinr.png" alt="" width="597" height="245" srcset="https://blog.sucuri.net/wp-content/uploads/2021/11/bitcoinr.png 597w, https://blog.sucuri.net/wp-content/uploads/2021/11/bitcoinr-390x160.png 390w, https://blog.sucuri.net/wp-content/uploads/2021/11/bitcoinr-560x230.png 560w, https://blog.sucuri.net/wp-content/uploads/2021/11/bitcoinr-80x33.png 80w" sizes="auto, (max-width: 597px) 100vw, 597px" /></p><p style="text-align: center;"><em>FOR RESTORE SEND 0.1 BITCOIN: 3BkiGYFh6QtjtNCPNNjGwszoqqCka2SDEc</em></p><p>As well as some basic PHP to generate the countdown clock:</p><p><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-28071" src="https://blog.sucuri.net/wp-content/uploads/2021/11/countdown.png" alt="" width="692" height="510" srcset="https://blog.sucuri.net/wp-content/uploads/2021/11/countdown.png 692w, https://blog.sucuri.net/wp-content/uploads/2021/11/countdown-600x442.png 600w, https://blog.sucuri.net/wp-content/uploads/2021/11/countdown-650x479.png 650w, https://blog.sucuri.net/wp-content/uploads/2021/11/countdown-390x287.png 390w, https://blog.sucuri.net/wp-content/uploads/2021/11/countdown-560x413.png 560w, https://blog.sucuri.net/wp-content/uploads/2021/11/countdown-80x59.png 80w" sizes="auto, (max-width: 692px) 100vw, 692px" /></p><p style="text-align: center;"><em>All cases so far have defaulted to November 20th, 2021</em></p><p>The desired date can be edited here to instill more panic into the request. Remember folks, rule number one about online scams like phishing is instilling a sense of <i>urgency</i> to the victim!</p><h2>Removing the Infection</h2><p>Getting this infection cleared up is easy enough, all we had to do was remove the plugin from the wp-content/plugins directory. However, once we got the main website page back all of their pages and posts were leading to 404 Not Found responses.</p><p>The reason for this is the last snippet of the malicious plugin:</p><p><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-28072" src="https://blog.sucuri.net/wp-content/uploads/2021/11/azze.png" alt="" width="757" height="86" srcset="https://blog.sucuri.net/wp-content/uploads/2021/11/azze.png 757w, https://blog.sucuri.net/wp-content/uploads/2021/11/azze-600x68.png 600w, https://blog.sucuri.net/wp-content/uploads/2021/11/azze-650x74.png 650w, https://blog.sucuri.net/wp-content/uploads/2021/11/azze-390x44.png 390w, https://blog.sucuri.net/wp-content/uploads/2021/11/azze-560x64.png 560w, https://blog.sucuri.net/wp-content/uploads/2021/11/azze-80x9.png 80w, https://blog.sucuri.net/wp-content/uploads/2021/11/azze-745x86.png 745w" sizes="auto, (max-width: 757px) 100vw, 757px" /></p><p>Here we see a basic SQL command which finds any posts and pages with the &#8220;<b>publish</b>&#8221; status and changes them to &#8220;<b>null</b>&#8220;. All the content was still in the database, just unable to be viewed! This can be reversed with an equally simple SQL command:</p><pre><i>UPDATE `wp_posts` SET `post_status` = 'publish' WHERE `post_status` = 'null';</i></pre><p>This will publish any content in the database marked as <i>null.</i> If you have other content marked as such, it will re-publish that, but that is certainly better than losing all your website posts and pages.</p><p>We also see the plugin including the following file:</p><pre>./wp-content/plugins/directorist/azz_encrypt.php</pre><p>However, so far we haven&#8217;t seen this file present in any of the infections. It&#8217;s possible that in other cases of this infection this file could contain functionality to encrypt the files, but so far we haven&#8217;t found a candidate with it present.</p><h2>Determining the Source</h2><p>In checking the access logs for the website it was easy enough to determine the IP address responsible. Our client was located in the southern United States, however we saw quite a few requests from a foreign IP address which was interacting with the directorist plugin using the plugin editor feature of wp-admin. This suggests that the legitimate plugin was already installed on the website and later tampered with by the attackers.</p><p>Interestingly, the very first request that we saw from the attacker IP address was from the wp-admin panel, suggesting that they had already established administrator access to the website before they began their shenanigans. Whether they had brute forced the admin password using another IP address or had acquired the already-compromised login from the black market is anybody&#8217;s guess.</p><h2>How to Protect your Site</h2><p>Once the plugin is removed and the nulled content in the database restored then tying up the loose ends is pretty straightforward!</p><ul><li aria-level="1">Review admin users on the site, remove any bogus accounts and update/change all wp-admin passwords</li><li aria-level="1">Secure your wp-admin administrator page</li><li aria-level="1">Change other access point passwords (database, FTP, cPanel, etc)</li><li aria-level="1">Ideally, place your website behind a <a href="https://sucuri.net/website-firewall/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">firewall</a></li><li aria-level="1">Don’t forget about reliable <a href="https://sucuri.net/website-backups/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">backups</a>! Even if hackers manage to encrypt your whole site, it will be easy to restore it from the latest backup.</li></ul><p>We recently published a detailed <a href="https://blog.sucuri.net/2021/07/basic-wordpress-hardening.html">article</a> on many of the different options that WordPress website owners have at their disposal to secure their admin page. Be sure to give it a read to make sure your website isn&#8217;t low hanging fruit for the bad guys! In particular, the <em>disallow_file_edit</em> function would have helped prevent this attack!</p><p>If you are a website owner and are affected by this attack our remediation analysts can help <a href="https://sucuri.net/website-security-platform/signup/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">remove the infection</a> for you!</p><p><strong>UPDATE:</strong> While the original point of this article was to mention this new malware infection and not locate its source we mentioned it may have been a compromised admin account. Upon further evaluation it was due to a cross-side request forgery vulnerability in the Directorist plugin which has since been patched. Thank you to Plugin Vulnerabilities for reading and paying close attention to our blog posts. Users of this plugin should update immediately!</p></div><div class="post-author"><div class="author-wrap"><div class="author"><div class="author-avatar"> <a href="https://blog.sucuri.net/author/benmartin" rel="author"> <img alt='' src='https://secure.gravatar.com/avatar/fcf2c7b3195ff9058d29af3b8a49fc43?s=120&#038;d=mm&#038;r=g' srcset='https://secure.gravatar.com/avatar/fcf2c7b3195ff9058d29af3b8a49fc43?s=240&#038;d=mm&#038;r=g 2x' class='avatar avatar-120 photo' height='120' width='120' loading='lazy' decoding='async'/> </a></div><div class="author-description"><h5 class="title-author"> <span class="fn"> <a href="https://blog.sucuri.net/author/benmartin" rel="author"> Ben Martin </a> </span></h5><p class="note">Ben Martin is a security analyst and researcher who joined the company in 2013.
