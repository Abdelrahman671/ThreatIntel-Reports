													<p><a href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2018/03/08080817/EB-YetiJuly2014-Public.pdf" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer"><em>Energetic Bear/Crouching Yeti</em></a><em> is a widely known APT group active since at least 2010. The group tends to attack different companies with a strong focus on the energy and industrial sectors. Companies attacked by Energetic Bear/Crouching Yeti are geographically distributed worldwide with a more obvious concentration in Europe and the US. In 2016-2017, the number of attacks on companies in Turkey increased significantly. </em></p>
<p><em>The main tactics of the group include sending phishing emails with malicious documents and infecting various servers. The group uses some of the infected servers for auxiliary purposes – to host tools and logs. Others are deliberately infected to use them in waterhole attacks in order to reach the group&#8217;s main targets. </em></p>
<p><em>Recent activity of the group against US organizations was discussed in a </em><a href="https://www.us-cert.gov/ncas/alerts/TA18-074A" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer"><em>US-CERT</em></a><em> advisory, which linked the actor to the Russian government, as well as an advisory by the </em><a href="https://www.ncsc.gov.uk/alerts/hostile-state-actors-compromising-uk-organisations-focus-engineering-and-industrial-control" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer"><em>UK National Cyber Security Centre</em></a><em>. </em></p>
<p><em>This report by </em><a href="https://ics-cert.kaspersky.com/" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer"><em>Kaspersky Lab ICS CERT</em></a><em> presents information on identified servers that have been infected and used by the group. The report also includes the findings of an analysis of several webservers compromised by the Energetic Bear group during 2016 and in early 2017</em>.</p>

<h2 id="waterhole">Waterhole</h2>
<p>All waterhole servers are infected following the same pattern: injecting a link into a web page or JS file with the following file scheme: file://IP/filename.png.</p>
<p><a href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2018/04/18124232/180418-energetic-bear-crouching-yeti-1.png" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer" class="magnificImage"><img decoding="async" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-81520" style="max-height: 60vh" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2018/04/18124232/180418-energetic-bear-crouching-yeti-1.png" alt="" /></a></p>
<p>The link is used to initiate a request for an image, as a result of which the user connects to the remote server over the SMB protocol. In this attack type, the attackers&#8217; goal is to extract the following data from the session:</p>
<ul>
<li>user IP,</li>
<li>user name,</li>
<li>domain name,</li>
<li>NTLM hash of the user&#8217;s password.</li>
</ul>
<p>It should be noted that the image requested using the link is not physically located on the remote server.</p>

<p>The sites and servers on this list do not seem to have anything in common. Even though the scanned servers do not necessarily look like potential final victims, it is likely that the attackers scanned different resources to find a server that could be used to establish a foothold for hosting the attackers&#8217; tools and, subsequently, to develop the attack.</p>

<p>Curiously, the sites scanned included a web developer&#8217;s website, kashey.ru, and resources links to which were found on this site. These may have been links to resources developed by the site&#8217;s owner: www.esodedi.ru, www.i-stroy.ru, www.saledoor.ru</p>
<h2 id="toolset-used">Toolset used</h2>
<h3 id="utilities">Utilities</h3>
<p>Utilities found on compromised servers are open-source and publicly available on GitHub:</p>
<ul>
<li>Nmap – an open-source utility for analyzing the network and verifying its security.</li>
<li><a href="https://github.com/maurosoria/dirsearch" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Dirsearch</a> — a simple command-line tool for brute forcing (performing exhaustive searches of) directories and files on websites.</li>
<li><a href="https://github.com/sqlmapproject/sqlmap" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Sqlmap</a> — an open-source penetration testing tool, which automates the process of identifying and exploiting SQL injection vulnerabilities and taking over database servers.</li>
<li><a href="https://github.com/aboul3la/Sublist3r" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Sublist3r</a> — a tool written in Python designed to enumerate website subdomains. The tool uses open-source intelligence (<a href="https://ru.wikipedia.org/wiki/OSINT" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">OSINT</a>). Sublist3r supports many different search engines, such as Google, Yahoo, Bing, Baidu and Ask, as well as such services as Netcraft, Virustotal, ThreatCrowd, DNSdumpster and ReverseDNS. The tool helps penetration testers to collect information on the subdomains of the domain they are researching.</li>
<li><a href="https://github.com/wpscanteam/wpscan" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Wpscan</a> — a WordPress vulnerability scanner that uses the blackbox principle, i.e., works without access to the source code. It can be used to scan remote WordPress sites in search of security issues.</li>
<li><a href="https://github.com/CoreSecurity/impacket" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Impacket</a> — a toolset for working with various network protocols, which is required by SMBTrap.</li>
<li><a href="https://github.com/CylanceSPEAR/SMBTrap" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">SMBTrap</a> — a tool for logging data received over the SMB protocol (user IP address, user name, domain name, password NTLM hash).</li>
<li><a href="https://github.com/commixproject/commix" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Commix</a> — a vulnerability search and command injection and exploitation tool written in Python.</li>
<li><a href="https://github.com/TheRook/subbrute" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Subbrute</a> – a subdomain enumeration tool available for Python and Windows that uses an open name resolver as a proxy and does not send traffic to the target DNS server.</li>
<li><a href="https://github.com/PHPMailer/PHPMailer" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">PHPMailer</a> – a mail sending tool.</li>
</ul>
<p>In addition, a custom Python script named ftpChecker.py was found on one of the servers. The script was designed to check FTP hosts from an incoming list.</p>

<h3 id="modified-sshd">Modified sshd</h3>
<p>A modified sshd with a preinstalled backdoor was found in the process of analyzing the server.</p>
<p>Patches with some versions of backdoors for sshd that are similar to the backdoor found are available on GitHub, for example:</p>
<p><a href="https://github.com/jivoi/openssh-backdoor-kit" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">https://github.com/jivoi/openssh-backdoor-kit</a></p>
<p>Compilation is possible on any OS with binary compatibility.</p>
<p>As a result of replacing the original sshd file with a modified one on the infected server, an attacker can use a &#8216;master password&#8217; to get authorized on the remote server, while leaving minimal traces (compared to an ordinary user connecting via ssh).</p>
<p>In addition, the modified sshd logs all legitimate ssh connections (this does not apply to the connection that uses the &#8216;master password&#8217;), including connection times, account names and passwords. The log is encrypted and is located at /var/tmp/.pipe.sock.</p>
<p><a href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2018/04/18124232/180418-energetic-bear-crouching-yeti-6.png" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer" class="magnificImage"><img decoding="async" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-81520" style="max-height: 60vh" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2018/04/18124232/180418-energetic-bear-crouching-yeti-6.png" alt="" /></a></p>
<h2 id="activity-of-the-attackers-on-compromised-servers">Activity of the attackers on compromised servers</h2>
<p>In addition to using compromised servers to scan numerous resources, other attacker activity was also identified.</p>
<p>After gaining access to the server, the attackers installed the tools they needed at different times. Specifically, the following commands for third-party installations were identified on one of the servers:</p>
<ul>
<li>apt install traceroute</li>
<li>apt-get install nmap</li>
<li>apt-get install screen</li>
<li>git clone https://github.com/sqlmapproject/sqlmap.git</li>
</ul>
<p>Additionally, the attackers installed any packages and tools for Python they needed.</p>
<p>The diagram below shows times of illegitimate logons to one of the compromised servers during one month. The attackers checked the smbtrap log file on working days. In most cases, they logged on to the server at roughly the same time of day, probably in the morning hours:</p>
<p><a href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2018/04/18124231/180418-energetic-bear-crouching-yeti-7.png" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer" class="magnificImage"><img decoding="async" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-81520" style="max-height: 60vh" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2018/04/18124231/180418-energetic-bear-crouching-yeti-7.png" alt="" /></a></p>
<p>In addition, in the process of performing the analysis, an active process was identified that exploited SQL injection and collected data from a database of one of the victims.</p>
<h2 id="conclusion">Conclusion</h2>
<p>The findings of the analysis of compromised servers and the attackers&#8217; activity on these servers are as follows:</p>
<ol>
<li>With rare exceptions, the group&#8217;s members get by with publicly available tools. The use of publicly available utilities by the group to conduct its attacks renders the task of attack attribution without any additional group &#8216;markers&#8217; very difficult.</li>
<li>Potentially, any vulnerable server on the internet is of interest to the attackers when they want to establish a foothold in order to develop further attacks against target facilities.</li>
<li>In most cases that we have observed, the group performed tasks related to searching for vulnerabilities, gaining persistence on various hosts, and stealing authentication data.</li>
<li>The diversity of victims may indicate the diversity of the attackers&#8217; interests.</li>
<li>It can be assumed with some degree of certainty that the group operates in the interests of or takes orders from customers that are external to it, performing initial data collection, the theft of authentication data and gaining persistence on resources that are suitable for the attack&#8217;s further development.</li>
</ol>
