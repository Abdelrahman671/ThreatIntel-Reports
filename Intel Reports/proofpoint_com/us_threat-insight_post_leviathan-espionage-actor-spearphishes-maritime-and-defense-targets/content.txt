    <div class="node-full__body blog-content__body">  <div><strong>Overview</strong></div>

<p>Proofpoint researchers are tracking an espionage actor targeting organizations and high-value targets in defense and government. Active since at least 2014, this actor has long-standing interest in maritime industries, naval defense contractors, and associated research institutions in the United States and Western Europe.</p>

<p>Key takeaways from this research include:</p>

<ul>
	<li><u>Industry targeting</u>: The actor targets defense contractors, universities (particularly those with military research ties), legal organizations [3] and government agencies [3]. The actor has particular interest in naval industries including shipbuilding and related research</li>
	<li><u>Geographical targeting</u>: Targeting includes United States, Western Europe, and South China Sea</li>
	<li><u>Tools</u>: Custom JavaScript <a href="https://www.proofpoint.com/us/threat-reference/malware">malware</a> known as “Orz” and “NanHaiShu”, Cobalt Strike, the SeDll JavaScript loader, and MockDll dll loader</li>
	<li><u>Delivery</u>: Emailed attachments and URLs, often employing a fraudulent lookalike domain and stolen branding</li>
	<li><u>Exploitation</u>: Microsoft Excel and Word documents with macros (sometimes password-protected), very recent vulnerabilities such as <a href="https://cve.mitre.org/cgi-bin/cvename.cgi?name=CVE-2017-0199">CVE-2017-0199</a> and <a href="https://cve.mitre.org/cgi-bin/cvename.cgi?name=CVE-2017-8759">CVE-2017-8759</a>, and malicious Microsoft Publisher files</li>
	<li><u>Installation</u>: JavaScript, JavaScript Scriptlets in XML, HTA, PowerShell, WMI, regsvr32, Squiblydoo</li>
	<li><u>Lateral Movement</u>: The actor sometimes utilizes access at one compromised organization to attack the next. For example, compromised email accounts at one organization were used to send the next wave of malicious attachments to potential victims in the same industry. Similarly the actor attempts to compromise servers within victim organizations and use them for command and control (C&amp;C) for their malware.</li>
</ul>

<p>This blog traces key activities connected to this actor and examines a number of their tools and techniques. Campaigns and details are presented in reverse chronological order to highlight the group’s most recent activities.</p>

<p><strong>Delivery and Exploitation</strong></p>

<p><em>September 2017</em></p>

<p>On September 15 and 19, 2017, Proofpoint detected and blocked <a href="https://www.proofpoint.com/us/threat-reference/spear-phishing">spearphishing </a>emails from this group targeting a US shipbuilding company and a US university research center with military ties. Example emails used the subject “Apply for internship position” and contained an attachment “resume.rtf”. Another attachment, “ARLUAS_FieldLog_2017-08-21.doc” contained a “Torpedo recovery experiment” lure. The attachments exploited CVE-2017-8759 which was discovered and documented only five days prior to the campaign [1].</p>

<p><img alt="Figure 1" src="/sites/default/files/navyf1.png"></p>

<p><em>Figure 1: Example attachment resume.rtf from September 2017 campaign</em></p>

<p><em>August 2017</em></p>

<p>Between August 2 and 4, the actor sent targeted spearphishing emails containing malicious URLs linking to documents to multiple defense contractors. Some of this activity was documented and observed by a fellow researcher [2]. Many of the documents, C&amp;C domains, and payload domains abused the brand of a major provider of ships, submarines, and other vessels with military applications. Some of the documents exploited CVE-2017-0199 to deliver the payload.</p>

<p><img alt="Figure 2" src="/sites/default/files/navyf2.png"></p>

<p><em>Figure 2: One of the documents involved in the campaign used Microsoft licensing lures purporting to be from a well-known shipbuilder (sha256: </em>6f6ee01e9dc2d8c4c260ef4131fe88dc152e53ee8afd3e66e92d4e1bf5fd2e92<em>).</em></p>

<p>Other documents were Microsoft Publisher files that relied on social engineering. The potential victims were lured into starting an embedded PowerPoint presentation, moving the mouse to trigger execution of an embedded JavaScript [5], and then pressing “Enable” in a warning dialog to cause the payload download. The Publisher files were poorly crafted, relied on multiple user interactions, and contained multiple grammatical and typographic errors.</p>

<p><img alt="Figure 3" src="/sites/default/files/navyf3.png"></p>

<p><em>Figure 3: Publisher document delivered via a link in email is in Italian, and is a simple reuse of a student’s<em> </em>work. </em></p>

<p><em>February 2015</em></p>

<p>From February to October of 2015, our colleagues at F-Secure and McAfee reported on campaigns [3][4] by this actor targeting South China Sea interests. During this time, the group utilized Microsoft Excel and Word documents with macros to target the Philippines Department of Justice, APEC organizers, and an international law firm. Targeting of these companies is different from that which we typically observe for this actor; however it still centers around marine and naval issues as related to South China sea politics.</p>

<p><img alt="Figure 4" src="/sites/default/files/navyf4.png"></p>

<p><em>Figure 4: Example attachment “DOJ Staff bonus&nbsp; January 13, 2015.xls”. Similar to this document attachment, most of the attachments in this campaign did not contain meaningful content </em></p>

<p><em>November 2014 </em></p>

<p>The period between November 2014 and January 2015 marked one of the earlier instances in which Proofpoint observed persistent exploitation attempts by this actor. The actor generally emailed Microsoft Excel documents with malicious macros to US universities with military interests, most frequently related to the Navy. The actor also occasionally used macro-laden Microsoft Word documents to target other US research and development organizations with military and intelligence ties during this period.</p>

<p>Emails were often very simple with a greeting and an attachment. On other occasions, it appears that the attackers used highly topical lures based on current events or legitimate documents stolen from previous victims. Lure topics included symposia, the Navy, IT, and relevant research.</p>

<p><img alt="Figure 5" src="/sites/default/files/navyf5.png"></p>

<p><em>Figure 5: Example Excel attachment “2014 Accomplishments Input Template.xls”</em></p>

<p><strong>Installation</strong></p>

<p>The actor continues to:</p>

<ul>
	<li>Innovate and modify the code that accomplishes the installation, while the backdoor code remains more static</li>
	<li>Use scripting languages such as JavaScript, JavaScript Scriptlets, VBScript, and XML</li>
	<li>Use simple obfuscation such as base64, gzip compression, and insertion of garbage characters</li>
	<li>Split functionality of the backdoor &amp; code that establishes persistence for the backdoor into separate files and scripts</li>
</ul>

<p><em>Example 1: Resume.rtf</em></p>

<p>The “resume.rtf” file from the September 19, 2017 attack retrieves the malicious SOAP WSDL definition named “readme.txt “ using an anonymous FTP logon to the attacker’s server.</p>

<p><img alt="Figure 6" src="/sites/default/files/navyf6.png"></p>

<p><em>Figure 6: SOAP WSDL definition (“readme.txt “)</em></p>

<p>This definition in turn downloads a VBScript favicon.ico file, which then creates and runs two JavaScript files in the %TMP% directory:</p>

<p><img alt="Figure 7" src="/sites/default/files/navyf7.png"></p>

<p><em>Figure 7: Code snipped showing VBScript dropping 2 JavaScript files</em></p>

<p>The job of the smaller of the two JavaScripts is to establish a system autostart mechanism. It accomplishes this by deobfuscating another script, link.js, into %TMP%. Link.js in turn creates a shortcut&nbsp; file "Java(TM) Platform SE Auto Updater.lnk" in the "Startup" special folder pointing to the main backdoor JavaScript.</p>

<p><img alt="Figure 8" src="/sites/default/files/navyf8.png"></p>

<p><em>Figure 8: Code for establishing persistence after reboot (autostart mechanism)</em></p>

<p>The job of the larger of the two JavaScripts is to download and execute the Cobalt Strike payload. It accomplishes this by writing more code to rWug5n0PHUFjDFyb8k.js in the temporary directory, which then runs a PowerShell command (obfuscated using garbage characters, base64 encoding, and Gzip compression). The PowerShell is a default Cobalt Strike downloader.</p>

<p><img alt="Figure 9" src="/sites/default/files/navyf9.png"></p>

<p><em>Figure 9: PowerShell code downloading Cobalt Strike</em></p>

<p><em>Example 2: Malicious Microsoft Publisher document</em></p>

<p>The malicious script executed by the Microsoft Publisher file downloads and runs yet another JavaScript file, 0.js, hosted on the attacker-controlled server:</p>

<p><img alt="Figure 10" src="/sites/default/files/navyf10.png"></p>

<p><em>Figure 10: Malicious script executed by the Microsoft Publisher file (sha256: </em><em>305f331bfb1e97028f8c92cbcb1dff2741dcddacc76843e65f9b1ec5a66f52bc)</em></p>

<p>Similar to the previous example (resume.rtf), the 0.js handles the system autostart mechanism via a shortcut file "office 365.lnk" in the "Startup" special folder. However, the shortcut abuses the “Squiblydoo” technique [6]. Moreover, the backdoor is not run directly but via an intermediary SeDll (see below).</p>

<p>0.js also downloads two additional files from the C&amp;C server (green.ddd and green.tmp) The first of these files, green.ddd, is an executable file internally named “SEDll_Win32.dll”. This is a known backdoor used by this actor since 2014 for the same purpose: decrypting and executing the final JavaScript backdoor “Orz”.</p>

<p><strong>Tools</strong></p>

<p><em>NanHaiShu</em></p>

<p>We have observed variants of this JavaScript backdoor used in various campaigns, including those publically reported. The actor continues to improve and refine the malware by, for example, wrapping it inside an HTA wrapper. Several good descriptions are available&nbsp; in analyses from fellow researchers [3][4]. Basic functionality includes:</p>

<ul>
	<li>Information gathering (computer name, user name, serial number, proxy server)</li>
	<li>Downloading from URL</li>
	<li>Executing other JavaScript</li>
	<li>Registry, system, process, directory, file operations</li>
	<li>SafeIE (change IE settings to reduce warnings about about malware activity)</li>
</ul>

<p><img alt="Figure 11" src="/sites/default/files/navyf11.png"></p>

<p><em>Figure 11: Screenshot from 2015 version of the malware dropped by “DOJ Staff bonus January 13, 2015.xls”</em></p>

<p><em>Orz</em></p>

<p>We observed this backdoor in an August 2017 campaign dropped by the Microsoft Publisher files, as well as much earlier in 2014. We named it due to a variable name “orz”, which is changed to “core” in the more recent version. The actor consistently tweaks and improves this backdoor as well. The backdoor is a fairly involved script malware. Its functionality includes:</p>

<ul>
	<li>Information gathering (IE version, OS version, OS 64-bit/32-bit, etc)</li>
	<li>Overwriting registry settings to reduce malware visibility on system</li>
	<li>Download file</li>
	<li>Upload file</li>
	<li>Execute a command with cscript</li>
	<li>Execute JavaScript</li>
	<li>Execute shell command</li>
	<li>Execute a dll (via an embedded ‘MockDll')</li>
	<li>Get proxy info</li>
	<li>Get process list</li>
	<li>Terminate process</li>
	<li>Get drive info</li>
	<li>GET request to a URL</li>
	<li>POST request to a URL</li>
</ul>

<p><img alt="Figure 12" src="/sites/default/files/navyf12.png"></p>

<p><em>Figure 12: Snippet of the Orz backdoor code delivered by the the Microsoft Publisher document. The URL domain is a fraudulent lookalike domain.</em></p>

<p>There is an extensive configuration section at the top of the script. The "jmpUrlList" provides the initial C&amp;C servers, which are used to determine the secondary C&amp;C server as well as additional commands to execute. It is worth noting that the secondary C&amp;C may be the same as the first. We have observed attacker-controlled web servers, compromised victim web servers, and Technet and Pastebin web pages used for the initial C&amp;C.</p>

<p>The initial C&amp;C response is parsed with a regex. The backdoor first looks for the secondary encoded C&amp;C server using the "jmpRegex" regex. Next, the backdoor looks for additional code to execute using the "codeRegex" regex. For additional code, we observed simple code blocks that provide a different upload/download functionality.</p>

<p><img alt="Figure 13" src="/sites/default/files/navyf13.png"></p>

<p><em>Figure 13: The encoded response "vcmQx3ELgTyTyOVSvsm7wrBKwraFw8VFwCuL" in the image above decodes to "hxxp://www.vitaminmain[.]info" which is the secondary C&amp;C server for an older backdoor variant (Decoder provided in Appendix).</em></p>

<p><em>MockDll</em></p>

<p>Some versions of the Orz backdoor have 32- and 64-bit embedded DLLs, stored internally as base64 strings. Their purpose is to simply run another binary. These are used as loaders for future executable payloads, using the well-known process hollowing technique. To use the MockDll, the backdoor creates a configuration .ini file like that shown in Figure 14:</p>

<p><img alt="Figure 14" src="/sites/default/files/navyf14.png"></p>

<p><em>Figure 14: MockDll configuration file</em></p>

<p>mock: defaults to 'regsvr32'</p>

<p>real: the dll, which is the ultimate goal to execute</p>

<p>args: arguments to the dll that will be executed, if any</p>

<p>outf: file in which to write results of the MockDll run</p>

<p>time: timeout defaults to 5</p>

<p>After the configuration file is created, the MockDll is executed with regsvr32. MockDll reads the mentioned .ini config file to determine what to execute. It can log its execution results into a file specified by the “outf” parameter, as shown in Figure 15:</p>

<p><img alt="Figure 15" src="/sites/default/files/navyf15.png"></p>

<p><em>Figure 15: Contents of the log file created by MockDll </em></p>

<p><em>SeDll</em></p>

<p>This DLL is used for decrypting and executing another JavaScript backdoor such as Orz. The DLL is registered by the installer using regsvr32. The DllRegisterServer export is then called, which performs checks on the commandline parameter. If the string “DR” is passed as an argument, or if the DLL is running in the active session with a username that is not “system”, the final JavaScript backdoor is decoded using a custom base64 alphabet. This backdoor has to be present in the same directory as the dll, with a “.tmp” file extension. The backdoor script is then executed using the IActiveScript and IActiveScriptParse32 COM interfaces.</p>

<p><img alt="Figure 16" src="/sites/default/files/navyf16.png"></p>

<p><em>Figure 16: Decoding and executing of the JavaScript backdoor</em></p>

<p>If those conditions are not met, it runs the following command line “"regsvr32 /s \"%s\" DR __CIM__"” to register the DLL, where %s is the path to the DLL. It tries to do this with the current user privileges, but if the privileges cannot be adjusted it defaults to the available execution environment.</p>

<p><em>Cobalt Strike</em></p>

<p>This is a penetration testing tool. The attackers often abuse the free trial version.</p>

<p><strong>Conclusion</strong></p>

<p>This actor, whose espionage activities primarily focus on targets in the US and Western Europe with military ties, has been active since at least 2014. The tools, techniques, and targets consistently connect their work, particular given their attention to naval and maritime defense interests and use of custom backdoors. While defense contractors and academic research centers with military ties should always be cognizant of the potential for cyberattacks, organizations fitting their targeting profiles should be especially wary of legitimate-looking but unsolicited emails from outside entities. Appropriate layered defenses at the firewall, email gateway, and endpoint can all help prevent the kinds of lateral movement we have observed with this actor, as well as the compromise and abuse of systems via which this group expands its attack surface to other organizations.</p>
