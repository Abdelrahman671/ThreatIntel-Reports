<p>Over a recent period, Upstreamâ€™s security platform Secure-D has blocked millions of suspicious mobile transaction requests coming from popular Android Emoji app ai.type. The app has been delivering millions of invisible ads and non-human clicks. While these ads are never seen by the users and do not appear on screen, genuine user data about real views, clicks and purchases are reported to ad networks.</p>
<h2>UNVEILING THE INVESTIGATION</h2>
<p>Secure-D identified and blocked more than 14 million suspicious transaction requests originating from the ai.type keyboard app. These transactions came from more than 110,000 unique devices and if not blocked, would have triggered the purchase of premium digital services, potentially costing users in 13 countries up to $18 million in unwanted charges. Most of the suspicious activity, which is still on-going, took place in Egypt and Brazil.</p>
<p>Ai.type is a customizable on-screen keyboard app for mobile devices that allows end-users to customize the keyboard to their personal preference. It also â€˜learnsâ€™ the userâ€™s writing style over time, anticipating commonly used words and phrases to speed up written composition.</p>
<p>Developed in Israel by ai.type LTD., the app <a href="https://play.google.com/store/apps/details?id=com.aitype.android" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">was available on Google Play</a> until June 2019 when it was removed. It has driven more than 10 million downloads on Google Play store and claims more than 40 million users.</p>

<p>Secure-D recorded a high number of suspicious transactions originating in multiple countries, and all from the same Android application. The source of the transactions, keyboard app ai.type, had been available on Google Play up to July 2019, drawing over 10 million downloads before it was removed.</p>
<p>The strange transaction behavior and the appâ€™s subsequent removal from Google Play prompted researchers to investigate further. Secure-D experts examined two Android devices with the ai.type app installed. <h2>SECURE-Dâ€™s FINDINGS: NON-HUMAN CLICKS &amp; UNWANTED PURCHASES â€“ TECHNICAL DATA</h2>
<p>Static and behavioral analysis showed that the ai.type versions installed on each device contained SDK frameworks with obfuscated hard-coded links back to advertising trackers.</p>
<p>These are servers used by mobile advertising networks to serve and display ads based on their inventory, and track who needs to be paid when a conversion (usually a sale or download) takes place. The SDKs identified by Secure-D are as follows:<br />
â€¢ com.holly.marge<br />

â€¢ com.bear.data<br />
â€¢ defpackage</p>

<p>The initial link for the advertising trackers was hidden, while the messages containing<br />

<p>The SDKs use the advertising trackers to access online ads via a series of re-directions. These included:<br />
â€¢ The host ors.mobtab.net was found hardcoded in the package com.mb.num<br />

<h2>CONCLUSIONS, EFFECTS ON USERS</h2>
<p>Ai.typeâ€™s popularity and useful features have been used to disguise systematic and worrying activity.
