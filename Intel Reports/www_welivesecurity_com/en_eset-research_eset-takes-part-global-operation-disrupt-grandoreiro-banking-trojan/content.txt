    <h1 class="page-headline">ESET takes part in global operation to disrupt the Grandoreiro banking trojan</h1>    <p class="sub-title">ESET provided technical analysis, statistical information, known C&C servers and was able to get a glimpse of the victimology</p>
    
    <div class="article-authors d-flex flex-wrap"><div class="article-author d-flex"><a href="/en/our-experts/eset-research/" title="ESET Research"><picture><source srcset="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/tn/-x45/wls/2022/03/twitter_profile_picture_400x400.png" media="(max-width: 768px)" /><img class="author-image me-3"  src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/tn/-x45/wls/2022/03/twitter_profile_picture_400x400.png" alt="ESET Research"  /></picture></a><div class="author-text"><p><a href="/en/our-experts/eset-research/" title="ESET Research"><b>ESET Research</b></a></p></div></div></div>

    <div class="hero-image-container">
        <picture><source srcset="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/tn/-x266/wls/2023/2024-01/grandoreiro-disruption/grandoreiro-banking-trojan-disruption.jpeg" media="(max-width: 768px)" /><source srcset="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/tn/-x425/wls/2023/2024-01/grandoreiro-disruption/grandoreiro-banking-trojan-disruption.jpeg" media="(max-width: 1120px)" /><img class="hero-image"  src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/tn/-x700/wls/2023/2024-01/grandoreiro-disruption/grandoreiro-banking-trojan-disruption.jpeg" alt="ESET takes part in global operation to disrupt the Grandoreiro banking trojan"  /></picture>    </div>

                    <div class="article-body">
    <p>ESET has collaborated with the Federal Police of Brazil in an attempt to disrupt the Grandoreiro botnet. ESET contributed to the project by providing technical analysis, statistical information, and known command and control (C&amp;C) server domain names and IP addresses. Due to a design flaw in Grandoreiro&rsquo;s network protocol, ESET researchers were also able to get a glimpse into the victimology.</p>
<p>ESET automated systems have processed tens of thousands of Grandoreiro samples. The domain generation algorithm (DGA) the malware has used since around October 2020 produces one main domain, and optionally several failsafe domains, per day. The DGA is the only way Grandoreiro knows how to report to a C&amp;C server. Besides the current date, the DGA accepts static configuration as well &ndash; we have observed 105 such configurations as of this writing.</p>
<p>Grandoreiro&rsquo;s operators have abused cloud providers such as Azure and AWS to host their network infrastructure. ESET researchers provided data crucial to identifying the accounts responsible for setting up these servers. Further investigation performed by the Federal Police of Brazil led to the <a href="https://www.gov.br/pf/pt-br/assuntos/noticias/2024/01/pf-combate-organizacao-criminosa-que-praticava-fraudes-bancarias-eletronicas-contra-vitimas-no-exterior" target="_blank" rel="noopener">identification and arrest</a> of the individuals in control of these servers. In this blogpost, we look at how we obtained the data to assist law enforcement to execute this disruption operation.</p>
<h2>Background</h2>
<p><a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/2020/04/28/grandoreiro-how-engorged-can-exe-get/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Grandoreiro</a> is one of many <a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/2021/12/15/dirty-dozen-latin-america-amavaldo-zumanek/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Latin American banking trojans</a>. It has been active since at least 2017 and ESET researchers have been closely tracking it ever since. Grandoreiro targets Brazil and Mexico, and since 2019 Spain as well (see Figure 1). While Spain was the most targeted country between 2020 and 2022, in 2023 we observed a clear switch of focus towards Mexico and Argentina, the latter being new to Grandoreiro.</p>
<figure class="image"><img title="Figure 1. Grandoreiro detection rate (data since January 2020)" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2023/2024-01/grandoreiro-disruption/grandoreiro-figure-01-heatmap.png" alt="Grandoreiro-Figure-01-Heatmap" width="" height="" />
<figcaption><em>Figure 1. Grandoreiro detection rate (data since January 2020)</em></figcaption>
</figure>
<p>Functionality-wise, Grandoreiro hasn&rsquo;t changed very much since our last <a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/2020/04/28/grandoreiro-how-engorged-can-exe-get/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">blogpost in 2020</a>. We offer a brief overview of the malware in this section and dive into the few changes, mainly new DGA logic, later.</p>
<p>When a Latin American banking trojan successfully compromises a machine, it usually issues an HTTP GET request to a remote server, sending some basic information about the compromised machine. While older Grandoreiro builds implemented this feature, over time, the developers decided to drop it.</p>
<p>Grandoreiro periodically monitors the foreground window to find one that belongs to a web browser process. When such a window is found and its name matches any string from a hardcoded list of bank-related strings, then and only then the malware initiates communication with its C&amp;C server, sending requests at least once a second until terminated.</p>
<p>The operator has to interact manually with the compromised machine in order to steal a victim&rsquo;s money. The malware allows:</p>
<ul>
<li>blocking the screen of the victim,</li>
<li>logging keystrokes,</li>
<li>simulating mouse and keyboard activity,</li>
<li>sharing the victim&rsquo;s screen, and</li>
<li>displaying fake pop-up windows.</li>
</ul>
<p>Grandoreiro undergoes rapid and constant development. Occasionally, we even saw several new builds a week, making it difficult to keep track. To demonstrate, in February 2022, Grandoreiro&rsquo;s operators added a version identifier to the binaries. In Figure 2 we show how quickly the version identifier changed. On average, it was a new version every four days between February 2022 and June 2022. In the month-long gap between May 24<sup>th</sup>, 2022 and June 22<sup>nd</sup>, 2022 we continued to see new samples with progressing PE compilation times, but they lacked the version identifier. On June 27<sup>th</sup>, 2022 the version identifier changed to <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">V37</span> and we haven&rsquo;t seen it change since then, leaving us to conclude that this feature was dropped.</p>
<figure class="image"><img style="width: 50%; margin: 0 auto; display: block;" title="" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2023/2024-01/grandoreiro-disruption/grandoreiro-figure-02-timeline.png" alt="Grandoreiro-Figure-02-Timeline" width="" height="" />
<figcaption><em>Figure 2. Grandoreiro version history between February and June 2022</em></figcaption>
</figure>
<p>Latin American banking trojans <a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/ESET_LATAM_financial_cybercrime.pdf" target="_blank" rel="noopener">share a lot of commonalities</a>. Grandoreiro is similar to other Latin American banking trojans mainly by the obvious core functionality and in bundling its downloaders within MSI installers. In the past, we have observed a few cases where its downloaders were shared with <a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/2020/08/13/mekotio-these-arent-the-security-updates-youre-looking-for/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Mekotio</a> and <a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/2021/01/21/vadokrist-wolf-sheeps-clothing/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Vadokrist</a>, though not in the last two years. The Grandoreiro banking trojan&rsquo;s main distinction from the other families had been its unique binary padding mechanism that massively engorges the final executable (described in our <a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/2020/04/28/grandoreiro-how-engorged-can-exe-get/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">blogpost in 2020</a>). Over time, Grandoreiro&rsquo;s operators added this anti-analysis technique to its downloaders as well. To our surprise, in Q3 2023, this feature was completely dropped from the banking trojan and downloader binaries and we haven&rsquo;t observed it since.</p>
<p>Since February 2022, we have been tracking a <a href="https://twitter.com/ESETresearch/status/1494249519030018050" target="_blank" rel="noopener">second variant</a> of Grandoreiro that differs significantly from the main one. We saw it, in small campaigns, in March, May, and June 2022. Based on the vast majority of its C&amp;C server domains not resolving, its core features changing quite often, and its network protocol not functioning properly, we strongly believe it is a work in progress; hence we will focus on the main variant in this blogpost.</p>
<h2>Grandoreiro long-term tracking</h2>
<p>ESET systems designed for automated, long-term tracking of selected malware families have been monitoring Grandoreiro since the end of 2017, extracting version information, C&amp;C servers, targets and, since the end of 2020, DGA configurations.</p>
<h3>DGA tracking</h3>
<p>The DGA configuration is hardcoded in the Grandoreiro binary. Each configuration can be referred to by a string we call <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">dga_id</span>. Using different configurations for the DGA yields different domains. We dive deeper into the DGA mechanism later in the text.</p>
<p>ESET has extracted a total of 105 different <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">dga_ids</span> from the Grandoreiro samples known to us. 79 of these configurations at least once generated a domain that resolved to an active C&amp;C server IP address during the course of our tracking.</p>
<p>The generated domains are registered via No-IP&rsquo;s Dynamic DNS service (DDNS). Grandoreiro&rsquo;s operators abuse the service to frequently change their domains to correspond with the DGA and to change IP addresses at will. The vast majority of the IP addresses these domains resolve to are provided by cloud providers, mainly AWS and Azure. Table 1 illustrates some statistics about IP addresses used for Grandoreiro C&amp;C servers.</p>

<p>In this section, we focus on what we&rsquo;ve found by querying the C&amp;C servers. All the statistical data listed in this section has been obtained directly from Grandoreiro C&amp;C servers, not from ESET telemetry.</p>
<h4>Old samples are still active</h4>
<p>Each <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">login_string</span> we observed contains the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">version_string</span> and the vast majority of those contain the timestamp information (see Table 3 and Table 4). While a lot of them contain only day and month, as seems to be the developer&rsquo;s choice occassionally, the oldest communicating sample was timestamped <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">15/09/2020</span> &ndash; that is from the time this DGA was first introduced to Grandoreiro. The most recent sample was timestamped <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">12/23/2023</span>.</p>

<h4>Number of victims</h4>
<p>We have observed that the average number of victims connected in a day is 563. However, this number certainly contains duplicates, because if a victim stays connected for a long time, which we&rsquo;ve observed is often the case, then the Grandoreiro C&amp;C server will report it on multiple requests.</p>
<p>Trying to address this issue, we defined a <em>unique</em> victim as one with a unique set of identifying characteristics (like computer name, username, etc.) while omitting those that are subject to change (like uptime). With that, we ended up with 551 <em>unique</em> victims connected in a day on average.</p>
<p>Taking into account that we have observed victims who were connecting to the C&amp;C servers constantly for over a year&rsquo;s period, we calculated an average number of 114 <em>new unique</em> victims connecting to the C&amp;C servers each day. We came to this number by disregarding <em>unique</em> victims that we have already observed before.</p>
<h2>Grandoreiro internals</h2>
<p>Let us focus, in depth, on the two most crucial features of Grandoreiro: the DGA and the network protocol.</p>
<h3>DGA</h3>
<p>Grandoreiro&rsquo;s operators have implemented several kinds of DGAs over the years, with the most recent one appearing in July 2020. While we noticed a few minor changes, the core of the algorithm hasn&rsquo;t change since.</p>
<p>The DGA utilizes a specific configuration that is hardcoded in the binary, stored as multiple strings. Figure 6 displays one such configuration (with <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">dga_id</span> &ldquo;bbj&rdquo;), reformatted in JSON for better readability.</p>
<figure class="image"><img title="Figure 6. Grandoreiro DGA configuration, reformatted in JSON" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2023/2024-01/grandoreiro-disruption/grandoreiro-figure-06-dgaconfig.png" alt="Grandoreiro-Figure-06-DgaConfig" width="" height="" />
<figcaption><em>Figure 6. Grandoreiro DGA configuration, reformatted in JSON</em></figcaption>
</figure>
<p>In the vast majority of cases, the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">base_domain</span> field is <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">freedynamicdns.org</span> or <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">zapto.org</span>. As already mentioned, Grandoreiro utilizes No-IP for its domain registration. The <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">base64_alpha</span> field corresponds to the custom base64 alphabet the DGA uses. The <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">month_substitution</span> is used to substitute a month number for a character.</p>
<p>The <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">dga_table</span> forms the main part of the configuration. It consists of 12 strings, each with 35 fields delimited by |. The first entry of each line is the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">dga_id</span>. The second and last entry represent the month the line is intended for. The remaining 32 fields each represent a value for a different day of the month (leaving at least one field unused).</p>
<p>The logic of the DGA is shown in Figure 7. The algorithm first selects the correct line and the correct entry from it, treating it as a four-byte key. It then formats the current date into a string and encrypts it with the key using a simple XOR. It then prepends the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">dga_id</span> to the result, encodes the result using base64 with a custom alphabet, and then removes any = padding characters. The final result is the subdomain that, together with <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">base_domain</span>, is to be used as the C&amp;C server for the current day. The part highlighted in red is a failsafe mechanism and we discuss it next.</p>
<figure class="image"><img title="Figure 7. Grandoreiro DGA computation reimplemented in Python" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2023/2024-01/grandoreiro-disruption/grandoreiro-figure-07-dgaalgo.png" alt="Grandoreiro-Figure-07-DgaAlgo" width="" height="" />
<figcaption><em>Figure 7. Grandoreiro DGA computation reimplemented in Python</em></figcaption>
</figure>
<p>Grandoreiro has implemented, in some builds, a failsafe mechanism for when the main domain fails to resolve. This mechanism is not present in all builds and its logic has changed a few times, but the basic idea is illustrated in Figure 7. It uses a configuration that is constant in the samples we analyzed and can be generated by the simple code shown in Figure 8. Each entry consists of a key, a prefix, and a base domain.</p>
<p>The failsafe algorithm takes a part of the main C&amp;C subdomain. It then iterates over all configuration entries, encrypts it using XOR and prepends a prefix, similar to the main algorithm part.</p>
<figure class="image"><img title="Figure 8. Failsafe DGA configuration generator reimplemented in Python" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2023/2024-01/grandoreiro-disruption/grandoreiro-figure-08-dgaconfigbackup.png" alt="Grandoreiro-Figure-08-DgaConfigBackup" width="" height="" />
<figcaption><em>Figure 8. Failsafe DGA configuration generator reimplemented in Python</em></figcaption>
</figure>
<p>Since September 2022, we have started to observe samples that utilize a slightly modified DGA. The algorithm remains almost identical, but rather than base64 encoding the subdomain in the final step, a hardcoded prefix is prepended to it. Based on our tracking, this method has become the dominant one since approximately July 2023.</p>
<h3>Network protocol</h3>
<p>Grandoreiro utilizes RTC Portal, a set of Delphi components built on top of the <a href="https://rtc.teppi.net/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">RealThinClient SDK</a> which is built on top of HTTP(S). The RTC Portal was <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20171221161334/http:/www.realthinclient.com/rtc-portal-discontinued-and-released-on-github/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">discontinued in 2017</a> and its source code published on <a href="https://github.com/CloudDelphi/RTC-Portal-VCL" target="_blank" rel="noopener">GitHub</a>. Essentially, RTC Portal allows one or more Controls to remotely access one or more Hosts. Hosts and Controls are separated by a mediator component called Gateway.</p>
<p>Grandoreiro operators use a console (acting as the Control) to connect to the C&amp;C server (acting as Gateway) and to communicate with the compromised machines (acting as Hosts). To connect to Gateway, three parameters are required: a secret key, the key length, and a login.</p>
<p>The secret key is used to encrypt the initial request sent to the server. Therefore, the server also needs to know the secret key so as to decrypt the initial client request.</p>
<p>The key length determines the length of the keys to encrypt the traffic, established during the handshake. The traffic is encrypted using a custom stream cipher. Two different keys are established &ndash; one for inbound and one for outbound traffic.</p>
<p>The login can be any string. The Gateway requires each connected component to have a unique login.</p>
<p>Grandoreiro uses two different combinations of secret key and key length values, always hardcoded in the binary, and we already discussed the <span style="font-family: courier new, courier, monospace;">login_string</span> that is used as the login.</p>
<p>The RTC documentation states that it can only handle a limited number of connections at once. Considering that each connected Host needs to send at least one request per second or else its connection is dropped, we believe that the reason Grandoreiro utilizes multiple C&amp;C servers is an attempt not to overwhelm any one of them.</p>
<h2>Conclusion</h2>
<p>In this blogpost, we have provided a peek behind the curtain of our long-term tracking of Grandoreiro that helped to make this disruption operation possible. We have described in depth how Grandoreiro&rsquo;s DGA works, how many different configurations exist simultaneously, and how we were able to spot many IP address overlaps among them.</p>
<p>We have also provided statistical information obtained from the C&amp;C servers. This information provides an excellent overview of the victimology and targeting, while also allowing us to see the actual level of impact.</p>
<p>The disruption operation led by the Federal Police of Brazil aimed at individuals who are believed to be high up in the Grandoreiro operation hierarchy. ESET will continue to track <a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/2021/12/15/dirty-dozen-latin-america-amavaldo-zumanek/">other Latin American banking trojans</a> while closely monitoring for any Grandoreiro activity following this disruption operation.</p>
