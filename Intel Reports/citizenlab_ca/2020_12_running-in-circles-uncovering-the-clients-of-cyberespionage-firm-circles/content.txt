                <section itemprop="articleBody" class="article-body mb4 mt4 pt2 bt b--light-gray">
                  <!DOCTYPE html PUBLIC "-//W3C//DTD HTML 4.0 Transitional//EN" "http://www.w3.org/TR/REC-html40/loose.dtd">
<html><body><h2 id="summary-key-findings" class=" lh-solid mb3"><strong style="">Summary &amp; Key Findings</strong></h2>
<ul class="mt0">
<li class="mt2"><em>Circles</em> is a surveillance firm that reportedly exploits weaknesses in the global mobile phone system to snoop on calls, texts, and the location of phones around the globe. Circles is affiliated with NSO Group, which develops the <a href="https://citizenlab.ca/2019/10/nso-q-cyber-technologies-100-new-abuse-cases/" class="pointer"><u>oft-abused</u></a> Pegasus spyware.</li>
<li class="mt2">Circles, whose products work without hacking the phone itself, says they sell only to nation-states. According to <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20190409205521/https://www.alaraby.co.uk/file/get/4749a75b-fc46-4917-85d3-7f8b41d4b34a" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><u>leaked documents</u></a>, Circles customers can purchase a system that they connect to their local telecommunications companies&rsquo; infrastructure, or can use a separate system called the &ldquo;Circles Cloud,&rdquo; which interconnects with telecommunications companies around the world.</li>
<li class="mt2">According to the U.S. Department of Homeland Security, all U.S. wireless networks are vulnerable to the types of weaknesses reportedly exploited by Circles. A majority of networks around the globe are similarly vulnerable.</li>
<li class="mt2">Using Internet scanning, we found a unique signature associated with the hostnames of Check Point firewalls used in Circles deployments. This scanning enabled us to identify Circles deployments in at least 25 countries.</li>
<li class="mt2">We determine that the governments of the following countries are likely Circles customers: Australia, Belgium, Botswana, Chile, Denmark, Ecuador, El Salvador, Estonia, Equatorial Guinea, Guatemala, Honduras, Indonesia, Israel, Kenya, Malaysia, Mexico, Morocco, Nigeria, Peru, Serbia, Thailand, the United Arab Emirates (UAE), Vietnam, Zambia, and Zimbabwe.</li>
<li class="mt2">Some of the specific government branches we identify with varying degrees of confidence as being Circles customers have a history of leveraging digital technology for human rights abuses. In a few specific cases, we were able to attribute the deployment to a particular customer, such as the Security Operations Command (ISOC) of the Royal Thai Army, which has allegedly tortured detainees.</li>
</ul>
<h2 id="h.rwibz0ny2hf6" class="c34 c14 lh-solid mb3"><span class="c2" style="">1. Background</span></h2>
<p class="mt0">The public discussion around surveillance and tracking largely focuses on well known technical means, such as targeted hacking and network interception. However, other forms of surveillance are regularly and extensively used by governments and third parties to engage in cross-border surveillance and monitoring.</p>
<p>One of the widest-used&mdash;but least appreciated&mdash;is the leveraging of weaknesses in the global mobile telecommunications infrastructure to monitor and intercept phone calls and traffic.</p>
<p>While well-resourced governments have long had the ability to conduct such activity, in recent years companies have emerged to sell these capabilities. For example, <em>the Guardian</em> <a href="https://www.theguardian.com/world/2020/mar/29/revealed-saudis-suspected-of-phone-spying-campaign-in-us" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><u>reported</u></a> in March 2020 that Saudi Arabia appeared to be &ldquo;exploiting weaknesses in the global mobile telecommunications network to track citizens as they travel around the US.&rdquo; Other investigative reports indicated that journalists, dissidents, and opposition politicians in <a href="https://www.premiumtimesng.com/investigationspecial-reports/204987-investigation-governors-dickson-okowa-spend-billions-high-tech-spying-opponents-others.html" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><u>Nigeria</u></a> and <a href="https://nomada.gt/pais/la-corrupcion-no-es-normal/espionaje-ilegal-del-gobierno-aqui-esta-la-investigacion-de-nuestro-diario-parte-i/" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><u>Guatemala</u></a> were similarly targeted.</p>
<p>Abuse of the global telephone system for tracking and monitoring is believed to be widespread, however it is difficult to investigate. When a device is tracked&mdash;or messages intercepted&mdash;there are not necessarily any traces on the target&rsquo;s device for researchers or investigators to find. Meanwhile, cellular carriers have many technical difficulties identifying and blocking abuses of their infrastructure.</p>
<h3 id="h.pbk2qm7xyl55" class="c28 c14 lh-solid mb3"><span class="c2" style="">SS7 Attacks</span></h3>
<p class="mt0">Signaling System 7 (SS7) is a protocol suite developed in 1975 for exchanging information and routing phone calls between different wireline telecommunications companies. At the time of SS7&rsquo;s development, the global phone network consisted of a small club of monopolistic telecommunications operators. Because these companies generally trusted each other, SS7 designers saw no pressing need to include authentication or access control. However, the advent of telecommunications deregulation and mobile technology soon began to challenge the assumption of trust. Even so, SS7 endured, thanks to a desire to maintain interoperability with older equipment.</p>
<p>Because of SS7&rsquo;s lack of authentication, any attacker that interconnects with the SS7 network (such as an intelligence agency, a cybercriminal <a href="https://www.theverge.com/2017/6/13/15794292/ss7-hack-dark-web-tap-phone-texts-cyber-crime" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><u>purchasing SS7 access</u></a>, or a surveillance firm running a <a href="https://www.intelligenceonline.com/surveillance--interception/2015/12/02/circles---mobile-phone--company-intercepts-3g,108114286-art" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><u>fake phone company</u></a>) can send commands to a subscriber&rsquo;s &ldquo;home network&rdquo; falsely indicating that the subscriber is roaming. These commands allow the attacker to track the victim&rsquo;s location, and intercept voice calls and SMS text messages. Such capabilities could also be used to intercept codes used for two-factor authentication sent via SMS. It is challenging and expensive for telecommunications operators to distinguish malicious traffic from benign behavior, making these attacks tricky to block.</p>
<p>Today, SS7 is predominantly used in 2G and 3G mobile networks (4G networks use the newer <em>Diameter</em> protocol). One of SS7&rsquo;s key functions in these networks is handling <em>roaming</em>, where a subscriber to a &ldquo;home network&rdquo; can connect to a different &ldquo;visited network,&rdquo; such as when traveling internationally. In this situation, SS7 is used to handle forwarding of phone calls and SMS text messages to the &ldquo;visited network.&rdquo; Although 4G&rsquo;s Diameter protocol includes features for authentication and access control, these <a href="https://www.riverpublishers.com/journal_read_html_article.php?j=JICTS/5/1/3" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><u>are optional</u></a>. Additionally, the need for Diameter networks to interconnect with SS7 networks also <a href="https://www.computer.org/csdl/proceedings-article/ifip-networking/2016/07497239/12OmNxzMnQD" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><u>introduces security issues</u></a>. There is widespread concern that 5G technology and other advances will <a href="https://slideplayer.com/slide/14231614/" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><u>inherit the risks</u></a> of these older systems.</p>
<h3 id="h.navaptmkz2ox" class="c28 c14 lh-solid mb3"><span class="c2" style="">Circles</span></h3>
<p class="mt0">While companies selling exploitation of the global cellular system tend to operate in secrecy, one company has emerged as a known player: Circles. The company was reportedly <a href="https://www.haaretz.com/israel-news/.premium.MAGAZINE-israel-s-cyber-spy-industry-aids-dictators-hunt-dissidents-and-gays-1.6573027" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><u>founded in 2008</u></a>, <a href="https://www.forbes.com/sites/thomasbrewster/2019/08/05/a-multimillionaire-surveillance-dealer-steps-out-of-the-shadows-and-his-9-million-whatsapp-hacking-van/#4be8195b31b7" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><u>acquired</u></a> in 2014 by Francisco Partners, and then <a href="https://www.forbes.com/sites/thomasbrewster/2019/08/05/a-multimillionaire-surveillance-dealer-steps-out-of-the-shadows-and-his-9-million-whatsapp-hacking-van/#3501f931b71c" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><u>merged</u></a> with NSO Group. Circles is known for <a href="https://www.vice.com/en/article/ep48kp/nso-group-cyprus-circles-bulgaria-ss7" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><u>selling systems</u></a> to exploit SS7 vulnerabilities, and <a href="https://forensicnews.net/2020/04/29/the-covert-reach-of-nso-group/" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><u>claims</u></a> to sell this technology exclusively to nation-states.</p>
<p>Unlike NSO Group&rsquo;s Pegasus spyware, the SS7 mechanism by which Circles&rsquo; product reportedly operates does not have an obvious signature on a target&rsquo;s phone, such as the telltale targeting SMS bearing a malicious link that is sometimes present on a phone targeted with Pegasus.</p>
<p>Most investigation of Circles has relied on inside sources and open source intelligence, rather than technical analysis. For example, a 2016 investigation by Nigerian newspaper <em>Premium Times</em> <a href="https://www.premiumtimesng.com/investigationspecial-reports/204987-investigation-governors-dickson-okowa-spend-billions-high-tech-spying-opponents-others.html" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><u>reported that</u></a> two state governors in Nigeria acquired Circles systems and used them to spy on political opponents. In one case, the system was installed at the residence of a governor. Our scanning found two Circles systems in Nigeria (<strong>Section 4</strong>).</p>
<p>Documents filed as part of a lawsuit against NSO Group in Israel <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20190409205521/https://www.alaraby.co.uk/file/get/4749a75b-fc46-4917-85d3-7f8b41d4b34a" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><u>purport to show</u></a> emails exchanged between Circles and several customers in the UAE. Most famously, the documents <a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2018/08/31/world/middleeast/hacking-united-arab-emirates-nso-group.html" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><u>show</u></a> Circles sending targets&rsquo; locations and phone records (<a href="https://www.onsip.com/voip-resources/voip-fundamentals/what-are-call-detail-records-cdrs" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><u>Call Detail Records</u></a> or CDRs) to the UAE Supreme Council on National Security (SCNS), apparently as part of a product demonstration. The emails also indicate that intercepting phone calls of a foreign target has a higher chance of success when the target is roaming.</p>
<figure class="center mw-100 ba b--light-gray" style="width:1244px;"><div class="tc pa2 bg-white"><a href="https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/webpc-passthru.php?src=https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2020/12/Figure-1-The-logo-of-Circles.jpg&amp;nocache=1" class="pointer"><img fetchpriority="high" decoding="async" class="size-full wp-image-74573" src="https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/webpc-passthru.php?src=https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2020/12/Figure-1-The-logo-of-Circles.jpg&amp;nocache=1" alt="" width="1244" height="526" title="Running in Circles: Uncovering the Clients of Cyberespionage Firm Circles 1"></a></div><figcaption class="f5-ns f6 black-70 pa2 bg-light-gray">Figure 1: The logo of Circles.</figcaption></figure>

The SCNS was set to receive two separate systems: a standalone system that could be used for local interceptions and a separate system connected to the &ldquo;Circles Cloud&rdquo; (an entity with roaming agreements around the world) that could be used for interceptions outside of the UAE if desired<span class="c3">.</span></p>

<p>In 2015, IntelligenceOnline <a href="https://www.intelligenceonline.com/surveillance--interception/2015/12/02/circles---mobile-phone--company-intercepts-3g,108114286-art" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><u>suggested</u></a> that Circles started a bogus phone company called &ldquo;Circles Bulgaria&rdquo; to facilitate interceptions around the world. More recently, a <a href="https://forensicnews.net/2020/04/29/the-covert-reach-of-nso-group/" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><u>2020 report</u></a> by Forensic News raised questions as to the true business of <em>FloLive</em>, purportedly an &ldquo;IoT connectivity&rdquo; company. Forensic News found that <em>FloLive</em> appeared to be closely associated with Circles, and suggested that the company might be a &ldquo;front for the hackers and private spies behind Circles.&rdquo;</p>
<p>There is also limited information about how the Circles system integrates with NSO Group&rsquo;s flagship Pegasus spyware, though a former NSO Group employee told <a href="https://www.vice.com/en/article/ep48kp/nso-group-cyprus-circles-bulgaria-ss7" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><u>Motherboard</u></a> that Pegasus had an &ldquo;awful integration with Circles,&rdquo; and that Circles had &ldquo;exaggerated their system&rsquo;s abilities.&rdquo;</p>
<h2 id="h.lox242brs9gx" class="c34 c14 lh-solid mb3"><span class="c2 c22" style="">2. Fingerprinting &amp; Scanning for Circles</span></h2>
<p class="mt0">While searching <a href="https://shodan.io/" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><u>Shodan</u></a>, we observed interesting results in AS200068, a block of IP addresses registered to Circles Bulgaria (<strong>Figure 2</strong>). These results show hostnames of firewalls manufactured by Check Point, as well as the hostnames of the firewalls&rsquo; <em>SmartCenter</em> instance. SmartCenter can be used to <a href="https://usercenter.checkpoint.com/uc/pmap/partners/resources/smartcenter/" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><u>centrally manage</u></a> multiple Check Point firewalls.<a id="fnref1" class="pointer" role="doc-noteref" href="#fn1"><sup>1</sup></a></p>
<figure class="center mw-100 ba b--light-gray" style="width:1032px;"><div class="tc pa2 bg-white"><a href="https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/webpc-passthru.php?src=https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2020/12/Cicles-Figure-2.png&amp;nocache=1" class="pointer"><img decoding="async" class="size-full wp-image-74574" src="https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/webpc-passthru.php?src=https://citizenlab.ca/wp-content/uploads/2020/12/Cicles-Figure-2.png&amp;nocache=1" alt="" width="1032" height="696" title="Running in Circles: Uncovering the Clients of Cyberespionage Firm Circles 2"></a></div><figcaption class="f5-ns f6 black-70 pa2 bg-light-gray">Figure 2: Shodan probe results for Check Point firewalls in AS200068 (Circles Bulgaria Ltd).</figcaption></figure>
<p>The SmartCenter hostnames in the Circles-registered AS200068 contain the domain name <em>tracksystem.info</em>. It seems clear that <em>tracksystem.info</em> is associated with Circles, as <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20190409205521/https://www.alaraby.co.uk/file/get/4749a75b-fc46-4917-85d3-7f8b41d4b34a" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><u>leaked documents</u></a> show Circles employees communicating from <em>@tracksystem.info</em> email addresses. Additionally, per RiskIQ, 17 of the 37 IP addresses pointed to by <em>tracksystem.info</em> or its subdomains are in AS200068 as well as AS60097, also registered to Circles Bulgaria.</p>
<p>We searched for Check Point firewalls whose SmartCenter hostname contained <em>tracksystem.info</em> on <a href="https://www.shodan.io/" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><u>Shodan</u></a>, <a href="https://censys.io/" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><u>Censys</u></a>, <a href="http://fofa.so/" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><u>Fofa</u></a>, and on Rapid7&rsquo;s historical<em> <a href="https://opendata.rapid7.com/sonar.ssl/" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><u>sonar-ssl</u></a> </em>dataset. We also searched for IPs that returned peculiar &ldquo;random&rdquo; TLS certificates<a id="fnref2" class="pointer" role="doc-noteref" href="#fn2"><sup>2</sup></a> matching the following regular expression, as we saw these certificates returned by Check Point firewalls with <em>tracksystem.info</em> in their SmartCenter hostnames:</p>
<div class="f5 bg-washed-yellow pa2 ba b--gray code">/^C=<span class="c31 c42">[a-zA-Z0-9]{2}</span><span class="c21">, ST=[a-zA-Z0-9]{3}, L=[a-zA-Z0-9]{3}, O=[a-zA-Z0-9]{4}, OU=[a-zA-Z0-9]{5}, CN=localhost$/</span></div>
<p>Overall, we identified 252 IP addresses in 50 ASNs matching our fingerprints. Many had a &ldquo;Firewall Host&rdquo; field seemingly indicating that the systems were client systems, e.g., <em>client-circles-thailand-nsb-node-2</em>, though some used the word <em>telco</em> in place of <em>client</em>, and some had a generic name rather than a client name, e.g., <em>cf-00-182-1</em>. In cases where we identified Circles&rsquo; Check Point firewalls on a <a href="https://www.caida.org/data/as-classification/" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><u>Transit/Access ISP</u></a> (i.e., a non-datacenter ISP), we assumed that some agency of that country&rsquo;s government was a customer of Circles.</p>
<p>Some of the clients that we identified have two-word nicknames, where the first word is a car brand that almost always shares the same first letter as the country or state of the apparent customer. For example, Circles firewalls whose IPs geolocate to Mexico are named &ldquo;Mercedes,&rdquo; those that geolocate to Thailand are named &ldquo;Toyota,&rdquo; those that geolocate to Abu Dhabi are named &ldquo;Aston,&rdquo; and those that geolocate to Dubai are named &ldquo;Dutton.&rdquo;</p>
<p>The use of car brands to refer to clients was first <a href="https://www.haaretz.com/middle-east-news/.premium-with-israel-s-encouragement-nso-sold-spyware-to-uae-and-other-gulf-states-1.9093465" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><u>reported by</u></a> Haaretz, though the report indicated that this was an NSO Group practice, as opposed to Circles. Haaretz reported the following codenames: Saudi Arabia is &ldquo;Subaru,&rdquo; Bahrain is &ldquo;BMW,&rdquo; and Jordan is &ldquo;Jaguar.&rdquo; Our scans did not reveal any Check Point firewalls linked to Circles with the names <em>Subaru</em> or <em>Jaguar</em>, though we did identify firewalls with the name &ldquo;BMW&rdquo; located in Belgium.</p>

<h4 id="h.1qhmpwgot0q9" class="c30 c14 lh-solid mb3"><span class="c9 c2" style="">Surveillance Abuses in Botswana</span></h4>
<p class="mt0">There are multiple recent reports of the abuse of surveillance equipment in Botswana to suppress reporting and public awareness of governmental corruption. In 2014, it was reported that the DISS participated in using surveillance and jamming technology developed by <a href="https://www.sundaystandard.info/khama-kgosi-network-of-shady-intelligence-security-big-shots-has-diss-over-a-barrel/" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><u>Elbit Systems</u></a> to conduct &ldquo;<a href="https://www.sundaystandard.info/diss-mi-launch-electronic-warfare-attacks-against-private-media/" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><u>electronic warfare</u></a>&rdquo; against the media. In addition, the DISS has reportedly engaged in attempts to compromise the privacy of relationships between <a href="https://www.sundaystandard.info/diss-mi-launch-electronic-warfare-attacks-against-private-media/" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><u>sources and reporters</u></a>.</p>
<h3 id="chile" class=" lh-solid mb3"><strong style="">Chile</strong></h3>
<p class="mt0">Our scanning identified what appeared to be a single Circles system in Chile, codename <strong>Cadillac Polaris</strong>. <h3 id="h.nemhb8j6afxs" class="c28 c14 lh-solid mb3"><span class="c2" style="">Mexico</span></h3>

Additional details about the Mexico Circles systems are in <strong><span class="c2">Appendix A</span></strong>.</p>
<p>Reporting has previously connected the Mexican government to the purchase of other SS7 surveillance equipment, such as <a href="https://www.forbes.com/sites/thomasbrewster/2017/09/25/mexico-surveillance-5m-ability-inc-unlimited-ss7-surveillance-technology/?sh=77851a525432" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><u>ULIN made by Ability</u></a>, as well as a <a href="https://resources.infosecinstitute.com/topic/ss7-protocol-how-hackers-might-find-you/" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><u>system codenamed SkyLock</u></a> sold by Verint Systems Inc.</p>
<h4 id="surveillance-abuses-in-mexico" class=" lh-solid mb3"><strong style="">Surveillance Abuses in Mexico</strong></h4>
<p class="mt0">Mexico has an extensive history of surveillance abuses. Notably, our prior research has shown that entities within Mexico&rsquo;s government serially <a href="https://citizenlab.ca/2019/03/nso-spyware-slain-journalists-wife/" class="pointer"><u>abused NSO Group&rsquo;s Pegasus spyware</u></a> to target over 25 reporters, human rights defenders, and the families of individuals killed and disappeared by cartels. The pattern of abuses extends to other forms of digital surveillance.</p>
<p>Human rights organizations have documented that Mexico&rsquo;s Navy has been responsible for civilian casualties in conflicts and <a href="https://www.amnesty.org/en/latest/news/2018/04/las-muertes-en-mexico-danos-colaterales-o-el-producto-de-una-politica-de-seguridad-fallida/" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><u>human rights violations</u></a>, including illegal detention, <a href="https://www.insightcrime.org/news/analysis/mexico-navy-kidnappings/" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><u>kidnapping</u></a>, torture, and sexual torture. Mexico&rsquo;s National Human Rights Commission recently confirmed this pattern <a href="https://centroprodh.org.mx/2019/02/21/recommendation-against-mexican-navy-due-to-gross-human-rights-violations-testifies-to-risks-of-militarization/?lang=en" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><u>in a recommendation</u></a>.</p>

<h4 id="surveillance-abuses-in-nigeria" class=" lh-solid mb3"><strong style="">Surveillance Abuses in Nigeria</strong></h4>
<p class="mt0">Members of civil society in Nigeria face a wide range of digital threats. A recent report by Front Line Defenders <a href="https://www.frontlinedefenders.org/en/statement-report/upr-submission-nigeria-2018" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><u>concluded</u></a> that Nigeria&rsquo;s government &ldquo;has conducted mass surveillance of citizens&rsquo; telecommunications.&rdquo; The Committee to Protect Journalists (CPJ) has also <a href="https://cpj.org/2020/02/nigeria-police-telecom-surveillance-lure-arrest-journalists/" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><u>reported</u></a> multiple cases of the Nigerian government abusing phone surveillance.</p>
<p>An <a href="https://www.premiumtimesng.com/investigationspecial-reports/204987-investigation-governors-dickson-okowa-spend-billions-high-tech-spying-opponents-others.html" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><u>investigation</u></a> by Nigerian newspaper <em>Premium Times</em> found that Nigerian governors of Bayelsa and Delta states purchased systems from Circles to spy on their political opponents. In Delta State, <em>Premium Times</em> reports that the system was installed at the &ldquo;governor&rsquo;s lodge,&rdquo; and operated by employees of the Governor, rather than police. In Bayelsa State, the governor reportedly used the Circles system to spy on his opponent in an election, as well as his opponent&rsquo;s wife and aides. The investigation also found that the two Circles systems were imported without the proper authorizations from Nigeria&rsquo;s Office of the National Security Adviser.</p>

<h4 id="h.jqngqpx9331i" class="c30 c14 lh-solid mb3"><span class="c2" style="">Surveillance Abuses in Thailand </span></h4>
<p class="mt0">Thailand has a history of leveraging <a href="https://tbinternet.ohchr.org/Treaties/CCPR/Shared%20Documents/THA/INT_CCPR_CSS_THA_26657_E.pdf" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><u>a wide range of surveillance technologies</u></a> to monitor and harass civil society. Previous Citizen Lab research <a href="https://citizenlab.ca/2018/09/hide-and-seek-tracking-nso-groups-pegasus-spyware-to-operations-in-45-countries/" class="pointer"><u>also identified</u></a> a Pegasus spyware operator active within Thailand.</p>
<p>The ISOC has been accused of <a href="https://www.newsweek.com/thailands-military-junta-tortured-man-and-then-sued-him-talking-about-it-808051" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><u>torturing and waterboarding activists</u></a>, and suing activists who allege torture at the hands of the military. Recently, disturbing reports have emerged of <a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2020/06/26/world/asia/thailand-dissidents-disappeared-military.html" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><u>abductions</u></a> of Thai dissidents who live outside of Thailand. In one case, three Thai dissidents living in Laos who criticized Thailand&rsquo;s military disappeared, and their bodies were later <a href="https://www.theguardian.com/world/2019/mar/17/thailand-dissidents-murder-mekong-election" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><u>discovered</u></a> by a Thai fisherman. Their bodies were &ldquo;disemboweled and stuffed with concrete posts&rdquo; and their limbs broken. While these abductions and killings have not been conclusively attributed to the Royal Thai Army, the disappearances are reported to have happened while the leader of Thailand&rsquo;s former military junta Prayut Chan-o-cha (&#3611;&#3619;&#3632;&#3618;&#3640;&#3607;&#3608;&#3660; &#3592;&#3633;&#3609;&#3607;&#3619;&#3660;&#3650;&#3629;&#3594;&#3634;) was visiting Laos. Chan-o-cha is the current Prime Minister of Thailand, as well as the <a href="https://www.isoc.go.th/?page_id=68" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><u>director of ISOC</u></a>.</p>

<h4 id="h.31b5k6byyfnu" class="c30 c14 lh-solid mb3"><span class="c9 c2" style="">Royal Group</span></h4>
<p class="mt0">We found an unnamed UAE Circles system whose Check Point firewalls were in the same /25 as websites for Royal Group companies including Mauqah Technology, which famously acquired and operated Hacking Team&rsquo;s RCS spyware and, in 2012, used the system to target (among others) UAE activist Ahmed Mansoor. The command and control (C&amp;C) server for that spyware <a href="https://citizenlab.ca/2012/10/backdoors-are-forever-hacking-team-and-the-targeting-of-dissent/" class="pointer"><u>briefly pointed to</u></a> an IP address registered to Sheikh Tahnoon bin Zayed Al-Nahyan, the chairman of Royal Group and the UAE&rsquo;s now National Security Advisor. Additionally, Al Thuraya&rsquo;s <a href="https://al-akhbar.com/ArticleFiles/201652615217461635998728674613328.pdf" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><u>commercial license</u></a> shows &ldquo;Dhahi Mohammed Hamad Al-Thumairi&rdquo; as one of the company&rsquo;s two partners. <h4 id="h.6t6apbt5lpwo" class="c30 c14 lh-solid mb3"><span class="c9 c2" style="">Surveillance Misuse in Zambia</span></h4>
<p class="mt0">In 2019, Zambia <a href="https://www.wsj.com/articles/huawei-technicians-helped-african-governments-spy-on-political-opponents-11565793017" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><u>reportedly arrested</u></a> a group of bloggers who ran an opposition news site with the aid of &ldquo;a cyber-surveillance unit in the offices of Zambia&rsquo;s telecommunications regulator,&rdquo; which &ldquo;pinpointed the bloggers&rsquo; locations&rdquo; and was &ldquo;in constant contact with police units deployed to arrest them.&rdquo; While Circles&rsquo; solution allows governments to track phones, it is not clear if Zambia&rsquo;s Circles system was used in this case.</p>
<h2 id="h.knknd2chh9h9" class="c34 c14 lh-solid mb3"><span class="c2" style="">Discussion</span></h2>
<p class="mt0">Circles<span class="c3"> is part of a large and growing global surveillance industry catering to government clients. Many of the government clients who appear to have acquired and/or deployed Circles technology have a dismal record of abuses of human rights and technical surveillance capabilities. Many lack public transparency and accountability, and have minimal or no independent oversight over the activities of their security agencies. </span></p>
<h3 id="h.h7pfudfij7uh" class="c28 c14 lh-solid mb3"><span class="c29 c2" style="">Circles: Another Industry Player Fueling the Proliferation of Unaccountable Surveillance</span></h3>
<p class="mt0">It is difficult to investigate and track surveillance companies like Circles that exploit flaws in the SS7 protocol. Many SS7 attacks require no engagement with targets themselves, and leave no visible artefacts on targets&rsquo; devices that may inadvertently reveal an operation.<span class="c3"> The lack of transparency from telecommunications providers about abuses also helps surveillance companies, and their customers, evade exposure, further increasing the likelihood of misuse.</span></p>
<p class="c5">The authoritarian profile of some of Circles&rsquo; apparent government clients is troubling, but not surprising. Over the past decade, the explosion of the global surveillance industry has fueled a massive transfer of spy technology to problematic regimes and security services. These customers have leveraged their newly-acquired capabilities to abuse human rights and neutralize political opposition, even beyond their borders. Circles is an especially concerning case because of their close relationship and reported integration with <span class="c6"><a class="pointer" href="https://forensicnews.net/2020/04/29/the-covert-reach-of-nso-group/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">NSO Group</a></span>,<span class="c3"> which has a notorious record of enabling surveillance abuses. </span></p>
<h4 id="h.66hzuzz7jzht" class="c30 c14 lh-solid mb3"><span class="c2 c9" style="">The Expanding, Unregulated Surveillance Industry</span></h4>
<p class="mt0">Research by the Citizen Lab and others, including <a href="https://www.amnesty.org/en/latest/research/2020/06/moroccan-journalist-targeted-with-network-injection-attacks-using-nso-groups-tools/" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><u>Amnesty</u> <u>International</u></a> and <a href="https://privacyinternational.org/long-read/4291/surveillance-disclosures-show-urgent-need-reforms-eu-aid-programmes" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><u>Privacy International</u></a>, has demonstrated that the surveillance industry is poorly regulated and its products are prone to abuse. The &ldquo;self-regulation&rdquo; that companies claim to practice does not seem to have stemmed the growing tide of abuse cases. In a <a href="https://www.ohchr.org/EN/Issues/FreedomOpinion/Pages/SR2019ReportToHRC.aspx" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><u>2019 report on the surveillance industry</u></a>, the U.N. Special Rapporteur on the promotion and protection of the right to freedom of opinion and expression called for &ldquo;an immediate moratorium on the global sale and transfer of private surveillance technology <em>until rigorous human rights safeguards are put in place</em> to regulate such practices and guarantee that governments and non-State actors use the tools in legitimate ways.&rdquo;</p>
<p>As the surveillance industry continues to grow relatively unimpeded, spaces for legitimate democratic activity will continue to shrink. Governments&rsquo; ability to protect their citizens, as well as their own essential services and national security, will also continue to erode. Fixing this problem will require a direct focus on reforming the surveillance industry, including, among <a href="https://citizenlab.ca/2017/03/whos-watching-little-brother-checklist-accountability-industry-behind-government-hacking/" class="pointer"><u>other</u></a> <a href="https://www.ohchr.org/EN/NewsEvents/Pages/DisplayNews.aspx?NewsID=24736" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><u>steps</u></a>:</p>
<ul class="c39 lst-kix_pts482f7rrv7-0 start">
<li class="mt2">The enactment of more robust domestic, regional, and international legal frameworks<span class="c38">&mdash;</span>equipped with meaningful transparency, enforcement, and oversight mechanisms<span class="c38">&mdash;</span><span class="c3">to control the export and import of surveillance technology;</span></li>
<li class="mt2"><span class="c3">Mandatory due diligence obligations on surveillance companies and enforcement mechanisms with tough penalties for breaches of such obligations; and,</span></li>
<li class="mt2"><span class="c3">Legislative amendments to fix any legal and regulatory gaps such that parties harmed by surveillance technology can bring claims against companies for these harms.</span></li>
</ul>
<p class="c5"><span class="c3">In addition to these broader measures focused on the surveillance industry, we believe that the vulnerabilities inherent in the global telecommunications system require urgent action by governments and telecommunications providers. The global telecommunications sector provides significant opportunity for abuse by the surveillance industry and its customers in light of the continued failure of telecommunications operators and states to prevent such exploitation. In the discussion below, we set out clear actions that legislators and wireless operators must take to prevent continued exploitation and abuse.</span></p>
<h3 id="sounding-the-alarm-a-clear-and-present-threat-to-national-security" class=" lh-solid mb3"><strong style="">Sounding the Alarm: A Clear and Present Threat to National Security</strong></h3>
<p class="mt0">According to a <a href="https://www.gsma.com/membership/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/SS7_Vulnerability_2017_A4.ENG_.0003.03.pdf" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><u>recent study</u></a>, the vast majority of telecommunications networks around the globe are vulnerable to the kind of techniques reportedly used by Circles. As has been <a href="https://www.vice.com/en/article/7x9q8y/telecom-lobbyists-downplayed-theoretical-security-flaws-in-mobile-data-backbone" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><u>widely reported</u></a>, the industry has sought to downplay and conceal these risks. It is no surprise that reporting indicates that SS7 has been abused by <a href="https://www.theguardian.com/world/2020/mar/29/revealed-saudis-suspected-of-phone-spying-campaign-in-us" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><u>countries like Saudi Arabia</u></a> to target individuals around the world, including in the U.S.</p>
<p>A <a href="https://www.enisa.europa.eu/publications/signalling-security-in-telecom-ss7-diameter-5g/at_download/fullReport" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><u>recent survey of E.U. wireless security</u></a> by the European Union Agency for Cybersecurity (ENISA) concluded that a majority of operators had security measures that could &ldquo;only cover basic attacks.&rdquo; Troublingly, reporting of the scale of these threats remains difficult to achieve, as SS7 abuse is <a href="https://www.enisa.europa.eu/news/enisa-news/annual-report-on-telecom-security-incidents-in-2019" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><u>not within current reporting obligations</u></a> for the European telecommunications sector.</p>
<p>In addition, it is known within the industry that some countries fail to meet basic obligations of due diligence and oversight with respect to their networks, enabling <a href="https://www.ptsecurity.com/upload/ptcom/SS7-VULNERABILITY-2016-eng.pdf" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><u>foreign entities</u></a> access to SS7 and Diameter for the purposes of conducting global surveillance.</p>
<p>We believe that the historically limited public information about abuses has enabled the telecommunications industry to further minimize the problem. There is no public reporting from most telecommunications companies about the scale of the threats to users, the number of attacks identified and blocked, or a roadmap for addressing these threats in the future. This state of affairs will result in predictable, preventable harm to customers across the globe.</p>
<h4 id="h.25db8odbav95" class="c30 c14 lh-solid mb3"><span class="c9 c2" style="">Risks in the U.S.</span></h4>
<p class="mt0">In April 2017, the U.S. Department of Homeland Security (DHS) <a href="https://www.dhs.gov/sites/default/files/publications/DHS%20Study%20on%20Mobile%20Device%20Security%20-%20April%202017-FINAL.pdf" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><u>conducted a major study that concluded</u></a>: &ldquo;all U.S. carriers are vulnerable to these exploits, resulting in risks to national security, the economy, and the Federal Government&rsquo;s ability to reliably execute national essential functions.&rdquo; According to the DHS report, &ldquo;SS7 and Diameter vulnerabilities can be exploited by criminals, terrorists, and nation-state actors/foreign intelligence organizations&rdquo; and &ldquo;many organizations appear to be sharing or selling expertise and services&rdquo; that could be used to conduct such espionage.</p>
<p>In response to a 2017 letter by Senator Ron Wyden requesting information about what steps U.S. carriers were taking to secure their networks, <a href="https://www.wyden.senate.gov/imo/media/doc/ATT%20SS7%20Response.pdf" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><u>AT&amp;T acknowledged</u></a> that &ldquo;hundreds of carriers now have access to SS7, many of them in unstable or unfriendly nations where credentials can be compromised&hellip;even sold on the open market for a fee.&rdquo; The company went on to acknowledge that &ldquo;the trust model is no longer fully reliable.&rdquo;</p>
<p><a href="https://www.wyden.senate.gov/imo/media/doc/wyden-fcc-ss7-letter-may-2018.pdf" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><u>In a 2018 letter to the Federal Communications Commission</u></a> (FCC), Senator Wyden revealed that an unnamed U.S. carrier had suffered a SS7-related breach of customer information, which it reported to federal authorities. <a href="https://arstechnica.com/features/2019/04/fully-compromised-comms-how-industry-influence-at-the-fcc-risks-our-digital-security/" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><u>Subsequent investigative reporting revealed</u></a> that the FCC had ignored expert recommendations by the DHS and instead espoused a voluntary compliance program at the urging of the wireless industry. Additionally, the reporting found that, although SMS messages are vulnerable to SS7 interception, the wireless industry successfully lobbied the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) to keep SMS text messages as an <a href="https://pages.nist.gov/800-63-3/" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><u>approved method of two factor authentication</u></a> in U.S. government standards.</p>
<p>The troubling inability of the U.S. government and telecommunications sector to address SS7 vulnerabilities is mirrored in many countries around the world.</p>
<h4 id="h.dplq8c9aip9i" class="c30 c14 lh-solid mb3"><span class="c9 c2" style="">Risks in Canada</span></h4>
<p class="mt0">In 2017, <a href="https://www.cbc.ca/news/politics/hackers-cellphone-security-1.4406338" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><u>a joint investigation undertaken by CBC News and Radio Canada</u></a>, in cooperation with German security researchers, demonstrated an SS7 attack against a sitting member of parliament, Matthew Dub&eacute;. With only a telephone number, the investigators were able to use SS7 vulnerabilities to track Dub&eacute;&rsquo;s precise movements and intercept his calls. The tests were conducted over both the Rogers and Bell networks.</p>
<p>In its 2018 annual <a href="https://www.priv.gc.ca/media/4831/ar_201718_eng.pdf" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><u>report</u></a>, Canada&rsquo;s Privacy Commissioner noted the investigation, flagged SS7 security weaknesses, and called on the Canadian government and industry to work together to resolve them. In response, Canada&rsquo;s signals intelligence agency, the Communications Security Establishment (CSE), <a href="https://www.cse-cst.gc.ca/en/media/2018-04-25" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><u>said</u></a> that &ldquo;the security issues surrounding SS7, have been known for some time&rdquo; and that it had been working with industry partners to resolve them. However, CSE also asserted that it &ldquo;is unable to discuss further details of meetings with industry partners, and we cannot disclose the participation of individual, private telecommunications partners.&rdquo;</p>
<p>For guidance, the CSE suggested the public visit a &ldquo;mobile security&rdquo; information page, now available on the newly established Canada Centre for Cyber Security <a href="https://cyber.gc.ca/en/infographics" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><u>website in a section</u></a> on &ldquo;infographics.&rdquo; However, the website and <a href="https://cyber.gc.ca/sites/default/files/publications/cse-its-mobile-security-ef.pdf" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><u>accompanying infographic</u></a> do not mention SS7 explicitly and provide only basic advice on mobile security practices.</p>
<h4 class=" lh-solid mb3">Legislators: Do This Now</h4>
<p class="mt0">Governments across the globe should take action to protect their citizens and their own operations. Telecommunications regulatory bodies should conduct regular audits of national networks and mandate carriers to identify, disclose, and address vulnerabilities.</p>
<p>The U.K. government has shown promising leadership in addressing carrier security, with <a href="https://publications.parliament.uk/pa/bills/cbill/58-01/0216/200216.pdf" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><u>recently proposed legislation</u></a> that requires carriers to secure their networks and gives Ofcom (the U.K. telecommunications regulator) the authority to ensure compliance. The newly proposed powers granted to Ofcom include the ability to conduct audits and to compel the production of records and other information related to a carrier&rsquo;s security efforts. The proposed law would also, for the first time, require carriers to disclose compromises to their customers and provide for fines in some cases.</p>
<p>The European Union has also taken note of telecommunication network vulnerabilities and made <a href="https://www.enisa.europa.eu/publications/signalling-security-in-telecom-ss7-diameter-5g/at_download/fullReport" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><u>recent recommendations</u></a> that encourage EU nations to conduct regular analyses of the threat landscape, adopt minimum security standards, and require incident reporting. We also note that Nordic regulators have <a href="https://resilience.enisa.europa.eu/article-13/workshops/24th-article-13a-eg-meeting-austria-for-providers/Nordic_Perspective_on_SS7.pptx" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><u>undertaken efforts</u></a> to establish best practices for protecting their infrastructure from SS7 attacks.</p>
<p>In contrast, the U.S. FCC has shown no will to compel carriers to report incidents or undertake serious security improvements. Given the rapid proliferation of SS7 and Diameter exploitation technologies both to states and non-state actors, it seems likely that without urgent action, U.S. consumers and government operations will be targeted by an increasingly wide range of potential threats.</p>
<h4 id="wireless-carriers-do-this-now" class=" lh-solid mb3"><strong style="">Wireless Carriers: Do This Now</strong></h4>
<p class="mt0">We urgently recommend that telecommunication companies examine SS7 and Diameter traffic originating from providers in countries where we have identified a Circles deployment for patterns of abuse. The SS7 and Diameter exploitation marketplace, as well as the wireless threat landscape, are constantly evolving. The <a href="https://www.dhs.gov/sites/default/files/publications/DHS%20Study%20on%20Mobile%20Device%20Security%20-%20April%202017-FINAL.pdf" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><u>recommendations provided by the DHS</u></a> are highly relevant: every major wireless carrier should receive an independent SS7 and Diameter audit every 12-18 months, and should address any identified vulnerabilities. The ENISA also provides a range of <a href="https://www.enisa.europa.eu/publications/signalling-security-in-telecom-ss7-diameter-5g/at_download/fullReport" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><u>security recommendations</u></a> for carriers.</p>
<p>We are aware that some providers, such as a number of U.S. companies, are experimenting with SS7 firewalls, which show promise in reducing some types of attacks. We urge providers to publicly disclose their roadmaps for addressing SS7 and Diameter vulnerabilities, and believe that information about SS7 threats should be included in telco companies&rsquo; transparency reporting going forward.</p>
<h3 id="h.nwpskzs4vsjg" class="c28 c14 lh-solid mb3"><span class="c2" style="">Sounding the Alarm</span><span class="c29 c2" style="">: Recommendations for High Risk Users</span></h3>
<p class="mt0">Whether you are a journalist, human rights defender, or government employee, telecommunication network vulnerabilities may make it possible for adversaries to intercept your verification SMSes and compromise your accounts. If you believe you face threats because of who you are or what you do from any of the countries mentioned in this report, or even a country not listed above, we urge you to migrate away from SMS-based two factor authentication immediately for all accounts where it is possible. Directions on <a href="https://securityplanner.consumerreports.org/tool/use-a-security-key-for-strongest-mfa" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span class="c6">how to use a </span><span class="c6 c2">security key </span><span class="c6">for some of your accounts are here</span></a>.</p>
<p class="c5"><span class="c3">In addition, for accounts on popular apps such as Signal, WhatsApp and Telegram, we urge you to immediately enable a security PIN or password for your account. </span></p>

<h3 id="h.885y90va1ty3" class="c28 c14 lh-solid mb3"><span class="c29 c2" style="">Unknown Countries</span></h3>
<p class="mt0">We found names for several other Circles systems appearing in IP ranges registered to Circles. Because these names were never recorded in IP ranges that might belong to Circles customers, we are unsure of their identity. The names were: <span class="c26">GTR Whitehippo</span>, <span class="c26">Icarus Shemer</span>, <span class="c26">Kodik Kite</span>, and <span class="c26">Opel Oranit</span><span class="c3">.</span></p>

<li id="fn1" role="doc-endnote" class="mt2">Check Point is aware that its firewalls publicly produce this hostname information, and <a href="https://supportcenter.checkpoint.com/supportcenter/portal;jsessionid=(J2EE8705100)ID0937160150DB00667728629220380580End;saplb_*=(J2EE8705100)8705150?eventSubmit_doGoviewsolutiondetails=&amp;solutionid=sk69360" class="pointer" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><u>does not consider</u></a> this to be a security issue, remarking that this is &ldquo;public information.&rdquo;<a class="pointer" role="doc-backlink" href="#fnref1">&#8617;&#65038;</a></li>
<li id="fn2" role="doc-endnote" class="mt2">We found a total of 19 distinct TLS certificates ever recorded as matching this regular expression, returned by a total of 57 IP addresses. Ultimately, our search for TLS certificates containing this regular expression did not help us identify any additional Circles systems, though it did help us tie IP addresses together as belonging to the same system, based on returning the same TLS certificate.<a class="pointer" role="doc-backlink" href="#fnref2">&#8617;&#65038;</a></li>

<li id="fn4" role="doc-endnote" class="mt2">Firewall IPs in the same /30 as smart.gov.ae registered to &ldquo;Dubai Government&rdquo; and same /26 as wsg.dubaipolice.net.<a class="pointer" role="doc-backlink" href="#fnref4">&#8617;&#65038;</a></li>
