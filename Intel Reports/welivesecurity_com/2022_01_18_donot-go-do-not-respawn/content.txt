Do not respawn!</h1>    <p class="sub-title">ESET researchers take a deep look into recent attacks carried out by Donot Team throughout 2020 and 2021, targeting government and military entities in several South Asian countries</p>

                    <div class="article-body">
    <p>Donot Team (also known as APT-C-35 and SectorE02) is a threat actor operating since at least 2016 and known for targeting organizations and individuals in South Asia with Windows and Android malware. A recent <a href="https://www.amnesty.org/en/latest/news/2021/10/togo-activist-targeted-with-spyware-by-notorious-hacker-group/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">report by Amnesty International</a> links the group’s malware to an Indian cybersecurity company that may be selling the spyware or offering a hackers-for-hire service to governments of the region.</p>
<p>We have been closely following the activities of Donot Team, and have traced several campaigns that leverage Windows malware derived from the group’s signature <a href="https://www.netscout.com/blog/asert/donot-team-leverages-new-modular-malware-framework-south-asia" target="_blank" rel="noopener">yty malware framework</a>. According to our findings, the group is very persistent and has consistently targeted the same organizations for at least the last two years.</p>
<p>In this blogpost, we document two variants of the malware used in recent campaigns – DarkMusical and Gedit. For each of the variants, we analyze the whole attack chain and provide insight into how the group updates its tools, tactics, and techniques.</p>
<h2>Targets</h2>
<p>The campaigns of Donot Team are motivated by espionage, using their signature malware: the “yty” malware framework, whose main purpose is to collect and exfiltrate data. According to our telemetry, Donot Team focuses on a small number of targets in South Asia – Bangladesh, Sri Lanka, Pakistan and Nepal – as seen in Figure 1.</p>
<p><div class="caption center"><a href="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2022/01/Figure-1.-Countries-targeted-in-recent-Donot-Team-campaigns.png"><img class="wp-image-158306" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2022/01/Figure-1.-Countries-targeted-in-recent-Donot-Team-campaigns.png" alt="" width="821" height="581" /></a><p class="caption-text"><em>Figure 1. Countries targeted in recent Donot Team campaigns</em></p></div></p>
<p>These attacks are focused on:</p>
<ul>
<li>Government and military organizations</li>
<li>Ministries of Foreign Affairs</li>
<li>Embassies</li>
</ul>
<p>Going as far as targeting embassies of these countries in other regions, such as the Middle East, Europe, North America, and Latin America, is also not outside Donot Team’s realm.</p>
<h2>Try, try, try again</h2>
<p>It’s not a rarity for APT operators to attempt to regain access to a compromised network after they have been ejected from it. In some cases this is achieved through the deployment of a stealthier backdoor that remains quiet until the attackers need it; in other cases they simply restart their operation with new malware or a variant of the malware they used previously. The latter is the case with Donot Team operators, only that they are remarkably persistent in their attempts.</p>
<p>According to ESET telemetry, Donot Team has been consistently targeting the same entities with waves of spearphishing emails with malicious attachments every two to four months. Interestingly, emails we were able to retrieve and analyze did not show signs of spoofing. Some emails were sent from the same organizations that were being attacked. It’s possible that the attackers may have compromised the email accounts of some of their victims in earlier campaigns, or the email server used by those organizations.</p>
<p>With spearphishing emails, the attackers use malicious Microsoft Office documents to deploy their malware. We have seen Donot Team using at least three techniques. One is macros in Word, Excel and PowerPoint documents, such as the example seen in Figure 2.</p>
<p><div class="caption center"><a href="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2022/01/Figure-2.-Malicious-macro-in-a-PowerPoint-document-that-drops-a-downloader-executable-and-creates-a-scheduled-task-to-run-it.png"><img class="wp-image-158308 size-full" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2022/01/Figure-2.-Malicious-macro-in-a-PowerPoint-document-that-drops-a-downloader-executable-and-creates-a-scheduled-task-to-run-it.png" alt="" width="821" height="324" /></a><p class="caption-text"><em>Figure 2. Malicious macro in a PowerPoint document that drops a downloader executable and creates a scheduled task to run it</em></p></div></p>
<p>The second technique is RTF files with <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">.doc</span> extensions that exploit memory corruption vulnerability <a href="https://msrc.microsoft.com/update-guide/en-us/vulnerability/CVE-2017-11882" target="_blank" rel="noopener">CVE‑2017‑11882</a> in Equation Editor, shown in Figure 3. These RTF documents also contain two embedded DLLs as OLE objects (see Figure 4) that are used to install and download further components (both DLLs are described in the Gedit section). This allows the attackers to execute shellcode and requires no user interaction. The shellcode deploys the main components of the malware.</p>
<p><div class="caption center"><a href="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2022/01/Figure-3.-CLSID-of-the-COM-object-used-by-the-RTF-document-to-load-the-Equation-Editor-the-ensuing-OLE-object-contains-the-CVE-2017-1182-exploit.png"><img class="wp-image-158309" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2022/01/Figure-3.-CLSID-of-the-COM-object-used-by-the-RTF-document-to-load-the-Equation-Editor-the-ensuing-OLE-object-contains-the-CVE-2017-1182-exploit.png" alt="" width="522" height="77" /></a><p class="caption-text"><em>Figure 3. CLSID of the COM object used by the RTF document to load the Equation Editor; the ensuing OLE object contains the CVE‑2017‑1182 exploit</em></p></div></p>

<h2>The yty malware framework</h2>
<p>Discovered by <a href="https://www.netscout.com/blog/asert/donot-team-leverages-new-modular-malware-framework-south-asia" target="_blank" rel="noopener">NetScout</a> in 2018, the yty malware framework is a less sophisticated and poorly developed successor to an older framework called EHDevel. The yty framework consists of a chain of downloaders that ultimately download a backdoor with minimal functionality, used to download and execute further components of Donot Team’s toolset.</p>
<p>These include file collectors based on file extension and year of creation, screen capturers, keyloggers, reverse shells, and more. As seen in Figure 6, components for exfiltration gather the collected intelligence from staging folders and upload every file to a designated server used only for this purpose.</p>
<p><div class="caption center"><a href="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2022/01/Figure-6.-Component-that-resolves-the-folder-name-for-staging-JPEG-screenshots-left-and-exfiltration-component-that-finds-all-files-in-the-staging-folder-right.png"><img class="wp-image-158312" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2022/01/Figure-6.-Component-that-resolves-the-folder-name-for-staging-JPEG-screenshots-left-and-exfiltration-component-that-finds-all-files-in-the-staging-folder-right.png" alt="" width="800" height="198" /></a><p class="caption-text"><em>Figure 6. Component that resolves the folder name for staging JPEG screenshots (left) and exfiltration component that finds all files in the staging folder (right)</em></p></div></p>
<p>Staging folder names and locations are changed with almost every new campaign, as well as some of the components’ filenames. However, there are cases in which the names of components have remained unchanged, for example: <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">gedit.exe</span>, <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">wuaupdt.exe</span>, <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">lmpss.exe</span>, <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">disc.exe</span>, among others. As seen in Figure 7, it seems that for every new campaign, in order to set new paths and filenames, these values must be changed in the source code and then recompiled, as none of these components use a configuration block or file.</p>
<p><div class="caption center"><a href="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2022/01/Figure-7.-Encrypted-strings-containing-locations-and-filenames-that-are-regularly-changed-top-and-unencrypted-values-used-in-constructing-the-CC-URL-bottom.png"><img class="wp-image-158313 size-full" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2022/01/Figure-7.-Encrypted-strings-containing-locations-and-filenames-that-are-regularly-changed-top-and-unencrypted-values-used-in-constructing-the-CC-URL-bottom.png" alt="" width="412" height="411" /></a><p class="caption-text"><em>Figure 7. Encrypted strings containing locations and filenames that are regularly changed (top) and unencrypted values used in constructing the C&amp;C URL (bottom)</em></p></div></p>
<p>The malware uses scheduled tasks for persistence, and alternates between DLL and EXE files between campaigns. In the case of DLLs, scheduled tasks execute <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">rundll32.exe</span> to load them and execute one of the exported functions.</p>
<p>The developers of the yty framework primarily rely on the C++ programming language. Likely in an attempt to evade detection, they have also ported their components to other languages such as VBScript, Python (packaged with PyInstaller), Visual C#, and AutoIt, among others. However, since 2019 we have only seen them leveraging components programmed in C++ (Figure 8) and Go (Figure 9).</p>

A different server is always used for the upload of collected information. In some attacks Donot Team has reused C&amp;C domains from previous attacks – both for downloads and exfiltration. As seen in Figure 10, Figure 11 and Figure 12, these components – later described as a variant we track as <em>DarkMusical</em> – used in the same attack, employed three different C&amp;C domains.</p>

<h3>DarkMusical</h3>
<p>According to ESET telemetry, the first wave of attacks where this variant was used occurred in June 2021, targeting military organizations in Bangladesh. We were only able to recover its chain of downloaders and its main backdoor. Given the small number of victims, we believe this might have been a highly targeted attack.</p>
<p>In September, a second wave of attacks that targeted military organizations in Nepal used new C&amp;C servers and file and staging folder names. We were able to recover a number of components downloaded by the backdoor, so we have decided to describe these attacks instead.</p>
<p>Spearphishing emails were sent with PowerPoint documents containing a macro that deploys the first component of a chain of downloaders and persists using a scheduled task. When potential victims open these documents, they will be presented with a fake error message, as seen in Figure 14, and the documents will remain devoid of any visible content.</p>
<p><div class="caption center"><a href="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2022/01/Figure-14.-Screenshot-of-a-blank-malicious-PowerPoint-document.png"><img class="wp-image-158320 size-full" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2022/01/Figure-14.-Screenshot-of-a-blank-malicious-PowerPoint-document.png" alt="" width="752" height="511" /></a><p class="caption-text"><em>Figure 14. <p>The backdoor downloads the components that handle the collection and exfiltration of information to a dedicated server. These components do not communicate with the backdoor or the C&amp;C to report on their activities – rather, they use a designated folder for the staging of the data, and a separate exfiltration component will collect everything and upload it.</p>

<h3>Gedit</h3>
<p>We detected the first attacks of the campaign using Gedit in September 2020, against organizations in Pakistan that had already been targeted with spearphishing and malicious RTF documents that installed the Jaca framework. The malware is clearly derived from the yty malware framework, but it is distinct enough to be separated from DarkMusical.</p>
<p>We were able to retrieve a spearphishing email corresponding to a Gedit campaign that occurred in February of 2021, which is shown in Figure 16. <h2>Henos campaign</h2>
<p>Finally, it is worth mentioning a wave of attacks that occurred between February and March 2021, targeting military organizations in Bangladesh and Sri Lanka. These attacks used the Gedit variant of the malware, but with some minor modifications. Therefore, we decided to name this campaign Henos in our timeline, after its backdoor DLL – <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">henos.dll</span>.</p>
<p>Samples belonging to components of this wave of attacks were also reported online in February, which probably explains why the group didn’t use the components again (see <a href="https://twitter.com/ShadowChasing1/status/1365304023775989761" target="_blank" rel="noopener">this tweet by Shadow Chaser Group researchers</a>, for example).</p>
<p>Although we didn’t find the corresponding spearphishing emails or malicious documents, the attack chain is presumably the same as we described above, with some minor differences in how the components are executed. An overview of this is shown in Figure 18.</p>
<p><div class="caption center"><a href="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2022/01/Figure-18.-Chain-of-compromise-of-the-Henos-campaign.png"><img class="wp-image-158324" src="https://web-assets.esetstatic.com/wls/2022/01/Figure-18.-Chain-of-compromise-of-the-Henos-campaign.png" alt="" width="800" height="414" /></a><p class="caption-text"><em>Figure 18. Chain of compromise of the Henos campaign</em></p></div></p>
<p>While some of the components of this campaign are named <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">javatemp.exe</span> and <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">pytemp.exe</span>, these filenames were probably only chosen in an attempt to mimic legitimate software such as Java or Python. While <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">pytemp.exe</span> and <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">plaapas.exe</span> were coded in the Go language, <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">javatemp.exe</span> was coded in C++ (compiled with MinGW).</p>
<p>One final note is that the component that performs exfiltration of files, <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">pytemp.exe</span>, performs a check to see if <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">gedit.exe</span> is running. If two or more instances are found, it exits. We believe this is a mistake by the programmers, as it should check for <span style="font-family: 'courier new', courier, monospace;">pytemp.exe</span> instead. However, this simple mistake helps us tie the Henos campaign to the Gedit variant of the malware (added to code similarity).</p>
<h2>Conclusion</h2>
<p>Donot Team makes up for its low sophistication with tenacity. We expect that it will continue to push on regardless of its many setbacks. Only time will tell if the group evolves its current TTPs and malware.</p>
