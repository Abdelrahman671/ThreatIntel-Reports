                <div class="post"><p>When it comes to North Korean cyber activity, analysts fall into one of two camps: The âitâs all Lazarusâ camp or the âitâs complicated campâ. Weâre in the second camp: DPRK activity should be clustered among a few related groups.</p>

<p>The DPRK doesnât make this clustering easy. Itâs often hard to know where the state-directed activity stops and the operator side-hustles start. We know the North Korean crews target cryptocurrency wallets and exchanges, but the split of the bounty/booty between the operatorsâ personal crypto-stashes and government coffers is a bit of a mystery.</p>

<p>Further, all the North Korean groups we list in this article use wipers or ransomware, and all <a href="https://blog.lexfo.fr/ressources/Lexfo-WhitePaper-The_Lazarus_Constellation.pdf">use similar packers</a> to mask malicious files. But overlapping geography and TTPs doesnât mean a single âLazarus Groupâ is orchestrating all these attacks. The overlaps are better explained by <a href="https://content.fireeye.com/apt/rpt-apt38">several operators sharing malware developed by a single team.</a></p>

<p>Now, we could sit back and go all <a href="https://giphy.com/gifs/angry-panda-ToMjGpIYtgvMP38WTFC">angry panda</a> whenever we see an analyst get it wrong, or we could have our own crack at defining whoâs who in the zoo. Bucketing the activity provides a clearer picture of North Koreaâs targeting habits and the risks that they pose so we can better predict and defend against them.</p>

<p>But first, a few caveats: linking the names different analysts give to each cluster of activity doesnât make those groups synonymous. Itâs a loose equivalency, at best. And our attempts to assign these groups some personality, well, that just makes our lives more bearable.</p>

<h3 id="the-lazarus-group">âThe Lazarus Groupâ<br /></h3>
<p><strong>aka Temp.Hermit, Labyrinth Chollima, RGB-D3, ZINC, Covellite, NICKEL ACADEMY</strong></p>

<p>Letâs start with the group that always gets the headlines. <a href="https://operationblockbuster.com/wp-content/uploads/2016/02/Operation-Blockbuster-Report.pdf">The Sony Hackers</a>. The <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bangladesh_Bank_robbery">Bangladesh Bank SWIFT heist</a>. <a href="https://www.mcafee.com/enterprise/en-us/assets/reports/rp-operation-sharpshooter.pdf">Attempts</a> to hack<a href="https://www.mcafee.com/enterprise/en-us/assets/reports/rp-operation-sharpshooter.pdf"> U.S. defense contractors</a>. And according to the DoJâs<a href="https://www.justice.gov/opa/pr/north-korean-regime-backed-programmer-charged-conspiracy-conduct-multiple-cyber-attacks-and"> Park indictment</a>, the group responsible (if only in part) for WannaCry 2.0.</p>

<p>Because of the clustering problems described earlier, itâs difficult â based on whatâs published â to know where this crew stops and all the others begin. For example, some researchers group all activity directed at banks under BlueNoroff (see below), others fold it under Lazarus.</p>

<p>What historically made Lazarus distinct â less so as time passes, were techniques (such as <a href="https://attack.mitre.org/techniques/T1001/003/">protocol impersonation</a>) used in its malware.</p>

<p><br />
Lazarus has strong associations with the<a href="https://us-cert.cisa.gov/ncas/alerts/TA17-318A"> Fallchill</a> remote access tool for Windows and similar capabilities that target<a href="https://securelist.com/operation-applejeus/87553/"> MacOS</a>,<a href="https://www.mcafee.com/blogs/other-blogs/mcafee-labs/malbus-popular-south-korean-bus-app-series-in-google-play-found-dropping-malware-after-5-years-of-development/"> Android</a>, and<a href="https://blog.netlab.360.com/dacls-the-dual-platform-rat-en/"> Linux</a>. More recently it has been linked with <a href="https://securelist.com/mata-multi-platform-targeted-malware-framework/97746/">multi-platform malware frameworks</a> and <a href="https://objective-see.com/blog/blog_0x51.html">fileless malware</a> campaigns.</p>

<p>Lazarus is connected to a <a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/2020/06/17/operation-interception-aerospace-military-companies-cyberspies/">long-running</a>, <a href="https://www.mcafee.com/blogs/other-blogs/mcafee-labs/operation-north-star-a-job-offer-thats-too-good-to-be-true/">global</a> <a href="https://www.clearskysec.com/operation-dream-job/">campaign</a> in which attackers built rapport with targets by posing as recruiters on LinkedIn, as well as campaigns that targeted <a href="https://www.dragos.com/threat/covellite/">the U.S electric sector</a>.</p>

<p>It has generated revenue through <a href="https://securelist.com/operation-applejeus/87553/">attacks on cryptocurrency exchanges,</a> but has of late tried its luck with<a href="https://www.zdnet.com/article/north-koreas-state-hackers-caught-engaging-in-bec-scams/"> BEC invoice fraud</a> (in the midst of a suspected espionage operation), <a href="https://sansec.io/research/north-korea-magecart">Magecart-style transaction skimming</a> and <a href="https://securelist.com/lazarus-on-the-hunt-for-big-game/97757/">big game ransomware</a> attacks that use VHD malware.</p>

<h3 id="apt38">âAPT38â<br /></h3>
<p><strong>aka BlueNoroff, Stardust Chollima, BeagleBoyz, NICKEL GLADSTONE</strong></p>

<p>Now letâs talk about the Dear Leaderâs primary breadwinners. APT38 are probably the most successful bank robbers in history.</p>

<p>While reporting on <a href="https://securelist.com/lazarus-under-the-hood/77908/">BlueNoroff </a>and<a href="https://content.fireeye.com/apt/rpt-apt38"> APT38</a> typically describe activity that uses the same tools and infrastructure as Lazarus, APT38 uses custom tools (<a href="https://us-cert.cisa.gov/ncas/analysis-reports/AR19-129A">ELECTRICFISH</a>,<a href="https://www.flashpoint-intel.com/blog/disclosure-chilean-redbanc-intrusion-lazarus-ties/"> PowerRatankba</a>,<a href="https://www.proofpoint.com/sites/default/files/pfpt-us-wp-north-korea-bitten-by-bitcoin-bug-180129.pdf"> PowerSpritz</a> and<a href="https://us-cert.cisa.gov/ncas/alerts/aa20-239a"> job application-themed </a>lures) in jobs entirely focused on generating revenue.</p>

<p>Banks are attacked via <a href="https://badcyber.com/several-polish-banks-hacked-information-stolen-by-unknown-attackers/">watering hole attacks</a> and <a href="https://us-cert.cisa.gov/ncas/analysis-reports/ar20-239c">ATM jackpotting</a>. Thereâs a <a href="https://hello.global.ntt/insights/blog/targeted-trickbot-activity-drops-powerbrace-backdoor">growing body</a> of <a href="https://assets.sentinelone.com/labs">evidence</a> that the group is able to scale its operations by purchasing access to systems previously infected by TrickBot, and by using commercially available tools like <a href="https://adeo.com.tr/wp-content/uploads/2020/05/ADEO-Lazarus-APT38.pdf">PowerShell Empire and Cobalt Strike</a> once they have a foothold.</p>

<p>And itâs all handled with the kind of planning and care youâd expect from a heist movie: they usually go to some effort to cover their tracks, even if that means <a href="https://www.welivesecurity.com/2018/04/03/lazarus-killdisk-central-american-casino/">covering them with ransomware or a destructive attack</a>.</p>

<h3 id="andariel">âAndarielâ<br /></h3>
<p><strong>aka Silent Chollima, Dark Seoul, Rifle, Wassonite</strong></p>

<p>Andariel is a bully. What it lacks in sophistication, it makes up for in belligerence.</p>

<p>This crew <a href="https://securelist.com/apt-trends-report-q3-2019/94530/">engages</a> in <a href="https://unit42.paloaltonetworks.com/inside-tdrop2-technical-analysis-of-new-dark-seoul-malware/">espionage</a>, but also gets tasked with <a href="https://www.mcafee.com//wp-content/uploads/2011/07/McAfee-Labs-10-Days-of-Rain-July-2011.pdf">disrupting</a> and <a href="https://www.sans.org/reading-room/whitepapers/critical/tracing-lineage-darkseoul-36787">wiping</a> things. A 10-day assault on South Korea in 2011 set a troubling new precedent for what pain the pariah state was prepared to inflict on their adversaries.</p>

<p>Andariel knows how to get noticed in all the places it probably shouldnât. It is one of the few groups bold enough to take aim at military targets in the<a href="https://www.mcafee.com/enterprise/en-us/assets/white-papers/wp-dissecting-operation-troy.pdf"> U.S.</a> and<a href="https://github.com/ssp4rk/slides/blob/master/2017_SAS_Endless_gunfire_in_South_Korea.pdf"> South Korea</a> or to <a href="https://www.slideshare.net/JackyMinseokCha/from-stealing-confidential-data-to-revenuegenerating-attacks">go after AV management servers</a> on a victimâs network. âYOLO, lets <a href="https://www.dragos.com/threat/wassonite/">hack a nuclear plant</a> and <a href="https://twitter.com/RungRage/status/1188853620541775872">leave admin credentials hardcoded in the malware</a>.â And while we donât know who the hell âHack Houndâ is, <a href="https://global.ahnlab.com/global/upload/download/techreport/%5BAhnLab%5DAndariel_a_Subgroup_of_Lazarus%20(3).pdf">Andariel has some nasty things to say about them</a>.</p>

<p>But like all the DPRK groups, thereâs a little cybercrime on the side, such as stealing from <a href="https://global.ahnlab.com/global/upload/download/asecreport/ASEC%2520REPORT_vol.91_ENG.pdf">online gambling sites</a> or ATMs in <a href="https://fsiceat.tistory.com/2">South Korea</a> or<a href="https://www.zdnet.com/article/new-north-korean-malware-targeting-atms-spotted-in-india/"> India</a>. It probably doesnât end there.</p>

<h3 id="apt37">âAPT37â<br /></h3>
<p><strong>aka Group123, Ricochet Chollima, RedEyes, Reaper, ScarCruft, Geumseong121</strong></p>

<p>APT37 is the wannabe hipster of DPRKâs hacking elite: they are constantly <a href="https://securelist.com/scarcruft-continues-to-evolve-introduces-bluetooth-harvester/90729/">upskilling</a> (âI just earned my <a href="https://www.fireeye.com/blog/threat-research/2018/02/apt37-overlooked-north-korean-actor.html">0-day</a> badge!â) while actively trying to <a href="https://github.com/ssp4rk/slides/blob/master/2019SAS_Behind_of_the_Mask_of_ScarCruft.pdf">emulate DarkHotel, a thoroughly more capable actor</a>.</p>

<p>APT37 often <a href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/2018/01/korea-in-crosshairs.html">goes after individuals, as opposed to organisations</a>. It has a taste for<a href="https://global.ahnlab.com/global/upload/download/techreport/[AhnLab]%20Red_Eyes_Hacking_Group_Report%20(1).pdf"> North Korean defectors, journalists and human rights activists</a>. <p>APT37 uses malware described by different intel shops as <a href="https://www.carbonblack.com/blog/threat-analysis-rokrat-malware/">âRedoorâ, âDOGCALLâ, âROKRATâ or âFinal1stspyâ</a>, as well as <a href="https://blog.talosintelligence.com/2018/04/fake-av-investigation-unearths-kevdroid.html">Android malware named Kevdroid</a>.</p>

<p>Even when they went <a href="https://unit42.paloaltonetworks.com/unit42-nokki-almost-ties-the-knot-with-dogcall-reaper-group-uses-new-malware-to-deploy-rat/">after sports fans</a>, the attack looked <a href="https://www.wired.com/story/untold-story-2018-olympics-destroyer-cyberattack/">inspired by the major leaguers</a>. Canât these kids do anything original?</p>

<h3 id="kimsuky">âKimsukyâ<br /></h3>
<p><strong>aka Velvet Chollima, THALLIUM, TEMP.Firework, RGB-D5, Black Banshee</strong></p>

<p>Kimsuky is called the â<a href="https://www.virusbulletin.com/blog/2020/03/vb2019-paper-kimsuky-group-tracking-king-spear-phishing/">King of Spearphishing</a>â. Like most fishermen, they <a href="https://blog.alyac.co.kr/2299">leave a stench</a>: their OPSEC is pretty amateur.</p>

<p>Kimsuky has been pinned for going after the<a href="https://securelist.com/the-kimsuky-operation-a-north-korean-apt/57915/"> South Korean government and Chinese organisations</a>, an attack related to the<a href="https://www.reuters.com/article/us-nuclear-southkorea-northkorea-idUSKBN0MD0GR20150317"> Korea Hydro &amp; Nuclear Plant</a>,<a href="https://blog.alyac.co.kr/2234"> and phishing attacks against U.S. military interests</a>.</p>

<p>Kimsuky is also known for its âcowboyâ loaders: part of an infection chain linked to <a href="https://www.netscout.com/blog/asert/stolen-pencil-campaign-targets-academia">attacks on U.S. academia</a> and <a href="https://unit42.paloaltonetworks.com/babyshark-malware-part-two-attacks-continue-using-kimjongrat-and-pcrat/">U.S. think tanks that used the BabyShark and KimJongRAT</a> implants. <p>Another cluster, named <a href="https://github.com/Azure/Azure-Sentinel/blob/master/Detections/MultipleDataSources/CERIUMOct292020IOCs.yaml">CERIUM</a> by Microsoft, targeted <a href="https://blogs.microsoft.com/on-the-issues/2020/11/13/health-care-cyberattacks-covid-19-paris-peace-forum/">pharmaceutical firms working on COVID-19 vaccines</a>.</p>

<p><a href="https://twitter.com/issuemakerslab/status/1243185687848837120?s=20">There are claims of Kimsuky using Konni malware</a> and vice versa, but itâs best to stay away from the mess that is Konni if you possibly can.</p>

<h3 id="darkhotel">âDarkHotelâ<br /></h3>
