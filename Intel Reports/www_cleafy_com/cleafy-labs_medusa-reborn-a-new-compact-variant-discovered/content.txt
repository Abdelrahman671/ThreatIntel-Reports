The entry of new affiliates has likely driven developers to create<strong> less detectable variants</strong>, potentially to test their reliability in previously unexplored geographical regions.</p><p>In this article, we will uncover the details of our findings and understand the full scope of Medusa&#x27;s evolution, the latest detected variant, and their implications.</p></div></section><section id="3" class="bigmarginbottom"><h3 class="titlesectionclab">Historical Overview</h3><div class="blog w-richtext"><p>First identified in 2020, the Turkish-linked Medusa banking Trojan has grown on the world stage to become a significant threat. Initially targeting <strong>Turkish financial</strong> <strong>institutions</strong>, Medusa&#x27;s scope expanded rapidly by 2022, launching major campaigns in <strong>North America and Europe</strong>. </p><p>This RAT (Remote Access Trojan) grants TAs complete control of compromised devices by exploiting VNC for real-time screen sharing and accessibility services for interaction. These capabilities provide TAs the ability to perform <strong>On-Device Fraud</strong> (ODF). ODF is one of the most dangerous types of banking fraud since wire transfers are initiated from the victimâs device and can be adapted for manual or automatic approaches, such as Account Takeover (ATO) or Automatic Transfer System (ATS).</p><figure style="max-width:1266pxpx" class="w-richtext-align-fullwidth w-richtext-figure-type-image"><div><img src="https://cdn.prod.website-files.com/60201cc2b6249b0358f70f8a/66799d415097974cece1a70b_66799c5905c607f6c6f91a50_f2.png" loading="lazy" alt="Figure 2 - VNC Service Routine"/></div><figcaption>Figure 2 - VNC Service Routine</figcaption></figure><p>By exploiting accessibility services, Medusa extends its functionality beyond simple remote control. This allows the Trojan to automate several features commonly associated with modern banking Trojans, including continuous <strong>Key-Logging</strong> and<strong> Dynamic Overlay Attacks</strong>. </p><p>The following Figure represents a high-level overview of the network communications between an infected device (bot) and the assigned C2 infrastructure, taking the Key-Logging feature as an example:</p><figure style="max-width:2196pxpx" class="w-richtext-align-fullwidth w-richtext-figure-type-image"><div><img src="https://cdn.prod.website-files.com/60201cc2b6249b0358f70f8a/66799d424aa6b5ed4b6af0e5_66799c5905c607f6c6f91a51_f3.png" loading="lazy" alt="Figure 3 - Key-logging in Action"/></div><figcaption>Figure 3 - Key-logging in Action</figcaption></figure><p>The malware coordinates its functionalities through a<strong> Web Secure Socket</strong> connection to the TA&#x27;s infrastructure. The C2 server URL is dynamically fetched from public social media profiles like Telegram, Twitter, and ICQ for enhanced obfuscation. This dynamic retrieval allows attackers to update the C2 server without modifying the malware, increasing its resilience against takedown attempts. Additionally, the malware employs backup channels on these social media platforms for further redundancy.</p><figure style="max-width:2114pxpx" class="w-richtext-align-fullwidth w-richtext-figure-type-image"><div><img src="https://cdn.prod.website-files.com/60201cc2b6249b0358f70f8a/66a9ea9100b9bd631ba6e55b_667a798d41b90f8f3e773eb1_Screenshot%25202024-06-25%2520at%252010.02.07.png" loading="lazy" alt="Figure 4 - C2 extracted from Social Media profiles (e.g., Telegram)"/></div><figcaption>Figure 4 - C2 extracted from Social Media profiles (e.g., Telegram)</figcaption></figure></div></section><section id="4" class="bigmarginbottom"><h3 class="titlesectionclab">Recent Campaigns</h3><div class="blog w-richtext"><p>Since<strong> July 2023</strong>, Medusa campaigns have been reborn with a <strong>new</strong> <strong>variant</strong>, changing TTPs and country targets.
