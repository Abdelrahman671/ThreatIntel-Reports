                    <div class="entry-content entry-single clearfix">
                        
<p class="has-normal-font-size">It has now been six months since the war in Ukraine began. Since then, pro-Russian and pro-Ukrainian hacker groups, like KillNet, Anonymous, IT Army of Ukraine, Legion Spetsnaz RF, have carried out cyberattacks. A lesser-known group called <em>NoName057(16)</em> is among the pro-Russian groups attacking Ukraine and the countries surrounding it and siding with Ukraine.</p>



<p class="has-normal-font-size"><em>NoName057(16)</em> is performing DDoS attacks on websites belonging to governments, news agencies, armies, suppliers, telecommunications companies, transportation authorities, financial institutions, and more in Ukraine and neighboring countries supporting Ukraine, like Ukraine itself, Estonia, Lithuania, Norway, and Poland. A full list of the group’s targets can be found at the end of this post.&nbsp;</p>



<p class="has-normal-font-size">To carry out DDoS attacks, hacker groups utilize botnets. They control them via C&amp;C servers, sending commands to individual bots, which essentially act as soldiers. Uncovering and tracking botnets is complex and time-consuming.</p>



<p class="has-normal-font-size">We got our hands on malware called <em>Bobik</em>. <em>Bobik</em> is not new, it’s been around since 2020, and is known as a Remote Access Trojan. Things have, however, recently changed. Devices infected with <em>Bobik</em> are now part of a botnet, and carrying out DDoS attacks for <em>NoName057(16)</em>. We can confidently attribute the attacks to the group, as we have analyzed and compared what the C&amp;C server is instructing devices infected with Bobik to do with the attacks the group claims to be responsible for on their Telegram channel.</p>



<h4 class="wp-block-heading">Toolset</h4>



<p class="has-normal-font-size">The bots used by the botnet are infected with malware called <em>Bobik</em>, which is written in .NET. The malware has not been tied to a certain group in the past, and is actually a Remote Access Trojan. Its <a href="https://www.fortiguard.com/encyclopedia/virus/8065384" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">spyware functionalities</a> include keylogging, running and terminating processes, collecting system information, downloading/uploading files, and dropping further malware onto infected devices.</p>



<h5 class="wp-block-heading">Kill Chain</h5>



<p class="has-normal-font-size">In the wild, one of the most monitored droppers for <em>Bobik</em> is <em>RedLine Stealer</em>, a botnet-as-a-service cybercriminals can pay for to spread their malware of choice. The usual workflow of <em>Bobik</em> is illustrated in the image below.</p>



<p class="has-normal-font-size">At first, an unknown group seems to have purchased <em>RedLine Stealer</em> to deploy &#8211; <em>Bobik</em>. The final DDoS module deployment is composed of two basic stages. The first executes <em>Bobik’s Updater</em> via a <em>RedLine Stealer</em> bot. In the second stage, <em>Bobik’s Updater</em> extracts and drops the final DDoS module (<em>Bobik’s RuntimeBroker</em>) and ensures the module&#8217;s persistence.</p>


<div class="wp-block-image">
<figure class="aligncenter size-full"><a href="https://decoded.avast.io/wp-content/uploads/sites/2/2022/08/Fig-01-1.png"><img decoding="async" width="681" height="601" src="https://decoded.avast.io/wp-content/uploads/sites/2/2022/08/Fig-01-1.png" alt="" class="wp-image-6408" srcset="https://decoded.avast.io/wp-content/uploads/sites/2/2022/08/Fig-01-1.png 681w, https://decoded.avast.io/wp-content/uploads/sites/2/2022/08/Fig-01-1-300x265.png 300w" sizes="(max-width: 681px) 100vw, 681px" /></a><figcaption><em>Bobik deployment</em></figcaption></figure></div>


<p class="has-normal-font-size">When <em>RuntimeBroker</em> is run, the module contacts a C&amp;C server and downloads a configuration file defining targets for DDoS attacks. The module then starts the attacks using a defined count of threads, usually five threads.</p>



<p class="has-normal-font-size">The detailed workflow of the <em>Bobik</em> deployment is shown below. The <em>RedLine Stealer Cryptic</em> (installer) deobfuscates the .NET payload of <em>Bobik’s Updater</em> and injects it into the newly created process of the <em>.NET ClickOnce Launch Utility</em> (<code>AppLaunch.exe</code>); see steps 1 &#8211; 5.</p>


<div class="wp-block-image">
<figure class="aligncenter size-full"><a href="https://decoded.avast.io/wp-content/uploads/sites/2/2022/08/Fig-02-2.png"><img decoding="async" width="685" height="830" src="https://decoded.avast.io/wp-content/uploads/sites/2/2022/08/Fig-02-2.png" alt="" class="wp-image-6411" srcset="https://decoded.avast.io/wp-content/uploads/sites/2/2022/08/Fig-02-2.png 685w, https://decoded.avast.io/wp-content/uploads/sites/2/2022/08/Fig-02-2-248x300.png 248w" sizes="(max-width: 685px) 100vw, 685px" /></a><figcaption><em>Bobik deployment using RedLine Stealer Cryptic</em></figcaption></figure></div>


<p class="has-normal-font-size">The same process is used to execute <em>Bobik’s RuntimeBroker</em> (the DDoS module), because the dropped <em>RuntimeBroker </em>is also packaged and obfuscated via <em>RedLine Stealer Cryptic</em>. Therefore, the dropped <em>Bobik’s RuntimeBroker</em> also deobfuscates the .NET payload of <em><em>Bobik’s</em> RuntimeBroker</em> and injects it into another <code>AppLaunch</code> process; see steps 6 &#8211; 8. After all these steps, the Bobik’s DDoS module is deployed, persistent, and ready to attack.</p>



<h4 class="wp-block-heading">C&amp;C Servers and Communication</h4>



<p class="has-normal-font-size">Since June 1, 2022, we have observed Bobik’s network activities. <em>Bobik bots</em> communicate with C&amp;C servers located in Russia and Romania. These two servers are already offline. However, another Romanian server is still active and able to send commands to the bots.</p>



<h5 class="wp-block-heading" id="C2s">C&amp;C Servers</h5>



<p class="has-normal-font-size">Since tracking the botnet activity, we have captured three production C&amp;C servers controlling <em>Bobik bots</em> and one development server. The servers run on OS Ubuntu with Nginx (v 1.18.0). <a href="https://www.riskiq.com/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">RiskIQ</a> reports all servers as malicious with self-signed certificates and servers with bad reputations that previously hosted many suspicious services.</p>



<h6 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Server 1&nbsp;</strong></h6>



<p class="has-normal-font-size">The last active server is <code>2.57.122.243</code>, located in Romania, and its first Bobik’s activity we saw was on June 13, 2022. We also have two DNS records for this malicious server:<br><code>v9agm8uwtjmz.sytes.net</code> and <code>q7zemy6zc7ptaeks.servehttp.com</code>.</p>



<h6 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Server 2</strong></h6>



<p class="has-normal-font-size">The second server <code>2.57.122.82</code> is also in Romania, but the communication with the <em>Bobik bots</em> was deactivated around July 14, 2022. The server is still active. Nevertheless, the server responds with a <kbd>502 HTTP</kbd> code (Bad Gateway).&nbsp; Based on the findings from <strong>Server 1</strong>, this server used the same <code>v9agm8uwtjmz.sytes.net</code> DNS record, which was reconfigured to <strong>Server 1</strong> in the middle of June.</p>



<h6 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Server 3</strong></h6>



<p class="has-normal-font-size">The first Bobik’s C&amp;C server we saw was <code>77.232.41.206</code> in Russia. The server had opened ports <code>80</code> and <code>443</code> until June 9, 2022. It is not usable, and therefore <em>de facto</em> offline, by <em>Bobik bots</em> because there is only one opened port for OpenSSH since <em>Bobik</em> requires port <code>80</code> for its C&amp;C communication.</p>



<h6 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Dev Server</strong></h6>



<p class="has-normal-font-size">One of the C&amp;C servers is a suspected&nbsp; development server at <code>109.107.181.130</code>, listening on port <code>5001</code>. The server has been active since April and is located in Russia; its reputation is also suspicious. Avast has not detected any hits for this server in the wild. However, one Python sample uses the server as a testing environment.</p>



<h5 class="wp-block-heading">C&amp;C Communication</h5>



<p class="has-normal-font-size">The communication between <em>Bobik bots</em> and the C&amp;C servers is mediated using a simple unsecured HTTP request and response via the Nginx web server. The bots obtain appropriate commands from the C&amp;Cs utilizing a URL, see the diagram below.</p>



<p></p>


<div class="wp-block-image">
<figure class="aligncenter size-full"><a href="https://decoded.avast.io/wp-content/uploads/sites/2/2022/08/Fig-03-1.png"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" width="791" height="181" src="https://decoded.avast.io/wp-content/uploads/sites/2/2022/08/Fig-03-1.png" alt="" class="wp-image-6410" srcset="https://decoded.avast.io/wp-content/uploads/sites/2/2022/08/Fig-03-1.png 791w, https://decoded.avast.io/wp-content/uploads/sites/2/2022/08/Fig-03-1-300x69.png 300w, https://decoded.avast.io/wp-content/uploads/sites/2/2022/08/Fig-03-1-768x176.png 768w" sizes="(max-width: 791px) 100vw, 791px" /></a><figcaption><em>HTTP communication</em></figcaption></figure></div>


<h6 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Request</strong></h6>



<p class="has-normal-font-size">The request URL uses the following template:<br><code>http://[<strong>ip</strong>]/[<strong>request</strong>]/update?<strong>id</strong>=[sha256]&amp;<strong>v</strong>=[version]&amp;<strong>pr</strong>=[flag]</code></p>



<p class="has-normal-font-size"><strong><code>ip</code></strong>: <em>Bobik bots</em> hardcode one of the C&amp;C IPs or one of the DNS records, see Section <a href="#C2s">C&amp;C Servers</a>.<br><strong><code>request</code></strong>: defines the purpose of the communications; we registered three types of requests in the form of a GUID.<br>  &#8211; notice: the bots report their states.<br>  &#8211; admin: this request can open the admin console of the Nginx web server.<br>  &#8211; dropper: is a path to a malicious executable representing <em>Bobik’s RuntimeBroker</em> followed by an exe file name.<br>The exact GUIDs are listed in <a href="#a-guids">Appendix</a>.<br><strong><code>id</code></strong>: the hash is computed from Windows Management Instrumentation (WMI) information about a victim&#8217;s machine like <kbd>Win32_DiskDrive</kbd>, <kbd>Win32_Processor</kbd>, <kbd>Win32_BaseBoard</kbd>, etc. The hash can provide a unique identifier for <em>Bobik bots</em>.<br><strong><code>v</code></strong>: the <em>Bobik</em> version; Avast has captured sample versions ranging from 8 to 19.<br><strong><code>pr</code></strong>: is a flag (0,1) representing whether the communication with C&amp;C has timed out at least once.&nbsp;</p>



<p class="has-normal-font-size">A body of the HTTP request contains one simple XML tag with information about the victim; for instance:<br><code>&lt;client a0="1" a1="en-US" a2="en-US" a3="14:03:53" a4="600"&gt;; where</code></p>



<ul class="has-normal-font-size wp-block-list"><li><strong><code>a0</code></strong>: ProductType (1: Workstation, 2: Domain Controller, 3: Server)</li><li><strong><code>a1</code></strong>: CultureInfo.InstalledUICulture</li><li><strong><code>a2</code></strong>: CultureInfo.CurrentUICulture</li><li><strong><code>a3</code></strong>: DateTime.Now</li><li><code><strong>a4</strong>:</code> Default timeout for the update of the DDoS target list from the C&amp;C server</li></ul>



<p class="has-normal-font-size">See the examples of the notice URLs:</p>



<ul class="has-normal-font-size wp-block-list"><li><code>http://2.57.122.82/d380f816-7412-400a-9b64-78e35dd51f6e/update?id=AEF97F87751C863548359181B65B60EE86A7D44724040229CDE4622C99AB0B59&amp;v=17&amp;pr=1</code></li><li><code>http://2.57.122.82/d380f816-7412-400a-9b64-78e35dd51f6e/update?id=67F5318073F09F03E762BF727015384589F00282EA26B1798C10581B8DC27F52&amp;v=16&amp;pr=1</code></li><li><code>http://v9agm8uwtjmz.sytes.net/d380f816-7412-400a-9b64-78e35dd51f6e/update?id=B5B72AEBEC4E2E9EE0DAC37AC77EBFB679B6EC6D7EE030062ED9064282F404A7&amp;v=18&amp;pr=1</code></li><li><code>http://q7zemy6zc7ptaeks.servehttp.com/d380f816-7412-400a-9b64-78e35dd51f6e/update?id=BADFD914A37A1FF9D2CBE8C0DBD4C30A9A183E5DF85FCAE4C67851369C2BAF87&amp;v=18&amp;pr=1</code></li></ul>



<h6 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Response</strong></h6>



<p class="has-normal-font-size">The body of the HTTP response contains an encrypted and gzipped XML file configuring bots to the defined DDoS attacks. See the example below:</p>

<p class="has-normal-font-size">The bot receives the encrypted data that is decrypted using a simple algorithm, as shown below. The full script is located in the <a href="https://github.com/avast/ioc/tree/master/Bobik/decryptor.py" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">IOC repository</a>.</p>

<p class="has-normal-font-size">The encrypted XML file has an elementary structure, as shown below:</p>



<figure class="wp-block-image size-full"><a href="https://decoded.avast.io/wp-content/uploads/sites/2/2022/08/Fig-05.png"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" width="850" height="191" src="https://decoded.avast.io/wp-content/uploads/sites/2/2022/08/Fig-05.png" alt="" class="wp-image-6379" srcset="https://decoded.avast.io/wp-content/uploads/sites/2/2022/08/Fig-05.png 850w, https://decoded.avast.io/wp-content/uploads/sites/2/2022/08/Fig-05-300x67.png 300w, https://decoded.avast.io/wp-content/uploads/sites/2/2022/08/Fig-05-768x173.png 768w" sizes="(max-width: 850px) 100vw, 850px" /></a><figcaption><em>Decrypted XML config</em></figcaption></figure>



<p class="has-normal-font-size">Most of the XML attributes are intuitive, so we will just explain the compound brackets in the path and body attributes. The configuration often uses dynamically generated pieces (definitions) like this: <code>{.,15,20}</code>. The definition dictates what long random text should be generated and in which position.</p>



<p class="has-normal-font-size">The definitions are abundantly applied in the path or body of the HTTP requests, where the attackers expect an increased load on the server. The effect is that bots flood servers with meaningless requests. For instance, the first <code>&lt;task&gt;</code> in the image directly above (decrypted XML config) uses this definition: <code>query={.,15,20}</code> which means that the bots generate random texts of 15 &#8211; 20 characters long as requests to, for example, the calendar of Poland&#8217;s presidential office. Similarly, the second <code>&lt;task&gt;</code> flooded the reference system of bus lines in Ukraine with requests for a password reset, as illustrated in this definition <code>email={.,5,15}%40gmail.com</code>.</p>



<p class="has-normal-font-size">For the most part, we captured definitions sending data to login pages, password recovery sites, and site searches; as can be seen from the XML config snippet below:</p>



<ul class="has-normal-font-size wp-block-list"><li>Login data<br><code>&lt;task</code><br><code>host="identity.tele2.lt"<br>path="/Account/Login"<br>body="SkipAutoLogin=False&amp;amp;Username={.,15,20}%40gmail.com&amp;amp;Password={.,15,20}&amp;amp"</code><br><code>/&gt;</code></li><li>Search requests<br><code>&lt;task</code><br><code>host="www.delfi.ee"</code><br><code>path="/otsing?search={.,3,12}&amp;amp;domain=kinoveeb.delfi.ee&amp;amp;categoryId&amp;amp;order=PUBLISH_AT&amp;amp;from=2012-08-22T{d,2,2}%3A{d,2,2}%3A{d,2,2}Z&amp;amp;to=2022-08-22T20%3A59%3A59Z"</code><br><code>/&gt;</code></li><li>Password recovery request<br><code>&lt;task</code><br><code>host="client.smscredit.lv"<br>path="/password-recovery"<br>body="utf8=%E2%9C%93&amp;amp;authenticity_token={.87,87}A%3D%3D&amp;amp;user%5Bemail%5D={.,15,20}%40gmail.com&amp;amp;g-recaptcha-response=03ANYolqu{.,539,539}"</code><br><code>/&gt;</code></li></ul>



<p class="has-normal-font-size">Consequently, the attackers try to overload a server with these requests, as they are computationally intensive. The requests require many accesses to server databases, e.g., verifying emails for password resetting, trying to login with random data (definitions), etc.</p>



<h4 class="wp-block-heading">Bobik Botnet</h4>



<p class="has-normal-font-size">The Avast telemetry data cannot paint a precise picture of the botnet’s size, but we can estimate the approximate representation of <em>Bobik</em> in the wild, see map below. The map shows where, according to Avast’s telemetry, the bots that attempt to carry out DDoS attacks for <em>NoName057(16)</em> are located. Avast has protected these devices from <em>Bobik </em>or from connecting to the C&amp;C server. Most of the bots are located in Brazil, India, and Southeast Asia.</p>


<div class="wp-block-image">
<figure class="aligncenter size-full"><a href="https://decoded.avast.io/wp-content/uploads/sites/2/2022/08/Fig-06.png"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" width="762" height="530" src="https://decoded.avast.io/wp-content/uploads/sites/2/2022/08/Fig-06.png" alt="" class="wp-image-6382" srcset="https://decoded.avast.io/wp-content/uploads/sites/2/2022/08/Fig-06.png 762w, https://decoded.avast.io/wp-content/uploads/sites/2/2022/08/Fig-06-300x209.png 300w" sizes="(max-width: 762px) 100vw, 762px" /></a><figcaption><em>Distribution of users Avast protected from Bobik</em></figcaption></figure></div>


<p class="has-normal-font-size">According to our data, the number of <em>Bobik bots</em> is a few hundred. However, the total number must be much larger considering the DDoS attacks&#8217; acute effectiveness and frequency. We, therefore, estimate there are thousands of <em>Bobik bots</em> in the wild.</p>



<h4 class="wp-block-heading">Selection of DDoS Targets</h4>



<p class="has-normal-font-size">We estimated a procedure as to how the attackers determine which web servers to DDoS attack because we have configurations of unsuccessful attacks.</p>



<p class="has-normal-font-size">The first step is looking for a target that supports Ukraine or a target with anti-Russian views. The attackers analyze the structure of the target’s&nbsp; website and identify pages that can cause server overloading, especially requests requiring higher computing time, such as searching, password resetting, login, etc.</p>



<p class="has-normal-font-size">The second step is filling in the XML template, encrypting it, and deploying it to the C&amp;C servers. The attackers monitor the condition of the target server and modify the XML configuration based on needs (modification of URL parameters, bodies, etc.) to be more effective. The configuration is changed approximately three times per day.</p>



<p class="has-normal-font-size">Suppose the configuration is successful and a targeted server is in trouble. In that case, the configuration is fixed until the web server crashes or a server admins implement anti-DDoS technique or firewall rules based on GeoIP.</p>



<p class="has-normal-font-size">If the attack is unsuccessful, a new target is selected, and the whole procedure of selection is repeated.</p>



<h4 class="wp-block-heading">Targets</h4>



<p class="has-normal-font-size">In the first phase, the attackers targeted Ukrainian news servers they defined as being against the war in Ukraine. Then, the attacks targeted websites belonging to Ukrainian cities, local governments, distribution of electrical power, Ukrainian companies supplying the Ukraine army with weapons, railway, bus, companies, and postal offices.&nbsp;</p>



<p class="has-normal-font-size">The second phase targeted organizations publicly supporting Ukraine financially or materially, like Ukraine banks and financial institutions, and operators of local Ukraine gas reservoirs that publicly declared help for the defenders of Ukraine.</p>



<p class="has-normal-font-size">As the political situation around the war changed, so did the targets of the DDoS attacks. <em>Bobik</em> performed DDoS attacks on GKN Aerospace, which is the supplier of the Northrop Grumman Corporation because the US Defense Department convened a meeting with America&#8217;s eight prime defense contractors (including Northrop Grumman Corporation) to ensure long-term readiness to meet “<a href="https://www.aa.com.tr/en/americas/us-to-convene-meeting-with-8-defense-contractors-on-ukraine/2562185" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">Ukraine&#8217;s weapons needs</a>”.&nbsp;</p>



<p class="has-normal-font-size">Another global company under attack was <a href="https://www.g4s.com/en-gb/-/media/g4s/unitedkingdom/files/conflict_in_ukraine_march_22_v4.ashx" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">Group 4 Securitas (G4S)</a>, which published a document assessing and exploring key elements of the conflict in Ukraine. In terms of telecommunications companies, we observed an attack on American telco company Verizon, which declared a <a href="https://www.wsj.com/livecoverage/russia-ukraine-latest-news/card/verizon-waives-calling-charges-to-and-from-ukraine-cvVIf5gJouWlK3OUEGI1" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">waiver of call charges</a> to and from Ukraine. And so, we could continue listing companies that were under <em>Bobik</em> attacks due to their support for Ukraine. You can see a few screenshots from affected websites below.</p>

<h4 class="wp-block-heading">Identifying NoName057(16)</h4>



<p class="has-normal-font-size">We have tried identifying the hacker group controlling <em>Bobik bots</em> and C&amp;C servers. It was evident that the group must be pro-Russia, so we looked for the most famous DDoS attacks.</p>



<p class="has-normal-font-size">Shortly after the war in Ukraine began, a pro-Russia hacking group called Killnet appeared and began carrying out <a href="https://cybernews.com/cyber-war/hacker-wars-heat-up-as-the-pro-russian-killnet-attacks-italy" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">DDoS attacks</a> against companies and governments supporting Ukraine, and even targeted the 2022 Eurovision Song Contest.</p>



<p class="has-normal-font-size"><em>Bobik</em> initially attacked websites Killnet has marked as “undesirable”. Killnet reports their DDoS attacks on their Telegram account. At first, it looked like the attacks carried out by<em> Bobik</em> distantly resembled Killnet’s activity, because the timeline of attacked countries was similar to the XML configurations. However, many successful DDoS attacks by <em>Bobik</em> were not posted by Killnet.</p>



<p class="has-normal-font-size">On June 21, 2022, the Killnet group publicly thanked a group called <em>NoName057(16)</em> for their support during a “special military operation”:</p>


<div class="wp-block-image">

All the XML configurations we captured from the <em>NoName057(16)</em> C&amp;C servers exactly match the posts on the Telegram channel.</p>



<h5 class="wp-block-heading">NoName057(16)</h5>



<p class="has-normal-font-size"><em>NoName057(16)</em> is a little-known pro-Russian hacker group. They boast about their successful attack attempts on their Telegram channel, which has more than 14K subscribers. The group was active before we began tracking them on June 1, 2022. Their Telegram channel was created on March 11, 2022. We suspect they were either using a different set of botnets before June 1, 2022, or updating the malware used to control the bots in June.</p>



<p class="has-normal-font-size"><em>NoName057(16)</em> has been threatening to punish “propaganda” sources that “lie” about the Russian “special operation” in Ukraine, as well as governments from neighboring countries supporting them in their fight against Russia. The group became visible in the media at the beginning of August after carrying out successful attacks on <a href="https://www.hs.fi/newsinrussian/art-2000008995244.html" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">Finnish</a> and <a href="https://ceo.com.pl/rosyjska-grupa-hakerow-noname05716-atakuje-polske-i-finlandie-32079" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">Polish parliaments</a>.</p>



<p class="has-normal-font-size">A <a href="https://ru.wikipedia.org/wiki/NoName057(16)" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">Wikipedia page</a> about <em>NoName057(16)</em> was created on August 17, 2022. The page summarizes the group’s main activity. It classifies the group as a pro-Russia hacker group that claimed responsibility for cyberattacks on Ukrainian, US, and European websites belonging to government agencies, media, and private companies.</p>



<p class="has-normal-font-size"><em>NoName057(16)</em> released a manifesto declaring cyberwar as an act of revenge for open information war against Russia:</p>

<h4 class="wp-block-heading">Success Rate</h4>



<p class="has-normal-font-size">The group only reports successful DDoS attacks on their Telegram channel. Although the reported number of successful attacks seems large, statistical information indicates the contrary.</p>



<p class="has-normal-font-size">The group exclusively concentrates on DDoS attacks. They do not try to steal data or gain access to systems like other dangerous groups. The question is if they have the necessary knowledge, strength, and infrastructure to do more. Carrying out DDoS attacks is straightforward and does not require deep technical knowledge. Furthermore, the<em> Bobik</em> implementation only sends a simple HTTP request.</p>



<p class="has-normal-font-size">Our three-month observation shows that the group’s attack success is <strong>around 40%</strong>. We compared XML configurations captured by Avast to the achievements the group posts on their Telegram channel. Moreover, there is a particular set of targets, making up <strong>~20%</strong> of their posts on Telegram, <em>NoName057(16)</em> claimed they successfully attacked, but we did not match them to the targets listed in their configuration files. For example, <em>NoName057(16)</em> claims to be responsible for attacking websites belonging to Lithuanian airports on June 25, 2022:</p>

<h5 class="wp-block-heading">Impact and Protection</h5>



<p class="has-normal-font-size">The power of the DDoS attacks performed by <em>NoName057(16)</em> is debatable, to say the least. At one time, they can effectively strike about thirteen URL addresses at once, judging by configuration history, including subdomains. Furthermore, one XML configuration often includes a defined domain/target as a set of subdomains, so <em>Bobik</em> effectively attacks five different domains within one configuration. Consequently, they cannot focus on more domains for capacity and efficiency reasons.</p>



<p class="has-normal-font-size">Most of the successful attacks result in servers being down for several hours or a few days. To handle the attacks, site operators often resort to blocking queries coming from outside of their country. It is a typical and suitable solution for local servers/domains such as local ticket portals of local bus/train companies, local institutions/companies, etc. Therefore, the DDoS impact on these domains has a minimal effect on the servers of local and smaller companies. Some operators or owners of affected servers have unregistered their domains, but these are extreme cases.</p>



<p class="has-normal-font-size">The DDoS attacks carried out were more difficult to handle for some site operators of prominent and significant domains, such as banks, governments, and international companies. After a successful attack, we noticed larger companies implementing enterprise solutions, like Cloudflare or BitNinja, which can filter incoming traffic and detect DDoS attacks in most cases. On the other hand, most large, international companies expect heavier traffic and run their web servers in the Cloud with anti-DDoS solutions, making them more resilient to attacks. For example, the group was unsuccessful in taking down sites belonging to Danish bank, Danske Bank (attacked June 19 &#8211; 21, 2022), and Lithuanian bank, SEB (attacked July 12 &#8211; 13, 2022 and July 20 &#8211; 21, 2022).&nbsp;</p>



<p class="has-normal-font-size">The success of DDoS attacks depends on victim selection. The more “successful” attacks affected companies with simple sites, including about us, our mission, and a contact page, for example. These types of companies do not use their web pages as the main part of their business. These servers are therefore not typically designed to be heavily loaded and do not implement anti-DDoS techniques, making them a very easy target.</p>



<p class="has-normal-font-size">The group’s&nbsp; DDoS attack on Poznań-Ławica Airport in Poland took the site offline for 16 minutes. <em>NoName057(16)</em> configured <em>Bobik bots</em> based on the <code>&lt;tasks&gt;</code> shown in the screenshot below:</p>

<h5 class="wp-block-heading">Performance</h5>



<p class="has-normal-font-size">The botnet went into an idle state on September 1, 2022, at 6 PM UTC, and remained idle persisted for 12 hours. The botnet was reactivated on September 2, 2022, at 4 AM UTC. The XML file sent to the bots contained empty <code>&lt;tasks&gt;</code>, like in this example: <code>&lt;config&gt;&lt;tasks delay="0" thread_count="-6"/&gt;&lt;/config&gt;</code></p>



<p class="has-normal-font-size">A decline in the botnet’s performance may be a possible explanation for this. The group only posted two general posts to their Telegram channel on September 1 and 2, 2022, instead of boasting about successful attacks, our first indication the botnet might not be performing well.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>



<p class="has-normal-font-size">The first post was about the beginning of the new school year and day of knowledge. The group also mentioned being on the defense of the cyber front for their country and the for the safety of the younger generation. The second post was about “information guns and DDoS tanks” that worked quietly on very difficult and important work.</p>



<p class="has-normal-font-size">In fact, <em>NoName057(16)</em> changed targets ten times each day in the XML configurations, which is abnormal. We monitored the targets for these days, and none of the attacks were successful. Therefore, it is evident that the botnet had some trouble.</p>



<p class="has-normal-font-size">Most of the sites attacked by the group have implemented anti-DDoS protections. This slowdown&nbsp; implies that the botnet is relatively static without many changes, such as recruiting new bots or dynamically changing bots’ IPs. A static botnet is an advantage for anti-DDoS protections, because malicious traffic can be easily identified.</p>



<p class="has-normal-font-size"><em><em>NoName057(16)</em></em> has continued to attack other easier targets since September. Only the future will reveal the Bobik botnet’s successes and failures. However, the attack&#8217;s success rate has been only around 25% since the beginning of September.</p>



<h4 class="wp-block-heading">Conclusion</h4>



<p class="has-normal-font-size">We investigated and analyzed malware used to carry out DDoS attacks on sites in and around Ukraine, starting in June, 2022. We identified the malware as a .NET variant of a RAT called <em>Bobik</em>, including a DDoS module, and spreading via a bot-net-as-a-service, <em>RedLine Stealer</em>.</p>



<p class="has-normal-font-size">The first technical part of this investigation uncovered C&amp;C servers and the HTTP communication protocol used by the <em>Bobik</em> <em>bots</em>. We also successfully decrypted the HTTP protocol, including its parameters. This allowed us to monitor the C&amp;C servers and collect information about the botnet architecture and XML configurations defining the DDoS targets.</p>



<p class="has-normal-font-size">The second aim was to determine the bad actors behind the attacks. We identified a pro-Russian hacker group called <em>NoName057(16)</em>, as the users or possibly even the authors of <em>Bobik</em>, based on the XML configurations and what the group posts to their Telegram channel.</p>



<p class="has-normal-font-size"><em>NoName057(16)</em> focuses exclusively on DDoS attacks and looks for companies and organizations that support Ukraine or are “anti-Russian”. They do not try to steal data or gain access to the system like other dangerous groups. Therefore, we can declare that their activities are only harmful in the sense that they can lose companies&#8217; business while their sites are offline, but attacked sites that have gone offline have luckily recovered quickly. Their activities are more annoying than dangerous.&nbsp;</p>



<p class="has-normal-font-size">We found that the successful attacks defined by <em>NoName057(16)</em> make up just ~ 40% of all of their attack attempts. The success of their attacks depends on the quality of the targeted infrastructure. The evidence suggests that well-secured and designed servers can withstand the group’s DDoS attacks.&nbsp;</p>



<p class="has-normal-font-size">The group focuses on servers/domains as retaliation for cyber-attacks and sanctions on Russia. All successful attacks, and even successful attacks the group is not responsible for (but claims to be), are posted to their Telegram channel.</p>

                            window._nslHasOpenedPopup = true;
                            e.preventDefault();
                        }
                    }

                    if (!success) {
                        window.location = href;
                        e.preventDefault();
                    }
                }
            }
        }
