							<div class="blog-title-date-author-area">
								<h1>Lazarus campaign TTPs and evolution</h1>
								<div class="date">July 6, 2021 &nbsp;|&nbsp;
									<a href="/blogs/author/fmartinez">Fernando Martinez</a></div>
							</div>
							<div class="blog-body">
								<h2>Executive summary</h2>

<p>LevelBlue Labs&trade; has observed new activity that has been attributed to the Lazarus adversary group potentially targeting engineering job candidates and/or employees in classified engineering roles within the U.S. and Europe. This assessment is based on malicious documents believed to have been delivered by Lazarus during the last few months (spring 2021). However, historical analysis shows the lures used in this campaign to be in line with others used to target these groups.</p>

<p>The purpose of this blog is to share the new technical intelligence and provide detection options for defenders. LevelBlue Labs will continue to report on any noteworthy changes.</p>

<p>Key Takeaways:</p>

<ul>
	<li>Lazarus has been identified targeting defense contractors with malicious documents.</li>
	<li>There is a high emphasis on renaming system utilities (Certutil and Explorer) to obfuscate the adversary&rsquo;s activities (<a href="https://attack.mitre.org/techniques/T1036/003/" target="_blank">T1036.003</a>).</li>
</ul>

<h2>Background</h2>

<p>Since 2009, the known tools and capabilities believed to have been used by the Lazarus Group include DDoS botnets, keyloggers, remote access tools (RATs), and drive wiper malware. The most publicly documented malware and tools used by the group actors include Destover, Duuzer, and Hangman.</p>

<h2>Analysis</h2>

<p>Several documents identified from May to June 2021 by Twitter users were identified as being linked to the Lazarus group. Documents observed in previous campaigns lured victims with job opportunities for Boeing and BAE systems. These new documents include:</p>

<ul>
	<li><i>Rheinmetall_job_requirements.doc</i>: identified by <a href="https://twitter.com/esetresearch/status/1389904254811394049" target="_blank">ESET Research</a>.</li>
	<li><i>General_motors_cars.doc</i>: identified by Twitter user <a href="https://twitter.com/1nternaut/status/1394050982632468481" target="_blank">@1nternaut</a>.</li>
	<li><i>Airbus_job_opportunity_confidential.doc</i>: identified by <a href="https://twitter.com/360CoreSec/status/1402920149754155010" target="_blank">360CoreSec</a>.</li>
</ul>

<p>The documents attempted to impersonate new defense contractors and engineering companies like Airbus, General Motors (GM), and Rheinmetall. All of these documents contain macro malware, which has been developed and improved during the course of this campaign and from one target to another. The core techniques for the three malicious documents are the same, but the attackers attempted to reduce the potential detections and increase the faculties of the macros.</p>

<h2>First iteration: Rheinmetall</h2>

<p>The first two documents from early May 2021 were related to a German Engineering company focused on the defense and automotive industries, Rheinmetall. The second malicious document appears to include more elaborate content, which may have resulted in the documents going unnoticed by victims.</p>

<p>The Macro has base64 encoded files, which are extracted and decoded during execution. Some of the files are split inside the Macro and are not combined until the time of decoding. One of the most distinctive characteristics of this Macro is how it evades detections of a MZ header encoded in base64 (TVoA, TVpB, TVpQ, TVqA, TVqQ or TVro), by separating the first two characters from the rest of the content, as seen in Figure 1.</p>

<p><img alt="MZ header conceal" src="https://cdn.levelblue.com/blog-content/MZ_header_conceal.jpg" /></p>

<p style="text-align:center">Figure 1: Concealing of MZ header, as captured by LevelBlue Labs.</p>

<p>The rest of the content is kept together in lines of 64 characters, and because of this, YARA rules can be used to detect other, typical executable content encoded in base64 aside of the MZ header. In this case, up to nine different YARA rules alerted to suspicious encoded strings in our LevelBlue Labs analysis, like VirtualProtect, GetProcAddress, IsDebuggerPresent, GetCurrentProcessId, etc.</p>

<p><img alt="YARA detections" src="https://cdn.levelblue.com/blog-content/YARA_detections.jpg" /></p>

<p style="text-align:center">Figure 2: YARA rules detections listed in the&nbsp;LevelBlue&nbsp;Labs Open Threat Exchange, OTX.</p>

<p>All files created by the executable and used by the different Macros are located in a new folder<i> C:/Drivers </i>with the purpose of masquerading their activity. The Macro copies and renames the Microsoft legitimate executable Certutil.exe into this folder. The reason for this copy is to avoid endpoint detection and response (EDR) signatures based on system utilities executed from non-standard sources (a Microsoft Office document in this case). Additionally, the copy of Certutil is disguised to avoid using the full string, by partially replacing it with an asterisk <i>%systemroot%\system32\certut*.exe. </i>As seen in later iterations, this technique was slightly modified to further reduce the number of detections.</p>

<p>Certutil is used to decode the previously mentioned file, doing it within the same folder and with a different executable name. Once the necessary files have been decoded, the encoded files are removed from the system.</p>

<p>The script queries WMI to list all the explorer.exe processes, where it will try to inject the malicious payload. For the injection, the attackers used Mavinject (a legitimate Windows component that can be used and abused) to perform arbitrary code injections inside any running process. Mavinject.exe has been abused for several years, as indicated in this <a href="https://reaqta.com/2017/12/mavinject-microsoft-injector/" target="_blank">blog</a> from 2017.</p>

<p><img alt="Mavinject" src="https://cdn.levelblue.com/blog-content/mavinject.jpg" /></p>

<p style="text-align:center">Figure 3: Mavinject injection to explorer, as captured by LevelBlue Labs.</p>

<p><img alt="malicious document execution" src="https://cdn.levelblue.com/blog-content/malicious_document_execution.jpg" /></p>

<p style="text-align:center">Figure 4: Malicious Document execution tree listed in the LevelBlue Labs OTX.</p>

<p>The payload is in fact a downloader, which requests the next stage to a hardcoded Command and Control (C&amp;C) server and saves it to a new file. This request is made through HTTPS with hardcoded headers. In this case, the domain used as C&amp;C has been registered for several years, and the domain continues to have a long expiration date. For these reasons, this domain was assessed as a compromised domain, leveraged by Lazarus as C&amp;C.</p>

<p><img alt="hardcoded HTTP headers" src="https://cdn.levelblue.com/blog-content/hardcoded_HTTP_headers.jpg" /></p>

<p style="text-align:center">Figure 5: Hardcoded HTTP headers, as captured by LevelBlue Labs.</p>

<h2>Second iteration: General Motors</h2>

<p>A few weeks after the Rheinmetall document was observed, a very similar document emerged targeting General Motors. The characteristics of this document were very similar to the previous one, but with minor updates in the C&amp;C communication process.</p>

<p>After attempting to execute all its code, the Macro reports back to the C&amp;C server with the status of the infection. In the code, a variable is updated from 1 to 3, signaling the status of the execution. The C&amp;C is capable of tracking where its execution is failing, or encountering unexpected behavior, based on the requests made to the different documents in the C&amp;C. This includes:</p>

<ol>
	<li>During copying, decoding, renaming and executing the payload</li>
	<li>After successfully executing the payload</li>
	<li>After (and if) the payload was able to contact the C&amp;C and download a file which is saved in the C:\Drivers folder with the inf extension.</li>
</ol>

<p>The status report to the C&amp;C also requests the lure document, which at this moment is downloaded from the same C&amp;C and opened.</p>

<p><img alt="C&amp;C beacon" src="https://cdn.levelblue.com/blog-content/CC_beacon.jpg" /></p>

<p style="text-align:center">Figure 6: C&amp;C beacon to report execution status, as captured by LevelBlue Labs.</p>

<p>The domain used in this document and payload allgraphicart[.]com is no longer a compromised domain. It was first registered on April 1, 2021. However, it did not have any noteworthy activity until these malicious documents showed up, at least a month after the domain was registered. The domain was registered with Porkbun LLC, who offers domains with the free option of protecting the whois information.</p>

<h2>Third iteration: Airbus</h2>

<p>In early June, a month after the first document of this campaign was observed, a new document was identified targeting Airbus. This time, the C&amp;C communications were very similar to the previous iteration of the document; however, the execution and injection processes were different.</p>

<p>This document continues utilizing and masquerading Certutil, but the copy command had the minor addition of another asterisk to reduce the detection risk: <i>%systemroot%\system32\*ertut*.exe</i>. In addition to Certutil, the legitimate explorer executable is copied to the <i>C:\Drivers</i> folder through a similar method <i>%systemroot%\exp*.exe</i>. As previously mentioned, these files will be copied and renamed to avoid EDR signature-based detections, but this time the destination filenames were carefully chosen. If alphabetically ordered, the first two files will correspond to legitimate software, followed by the malicious files, which could mislead investigators on their first peak on the <i>C:\Drivers</i> folder.</p>

<p>This Macro contains three files encoded with base64 which are copied to disk and decoded during execution:</p>

<ul>
	<li>The first .tmp file will become a .lnk file after decoding. This lnk file is executed with Explorer and performs the next decoding.</li>
	<li>The second tmp file is concatenated (linked) with a third one, from the next-stage payload after decoding.</li>
</ul>

<p>After the actions, the Macro executes the mentioned payload with an updated technique. The attackers are no longer using Mavinject, but directly executing the payload with explorer.exe, significantly modifying the resulting execution tree as seen in Figure 10. Once the payload has been executed, the Macro waits for three seconds before creating of an .inf file in the same folder. Whether it was successfully executed or not, the Macro will proceed to send the beacon to the C&amp;C with the execution status and delete all the temporary files, attempting to clean their tracks. The only files left in C:\Drivers\ at this point are the payload and the .inf file.</p>
