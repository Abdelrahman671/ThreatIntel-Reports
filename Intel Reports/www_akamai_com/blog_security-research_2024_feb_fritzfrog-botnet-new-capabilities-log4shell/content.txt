Newer variants now read several system files on compromised hosts to detect potential targets for this attack that have a high likelihood of being vulnerable.&lt;/p>\r\n&lt;/li>\r\n&lt;li>&lt;p>The vulnerability is exploited in a brute-force manner that attempts to target as many vulnerable Java applications as possible.&lt;/p>\r\n&lt;/li>\r\n&lt;li>&lt;p>The malware also now also includes a module to exploit &lt;a href=\&#34;https://nvd.nist.gov/vuln/detail/CVE-2021-4034\&#34; target=\&#34;_blank\&#34; rel=\&#34;nofollow noreferrer\&#34;>CVE-2021-4034&lt;/a>, a privilege escalation in the polkit Linux component.     <ul>
<li><p>The Akamai Security Intelligence Group (SIG) has uncovered details about a new variant of the FritzFrog botnet, which abuses the 2021 Log4Shell vulnerability.</p>
</li>
<li><p>Over the years we have seen more than 20,000 FritzFrog attacks, and 1,500+ victims.</p>
</li>
<li><p>The malware infects internet-facing servers by brute forcing weak SSH credentials. Newer variants now read several system files on compromised hosts to detect potential targets for this attack that have a high likelihood of being vulnerable.</p>
</li>
<li><p>The vulnerability is exploited in a brute-force manner that attempts to target as many vulnerable Java applications as possible.</p>
</li>
<li><p>The malware also now also includes a module to exploit <a href="https://nvd.nist.gov/vuln/detail/CVE-2021-4034" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noreferrer">CVE-2021-4034</a>, a privilege escalation in the polkit Linux component. This module enables the malware to run as root on vulnerable servers.</p>
</li>
<li>We have included <a href="#mitigations" target="_self">indicators of compromise (IOCs) and additional mitigation measures</a> in this blog post to assist in the prevention of FritzFrog infection.</li>
</ul>

      <h2 class="cmp-title__text">Background on FritzFrog<br />
</h2>
    
</div>

    


</div>
<div class="text aem-GridColumn aem-GridColumn--default--12">
<div data-cmp-is="text" class="cmp-text" data-cmp-data-layer="{&#34;text-5e13b225a3&#34;:{&#34;@type&#34;:&#34;akamai/components/text&#34;,&#34;repo:modifyDate&#34;:&#34;2024-01-31T14:51:53Z&#34;,&#34;xdm:text&#34;:&#34;&lt;p>Akamai is continuously monitoring threats via our global network of sensors, including threats we previously discovered. Among these is the &lt;a href=\&#34;/content/akamai/en/blog/security/fritzfrog-a-new-generation-of-peer-to-peer-botnets.html\&#34; target=\&#34;_self\&#34;>FritzFrog botnet&lt;/a> (originally identified &lt;a href=\&#34;/content/akamai/en/blog/security/fritzfrog-a-new-generation-of-peer-to-peer-botnets.html\&#34; target=\&#34;_self\&#34;>in 2020&lt;/a>) a sophisticated, Golang-based peer-to-peer botnet compiled to support both AMD- and ARM-based machines. The malware is actively maintained and has &lt;a href=\&#34;/content/akamai/en/blog/security-research/fritzfrog-p2p.html\&#34; target=\&#34;_self\&#34;>evolved over the years&lt;/a> by adding and improving capabilities.&lt;/p>\r\n&lt;p>FritzFrog has traditionally hopped around by using SSH brute force, and has successfully compromised thousands of targets over the years as a result. Each compromised host becomes part of FritzFrog’s network — it communicates with its infected peers to share information, payloads, and configuration.&lt;/p>\r\n&lt;p>Thanks to the consistent upkeep, the malware includes many interesting features in its arsenal, including the additions we’ll discuss in this blog, such as the &lt;b>introduction of Log4Shell exploitation&lt;/b>.     <p>Akamai is continuously monitoring threats via our global network of sensors, including threats we previously discovered. Among these is the <a href="/blog/security/fritzfrog-a-new-generation-of-peer-to-peer-botnets" target="_self">FritzFrog botnet</a> (originally identified <a href="/blog/security/fritzfrog-a-new-generation-of-peer-to-peer-botnets" target="_self">in 2020</a>) a sophisticated, Golang-based peer-to-peer botnet compiled to support both AMD- and ARM-based machines. The malware is actively maintained and has <a href="/blog/security-research/fritzfrog-p2p" target="_self">evolved over the years</a> by adding and improving capabilities.</p>
<p>FritzFrog has traditionally hopped around by using SSH brute force, and has successfully compromised thousands of targets over the years as a result. Each compromised host becomes part of FritzFrog’s network — it communicates with its infected peers to share information, payloads, and configuration.</p>
<p>Thanks to the consistent upkeep, the malware includes many interesting features in its arsenal, including the additions we’ll discuss in this blog, such as the <b>introduction of Log4Shell exploitation</b>. For example, it attempts to avoid touching the disk to limit detection opportunities, supports communication over TOR, and <b>even has an “AntiVirus” module that kills competing malware</b>.</p>

      <h2 class="cmp-title__text">Using Log4Shell as an infection vector<br />
</h2>
    
</div>

    


</div>
<div class="text text__pb-20 aem-GridColumn aem-GridColumn--default--12">
<div data-cmp-is="text" class="cmp-text" data-cmp-data-layer="{&#34;text-63ca31fbc8&#34;:{&#34;@type&#34;:&#34;akamai/components/text&#34;,&#34;repo:modifyDate&#34;:&#34;2024-01-31T15:20:29Z&#34;,&#34;xdm:text&#34;:&#34;&lt;p>Traditionally, FritzFrog relied on SSH brute force as its sole infection vector, but recent versions of the malware now include a new one: &lt;a href=\&#34;/content/akamai/en/blog/security/threat-intelligence-on-log4j-cve-key-findings-and-their-implications.html\&#34; target=\&#34;_self\&#34;>Log4Shell&lt;/a> exploitation, which in our pond is known as the toadally rad “Frog4Shell”.&lt;/p>\r\n&lt;p>The Log4Shell vulnerability was initially identified in December 2021 and triggered an industry-wide patching frenzy that lasted for months. Even today, 2 years later, there are many internet-facing applications that are still vulnerable to this exploit.&lt;/p>\r\n&lt;p>Vulnerable internet-facing assets are a serious problem, but FritzFrog actually poses a risk to an additional type of assets — internal hosts. When the vulnerability was first discovered, internet-facing applications were prioritized for patching because of their significant risk of compromise. &lt;b>Contrastly, internal machines, which were less likely to be exploited, were often neglected and remained unpatched — a circumstance that FritzFrog takes advantage of.&lt;/b>&lt;/p>\r\n&lt;p>As part of its spreading routine, the malware attempts to target all hosts in the internal network. It does so by calling the &lt;i>net__Interface_Addrs&lt;/i> standard Go function to identify reachable subnets and target the possible addresses in each of them. In Figure 1, we can see the malware attempting to connect to all the addresses in the local network.&lt;/p>\r\n&#34;}}">
    <p>Traditionally, FritzFrog relied on SSH brute force as its sole infection vector, but recent versions of the malware now include a new one: <a href="/blog/security/threat-intelligence-on-log4j-cve-key-findings-and-their-implications" target="_self">Log4Shell</a> exploitation, which in our pond is known as the toadally rad “Frog4Shell”.</p>
<p>The Log4Shell vulnerability was initially identified in December 2021 and triggered an industry-wide patching frenzy that lasted for months. Even today, 2 years later, there are many internet-facing applications that are still vulnerable to this exploit.</p>
<p>Vulnerable internet-facing assets are a serious problem, but FritzFrog actually poses a risk to an additional type of assets — internal hosts. When the vulnerability was first discovered, internet-facing applications were prioritized for patching because of their significant risk of compromise. <b>Contrastly, internal machines, which were less likely to be exploited, were often neglected and remained unpatched — a circumstance that FritzFrog takes advantage of.</b></p>
<p>As part of its spreading routine, the malware attempts to target all hosts in the internal network. It does so by calling the <i>net__Interface_Addrs</i> standard Go function to identify reachable subnets and target the possible addresses in each of them. In Figure 1, we can see the malware attempting to connect to all the addresses in the local network.</p>

    <p>The <a href="/blog/security/ransomware-on-the-move-evolving-exploitation-techniques" target="_self">shift in tactics toward exploitation</a> was a major trend for threat actors in 2023 — one-day and zero-day <a href="/blog/security-research/moveit-sqli-zero-day-exploit-clop-ransomware" target="_self">exploits were used extensively</a> and proved to be some of the most effective methods to breach into organizations.</p>
<p>FritzFrog’s addition of exploitation capabilities to its arsenal shows a similar shift in this direction. The additional infection vector that is abusing the Log4Shell vulnerability, and the pkexec exploit module are two additions explored in this blog post that exemplify this shift. We believe that this trend will continue in upcoming FritzFrog versions, and it's likely only a matter of time before additional exploits are added to the malware.</p>
<p>The Akamai SIG will continue to monitor this threat and others like it and publish our findings. To keep up with FritzFrog updates and other security research, you can <a href="https://twitter.com/akamai_research" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noreferrer">follow us on X</a> (formerly Twitter).</p>
