													<h2 id="introduction">Introduction</h2>
<p>In the nebula of Chinese-speaking threat actors, it is quite common to see tools and methodologies being shared. One such example of this is the infamous &#8220;DLL side-loading triad&#8221;: a legitimate executable, a malicious DLL to be <a href="https://attack.mitre.org/techniques/T1574/002/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">sideloaded</a> by it, and an encoded payload, generally dropped from a self-extracting archive. Initially considered to be the signature of LuckyMouse, we observed other groups starting to use similar &#8220;triads&#8221; such as HoneyMyte. While it implies that it is not possible to attribute attacks based on this technique alone, it also follows that efficient detection of such triads reveals more and more malicious activity.</p>
<p>The investigation described in this article started with one such file which caught our attention due to the various improvements it brought to this well-known infection vector.</p>
<h2 id="foundcore-loader">FoundCore Loader</h2>
<p>This malware sample was discovered in the context of an attack against a high-profile organization located in Vietnam. From a high-level perspective, the infection chain follows the expected execution flow:</p>
<p><a href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/04/06085101/Cycldek_01.jpg" class="magnificImage"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/04/06085101/Cycldek_01.jpg" alt="" width="1237" height="519" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-101783" srcset="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/04/06085101/Cycldek_01.jpg 1237w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/04/06085101/Cycldek_01-300x126.jpg 300w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/04/06085101/Cycldek_01-1024x430.jpg 1024w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/04/06085101/Cycldek_01-768x322.jpg 768w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/04/06085101/Cycldek_01-417x175.jpg 417w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/04/06085101/Cycldek_01-370x155.jpg 370w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/04/06085101/Cycldek_01-667x280.jpg 667w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/04/06085101/Cycldek_01-800x336.jpg 800w" sizes="(max-width: 1237px) 100vw, 1237px" /></a></p>
<p>After being loaded by a legitimate component from Microsoft Outlook (FINDER.exe, MD5 <a href="https://opentip.kaspersky.com/9F1D6B2D45F1173215439BCC4B00B6E3/?utm_source=SL&amp;utm_medium=SL&amp;utm_campaign=SL" target="_blank" rel="noopener">9F1D6B2D45F1173215439BCC4B00B6E3</a>), outlib.dll (MD5 <a href="https://opentip.kaspersky.com/F267B1D3B3E16BE366025B11176D2ECB/?utm_source=SL&amp;utm_medium=SL&amp;utm_campaign=SL" target="_blank" rel="noopener">F267B1D3B3E16BE366025B11176D2ECB</a>) hijacks the intended execution flow of the program to decode and run a shellcode placed in a binary file, rdmin.src (MD5 <a href="https://opentip.kaspersky.com/DF46DA80909A6A641116CB90FA7B8258/?utm_source=SL&amp;utm_medium=SL&amp;utm_campaign=SL" target="_blank" rel="noopener">DF46DA80909A6A641116CB90FA7B8258</a>). Such shellcodes that we had seen so far, however, did not involve any form of obfuscation. So, it was a rather unpleasant surprise for us when we discovered the first instructions:</p>
<p><a href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/04/01140032/Cycldek_02.png" class="magnificImage"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-101722" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/04/01140032/Cycldek_02.png" alt="" width="687" height="238" srcset="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/04/01140032/Cycldek_02.png 687w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/04/01140032/Cycldek_02-300x104.png 300w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/04/01140032/Cycldek_02-505x175.png 505w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/04/01140032/Cycldek_02-370x128.png 370w" sizes="(max-width: 687px) 100vw, 687px" /></a></p>
<p>Experienced reverse-engineers will immediately recognize disassembler-desynchronizing constructs in the screenshot above. The conditional jumps placed at offsets 7 and 9 appear to land in the middle of an address (as evidenced by the label loc_B+1), which is highly atypical for well-behaved assembly code. Immediately after, we note the presence of a call instruction whose destination (highlighted in red) is identified as bogus by IDA Pro, and the code that follows doesn&#8217;t make any sense.</p>
<p>Explaining what is going on requires taking a step back and providing a bit of background about how disassemblers work. At the risk of oversimplifying, flow-oriented disassemblers make a number of assumptions when processing files. One of them is that, when they encounter a conditional jump, they start disassembling the &#8220;false&#8221; branch first, and come back to the &#8220;true&#8221; branch later on. This process is better evidenced by looking at the opcodes corresponding to the code displayed above, again starting from offset 7:</p>
<p style="text-align: center"><a href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/04/01140052/Cycldek_03.png" class="magnificImage"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-101723" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/04/01140052/Cycldek_03.png" alt="" width="499" height="199" srcset="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/04/01140052/Cycldek_03.png 499w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/04/01140052/Cycldek_03-300x120.png 300w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/04/01140052/Cycldek_03-439x175.png 439w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/04/01140052/Cycldek_03-370x148.png 370w" sizes="(max-width: 499px) 100vw, 499px" /></a></p>
<p>It is now more obvious that there are two ways to interpret the code above: the disassembler can either start from &#8220;E8&#8221;, or from &#8220;81&#8221; – by default, IDA will choose the latter: E8 is in fact the opcode for the call instruction. But astute readers will notice that &#8220;JLE&#8221; (jump if lower or equal) and &#8220;JG&#8221; (jump if greater) are opposite conditions: no matter what, one of those will always be true and as such the actual code, as seen by the CPU during the execution, will start with the byte &#8220;81&#8221;. Such constructs are called <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Opaque_predicate" target="_blank" rel="noopener">opaque predicates</a>, and this E8 byte in the middle was only added there in order to trick the disassembler.</p>
<p>Defeating this trick is but a trivial matter for IDA Pro, as it is possible to manually correct the disassembling mistake. However, it was immediately obvious that the shellcode had been processed by an automated obfuscation tool. Opaque predicates, sometimes in multiples, and dead code were inserted between every single instruction of the program. In the end, cleaning up the program automatically was the only practical approach, and we did so by modifying an <a href="https://github.com/RolfRolles/FinSpyVM/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">existing script</a> for the FinSpy malware family created by the respected reverse-engineer Rolf Rolles.</p>
<p>This step allowed us to discover the shellcode&#8217;s purpose: to decrypt and decompress the final payload, using a combination of RC4 and LZNT1. Even then, it turned out that the attackers had more tricks up their sleeve. Normally, at this stage, one would have expected to find a PE file that the shellcode would load into memory. But instead, this is what we got:</p>
<p><a href="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/04/01140315/Cycldek_04.png" class="magnificImage"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-101724" src="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/04/01140315/Cycldek_04.png" alt="" width="2105" height="1547" srcset="https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/04/01140315/Cycldek_04.png 2105w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/04/01140315/Cycldek_04-300x220.png 300w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/04/01140315/Cycldek_04-1024x753.png 1024w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/04/01140315/Cycldek_04-768x564.png 768w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/04/01140315/Cycldek_04-1536x1129.png 1536w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/04/01140315/Cycldek_04-2048x1505.png 2048w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/04/01140315/Cycldek_04-238x175.png 238w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/04/01140315/Cycldek_04-370x272.png 370w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/04/01140315/Cycldek_04-381x280.png 381w, https://media.kasperskycontenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2021/04/01140315/Cycldek_04-800x588.png 800w" sizes="(max-width: 2105px) 100vw, 2105px" /></a><br />
The recovered file was indeed a PE, but it turned out that most of its headers had been scrubbed. In fact, even the scarce ones remaining contained incoherent values – for instance, here, a number of declared sections equal to 0xAD4D. Since it is the shellcode (and not the Windows loader) that prepares this file for execution, it doesn&#8217;t matter that some information, such as the magic numbers, is missing. For instance, in the decompiled code above (as for all references to the file&#8217;s number of sections) the value read in the headers is subtracted by 44361. For the attackers, the advantage is two-fold. First, it makes acquiring the final payload statically a lot more difficult for potential reverse-engineers. Second, it also ensures that the various components of the toolchain remain tightly coupled to each other. If only a single one of them finds itself uploaded to a multi-scanner website, it will be unexploitable for defenders. This is a design philosophy that we had observed from the LuckyMouse APT in the past, and is manifest in other parts of this toolchain too, as we will see later on. Eventually, we were able to reconstruct the file&#8217;s headers and move on with our analysis – but we found this loader so interesting from an educational standpoint that we decided to base one track of our online reverse-engineering course on it. For more detailed steps on how we approached this sample, please have a look at <a href="https://xtraining.kaspersky.com/courses/targeted-malware-reverse-engineering" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Targeted Malware Reverse Engineering</a>.</p>
<h2 id="foundcore-payload">FoundCore payload</h2>
<p>The final payload is a remote administration tool that provides full control over the victim machine to its operators. Upon execution, this malware starts 4 threads:</p>
<ul>
<li>The first one establishes persistence by creating a service.</li>
<li>The second one sets inconspicuous information for the service by changing its &#8220;Description&#8221;, &#8220;ImagePath&#8221;, &#8220;DisplayName&#8221; fields (among others).</li>
<li>The third sets an empty DACL (corresponding to the SDDL string &#8220;D:P&#8221;) to the image associated to the current process in order to prevent access to the underlying malicious file.</li>
<li>Finally, a worker thread bootstraps execution and establishes connection with the C2 server. Depending on its configuration, it may also inject a copy of itself to another process.</li>
</ul>
<p>Communications with the server can take place either over raw TCP sockets encrypted with RC4, or via HTTPS. Commands supported by FoundCore include filesystem manipulation, process manipulation, screenshot captures and arbitrary command execution.</p>
<h2 id="royalroad-documents-dropphone-and-coreloader">RoyalRoad documents, DropPhone and CoreLoader</h2>
<p>Taking a step back from the FoundCore malware family, we looked into the various victims we were able to identify to try to gather information about the infection process. In the vast majority of the incidents we discovered, it turned out that FoundCore executions were preceded by the opening of a malicious RTF documents downloaded from static.phongay[.]com. They all were generated using <a href="https://malpedia.caad.fkie.fraunhofer.de/details/win.8t_dropper" target="_blank" rel="noopener">RoyalRoad</a> and attempt to exploit CVE-2018-0802.</p>
<p>Interestingly, while we would have expected them to contain decoy content, all of them were blank. We, therefore, hypothesize the existence of precursor documents, possibly delivered through spear-phishing, or precursor infections, which would trigger the download of one of these RTF files.</p>
<p>Successful exploitation leads to the deployment of yet another malware that we named DropPhone:</p>

<p>Finally, CoreLoader, the last malware we found associated to this set of activity, is a simple shellcode loader which performs anti-analysis and loads additional code from a file named WsmRes.xsl. Again, this specific file eluded our attempts to catch it but we suspect it to be, one way or another, related to FoundCore (described in the previous section).</p>

<h2 id="victimology-and-attribution">Victimology and attribution</h2>
<p>We observed this campaign between June 2020 and January 2021. According to our telemetry, dozens of organizations were affected. 80% of them are based in Vietnam and belong to the government or military sector, or are otherwise related to the health, diplomacy, education or political verticals. We also identified occasional targets in Central Asia and in Thailand.</p>
<p>For the reasons laid-out in the introduction, attribution based on tooling alone is risky when it comes to this nebula. At first glance, the use of a &#8220;triad&#8221;, the general design philosophy and the obvious effort spent to make reverse-engineering as complex as possible are reminiscent of LuckyMouse. However, we also observed code similarities between CoreLoader or FoundCore and programs associated with the Cycldek threat actor – namely, RedCore Loader (MD5: <a href="https://opentip.kaspersky.com/1B6BCBB38921CAF347DF0A21955771A6/?utm_source=SL&amp;utm_medium=SL&amp;utm_campaign=SL" rel="noopener" target="_blank">1B6BCBB38921CAF347DF0A21955771A6</a>).</p>
<p>While Cycldek was, so far, considered to be one of the lesser sophisticated threat actors from the Chinese-speaking nexus, its targeting is known to be consistent with what we observed in this campaign. Therefore, we are linking the activities described in this post with Cycldek with low confidence.</p>
<input type="hidden" class="category_for_banner" data-type="posts" value="rema-training-cycledeck" />
<h2 id="conclusion">Conclusion</h2>
<p>No matter which group orchestrated this campaign, it constitutes a significant step up in terms of sophistication. The toolchain presented here was willfully split into a series of interdependent components that function together as a whole. Single pieces are difficult – sometimes impossible – to analyze in isolation, because they rely on code or data provided at other stages of the infection chain. We regretfully admit that this strategy was partly successful in preventing us from obtaining a complete picture of this campaign. As such, this report is as much about the things we know as it is about figuring out what we don&#8217;t. We hereby extend our hand to fellow researchers who might be seeing other pieces of this vast puzzle, because we strongly believe that the challenges ahead of us can only be overcome through information sharing among trusted industry partners.</p>
<p>Some readers from other regions of the world might dismiss this local activity as irrelevant to their interests. We would advise them to take heed. Experience shows that regional threat actors sometimes widen their area of activity as their operational capabilities increase, and that tactics or tools are vastly shared across distinct actors or intrusion-sets that target different regions. Today, we see a group focused on South-East Asia taking a major leap forward. Tomorrow, they may decide they&#8217;re ready to take on the whole world.</p>
