							APT Group Kimsuky Targets University Researchers						</h1>
						<div class="blog2__text">

								by <u>Resilience Threat Intelligence</u>

					<h3><strong>Executive Summary</strong></h3>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Kimsuky is a North Korean APT group tasked with global intelligence collection operations aligned with the North Korean government&#8217;s interests. The group has been active since at least 2012 and has a particular interest in South Korean think tanks and government entities; however, it also targets the United States, the United Kingdom, and other European countries. Kimsuky specializes in targeted phishing campaigns, leveraging malicious attachments in follow-on emails after establishing trust through email correspondence [1][2]. </span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">In the Spring of 2024, the NSA and FBI released a joint cybersecurity advisory pointing to the Democratic People’s Republic of Korea (DPRK, a.k.a. North Korea), specifically Kimsuky exploiting improperly configured DNS Domain-based Message Authentication, Reporting and Conformance (DMARC) record policies to conceal social engineering attempts [3]. This advisory characterized DPRK’s tendency to pose as academics, journalists, or other East Asian experts for campaigns against South Korea, the United States, and Europe [2][3].</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">In late July 2024, Resilience analysts observed an Operational Security (OPSEC) mistake made by threat actors assessed to be Kimsuky. Our analysts took advantage of this to collect a trove of source code, login credentials, traffic logs, and internal notes. From the data we collected, we assess that Kimsuky is phishing University staff, researchers, and professors to conduct espionage. Once inside University networks, Kimsuky can pilfer research and intelligence for the North Korean government. </span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">These actions align with the goals of the Reconnaissance General Bureau (RGB), which is the primary foreign intelligence-gathering organization of the North Korean government. Kimsuky has previously attempted to steal nuclear weapons research, healthcare, and pharmaceutical secrets [4]. In some instances, Kimsuky has also been identified as conducting financially motivated cybercrime, believed to be funding their espionage activities [4]. North Korea seeks to use this information to improve its country, which severely lacks an established scientific community.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">The technical details and recommendations below provide additional information on how Kimsuky operates and targets Universities to further North Korean espionage operations.</span></p>
<h3><strong>Resilience Recommendations</strong></h3>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Resilience recommends the following high-level strategies for persons who may be the target of a state-backed campaign:</span></p>
<ul>
<li style="font-weight: 400;" aria-level="1"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Leverage phish-resistant Multi-factor authentication such as FIDO-compliant hardware tokens or push-based mobile applications. </span></li>
<li style="font-weight: 400;" aria-level="1"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Always double-check that the URL you are logging into matches the page you expect to be logged into. Some password managers will do this automatically. </span></li>
<li style="font-weight: 400;" aria-level="1"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Review and test the Breach and Attack Simulation packages that simulate Kimsuky activity. </span></li>
</ul>
<h3><strong>Technical Details</strong></h3>
<p><b>Staging Infrastructure</b></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Previous reports from CISA indicate that Kimsuky uses compromised internet hosts to stage their attacks. From the data we collected, they had access to audko[.]store, dorray[.]site, nusiu[.]live, osihi[.]store, simos[.]online, sorsi[.]online, wodos[.]online, and wodods[.]xyz. </span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">After gaining access to infrastructure to conduct their attacks, the threat group staged a webshell called “Green Dinosaur” on the host. This webshell allows operators to upload, download, rename, and delete files. This webshell appears to be based on code taken from an old webshell called Indrajith Mini Shell 2.0 [5]. Green Dinosaur, however, removed functionality in a way that limits detection possibilities while allowing the features for operators to put phishing websites into place. </span></p>
<div id="attachment_3225" style="width: 642px" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><picture><source srcset="https://www.cyberresilience.com/wp-content/webp-express/webp-images/uploads/2024/08/greendino-400x100.png.webp 400w, https://www.cyberresilience.com/wp-content/webp-express/webp-images/uploads/2024/08/greendino-1024x256.png.webp 1024w, https://www.cyberresilience.com/wp-content/webp-express/webp-images/uploads/2024/08/greendino-768x192.png.webp 768w, https://www.cyberresilience.com/wp-content/webp-express/webp-images/uploads/2024/08/greendino.png.webp 1211w" sizes="(max-width: 632px) 100vw, 632px" type="image/webp"><img decoding="async" aria-describedby="caption-attachment-3225" class="wp-image-3225 webpexpress-processed" src="https://www.cyberresilience.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/08/greendino-400x100.png" alt="Image: “Green Dinosaur” webshell" width="632" height="158" srcset="https://www.cyberresilience.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/08/greendino-400x100.png 400w, https://www.cyberresilience.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/08/greendino-1024x256.png 1024w, https://www.cyberresilience.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/08/greendino-768x192.png 768w, https://www.cyberresilience.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/08/greendino.png 1211w" sizes="(max-width: 632px) 100vw, 632px"></picture><p id="caption-attachment-3225" class="wp-caption-text">Image: “Green Dinosaur” webshell</p></div>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">The GreenDinosaur samples we obtained from Kimsuky’s infrastructure are all obfuscated with multiple layers of base64 and gz deflated strings, likely to prevent detection by hosting companies. The final layer was a mixture of base64 and multi-character substitution using the PHP function `strtr`.</span></p>

<p><b>Phishing Pages</b></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">After setting up Green Dinosaur, the operators upload prebuilt phishing pages. These phishing pages are created by first scraping the real login portals they are imitating. The scraped page is then modified with additional code to log any entered credentials. In this campaign phishing pages were created for Dongduk University, Korea University, and Yonsei University. Additionally, we discovered a phishing toolkit for stealing Naver accounts.</span></p>
<p><b>Dongduk University</b></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">The Dongduk University phishing page is visually identical to the real Dongduk email login page but contains modifications to weaponize it.</span></p>

<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Additionally on Kimsuky’s phishing sites, there is a non-target specific phishing toolkit to gather Naver accounts. This toolkit is a rudimentary proxy akin to Evilginx for stealing cookies and credentials from visitors and shows popups telling users they need to login again because communication with the server was disrupted.</span></p>
<div id="attachment_3233" style="width: 493px" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><picture><source srcset="https://www.cyberresilience.com/wp-content/webp-express/webp-images/uploads/2024/08/NaverPopup-400x150.png.webp 400w, https://www.cyberresilience.com/wp-content/webp-express/webp-images/uploads/2024/08/NaverPopup.png.webp 527w" sizes="(max-width: 483px) 100vw, 483px" type="image/webp"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" aria-describedby="caption-attachment-3233" class="wp-image-3233  webpexpress-processed" src="https://www.cyberresilience.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/08/NaverPopup-400x150.png" alt="Image: Screenshot of Kimsuky’s malicious popup telling user to login again due to server communication error" width="483" height="181" srcset="https://www.cyberresilience.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/08/NaverPopup-400x150.png 400w, https://www.cyberresilience.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/08/NaverPopup.png 527w" sizes="(max-width: 483px) 100vw, 483px"></picture><p id="caption-attachment-3233" class="wp-caption-text">Image: Screenshot of Kimsuky’s malicious popup telling user to login again due to server communication error</p></div>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">After clicking ok on the popup, the victim is redirected to a fake Naver login page. </span></p>
<div id="attachment_3232" style="width: 691px" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><picture><source srcset="https://www.cyberresilience.com/wp-content/webp-express/webp-images/uploads/2024/08/NaverLogin-400x207.png.webp 400w, https://www.cyberresilience.com/wp-content/webp-express/webp-images/uploads/2024/08/NaverLogin-1024x531.png.webp 1024w, https://www.cyberresilience.com/wp-content/webp-express/webp-images/uploads/2024/08/NaverLogin-768x398.png.webp 768w, https://www.cyberresilience.com/wp-content/webp-express/webp-images/uploads/2024/08/NaverLogin.png.webp 1211w" sizes="(max-width: 681px) 100vw, 681px" type="image/webp"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" aria-describedby="caption-attachment-3232" class=" wp-image-3232 webpexpress-processed" src="https://www.cyberresilience.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/08/NaverLogin-400x207.png" alt="Image: Screenshot of Kimsuky’s malicious Naver login page" width="681" height="352" srcset="https://www.cyberresilience.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/08/NaverLogin-400x207.png 400w, https://www.cyberresilience.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/08/NaverLogin-1024x531.png 1024w, https://www.cyberresilience.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/08/NaverLogin-768x398.png 768w, https://www.cyberresilience.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/08/NaverLogin.png 1211w" sizes="(max-width: 681px) 100vw, 681px"></picture><p id="caption-attachment-3232" class="wp-caption-text">Image: Screenshot of Kimsuky’s malicious Naver login page</p></div>
<p><b>SendMail</b></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">To get targets to visit these phishing sites, Kimsuky uploaded a custom PHPMailer implementation using GreenDinosaur simply called “SendMail”. For the campaign we observed, this PHPMailer utilized the compromised email account of a professor at Seoul National University to access a SMTP server hosted in South Korea for the CRM app Dooray. In SendMail, multiple gmail and daum accounts were also used as source addresses. In a text file of notes written by the operators, several gmail and daum accounts were listed with their password, recovery email, and 2fa backup codes. These email accounts were used to send phishing emails to employees of Dongduk University, Korea University, Yonsei University, and other targets. Links in the notes also point to the “send” tool being hosted at “http://penlu[.]or[.]kr/data/view[.]php” at some point. All of the email accounts we discovered had the same password “1qaz2wsx#EDC$RFV” and the same recovery email: “nasndaoop@gmail[.]com”.</span></p>
<p><strong>Phishing Accounts</strong></p>

<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">The configuration for SendMail we obtained was preloaded for multiple Naver themed phishing email with the subject line “네이버 아이디 탈퇴가 완료되었습니다”(Translation: Naver ID was successfully deleted) and “[네이버]메일 송수신 기능 제한 알림.” (Translation: There are restrictions with sending and receiving Naver mail.) These and other phishing emails linked back to numerous phishing sites.</span></p>
<div id="attachment_3234" style="width: 803px" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><picture><source srcset="https://www.cyberresilience.com/wp-content/webp-express/webp-images/uploads/2024/08/SendMail-400x368.png.webp 400w, https://www.cyberresilience.com/wp-content/webp-express/webp-images/uploads/2024/08/SendMail-1024x941.png.webp 1024w, https://www.cyberresilience.com/wp-content/webp-express/webp-images/uploads/2024/08/SendMail-768x706.png.webp 768w, https://www.cyberresilience.com/wp-content/webp-express/webp-images/uploads/2024/08/SendMail-1536x1412.png.webp 1536w, https://www.cyberresilience.com/wp-content/webp-express/webp-images/uploads/2024/08/SendMail.png.webp 1736w" sizes="(max-width: 793px) 100vw, 793px" type="image/webp"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" aria-describedby="caption-attachment-3234" class=" wp-image-3234 webpexpress-processed" src="https://www.cyberresilience.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/08/SendMail-400x368.png" alt="Image: Screenshot of Kimsuky’s SendMail tool" width="793" height="730" srcset="https://www.cyberresilience.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/08/SendMail-400x368.png 400w, https://www.cyberresilience.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/08/SendMail-1024x941.png 1024w, https://www.cyberresilience.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/08/SendMail-768x706.png 768w, https://www.cyberresilience.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/08/SendMail-1536x1412.png 1536w, https://www.cyberresilience.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/08/SendMail.png 1736w" sizes="(max-width: 793px) 100vw, 793px"></picture><p id="caption-attachment-3234" class="wp-caption-text">Image: Screenshot of Kimsuky’s SendMail tool</p></div>
