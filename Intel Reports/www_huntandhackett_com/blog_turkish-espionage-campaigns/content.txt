<h2 style="text-align: left;"><span style="color: #080630;">Background, Motivations &amp; Targets&nbsp;</span></h2>
<p><span data-contrast="auto">Hunt &amp; Hackett believes that Sea Turtle is a Turkey based Advanced Persistent Threat (APT) actor that is motivated by espionage by means of information theft that targets public and private entities. From 2017 to 2019, this actor has been mainly known for DNS hijacking<a href="#tur1" rel="noopener"><span style="font-size: 14px;">[1]</span></a></span><span data-contrast="auto"> to achieve their ultimate objectives. The threat actor has since continued to target similar sectors but has altered its capabilities in a likely attempt to evade detection. </span><span data-contrast="auto">Since then, the public information on this threat actor has remained limited. In October 2021, Microsoft<a href="#tur2" rel="noopener"><span style="font-size: 14px;">[2]</span></a></span><span data-contrast="auto"> shed light on SILICON, also recognized as Sea Turtle<a href="#tur3" rel="noopener"><span style="font-size: 14px;">[3]</span></a></span><span data-contrast="auto">, revealing their pursuit of intelligence gathering aligned with strategic Turkish interests. Other organizations such as the Greek National CERT<a href="#tur4" rel="noopener"><span style="font-size: 14px;">[4]</span></a></span><span data-contrast="auto"> have observed this actor as well and shared a number of Indicators of Compromise (IOCs) related to this group and their modus operandi in 2022. Other than that, the flow of information has remained limited, and this actor seemed to be operating primarily under the radar.</span><span data-contrast="auto"> </span><span data-contrast="auto">The limited public knowledge base was recently enriched with the PwC threat intelligence report<a href="#tur5" rel="noopener"><span style="font-size: 14px;">[5]</span></a></span><span data-contrast="auto"> </span><i><span data-contrast="auto">‘The Tortoise and The Malwahare</span></i><span data-contrast="auto">’, and a&nbsp; blogpost by StrikeReady<a href="#tur6" rel="noopener"><span style="font-size: 14px;">[6]</span></a></span><span data-contrast="auto">, detailing this threat actor’s methods. </span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335551550&quot;:6,&quot;335551620&quot;:6,&quot;335559740&quot;:276}">&nbsp;</span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335551550&quot;:6,&quot;335551620&quot;:6,&quot;335559740&quot;:276}">&nbsp;</span></p>
<p><span data-contrast="auto">What is known to date is that the Sea Turtle group focuses primarily on targeting organizations in Europe and the Middle East. </span><span data-contrast="auto">Research suggests this threat actor primarily focuses on governmental bodies, Kurdish (political) groups such as PKK, NGOs, telecommunication entities, ISPs, IT service providers, and Media &amp; Entertainment organisations, mainly aiming at repositories housing valuable and sensitive data. As noted by PwC, telecommunication companies safeguard customer information such as metadata pertaining to website connections and call logs. Additionally, companies providing technological services such as ISP hosting, IT, and cybersecurity are susceptible to attacks directly or through supply chains and island-hopping strategies. When successful, the stolen information is then most likely utilized for surveillance or gathering intelligence on specific targets. The modus operandi of Sea Turtle involves intercepting internet traffic directed at victimized websites, potentially allowing unauthorized access to government networks and other organizational systems. This targeting approach aids in associating actions with the threat actor and provides valuable insights for organizations operating within similar geographic zones or sectors. Their use of a reverse shell mechanism in operations streamlines the collection and extraction of sensitive data, furthering their agenda. An in-depth analysis of victimology reveals the specific types of data sought by this threat actor.</span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335551550&quot;:6,&quot;335551620&quot;:6,&quot;335559740&quot;:276}">&nbsp;</span></p>

<h2 style="text-align: left;"><span style="color: #080630;">Sea Turtle campaigns in the Netherlands</span></h2>
<p><span data-contrast="auto">Hunt &amp; Hackett observed Sea Turtle conducting multiple campaigns in the Netherlands. The modus operandi used in these attacks is largely consistent with the modus operandi and information published in the earlier mentioned threat intelligence reports.&nbsp;</span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335551550&quot;:6,&quot;335551620&quot;:6,&quot;335559740&quot;:276}">&nbsp;</span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335551550&quot;:6,&quot;335551620&quot;:6,&quot;335559740&quot;:276}">&nbsp;</span></p>
<p><span data-contrast="auto">Our investigation into one of their attacks indicated that this group exhibits characteristics of a state-supported cyber espionage group, primarily focused on acquiring economic and political intelligence through espionage with the aim of advancing </span><span data-contrast="auto">Turkey’s interests</span><span data-contrast="auto">. Hunt &amp; Hackett has started tracking this group and has observed more campaigns from this threat actor targeting specific organizations in the Netherlands. These cyberattacks are believed to be orchestrated by Sea Turtle operating in alignment with Turkish interests, signalling an escalation in Turkey's pursuit of objectives within the Netherlands.&nbsp;</span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335551550&quot;:6,&quot;335551620&quot;:6,&quot;335559740&quot;:276}">&nbsp;</span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335551550&quot;:6,&quot;335551620&quot;:6,&quot;335559740&quot;:276}">&nbsp;</span></p>
<p><span data-contrast="auto"><span data-contrast="auto" xml:lang="EN-GB" lang="EN-GB">The campaigns observed in the Netherlands appear to focus on telecommunication, media, ISPs and IT-service providers and more specifically Kurdish websites (among others PKK affiliated)</span>. The infrastructure of the targets was susceptible to supply chain and island-hopping attacks, which the attack group used to collect politically motivated information such as personal information on minority groups and potential political dissents. The stolen information is likely to be exploited for surveillance or intelligence gathering on specific groups and or individuals. This appears to be consistent with claims from </span><span data-contrast="auto">US officials in 2020 about hacker groups acting in Turkey’s interest, focusing on the identities and locations of the victims, which included governments of countries that are geopolitically significant to Turkey<a href="#tur7" rel="noopener"><span style="font-size: 14px;">[</span></a></span><a href="#tur7" rel="noopener"><span data-contrast="auto" style="font-size: 14px;">7]</span></a><span data-contrast="auto">.&nbsp;</span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335551550&quot;:6,&quot;335551620&quot;:6,&quot;335559740&quot;:276}">&nbsp;</span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335551550&quot;:6,&quot;335551620&quot;:6,&quot;335559740&quot;:276}">&nbsp;</span></p>
<p><span data-contrast="auto">Hunt &amp; Hackett has observed the threat actor executing defense evasion techniques to avoid being detected, and the threat actor has also been observed collecting potentially sensitive data such as email archives. </span><span data-contrast="auto">Their modus operandi includes intercepting internet traffic to victim websites, and potentially granting unauthorized access to government networks and other organizations.</span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335551550&quot;:6,&quot;335551620&quot;:6,&quot;335559740&quot;:276}">&nbsp;</span></p>

<h2 style="text-align: left;"><span style="color: #080630;">Technical Campaign Details</span></h2>
<p><span data-contrast="auto">While researching this actor, the most recent campaigns observed by Hunt &amp; Hackett, were initiated early 2023 when the threat actor targeted multiple organizations. During one of the attacks, the threat actor logged on to cPanel, a web hosting control panel used by multiple organizations world-wide, from an IP address<a href="#tur8" rel="noopener"><span style="font-size: 14px;">[</span></a></span><a href="#tur8" rel="noopener"><span data-contrast="auto" style="font-size: 14px;">8]</span></a><span data-contrast="auto"> that belonged to the range of a VPN provider. This was a legitimate cPanel account, compromised by the attacker. Unfortunately, it is unclear how they obtained access to the cPanel credentials. Days later a cPanel WebMail session was created for that same cPanel account, when it logged on from an IP address<a href="#tur9" rel="noopener"><span style="font-size: 14px;">[</span></a></span><a href="#tur9" rel="noopener"><span data-contrast="auto" style="font-size: 14px;">9]</span></a><span data-contrast="auto"> belonging to the range of a hosting provider. In addition, the account was used to perform an SSH logon from that same IP-address. Following these logons, source codes files of a reverse shell written in the programming language ‘C’ were downloaded from that .245 IP-address. These source code files were then compiled using GCC. Analysis of the source code files revealed that they contained specific code identical to a reverse shell<a href="#tur10" rel="noopener"><span style="font-size: 14px;">[</span></a></span><a href="#tur10" rel="noopener"><span data-contrast="auto" style="font-size: 14px;">10]</span></a><span data-contrast="auto"><a href="#tur10" rel="noopener"><span style="font-size: 14px;"> </span></a>that was stored in a publicly accessible GitHub repository<a href="#tur11" rel="noopener" id="__hsNewLink"><span style="font-size: 14px;">[</span></a></span><a href="#tur11" rel="noopener" id="__hsNewLink"><span data-contrast="auto" style="font-size: 14px;">11]</span></a><span data-contrast="auto"> that is believed to be used by the Sea Turtle attack group. Independent from Hunt &amp; Hackett, PwC recently observed usage of this reverse shell named </span><span data-contrast="auto" style="font-family: 'Courier New', Courier, monospace; font-weight: normal;">SnappyTCP</span><span data-contrast="auto"> and similarly attributed this to the threat actor Sea Turtle.</span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335551550&quot;:6,&quot;335551620&quot;:6,&quot;335559740&quot;:276}">&nbsp;</span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335551550&quot;:6,&quot;335551620&quot;:6,&quot;335559740&quot;:276}">&nbsp;</span></p>
<p><span data-contrast="auto">Before executing </span><span style="font-family: 'Courier New', Courier, monospace; font-weight: normal;">SnappyTCP</span><span data-contrast="auto"> using the tool </span><span data-contrast="auto" style="font-family: 'Courier New', Courier, monospace; font-weight: normal;">NoHup</span><span data-contrast="auto">, the domain name </span><span style="font-weight: normal;"><span data-contrast="auto" style="font-family: 'Courier New', Courier, monospace;">forward.boord[.]inf</span><span data-contrast="auto" style="font-family: 'Courier New', Courier, monospace;">o</span></span><span data-contrast="auto"> and <span style="font-family: 'Courier New', Courier, monospace; font-weight: normal;">port 443</span><span style="font-family: Andale Mono, AndaleMono, Lucida Console, monospace;"> </span></span><span data-contrast="auto">was written to a configuration file, with which it connected over TCP using HTTP to establish a command-and-control (C&amp;C) channel. </span><span data-contrast="auto" style="font-family: 'Courier New', Courier, monospace; font-weight: normal;">NoHup</span><span data-contrast="auto"> ensured </span><span data-contrast="auto" style="font-family: 'Courier New', Courier, monospace; font-weight: normal;">SnappyTCP</span><span data-contrast="auto"> remained running on the system even after the shell or terminal was exited. At the end of the SSH session anti-forensics was performed by unsetting the command history (Bash) and MySQL history file, and overwriting Linux system logs.&nbsp;</span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335551550&quot;:6,&quot;335551620&quot;:6,&quot;335559740&quot;:276}">&nbsp;</span></p>
<p><span data-contrast="auto">Weeks later, the cPanel Web Disk Feature accepted another connection. This was an indication that the threat actor was still using this cPanel feature. Based on the actions following the connection, combined with the source being an unfamiliar VPN connection, this activity was classified as malicious. Shortly after, the tool Adminer<a href="#tur12" rel="noopener" id="__hsNewLink"><span style="font-size: 14px;">[</span></a></span><a href="#tur12" rel="noopener" id="__hsNewLink"><span data-contrast="auto" style="font-size: 14px;">12]</span></a><span data-contrast="auto"> was installed in the public web directory of one of the compromised cPanel accounts. Adminer is a publicly available database management tool that can be used to remotely logon to the MySQL service of a system. The earlier identified Github repository stores the source code of </span><span data-contrast="auto" style="font-family: 'Courier New', Courier, monospace; font-weight: normal;">SnappyTCP</span><span data-contrast="auto">, as well as the tool Adminer which indicated that the threat actor was using the software hosted in the earlier mentioned GitHub repository.&nbsp;</span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335551550&quot;:6,&quot;335551620&quot;:6,&quot;335559740&quot;:276}">&nbsp;</span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335551550&quot;:6,&quot;335551620&quot;:6,&quot;335559740&quot;:276}">&nbsp;</span></p>
<p><span data-contrast="auto">Several weeks after the second cPanel Web Disk connection, the threat actor logged on to cPanel from the VPN provider M2<span style="font-weight: normal;">47 (</span></span><span data-contrast="auto" style="font-family: 'Courier New', Courier, monospace; font-weight: normal;">82.102.19[.]88</span><span data-contrast="auto">), which can be interpreted as the compromise of a second cPanel account. Subsequently, a logon was performed on the same cPanel account and a WebMail session was created for that cPanel account.</span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335551550&quot;:6,&quot;335551620&quot;:6,&quot;335559740&quot;:276}">&nbsp;</span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335551550&quot;:6,&quot;335551620&quot;:6,&quot;335559740&quot;:276}">&nbsp;</span></p>
<p><span data-contrast="auto">Finally, using </span><span data-contrast="auto" style="font-family: 'Courier New', Courier, monospace; font-weight: normal;">SnappyTCP</span><span data-contrast="auto"> the threat actor sent commands to the system to create a copy of an e-mail archive created with the tool tar, in the public web directory of the website that was accessible from the internet. It is highly likely that the threat actor exfiltrated the e-mail archive by downloading the file directly from the web directory.&nbsp;&nbsp;</span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335551550&quot;:6,&quot;335551620&quot;:6,&quot;335559740&quot;:276}">&nbsp;</span><span style="color: #080630;"><span style="font-weight: normal;"></span></span></p>
<h3><span style="color: #080630;"><span style="font-weight: normal;">Command &amp; Control</span>&nbsp;</span></h3>
<p><span data-contrast="auto">Hunt &amp; Hackett was able to download the source code of the </span><span data-contrast="auto" style="font-family: 'Courier New', Courier, monospace; font-weight: normal;">SnappyTCP</span><span data-contrast="auto"> from one of the servers used by Sea Turtle (</span><span data-contrast="auto" style="font-family: 'Courier New', Courier, monospace; font-size: 18px; font-weight: normal;">http[://]193.34.167[.]245/c00n/connn.c</span><span data-contrast="auto">) alongside other files. As previously described, the </span><span data-contrast="auto" style="font-family: 'Courier New', Courier, monospace; font-weight: normal;">SnappyTCP</span><span data-contrast="auto"> malware reads a config file that contains a domain name and port number. Depending on the version of the malware and whether the connection must be encrypted or not, the malware does an HTTP GET with the request URI <span style="font-weight: normal;">‘</span></span><span data-contrast="auto" style="font-family: 'Courier New', Courier, monospace; font-weight: normal;">sy.php</span><span data-contrast="auto">’</span><span data-contrast="auto">.&nbsp;</span><span data-contrast="auto">If the header ‘</span><span data-contrast="auto" style="font-family: 'Courier New', Courier, monospace; font-weight: normal;">X-Auth-43245-S-20</span><span data-contrast="auto">’ is returned by the server, </span><span data-contrast="auto" style="font-family: 'Courier New', Courier, monospace; font-weight: normal;">SnappyTCP</span><span data-contrast="auto"> then checks if output has sufficient size and if the first character does not start with an ‘@’. If this is the case, a reverse shell is spawned using the IP and port returned by the server. Otherwise, the whole sequence will restart after a short amount of sleep.</span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335551550&quot;:6,&quot;335551620&quot;:6,&quot;335559740&quot;:276}">&nbsp;</span><span data-contrast="auto">&nbsp;</span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335551550&quot;:6,&quot;335551620&quot;:6,&quot;335559740&quot;:276}">&nbsp;</span></p>
<p><span data-contrast="auto">The command-and-control (C&amp;C) channel is setup with what Hunt &amp; Hackett believes is a form of Socat, as detected by the THOR APT Scanner on Virustotal<a href="#tur13" rel="noopener" id="__hsNewLink"><span style="font-size: 14px;">[</span></a></span><a href="#tur13" rel="noopener" id="__hsNewLink"><span data-contrast="auto" style="font-size: 14px;">13]</span></a><span data-contrast="auto">. This also collaborates with the fact that Socat shares the same commandline characteristics and the fact that Socat was also found on the same server (</span><span data-contrast="auto" style="font-family: 'Courier New', Courier, monospace; font-weight: normal;">http[://]193.34.167.245/c00n/socat</span><span data-contrast="auto">).</span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335551550&quot;:6,&quot;335551620&quot;:6,&quot;335559740&quot;:276}">&nbsp;</span><span data-contrast="auto">Running the tool Socat (or a modified version of it) targeting known C&amp;C servers of Sea Turtle resulted mostly in the HTTP response </span><span data-contrast="auto">‘@<span style="font-family: 'Courier New', Courier, monospace; font-weight: normal;">8.8.8.8:443</span></span><i><span data-contrast="auto">’</span></i><span data-contrast="auto">. Since the code checks if the start of the string starts with ‘@’, this output is ignored.&nbsp;</span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335551550&quot;:6,&quot;335551620&quot;:6,&quot;335559740&quot;:276}"><span data-contrast="auto" xml:lang="EN-US" lang="EN-US">As shown in </span><span data-contrast="auto" xml:lang="EN-US" lang="EN-US" style="font-style: italic;">Table 2</span><span data-contrast="auto" xml:lang="EN-US" lang="EN-US">, the C&amp;C servers of Sea Turtle returned the IP-addresses and a domain name related to DNS services of Google, during the time of writing.</span>&nbsp;</span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335551550&quot;:6,&quot;335551620&quot;:6,&quot;335559740&quot;:276}"></span><span data-ccp-props="{&quot;201341983&quot;:0,&quot;335551550&quot;:6,&quot;335551620&quot;:6,&quot;335559740&quot;:276}"></span></p>
