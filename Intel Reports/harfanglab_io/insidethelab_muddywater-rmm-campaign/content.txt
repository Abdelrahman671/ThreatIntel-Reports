<h2>Summary</h2>
<p>We have been closely monitoring the activities of the Iranian state-sponsored threat actor MuddyWater since the beginning of 2024. Our investigations reveal an active campaign that has been ramping up since October 2023, aligning with the Hamas attack that took place that month<sup id="fnref1:1"><a href="#fn:1">1</a></sup>. In this latest campaign, MuddyWater has been heavily relying on a legitimate remote monitoring and management (RMM) tool called Atera Agent.</p>
<p>Over the course of our investigation, several publications<sup id="fnref1:2"><a href="#fn:2">2</a></sup><sup id="fnref1:3"><a href="#fn:3">3</a></sup><sup id="fnref1:4"><a href="#fn:4">4</a></sup> have detailed various aspects of this attack campaign. Consequently, our report shares new insights and information regarding MuddyWater use of Atera Agents, not previously highlighted in these reports.</p>
<h2>Background</h2>
<p>MuddyWater, an Iranian state-sponsored threat actor, has been relying on legitimate remote monitoring and management (RMM) software as a first stage payload in its attacks, since at least 2021<sup id="fnref1:5"><a href="#fn:5">5</a></sup>. Since then, the actor has tested different RMM tools, from ScreenConnect, Syncro, SimpleHelp, RemoteUtilies and most recently, Atera Agent. Atera Agent does not require any infrastructure to be set up by the attackers, providing them with better operational security.</p>
<p>Since late October 2023, we have noticed a significant increase in the use of Atera Agent installation packages linked to MuddyWater, continuing through to April 2024:</p>
<p><figure class="__illustration"><picture decoding="async" class="img-fluid full">
<source type="image/webp" srcset="https://harfanglab.io/medias/2024/04/timeline-for-ariel-1.png.webp"/>
<img width="1522" height="514" decoding="async" src="https://harfanglab.io/medias/2024/04/timeline-for-ariel-1.png" alt="Estimated timeline of Atera agent activity"/>
</picture>
</figure> Figure 1 &#8211; Estimated timeline of Atera agent activity</p>
<p>Similarly to previously reported misuse cases<sup id="fnref1:6"><a href="#fn:6">6</a></sup>, MuddyWater exploited Atera&#8217;s free trial offers. The Atera Agent agents seen in this campaign has been registered using a mix of compromised business and private email accounts. We suspect that the threat actor accessed these accounts through a variety of methods, such as password spraying, exploiting reused passwords, utilizing credentials from data breaches, or even purchasing them. Additionally, there is a possibility that private email accounts were specifically registered for the purpose of creating Atera accounts.</p>
<h2>Infection chain</h2>
<p>The group continues to leverage free file hosting platforms for hosting their RMM installers, employing spearphishing emails to direct recipients to these files.</p>

<figure id="attachment_86554" class="wp-caption alignnone"><picture decoding="async" class="img-fluid full size-full wp-image-86554">

<p>These emails contained links leading to various file-sharing websites, which either hosted an archive with the Atera Agent installer or provided direct access to the installer itself.</p>
<p>It is important to note that, to our knowledge, the email accounts registered with Atera were distinct from those used to disseminate the spearphishing emails.</p>
<h3>Spearphishing</h3>
<p>The quality of MuddyWater&#8217;s spearphishing emails has significantly improved since their earlier emails in October 2023. The threat actor has refined their social engineering tactics, crafting more convincing and tailored lures to deceive their targets. The emails observed in the recent campaign from April 2024 (see Fig. 3) demonstrate a higher level of sophistication compared to those from October 2023 (see Fig. 4), with more persuasive content and professional formatting:</p>
<p><figure class="__illustration"><picture decoding="async" class="img-fluid full">
<source type="image/webp" srcset="https://harfanglab.io/medias/2024/04/megolan.png.webp"/>

</picture>
</figure> Figure 3 &#8211; Spearphishing email from April 2024. Atera Agent linked, hosted using &#8220;Egnyte&#8221; file sharing</p>
<p>Which translates to:</p>
<pre><code>Hello,

Dear friends and colleagues

The Golan Regional Council has launched a national program to attract local companies, including insurance, avation, medicine, shipping, education, tourism, etc.

Dear friends and colleagues, please support us regarding the adoption of the Golan regional program.

To download the program, click the link below and wait for the information to be uploaded after receiving the program.

Golan Regional Council program [Link]

Thanks</code></pre>
<p>Where as the email from October 2023 (Fig. 4) translates to:</p>
<pre><code>Hello
Use the signaltours [Link] program to recieve files and get information about flight status.</code></pre>

<h2>Atera RMM</h2>
<p>Atera offers users a free 30-day trial of their platform, which allows the generation of Atera &#8216;Agents&#8217;, using any email account. These agents are configured to call back to this account and could be controlled directly via the website:</p>
<p><figure class="__illustration"><picture decoding="async" class="img-fluid full">
<source type="image/webp" srcset="https://harfanglab.io/medias/2024/04/atera-control-panel.png.webp"/>
<img width="1437" height="882" decoding="async" src="https://harfanglab.io/medias/2024/04/atera-control-panel.png" alt="Atera Web UI"/>
</picture>
</figure> Figure 5 &#8211; Atera Web UI</p>
<p>This eliminates the need for the attacker to establish its own C2 infrastructure, making it a more appealing choice compared to previously used RMM solutions like SimpleHelp.</p>
<p>Atera provides comprehensive remote control capabilities directly from the Web UI, including the ability to upload/download files, run an interactive shell (see Fig. 6), and even use generative AI command assistance.</p>
<p><figure class="__illustration"><picture decoding="async" class="img-fluid full">
<source type="image/webp" srcset="https://harfanglab.io/medias/2024/04/atera-ps-ui.png.webp"/>
<img width="926" height="854" decoding="async" src="https://harfanglab.io/medias/2024/04/atera-ps-ui.png" alt="Atera Web PowerShell UI"/>
</picture>
</figure></p>
<p>Figure 6 &#8211; Atera Web PowerShell UI</p>
<p>While email verification is not required prior to generating agents, a &#8216;registration successful&#8217; email is sent to the address used. Although the owner of the email address could naturally reset the password and access the Atera account, in the cases we studied, the attacker had access to the email account used to register with Atera.</p>
<p>We observed Atera Agent binaries configured using emails belonging to individuals whose other email account passwords had been leaked. We believe that MuddyWater exploited password reuse to gain access to additional email accounts, which were then used to register Atera Agents. In some cases, the email accounts used appeared to have been obtained through the sale of leaked OWA credentials. In other instances, the credentials of another email account belonging to the same person had been compromised, allowing the threat actor to access additional accounts through password reuse.</p>

<p>Like other Atera Agent installers discribed in this report, this installer was packed in a ZIP archive. However, this parent archive was hosted on Zendesk Chat&#8217;s (formerly Zopim Chat) file sharing infrastructure: <code>https://v2uploads.zopim[.]io/2/u/K/2uKM8Mhn4WHvqm9pjHrjaogyOYub9ouO/892fedae59b274ca24916de33650d318168ce335.zip</code></p>
<p>We suspect that the attacker uploaded the malicious archive during a chat session, likely posing as a visitor/customer, but possibly as an agent/support provider.</p>
<p>According to Zendesk&#8217;s documentation, &#8220;<em>Attached files are uploaded as links in a chat session. Regardless of your authentication settings, those links can be accessed by anyone with the URL.</em><sup id="fnref1:7"><a href="#fn:7">7</a></sup>&#8220;. This allowed the attacker to obtain a direct, non-expiring link to the archive, which they could then distribute via email.</p>
<p>Further investigation revealed that the email address used to register the Atera Agent was part of multiple data breaches dating back to 2016, with the most recent breach potentially occurring just a month before the registration.</p>
<p>Hunting for additional Atera Agents distributed via Zendesk, we found another suspicious agent configured with a Gmail account. This particular agent dates back to October 2023:</p>

<h2>Conclusions</h2>
<p>MuddyWater places a high priority on gaining access to business email accounts as part of their ongoing attack campaigns. These compromised accounts serve as valuable resource, enabling the group to enhance the credibility and effectiveness of their spear-phishing efforts, establish persistence within targeted organizations, and evade detection by blending in with legitimate network traffic. Adding to that the use of RMM software (previously self-hosted, now in-cloud), as well as using various file hosting providers, makes this sort of activity challenging to detect and track.</p>
<p>Moreover, as highlighted in the Deep Instinct report on DarkBeatC2<sup id="fnref2:3"><a href="#fn:3">3</a></sup>, there are indications of collaboration and hand-off of compromised targets between Iranian threat actors to conduct supply-chain attacks. This suggests that MuddyWater may not only actively compromise business email accounts themselves but also receive access to previously breached accounts from other affiliate groups. Similarly, the OP INNOVATE blog post on Lord Nemesis<sup id="fnref2:9"><a href="#fn:9">9</a></sup> demonstrates how the group exploited privileged credentials obtained from the Rashim Software breach to infiltrate the networks of Rashim&#8217;s clients in the Israeli academic sector. This further underscores the value of compromised business email accounts as a stepping stone for launching additional attacks.</p>
<p>Unfortunately, we do not hold any information about the specific stages and actions the actor takes once it has successfully deployed the Atera Agent on a targeted system. We suspect that the group does not rely on the deployed RMM software for a long term, but rather uses it as a first stage in their attack, likely deploying a PowerShell implant as the next stage, as seen in previous attacks<sup id="fnref1:10"><a href="#fn:10">10</a></sup>.</p>
<p>We encourage affected organizations and fellow researchers to collaborate with us in gaining a more comprehensive understanding of MuddyWater attack campaigns. Together we can offer the community better chances to detect, respond to and hunt for new malicious activity.</p>
