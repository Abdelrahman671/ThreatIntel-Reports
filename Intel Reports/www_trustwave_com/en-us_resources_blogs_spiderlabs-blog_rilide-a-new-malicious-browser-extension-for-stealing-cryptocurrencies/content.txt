          <h1>Rilide: A New Malicious Browser Extension for Stealing Cryptocurrencies</h1>          
          
        </div>
        <div class="blog-details-header-right">
          
            
            <a href="#" class="theme-changer">Change theme to light</a>
            
            
            <img src="https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Web/Spiders/2024/spider_01-mob.png?width=450&amp;height=250&amp;name=spider_01-mob.png" loading="eager" alt="Rilide: A New Malicious Browser Extension for Stealing Cryptocurrencies" class="hero-spider">
            
          
        </div>
      </div>
      <div class="toc-content content-wrapper">
        
        <div class="wrapper-main_content">
          
          <div class="blog-details-metadata">
            <span class="date-time">
              <time datetime="2023-04-04 05:00:00">April 04, 2023</time>
            </span>
            <span class="minread">7 Minute Read</span>
            
              <span class="blog-author">by Pawel Knapczyk, Wojciech Cieslak</span>
            
          </div> 
          
          
          <div class="wrapper-blog-content">
            <span id="hs_cos_wrapper_post_body" class="hs_cos_wrapper hs_cos_wrapper_meta_field hs_cos_wrapper_type_rich_text" style="" data-hs-cos-general-type="meta_field" data-hs-cos-type="rich_text"><p>Trustwave SpiderLabs uncovered a new strain of malware that it dubbed Rilide, which targets Chromium-based browsers such as Google Chrome, Microsoft Edge, Brave, and Opera.</p>
<!--more-->
<p>Rilide malware is disguised as a legitimate Google Drive extension and enables threat actors to carry out a broad spectrum of malicious activities, including monitoring browsing history, taking screenshots, and injecting malicious scripts to withdraw funds from various cryptocurrency exchanges.</p>
<p>Rilide is not the first malware SpiderLabs has observed using malicious browser extensions. Where this malware differs is it has the effective and rarely used ability to utilize forged dialogs to deceive users into revealing their two-factor authentication (2FA) and then withdraw cryptocurrencies in the background. During our investigation into Rilideâ€™s origins, we uncovered similar browser extensions being advertised for sale. Additionally, we found that part of its source code was recently leaked on an underground forum due to a payment dispute.</p>
<h3>&nbsp;</h3>
<h3>Malicious Campaigns Leading to Rilide Stealer Extension</h3>
<p>SpiderLabs uncovered two malicious campaigns leading to the installation of the Rilide extension.</p>
<p><img loading="lazy" src="https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Web/Blogs/SpiderLab/19809_image002.webp?width=611&amp;height=493&amp;name=19809_image002.webp" alt="19809_image002" width="611" height="493" style="display: block; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto; height: auto; max-width: 100%; width: 611px;" srcset="https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Web/Blogs/SpiderLab/19809_image002.webp?width=306&amp;height=247&amp;name=19809_image002.webp 306w, https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Web/Blogs/SpiderLab/19809_image002.webp?width=611&amp;height=493&amp;name=19809_image002.webp 611w, https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Web/Blogs/SpiderLab/19809_image002.webp?width=917&amp;height=740&amp;name=19809_image002.webp 917w, https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Web/Blogs/SpiderLab/19809_image002.webp?width=1222&amp;height=986&amp;name=19809_image002.webp 1222w, https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Web/Blogs/SpiderLab/19809_image002.webp?width=1528&amp;height=1233&amp;name=19809_image002.webp 1528w, https://www.trustwave.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Web/Blogs/SpiderLab/19809_image002.webp?width=1833&amp;height=1479&amp;name=19809_image002.webp 1833w" sizes="(max-width: 611px) 100vw, 611px"></p>
<p style="text-align: center; font-size: 14px;"><em><strong>Figure 1</strong>. Infection Chains Leading to the Execution of the Rilide Extension</em></p>
<h3>&nbsp;</h3>
<h3>Campaign 1: Ekipa RAT Installing Rilide Stealer</h3>
<p>One of the Rilide samples identified by Trustwave SpiderLabs was distributed through a malicious Microsoft Publisher <a href="https://www.virustotal.com/gui/file/0e31ff6406b03982581246b7dd60f3b96edcf0bd007b31766954df001fd68f69/details">file</a>. This file is part of Ekipa RAT, a Remote Access Trojan (RAT), designed for targeted attacks and often sold on underground forums.</p>
<p>We previously described Ekipa RAT in one of our <a href="https://www.trustwave.com/en-us/resources/blogs/spiderlabs-blog/malicious-macros-adapt-to-use-microsoft-publisher-to-push-ekipa-rat/">blogs</a>. It is important to note that Microsoft Publisher was not affected by Microsoft's decision to <a href="https://learn.microsoft.com/en-gb/DeployOffice/security/internet-macros-blocked#block-macros-from-running-in-office-files-from-the-internet%22">block macros</a> from executing files downloaded from the Internet. As a result, when a user attempted to open a Publisher file, they would receive a warning but could still enable the execution of malicious content by clicking the â€˜Enable Macrosâ€™ button. On 14 February 2023, Microsoft issued an <a href="https://msrc.microsoft.com/update-guide/en-US/vulnerability/CVE-2023-21715">update</a> that resolved the Publisher security flaw. With the implementation of the â€˜Mark of the Webâ€™ feature on the .pub file, users are now left with only one option, â€˜Disable Macros,â€™ which should have been the case all along.</p>
<p>Any association between the threat actors behind Ekipa RAT and those using the Rilide infostealer remains unclear. However, it is probable that Ekipa RAT was tested as a means of distribution for Rilide, before finally switching to Aurora stealer.</p>

<h3>&nbsp;</h3>
<h3>Campaign 2: Aurora Stealer Abusing Google Ads</h3>
<p>Aurora is a Go-based stealer, which was initially spotted being advertised in April 2022 as a Malware-as-a-Service (MaaS) on Russian-speaking underground forums. The malware is designed to target data from multiple web browsers, cryptocurrency wallets, and local systems.</p>
<p>Recently, the threat actors behind Aurora have been observed abusing the Google Ads platform to spread the malware. According to a report published by <a href="https://blog.cyble.com/2023/01/18/aurora-a-stealer-using-shapeshifting-tactics/">Cyble</a>, campaigns mimicking legitimate Team Viewer installers have been utilized to deploy Aurora. As reported by <a href="https://twitter.com/1ZRR4H">@1ZRR4H</a> and <a href="https://twitter.com/malwrhunterteam">@malwrhunterteam</a>, Aurora was also spread via another campaign that imitated an NVIDIA Drivers installer. A downloaded sample was packed with Themida, a well-known commercial protector for executables. We used the UnpacMe service to unpack the sample.</p>

<h3>Restoring Function Names</h3>
<p>The Aurora Stealer sample was stripped of debugging symbols, thus making the analysis harder. Since Go binaries are statically linked, which means that all the necessary libraries are included in the compiled binary, the number of potential functions to analyze is large. However, the original function names can be restored from the pclntab structure, as described in the CUJOAI Senior Threat Researcher Dorka Palotayâ€™s <a href="https://cujo.com/reverse-engineering-go-binaries-with-ghidra/">post</a>. Using the <a href="https://github.com/getCUJO/ThreatIntel/blob/master/Scripts/Ghidra/go_func.py">go_func.py</a> script for Ghidra we were able to restore the functions names.</p>
<h3><br>How an Aurora Module Downloaded Rilide Stealer</h3>

<h3>&nbsp;</h3>
<h3>Rilide Stealer Origins</h3>
<p>In the course of our research, we have encountered several stealer extensions for sale that advertised capabilities closely resembling those of the Rilide samples. However, we were unable to definitively link any of them to Rilide. One noteworthy finding was a botnet sale advertisement from an underground forum dated March 2022. Although the advertised functionalities matched those of Rilide, the botnet also included additional features such as a reverse proxy and ad clicker. <h3>&nbsp;</h3>
<h3>Conclusions:</h3>
<p>The Rilide stealer is a prime example of the increasing sophistication of malicious browser extensions and the dangers they pose. Disguised as a legitimate Google Drive extension, Rilide provides threat actors with the ability to carry out a wide range of malicious activities, including monitoring browsing history, capturing screenshots, and injecting malicious scripts to steal funds from cryptocurrency exchanges.</p>
<p>While the upcoming enforcement of manifest v3 may make it more challenging for threat actors to operate, it is unlikely to solve the issue entirely as most of the functionalities leveraged by Rilide will still be available.</p>
<p>Informational overload can dull our ability to interpret facts accurately and make us more vulnerable to phishing attempts. It is important to remain vigilant and skeptical when receiving unsolicited emails or messages, and to never assume that any content on the Internet is safe, even if it appears to be.</p>
<p>Ultimately, it is crucial to stay informed and educated about the latest cybersecurity threats and best practices to minimize the risk of falling victim to phishing attacks.</p>
