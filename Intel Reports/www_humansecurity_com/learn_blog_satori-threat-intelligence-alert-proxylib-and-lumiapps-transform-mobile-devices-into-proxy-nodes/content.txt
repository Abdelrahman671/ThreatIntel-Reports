										<span id="hs_cos_wrapper_post_body" class="hs_cos_wrapper hs_cos_wrapper_meta_field hs_cos_wrapper_type_rich_text" style="" data-hs-cos-general-type="meta_field" data-hs-cos-type="rich_text"><p><span style="font-weight: bold;">Researchers</span>: Gabi Cirlig, Maor Elizen, Lindsay Kaye, Joao Marques, Vikas Parthasarathy, Joao Santos, Adam Sell, Inna Vasilyeva</p>
<!--more-->
<h1><span style="font-size: 60px;">Executive Summary</span></h1>
<p>Residential proxies are frequently used by threat actors to conceal malicious activity, including advertising fraud and the use of bots. Access to residential proxy networks is often purchased from other threat actors who create them through enrolling unwitting users’ devices as nodes in the network through malware embedded in mobile, CTV or desktop applications.</p>
<p><a href="https://www.humansecurity.com/company/satori-threat-intelligence"><span>HUMAN’s Satori Threat Intelligence team</span></a> recently identified a cluster of VPN apps available on the Google Play Store that transformed the user’s device into a proxy node without their knowledge. We’ve dubbed this operation <strong>PROXYLIB</strong> after the Golang library responsible for the proxy node enrollment in each of the apps.&nbsp;</p>
<p>In May 2023, researchers at a measurement company found malicious behavior in a single free VPN application—<strong>Oko VPN</strong>— on Google’s Play Store. The research resulted in Oko VPN’s removal from the Play Store.&nbsp;</p>
<p>Based on further analysis of Oko VPN, Satori researchers uncovered <strong>28 applications related to PROXYLIB</strong>.&nbsp;</p>
<p>Satori researchers later discovered a subsequent version of PROXYLIB offered online via the <strong>LumiApps SDK</strong> as well as other adaptations by the threat actor that used the same Golang library to turn the device into a proxy node.</p>
<p>HUMAN has also found evidence connecting PROXYLIB to Asocks, a residential proxy seller and believes the threat actor is using Asocks as a way to monetize the PROXYLIB network.</p>
<p>Following the Satori team’s research, the Google Play Store has removed <strong>28</strong> apps from their listings. Additionally, HUMAN protects customers against many types of attacks that are often conducted using residential proxies, including <a href="https://www.humansecurity.com/learn/topics/what-is-account-takeover"><span>account takeover attacks</span></a>, <a href="https://www.humansecurity.com/platform/defense-modules/transaction-abuse"><span>transaction abuse</span></a>, <a href="https://www.humansecurity.com/topics/what-is-ad-fraud"><span>programmatic advertising fraud</span></a>, and <a href="https://www.humansecurity.com/learn/topics/what-is-scraping"><span>web scraping</span></a>.</p>
<p>Going forward, Android users are automatically protected from the PROXYLIB attack by <a href="https://support.google.com/googleplay/answer/2812853?hl=en" rel="noopener">Google Play Protect</a>, which is on by default on Android devices with Google Play Services. Google Play Protect can warn users or block apps known to exhibit malicious behavior, even when those apps come from sources outside of Play. HUMAN continues to work closely with the Google Play Store and other entities to reduce PROXYLIB’s impact.&nbsp;</p>
<p><img src="https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/3-24%20PROXYLIB%20Process%20diagram.jpg?width=1600&amp;height=959&amp;name=3-24%20PROXYLIB%20Process%20diagram.jpg" width="1600" height="959" loading="lazy" alt="3-24 PROXYLIB Process diagram" style="height: auto; max-width: 100%; width: 1600px; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto; display: block;" srcset="https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/3-24%20PROXYLIB%20Process%20diagram.jpg?width=800&amp;height=480&amp;name=3-24%20PROXYLIB%20Process%20diagram.jpg 800w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/3-24%20PROXYLIB%20Process%20diagram.jpg?width=1600&amp;height=959&amp;name=3-24%20PROXYLIB%20Process%20diagram.jpg 1600w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/3-24%20PROXYLIB%20Process%20diagram.jpg?width=2400&amp;height=1439&amp;name=3-24%20PROXYLIB%20Process%20diagram.jpg 2400w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/3-24%20PROXYLIB%20Process%20diagram.jpg?width=3200&amp;height=1918&amp;name=3-24%20PROXYLIB%20Process%20diagram.jpg 3200w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/3-24%20PROXYLIB%20Process%20diagram.jpg?width=4000&amp;height=2398&amp;name=3-24%20PROXYLIB%20Process%20diagram.jpg 4000w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/3-24%20PROXYLIB%20Process%20diagram.jpg?width=4800&amp;height=2877&amp;name=3-24%20PROXYLIB%20Process%20diagram.jpg 4800w" sizes="(max-width: 1600px) 100vw, 1600px"></p>
<h1 style="font-size: 60px;">Background</h1>
<p>Residential proxies are networks of residential IPs that route traffic from one device through another; they can be used by threat actors to hide malicious activity, including password spraying, large-scale advertising fraud or credential stuffing attacks. When a threat actor uses a residential proxy, the traffic from these attacks appears to be coming from different residential IP addresses instead of an IP of a data center or other parts of a threat actor’s infrastructure. As a result, the traffic’s origin is obfuscated.</p>
<p>Many threat actors purchase access to these networks to facilitate their operations. Some of these networks are created by other threat actors enrolling unwitting users’ devices as proxy nodes and selling access to the network.</p>
<p><span style="font-weight: bold;">Oko VPN</span> (<em>com.okovpn.app</em>) is a free VPN app that has both Android and iOS versions and was first <a href="https://go.integralads.com/rs/469-VBI-606/images/AMER_Threat_Lab_Oko_VPN_Technical_Analysis_IAS.pdf" rel="noopener">reported as a threat</a> by IAS in May 2023. The Android application enrolls the user in a proxy network and receives instructions from a series of command-and-control (C2) servers for port/IP connections. After the enrollment, the infected device relays web requests to email sites, online retailers, Twitch streaming platforms, and more. The Oko VPN operators used the residential IP network created by the devices enrolled via the Oko VPN application in order to conduct ad fraud, mostly directed at video streaming platforms. The app was removed from the Google Play Store by Google on March 23, 2023. Satori confirmed that the iOS version of the app was not malicious and did not contain the PROXYLIB library.&nbsp;</p>
<p><img src="https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/3-24%20PROXYLIB%20timeline.jpg?width=1600&amp;height=1466&amp;name=3-24%20PROXYLIB%20timeline.jpg" width="1600" height="1466" loading="lazy" alt="3-24 PROXYLIB timeline" style="height: auto; max-width: 100%; width: 1600px; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto; display: block;" srcset="https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/3-24%20PROXYLIB%20timeline.jpg?width=800&amp;height=733&amp;name=3-24%20PROXYLIB%20timeline.jpg 800w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/3-24%20PROXYLIB%20timeline.jpg?width=1600&amp;height=1466&amp;name=3-24%20PROXYLIB%20timeline.jpg 1600w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/3-24%20PROXYLIB%20timeline.jpg?width=2400&amp;height=2199&amp;name=3-24%20PROXYLIB%20timeline.jpg 2400w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/3-24%20PROXYLIB%20timeline.jpg?width=3200&amp;height=2932&amp;name=3-24%20PROXYLIB%20timeline.jpg 3200w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/3-24%20PROXYLIB%20timeline.jpg?width=4000&amp;height=3665&amp;name=3-24%20PROXYLIB%20timeline.jpg 4000w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/3-24%20PROXYLIB%20timeline.jpg?width=4800&amp;height=4398&amp;name=3-24%20PROXYLIB%20timeline.jpg 4800w" sizes="(max-width: 1600px) 100vw, 1600px"></p>
<h1 style="font-size: 60px;">PROXYLIB Analysis</h1>
<p>Satori researchers analyzed both the earlier versions of the PROXYLIB applications (related to Oko VPN) as well as newer versions of the threat that used an SDK known as LumiApps. Both versions of the malware function similarly, turning a device onto which a malicious app is installed into a residential proxy node using native library code written in Golang.</p>
<h2>PROXYLIB v1</h2>
<p>HUMAN verified the findings in the IAS report and identified 28 additional apps that contained PROXYLIB v1. These apps all included a malicious library which establishes a bidirectional connection to a proxy network, turning the device into a residential proxy node without the user’s awareness. Satori researchers identified 14 applications published on or before mid-May 2023 that implemented this malicious SDK. The majority of these applications masquerade as free VPN apps.</p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><img src="https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Revised%20VPN%20apps.png?width=11830&amp;height=3601&amp;name=Revised%20VPN%20apps.png" width="11830" height="3601" loading="lazy" alt="Revised VPN apps" style="height: auto; max-width: 100%; width: 11830px; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto; display: block;" srcset="https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Revised%20VPN%20apps.png?width=5915&amp;height=1801&amp;name=Revised%20VPN%20apps.png 5915w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Revised%20VPN%20apps.png?width=11830&amp;height=3601&amp;name=Revised%20VPN%20apps.png 11830w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Revised%20VPN%20apps.png?width=17745&amp;height=5402&amp;name=Revised%20VPN%20apps.png 17745w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Revised%20VPN%20apps.png?width=23660&amp;height=7202&amp;name=Revised%20VPN%20apps.png 23660w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Revised%20VPN%20apps.png?width=29575&amp;height=9003&amp;name=Revised%20VPN%20apps.png 29575w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/Revised%20VPN%20apps.png?width=35490&amp;height=10803&amp;name=Revised%20VPN%20apps.png 35490w" sizes="(max-width: 11830px) 100vw, 11830px"><span style="font-size: 16px;">Two apps from the first variant of PROXYLIB (<span style="font-weight: bold;">Source</span>: HUMAN Threat Intelligence)</span><br><br></p>
<p>While the applications themselves were different, they all followed approximately the same process for loading the malicious library, enrolling the device in the proxy network, and running the proxy functionality.&nbsp;</p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><img src="https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/permissions.png?width=883&amp;height=135&amp;name=permissions.png" width="883" height="135" loading="lazy" alt="permissions" style="height: auto; max-width: 100%; width: 883px; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto; display: block;" srcset="https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/permissions.png?width=442&amp;height=68&amp;name=permissions.png 442w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/permissions.png?width=883&amp;height=135&amp;name=permissions.png 883w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/permissions.png?width=1325&amp;height=203&amp;name=permissions.png 1325w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/permissions.png?width=1766&amp;height=270&amp;name=permissions.png 1766w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/permissions.png?width=2208&amp;height=338&amp;name=permissions.png 2208w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/permissions.png?width=2649&amp;height=405&amp;name=permissions.png 2649w" sizes="(max-width: 883px) 100vw, 883px"><span style="font-size: 16px;">Relevant permissions used by the malware (<span style="font-weight: bold;">Source</span>: HUMAN Threat Intelligence)</span></p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><img src="https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/override.png?width=489&amp;height=149&amp;name=override.png" width="489" height="149" loading="lazy" alt="override" style="height: auto; max-width: 100%; width: 489px; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto; display: block;" srcset="https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/override.png?width=245&amp;height=75&amp;name=override.png 245w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/override.png?width=489&amp;height=149&amp;name=override.png 489w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/override.png?width=734&amp;height=224&amp;name=override.png 734w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/override.png?width=978&amp;height=298&amp;name=override.png 978w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/override.png?width=1223&amp;height=373&amp;name=override.png 1223w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/override.png?width=1467&amp;height=447&amp;name=override.png 1467w" sizes="(max-width: 489px) 100vw, 489px"><span style="font-size: 16px;">Receiver for the BOOT_COMPLETED event (<span style="font-weight: bold;">Source</span>: HUMAN Threat Intelligence)</span></p>
<p>Once the application starts, the app will connect to a C2 server, the address of which was retrieved in a previous request. Then, a subsequent request enrolls the device in the network and creates a TCP connection between the device and the proxy network.</p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><img src="https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/request%20response.png?width=997&amp;height=609&amp;name=request%20response.png" width="997" height="609" loading="lazy" alt="request response" style="height: auto; max-width: 100%; width: 997px;" srcset="https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/request%20response.png?width=499&amp;height=305&amp;name=request%20response.png 499w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/request%20response.png?width=997&amp;height=609&amp;name=request%20response.png 997w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/request%20response.png?width=1496&amp;height=914&amp;name=request%20response.png 1496w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/request%20response.png?width=1994&amp;height=1218&amp;name=request%20response.png 1994w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/request%20response.png?width=2493&amp;height=1523&amp;name=request%20response.png 2493w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/request%20response.png?width=2991&amp;height=1827&amp;name=request%20response.png 2991w" sizes="(max-width: 997px) 100vw, 997px"><br><span style="font-size: 16px;">api.yellowflashvpn.com/api/v1/register to get C2 info (<span style="font-weight: bold;">Source</span>: HUMAN Threat Intelligence)</span></p>
<p>Once the connection is established, the library <em>libgojni.so</em> handles any incoming requests, maintaining persistence using the <em>FOREGROUND_SERVICE</em> and <em>BOOT_COMPLETED</em> permissions.</p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><img src="https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/start%20server.png?width=643&amp;height=231&amp;name=start%20server.png" width="643" height="231" loading="lazy" alt="start server" style="height: auto; max-width: 100%; width: 643px;" srcset="https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/start%20server.png?width=322&amp;height=116&amp;name=start%20server.png 322w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/start%20server.png?width=643&amp;height=231&amp;name=start%20server.png 643w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/start%20server.png?width=965&amp;height=347&amp;name=start%20server.png 965w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/start%20server.png?width=1286&amp;height=462&amp;name=start%20server.png 1286w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/start%20server.png?width=1608&amp;height=578&amp;name=start%20server.png 1608w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/start%20server.png?width=1929&amp;height=693&amp;name=start%20server.png 1929w" sizes="(max-width: 643px) 100vw, 643px"><br><span style="font-size: 16px;">startServer call with nsignal[.]net as argument (<span style="font-weight: bold;">Source</span>: HUMAN Threat Intelligence)</span></p>
<p>The native library initiates and maintains a connection with the C2. The C2 (<em>nsignal[.]net</em> in the example below) is used to initiate a bidirectional socket on port 1334 through which the proxy traffic will be forwarded. Every 10 seconds, a new socket will be created via the <em>client.NewServer</em> function.</p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><img src="https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/couldnt%20parse.png?width=1008&amp;height=688&amp;name=couldnt%20parse.png" width="1008" height="688" loading="lazy" alt="couldnt parse" style="height: auto; max-width: 100%; width: 1008px;" srcset="https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/couldnt%20parse.png?width=504&amp;height=344&amp;name=couldnt%20parse.png 504w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/couldnt%20parse.png?width=1008&amp;height=688&amp;name=couldnt%20parse.png 1008w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/couldnt%20parse.png?width=1512&amp;height=1032&amp;name=couldnt%20parse.png 1512w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/couldnt%20parse.png?width=2016&amp;height=1376&amp;name=couldnt%20parse.png 2016w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/couldnt%20parse.png?width=2520&amp;height=1720&amp;name=couldnt%20parse.png 2520w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/couldnt%20parse.png?width=3024&amp;height=2064&amp;name=couldnt%20parse.png 3024w" sizes="(max-width: 1008px) 100vw, 1008px"><br><span style="font-size: 16px;">client.StartServer function that initiates a connection with the server appends nsignal[.]net on port 1334 (<span style="font-weight: bold;">Source</span>: HUMAN Threat Intelligence)</span></p>
<p>The socket maintains communication by using a “heartbeat” with a “ping pong” message in order to confirm that it has an established connection with the C2 and only then processes any request from the proxy network.&nbsp;</p>

<h2>PROXYLIB v2: LumiApps SDK</h2>
<p>In late May 2023, Satori researchers observed activity on hacker forums and new VPN applications referencing a monetization SDK, <strong><em>lumiapps[.]io</em></strong>. Upon further investigation, the team determined that this SDK has exactly the same functionality and uses the same server infrastructure as the malicious applications analyzed as part of the investigation into the earlier version of PROXYLIB.&nbsp;</p>
<p>In addition to an SDK that developers can integrate into their code during development, LumiApps provides a service where any user can upload an APK to have the SDK added to it.&nbsp; Users can then re-download or share this new version.</p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><img src="https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/lumi%20home.png?width=498&amp;height=374&amp;name=lumi%20home.png" width="498" height="374" loading="lazy" alt="lumi home" style="height: auto; max-width: 100%; width: 498px; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto; display: block;" srcset="https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/lumi%20home.png?width=249&amp;height=187&amp;name=lumi%20home.png 249w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/lumi%20home.png?width=498&amp;height=374&amp;name=lumi%20home.png 498w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/lumi%20home.png?width=747&amp;height=561&amp;name=lumi%20home.png 747w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/lumi%20home.png?width=996&amp;height=748&amp;name=lumi%20home.png 996w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/lumi%20home.png?width=1245&amp;height=935&amp;name=lumi%20home.png 1245w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/lumi%20home.png?width=1494&amp;height=1122&amp;name=lumi%20home.png 1494w" sizes="(max-width: 498px) 100vw, 498px"><span style="font-size: 16px;">lumiapps[.]io landing page<br>(<span style="font-weight: bold;">Source</span>: HUMAN Threat Intelligence)</span></p>

<p>Satori has observed individuals using the LumiApps toolkit in the wild; most of the applications we identified between May and October 2023 appear to be modified versions of known legitimate applications, further indicating that users do not necessarily need to have access to the applications’ source code in order to modify them using LumiApps. These apps are largely named as “mods” or indicated as patched versions and shared outside of the Google Play Store.</p>
<h3>Technical Analysis</h3>
<p>As previously mentioned, LumiApps also provides developers the ability to integrate their SDK at development time into their Java, Kotlin or Unity projects as seen below in LumiApps’ documentation. The integration requires the inclusion of a library called app_pro.aar and the addition of 3 classes to the project: <em>AppProService</em>, <em>AppProReceiver</em> and <em>AppProWorker</em>.</p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><img src="https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/3-24%20Combined%20Lumi%20Docs.png?width=654&amp;height=881&amp;name=3-24%20Combined%20Lumi%20Docs.png" width="654" height="881" loading="lazy" alt="3-24 Combined Lumi Docs" style="height: auto; max-width: 100%; width: 654px; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto; display: block;" srcset="https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/3-24%20Combined%20Lumi%20Docs.png?width=327&amp;height=441&amp;name=3-24%20Combined%20Lumi%20Docs.png 327w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/3-24%20Combined%20Lumi%20Docs.png?width=654&amp;height=881&amp;name=3-24%20Combined%20Lumi%20Docs.png 654w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/3-24%20Combined%20Lumi%20Docs.png?width=981&amp;height=1322&amp;name=3-24%20Combined%20Lumi%20Docs.png 981w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/3-24%20Combined%20Lumi%20Docs.png?width=1308&amp;height=1762&amp;name=3-24%20Combined%20Lumi%20Docs.png 1308w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/3-24%20Combined%20Lumi%20Docs.png?width=1635&amp;height=2203&amp;name=3-24%20Combined%20Lumi%20Docs.png 1635w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/3-24%20Combined%20Lumi%20Docs.png?width=1962&amp;height=2643&amp;name=3-24%20Combined%20Lumi%20Docs.png 1962w" sizes="(max-width: 654px) 100vw, 654px"><span style="font-size: 16px;">Integration of SDK into project at development time</span><br><span style="font-size: 16px;">(<span style="font-weight: bold;">Source</span>: HUMAN Threat Intelligence)</span></p>
<p>Satori identified hundreds of apps in online third party repositories containing these IOCs, mainly “mods” distributed outside of the Google Play Store. We also found multiple developers who integrated the LumiApps SDK into their apps and distributed them in the Google Play Store.</p>

<p>These applications work very similarly to the applications analyzed as part of the first version of PROXYLIB threat. The <em>AppProService</em> class contains all of the malicious code to obtain the C2 server domain and load the <em>libgojni.so</em> native library. The <em>AppProWorker </em>and the <em>AppProReceiver</em> classes are simply responsible for starting the service. The former starts the service when the application runs for the first time, and the latter enables the service’s persistence and executes whenever the device is booted.</p>
<p>To obtain the C2 address, the application connects with either <em>app-sdk-reg</em> or <em>app-sdk-poll</em> endpoints on <em>api[.]lumiapps[.]io/api/v1/clients</em> and returns the C2 in the “signaling” JSON key and locally stores it in the shared preferences with the same.</p>

<p>Just as with the first version of PROXYLIB, the C2 URL is provided as an argument for the <em>proxylib.Proxylib.startServer</em> method that executes native code as seen below.</p>

<h2>Monetizing PROXYLIB: Asocks</h2>
<p>Satori researchers believe the threat actor behind PROXYLIB is selling access to the proxy network created by the infected devices through Asocks, a company that advertises itself as a seller of residential proxies. While monitoring the traffic from the PROXYLIB applications, Satori researchers observed several connections being made to <em>asocks[.]com</em>, a residential proxy network.</p>
<p><img src="https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/asocks%20tos.png?width=394&amp;height=270&amp;name=asocks%20tos.png" width="394" height="270" loading="lazy" alt="asocks tos" style="height: auto; max-width: 100%; width: 394px; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto; display: block;" srcset="https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/asocks%20tos.png?width=197&amp;height=135&amp;name=asocks%20tos.png 197w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/asocks%20tos.png?width=394&amp;height=270&amp;name=asocks%20tos.png 394w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/asocks%20tos.png?width=591&amp;height=405&amp;name=asocks%20tos.png 591w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/asocks%20tos.png?width=788&amp;height=540&amp;name=asocks%20tos.png 788w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/asocks%20tos.png?width=985&amp;height=675&amp;name=asocks%20tos.png 985w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/asocks%20tos.png?width=1182&amp;height=810&amp;name=asocks%20tos.png 1182w" sizes="(max-width: 394px) 100vw, 394px"><br>The Asocks website provides no information on how their residential proxies are obtained. One of the sentences of the Terms of Service references a sentence that can be interpreted as the definition of proxy service.</p>
<h3>Linking Asocks to PROXYLIB</h3>
<p>The Asocks platform allows users to create and manage their proxy connections and exit points, while it offers proxy nodes for nearly all countries, the availability of residential exit points varies significantly by country.</p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><img src="https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/asocks%20countries.png?width=576&amp;height=506&amp;name=asocks%20countries.png" width="576" height="506" loading="lazy" alt="asocks countries" style="height: auto; max-width: 100%; width: 576px; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto; display: block;" srcset="https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/asocks%20countries.png?width=288&amp;height=253&amp;name=asocks%20countries.png 288w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/asocks%20countries.png?width=576&amp;height=506&amp;name=asocks%20countries.png 576w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/asocks%20countries.png?width=864&amp;height=759&amp;name=asocks%20countries.png 864w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/asocks%20countries.png?width=1152&amp;height=1012&amp;name=asocks%20countries.png 1152w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/asocks%20countries.png?width=1440&amp;height=1265&amp;name=asocks%20countries.png 1440w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/asocks%20countries.png?width=1728&amp;height=1518&amp;name=asocks%20countries.png 1728w" sizes="(max-width: 576px) 100vw, 576px"><span style="font-size: 16px;">Country selection menu</span><br><span style="font-size: 16px;">(<span style="font-weight: bold;">Source</span>: HUMAN Threat Intelligence)</span></p>
<p>Of the more than 150,000 combinations of IP addresses and port numbers returned by an Asocks proxy-list endpoint, HUMAN found only 170 unique IP addresses that we believe are the proxy entry points.<br><br>After observing requests from the PROXYLIB malware directly to asocks[.]com, Satori researchers observed requests made to Asocks exiting through our infected test device, indicating that the device was now enrolled as a proxy node in the Asocks residential proxy network. This can be observed in the following screenshots: the first screenshot shows the IP of our Android test device with the PROXYLIB malware installed, and the second screenshot shows the exit IP address of a different device using the Asocks residential proxy service. Notice that the second device’s traffic is exiting through the test device’s IP address.</p>

<h3>Monetizing LumiApps SDK via Asocks</h3>
<p>The threat actor behind PROXYLIB makes money by selling access to the residential proxy network to others. As a result, the threat actor must incentivize developers or other threat actors to build the LumiApps SDK and platform into their apps to ensure that as many proxy nodes are enrolled in the network as possible.&nbsp;<br><br>The LumiApps platform promotes itself and its SDK as an alternative app monetization method to rendering ads to users. According to their FAQ and available information, the platform rewards developers with cash payment based on the amount of traffic that gets routed through user devices.</p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><img src="https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/3-24%20lumi%20wallet.png?width=1434&amp;height=680&amp;name=3-24%20lumi%20wallet.png" width="1434" height="680" loading="lazy" alt="3-24 lumi wallet" style="height: auto; max-width: 100%; width: 1434px;" srcset="https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/3-24%20lumi%20wallet.png?width=717&amp;height=340&amp;name=3-24%20lumi%20wallet.png 717w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/3-24%20lumi%20wallet.png?width=1434&amp;height=680&amp;name=3-24%20lumi%20wallet.png 1434w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/3-24%20lumi%20wallet.png?width=2151&amp;height=1020&amp;name=3-24%20lumi%20wallet.png 2151w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/3-24%20lumi%20wallet.png?width=2868&amp;height=1360&amp;name=3-24%20lumi%20wallet.png 2868w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/3-24%20lumi%20wallet.png?width=3585&amp;height=1700&amp;name=3-24%20lumi%20wallet.png 3585w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/3-24%20lumi%20wallet.png?width=4302&amp;height=2040&amp;name=3-24%20lumi%20wallet.png 4302w" sizes="(max-width: 1434px) 100vw, 1434px"><br><span style="font-size: 16px;">Cashout options, allows for payments in Paypal and crypto currencies</span><br><span style="font-size: 16px;">(<span style="font-weight: bold;">Source</span>: HUMAN Threat Intelligence)</span></p>
<p>There is specific language that attempts to legitimize the SDK, including a dialog box that purports to get user consent to the proxy service.</p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><img src="https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/dialog.png?width=217&amp;height=426&amp;name=dialog.png" width="217" height="426" loading="lazy" alt="dialog" style="height: auto; max-width: 100%; width: 217px; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto; display: block;" srcset="https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/dialog.png?width=109&amp;height=213&amp;name=dialog.png 109w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/dialog.png?width=217&amp;height=426&amp;name=dialog.png 217w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/dialog.png?width=326&amp;height=639&amp;name=dialog.png 326w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/dialog.png?width=434&amp;height=852&amp;name=dialog.png 434w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/dialog.png?width=543&amp;height=1065&amp;name=dialog.png 543w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/dialog.png?width=651&amp;height=1278&amp;name=dialog.png 651w" sizes="(max-width: 217px) 100vw, 217px"><span style="font-size: 16px;">User consent screen shown in the lumiapps[.]io claims to render on a users device for each SDK integration</span><br><span style="font-size: 16px;">(<span style="font-weight: bold;">Source</span>: HUMAN Threat Intelligence)</span></p>
<p>The LumiApps SDK contains a sample MainActivity class that is responsible for showing the consent screen above, but HUMAN did not observe the class in any of the applications found in the wild. Furthermore, uploading an APK into the LumiApps tool does not add the disclosure message class to the final APK, it simply adds the backdoor code.</p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><img src="https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/show%20dialog.png?width=535&amp;height=215&amp;name=show%20dialog.png" width="535" height="215" loading="lazy" alt="show dialog" style="height: auto; max-width: 100%; width: 535px; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto; display: block;" srcset="https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/show%20dialog.png?width=268&amp;height=108&amp;name=show%20dialog.png 268w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/show%20dialog.png?width=535&amp;height=215&amp;name=show%20dialog.png 535w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/show%20dialog.png?width=803&amp;height=323&amp;name=show%20dialog.png 803w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/show%20dialog.png?width=1070&amp;height=430&amp;name=show%20dialog.png 1070w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/show%20dialog.png?width=1338&amp;height=538&amp;name=show%20dialog.png 1338w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/show%20dialog.png?width=1605&amp;height=645&amp;name=show%20dialog.png 1605w" sizes="(max-width: 535px) 100vw, 535px"><span style="font-size: 16px;">Sample <em>MainActivity</em> class with <em>showTermsDialog</em> that shows the disclosure</span><br><span style="font-size: 16px;">(<span style="font-weight: bold;">Source</span>: HUMAN Threat Intelligence)</span></p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><img src="https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/testapp.jpg?width=215&amp;height=371&amp;name=testapp.jpg" width="215" height="371" loading="lazy" alt="testapp" style="height: auto; max-width: 100%; width: 215px; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto; display: block;" srcset="https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/testapp.jpg?width=108&amp;height=186&amp;name=testapp.jpg 108w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/testapp.jpg?width=215&amp;height=371&amp;name=testapp.jpg 215w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/testapp.jpg?width=323&amp;height=557&amp;name=testapp.jpg 323w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/testapp.jpg?width=430&amp;height=742&amp;name=testapp.jpg 430w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/testapp.jpg?width=538&amp;height=928&amp;name=testapp.jpg 538w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/testapp.jpg?width=645&amp;height=1113&amp;name=testapp.jpg 645w" sizes="(max-width: 215px) 100vw, 215px"><span style="font-size: 16px;">Disclosure not present in a testing application uploaded to the automatic integration</span><br><span style="font-size: 16px;">(<span style="font-weight: bold;">Source</span>: HUMAN Threat Intelligence)</span></p>
<p>The threat actors behind LumiApps seek to recruit developers and other users to their platform to increase the number of users whose devices become nodes in their residential proxy network. Satori researchers found several instances of LumiApps promoting its SDK on social media and in hacker forums, showing that the threat actors target legitimate developers as well as cybercriminals.</p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><img src="https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/3-24%20combined%20social%20image.png?width=426&amp;height=334&amp;name=3-24%20combined%20social%20image.png" width="426" height="334" loading="lazy" alt="3-24 combined social image" style="height: auto; max-width: 100%; width: 426px; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto; display: block;" srcset="https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/3-24%20combined%20social%20image.png?width=213&amp;height=167&amp;name=3-24%20combined%20social%20image.png 213w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/3-24%20combined%20social%20image.png?width=426&amp;height=334&amp;name=3-24%20combined%20social%20image.png 426w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/3-24%20combined%20social%20image.png?width=639&amp;height=501&amp;name=3-24%20combined%20social%20image.png 639w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/3-24%20combined%20social%20image.png?width=852&amp;height=668&amp;name=3-24%20combined%20social%20image.png 852w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/3-24%20combined%20social%20image.png?width=1065&amp;height=835&amp;name=3-24%20combined%20social%20image.png 1065w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/3-24%20combined%20social%20image.png?width=1278&amp;height=1002&amp;name=3-24%20combined%20social%20image.png 1278w" sizes="(max-width: 426px) 100vw, 426px"><span style="font-size: 16px;">Social media presence, profiles were also found on Facebook and X</span><br><span style="font-size: 16px;">(<span style="font-weight: bold;">Source</span>: HUMAN Threat Intelligence)</span></p>

<h3>Connecting LumiApps to Asocks</h3>
<p>Satori researchers identified a connection between Asocks—the seller of residential proxies described above—and LumiApps through registration data from LumiApps platform. When a user registers an account on lumiapps[.]io, the headers from the confirmation email contain the domain bproxy[.]one, which no longer has an accessible web page. However, when searching for this domain on archive[.]org, there was a non-stylized version of the Asocks website as recently as February 23, 2023, as seen in the image below. As a result, Satori researchers have high confidence that the two services are connected and potentially owned or operated by the same threat actor.</p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><span style="font-size: 16px;"><img src="https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/3-24%20edited%20broxy.png?width=697&amp;height=65&amp;name=3-24%20edited%20broxy.png" width="697" height="65" loading="lazy" alt="3-24 edited broxy" style="height: auto; max-width: 100%; width: 697px;" srcset="https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/3-24%20edited%20broxy.png?width=349&amp;height=33&amp;name=3-24%20edited%20broxy.png 349w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/3-24%20edited%20broxy.png?width=697&amp;height=65&amp;name=3-24%20edited%20broxy.png 697w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/3-24%20edited%20broxy.png?width=1046&amp;height=98&amp;name=3-24%20edited%20broxy.png 1046w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/3-24%20edited%20broxy.png?width=1394&amp;height=130&amp;name=3-24%20edited%20broxy.png 1394w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/3-24%20edited%20broxy.png?width=1743&amp;height=163&amp;name=3-24%20edited%20broxy.png 1743w, https://www.humansecurity.com/hs-fs/hubfs/3-24%20edited%20broxy.png?width=2091&amp;height=195&amp;name=3-24%20edited%20broxy.png 2091w" sizes="(max-width: 697px) 100vw, 697px"><br>Email header of from LumiApps[.]io when registering a new account</span><br><span style="font-size: 16px;">(<span style="font-weight: bold;">Source</span>: HUMAN Threat Intelligence)</span></p>

<h2>Conclusion</h2>
<p>At the time of the writing of this report, 28 applications containing the PROXYLIB SDK identified in this report have been removed from the Play Store and HUMAN continues to work to disrupt the threat posed by PROXYLIB.&nbsp;</p>
<p><strong>However, the threat actor continues to operate the LumiApps platform and release new versions of the SDK that can be built into additional apps.</strong> As a result, we expect to see the threat actor continue to evolve their TTPs in order to continue selling access to the residential proxy network generated by apps containing PROXYLIB. HUMAN Bot Defender blocked the vast majority of the traffic from IPs associated with Asocks that were used in ATO, scraping, scalping and other related attacks This is an ongoing investigation and effort to block malicious threat actors and fraud operations as cybercriminals continue to update the malicious SDKs.&nbsp;</p>
<p>HUMAN recommends that users download mobile apps exclusively from official marketplaces, such as the Google Play Store or iOS App Store. Further, users should avoid clones or “mods” of popular apps which may allow malware or undesired functionality such as the PROXYLIB residential proxy node enrollment discussed in this report to masquerade as benign software.</p>
<p>Residential proxy networks aren’t going away, but HUMAN protects customers against many common attacks conducted using proxy networks. <a href="https://www.humansecurity.com/platform/defense-modules/account-takeover"><span>Account takeover attacks</span></a>, <a href="https://www.humansecurity.com/platform/defense-modules/programmatic-ad-fraud"><span>programmatic ad fraud</span></a>, <a href="https://www.humansecurity.com/platform/defense-modules/scraping"><span>web scraping</span></a>, and <a href="https://www.humansecurity.com/platform/defense-modules/transaction-abuse"><span>transaction abuse</span></a> all often operate through proxy networks as a mechanism to hide the true source of fraudulent traffic. But the Human Defense Platform doesn’t rely on IP addresses—the characteristic masked by using a residential proxy—to determine whether traffic is fraudulent or legitimate.</p>
<p>HUMAN will continue to monitor for adaptations of PROXYLIB and for attacks carried out through residential proxy networks.</p>
