<div style="padding: 30px;border-radius: 10px;background-color: #f0f0fa;margin-bottom:20px"><h4 style="padding-top: 0;margin-top: 0;font-size:24px">Authors&#8217; Note</h4>
<p>This blog post is the second in a series of two on the Quad7 botnet. It presents the botnets operated by the Quad7 operators and deduce their possible next moves. However, if you are new on this topic, you can read the first blog post here: <strong><a href="https://blog.sekoia.io/solving-the-7777-botnet-enigma-a-cybersecurity-quest/" title="Solving the 7777 Botnet enigma: A cybersecurity quest" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Solving the 7777 Botnet enigma: A cybersecurity quest</a></strong></p></div>



<div style="padding: 20px;border-radius: 10px;background-color: #FAFAFA;margin-bottom:20px">
<h2 style="padding-top: 0;margin-top: 0">Key Takeaways</h2>

<ul>
<li>The Sekoia TDR team has recently identified new staging servers, leading to the discovery of additional targets, implants, and botnet clusters tied to the Quad7 operators.</li><br />
<li>The Quad7 botnet operators seem to be compromising several brands of SOHO routers and VPN appliances, including TP-LINK, Zyxel, Asus, Axentra, D-Link, and Netgear, using multiple vulnerabilities—some of which are previously unknown.</li>
<br />
<li>The Quad7 botnet operators appear to be evolving their toolset, introducing a new backdoor and exploring new protocols, with the aim of enhancing stealth and evading the tracking capabilities of their operational relay boxes (ORBs).</li>
<br />
<li>Given these developments, it is possible that without interception capabilities, tracking the evolution of Quad7 botnets could become nearly impossible in the near future.</li>
</ul>
</div>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-introduction"><strong>Introduction</strong></h2>



<p>Previously, we detailed <a href="https://blog.sekoia.io/solving-the-7777-botnet-enigma-a-cybersecurity-quest" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">our investigation on the Quad7 botnet</a> and our live forensic methodology against a TP-LINK router to get related implants on it. Following this first publication, we continued to track this botnet activity and new clusters related to the Quad7 botnet operators.</p>



<p>Before our blogpost on the Quad7 botnet, we started to monitor another botnet, the alogin botnet (aka 63256 botnet) and attributed this botnet to the Quad7 botnet operators with medium confidence. On 7 August 2024, Team Cymru published a <a href="https://www.team-cymru.com/post/botnet-7777-are-you-betting-on-a-compromised-router" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">blogpost</a> confirming the two botnets were operated by the same group as they shared some common administration servers.</p>



<p>Recently, we came across several staging servers, leading us to discover <strong>new targets</strong>, <strong>implants</strong> and <strong>botnet clusters</strong> associated with this threat actor. This new discovery allows us to have a glimpse of the Quad7 next moves as it seems that the operators are developing <strong>HTTP reverse shells</strong> and test <strong>new projects</strong> to relay their attacks instead of using simple <strong>open socks proxies</strong> to be more stealthy and prevent tracking.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-the-wildcard-login-galaxy"><strong>The wildcard login galaxy</strong></h2>



<p>As of this writing, we are aware of five different *login clusters linked to this threat actor (<strong>alogin</strong>, <strong>xlogin</strong>, <strong>axlogin</strong>, <strong>rlogin</strong> and <strong>zylogin</strong>), which we are temporarily referring to internally as the <strong>Quad7 Botnet Operators</strong>.</p>



<p>The Quad7 botnet (aka 7777 botnet, <strong>xlogin</strong> botnet) is a botnet composed of compromised <strong>TP-Link routers</strong> which have both TCP ports TELNET/7777 and 11288 opened. The 7777 port is the administration port hosting a bind shell with root privileges (xlogin). This bind shell is password protected. The 11288 port is the Socks5 proxy port, mostly password protected, and this proxy is used to relay <strong>M365 accounts brute force attacks</strong>.</p>



<p>The <strong>alogin</strong> botnet is a botnet composed of compromised <strong>Asus routers</strong> which have both TCP ports 63256 and 63260 opened. The TELNET/63256 port is the administration port hosting a bind shell with root privileges (alogin). This bind shell is also password protected. The SOCKS/63260 port is hosting a password protected Socks5 proxy.</p>



<p>According to our telemetry the alogin botnet is used to relay <strong>brute force attempts on internet exposed services such as VPN, telnet and SSH</strong>. However, we took these observations with a grain of salt as different threat actors can relay attacks from the same compromised assets.</p>



<p>While looking for alogin samples, we found one on VirusTotal, which was hosted on a staging server controlled by the Quad7 operators. This led us to discover another *login sample, named <strong>rlogin</strong> and used to target <a href="https://www.ruckusnetworks.com" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener"><strong>Ruckus Wireless</strong></a> devices.</p>



<p>The bind shell of rlogin is listening on TCP port TELNET/63210. The bind shell is password protected and sometimes includes a challenge as well after the password prompt. The rlogin botnet does not seem to open a proxy port on compromised Ruckus devices. Another notable difference with the other *login botnets is the botnet size, on one hand, there is the alogin and xlogin botnets with several thousand compromised devices. On the other hand, there is the rlogin botnet, which is <strong>composed of 213 devices as of 26 August 2024 according to Censys</strong>. The first infections for this botnet seem relatively new, with most of the devices compromised on 16 June 2024.</p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-when-the-quad7-shells-start-calling-home"><strong>When the Quad7 shells start calling home…</strong></h2>



<p>Beside the discovery of the new *login samples, we also found three backdoors acting as HTTP-based reverse shells, nicknamed <strong>UPDTAE backdoor</strong> because of a typo. These backdoors seem to be currently tested by the operators prior to deployment on compromised routers. We discovered three UPDTAE implants:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Two for MIPS apparently deployed on <strong>ASUS routers</strong>;</li>



<li>Another one packed with UPX, for ARM and apparently to be deployed on <strong>Axentra NAS</strong>.</li>
</ul>



<p>These backdoors are statically linked with the <a href="https://curl.se/libcurl/" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">libcurl library</a>, beaconing through HTTP requests every 30 seconds over HTTP with a User-Agent taking the value of <span style="background-color:#FAFAFA;border-radius:3px;padding3px;border:1px solid #CCC;padding-right:5px;padding-left:5px;font-family:monospace">IOT</span>. The code is poorly designed with several mistakes and remains very simple.</p>



<p>Despite the beaconing requests and command results being sent via JSON POST requests, the commands are transmitted from the server to the infected hosts through a simple webpage with the following body content: .</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Command <span style="background-color:#FAFAFA;border-radius:3px;padding3px;border:1px solid #CCC;padding-right:5px;padding-left:5px;font-family:monospace">1</span>: Update the implant C2 URL;</li>



<li>Command <span style="background-color:#FAFAFA;border-radius:3px;padding3px;border:1px solid #CCC;padding-right:5px;padding-left:5px;font-family:monospace">3</span>: Execute a command on the compromised device.</li>
</ul>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-meet-fsynet">Meet FsyNet</h3>



<p>While looking on this server, we discovered another potential move from the Quad7 threat actor to prevent the reuse / correlation. In one folder of the server there was a bash file named <span style="background-color:#FAFAFA;border-radius:3px;padding3px;border:1px solid #CCC;padding-right:5px;padding-left:5px;font-family:monospace">fsy.bin</span> concatenated with a tarball. This bash file aimed to set up firewall rules and launch three binaries (<span style="background-color:#FAFAFA;border-radius:3px;padding3px;border:1px solid #CCC;padding-right:5px;padding-left:5px;font-family:monospace">asr_node</span>, <span style="background-color:#FAFAFA;border-radius:3px;padding3px;border:1px solid #CCC;padding-right:5px;padding-left:5px;font-family:monospace">node-r-control</span>, <span style="background-color:#FAFAFA;border-radius:3px;padding3px;border:1px solid #CCC;padding-right:5px;padding-left:5px;font-family:monospace">node-relay</span>) relaying a project nicknamed “<strong>FsyNet</strong>” which uses <a href="https://github.com/skywind3000/kcp/blob/master/README.en.md" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener"><strong>KCP</strong></a> as a communication protocol.</p>



<p>Here is the installation script:</p>



<div style="background-color:#FAFAFA;border-radius:3px;padding3px;border:1px solid #CCC;padding:10px;font-family:monospace;font-size:16px">#!/bin/sh<br />
firewall-cmd &#8211;permanent &#8211;add-port 9999/udp<br />
firewall-cmd &#8211;permanent &#8211;add-port 10000-40000/tcp<br />
firewall-cmd &#8211;permanent &#8211;add-port 10000-40000/udp<br />
mkdir /tmp/fsynet<br />
tail -n +20 $0 >/tmp/fsynet/fsy.tar.gz<br />
path=`pwd`<br />
cd /tmp/fsynet<br />
tar zxf fsy.tar.gz<br />
chmod -R 777 /tmp/fsynet/<br />
sleep 1<br />
./asr_node<br />
./node-r-control<br />
./node-relay<br />
sleep 1<br />
cd $path<br />
rm -rf $0<br />
rm -rf /tmp/fsynet<br />
exit 0</div><br />



<p>These three binaries are compiled for the MIPS architecture, developed in C++, and share the same codebase. We began our analysis with the <span style="background-color:#FAFAFA;border-radius:3px;padding3px;border:1px solid #CCC;padding-right:5px;padding-left:5px;font-family:monospace">asr_node</span> binary (analysis of <span style="background-color:#FAFAFA;border-radius:3px;padding3px;border:1px solid #CCC;padding-right:5px;padding-left:5px;font-family:monospace">node-r-control</span> and <span style="background-color:#FAFAFA;border-radius:3px;padding3px;border:1px solid #CCC;padding-right:5px;padding-left:5px;font-family:monospace">node-relay</span> is still in progress) because it:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Opens a listener port on the network (UDP port 9999) &#8211; useful for scanning ORBs;</li>



<li>Contains the most symbols;</li>



<li>Includes noteworthy symbols such as <span style="background-color:#FAFAFA;border-radius:3px;padding3px;border:1px solid #CCC;padding-right:5px;padding-left:5px;font-family:monospace">fsy::CommandExecutor</span> and <span style="background-color:#FAFAFA;border-radius:3px;padding3px;border:1px solid #CCC;padding-right:5px;padding-left:5px;font-family:monospace">fsy::FileSender</span></li>
</ul>



<p>The binary <span style="background-color:#FAFAFA;border-radius:3px;padding3px;border:1px solid #CCC;padding-right:5px;padding-left:5px;font-family:monospace">asr_node</span> creates and starts an instance of an <span style="background-color:#FAFAFA;border-radius:3px;padding3px;border:1px solid #CCC;padding-right:5px;padding-left:5px;font-family:monospace">fsy::CommandExecutor</span> and <span style="background-color:#FAFAFA;border-radius:3px;padding3px;border:1px solid #CCC;padding-right:5px;padding-left:5px;font-family:monospace">fsy::FsyApplication</span> object. As shown in the following figure, this object consists of several modules, including:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li><span style="background-color:#FAFAFA;border-radius:3px;padding3px;border:1px solid #CCC;padding-right:5px;padding-left:5px;font-family:monospace">CommandExecutor</span></li>



<li><span style="background-color:#FAFAFA;border-radius:3px;padding3px;border:1px solid #CCC;padding-right:5px;padding-left:5px;font-family:monospace">ConUdpServer</span></li>



<li>Two <span style="background-color:#FAFAFA;border-radius:3px;padding3px;border:1px solid #CCC;padding-right:5px;padding-left:5px;font-family:monospace">ConTcpListener</span></li>



<li><span style="background-color:#FAFAFA;border-radius:3px;padding3px;border:1px solid #CCC;padding-right:5px;padding-left:5px;font-family:monospace">ConKcpClient</span></li>

<p>Once decrypted, the message contains another header that include several fields, like <span style="background-color:#FAFAFA;border-radius:3px;padding3px;border:1px solid #CCC;padding-right:5px;padding-left:5px;font-family:monospace">src</span> (source), <span style="background-color:#FAFAFA;border-radius:3px;padding3px;border:1px solid #CCC;padding-right:5px;padding-left:5px;font-family:monospace">tar</span> (target), and <span style="background-color:#FAFAFA;border-radius:3px;padding3px;border:1px solid #CCC;padding-right:5px;padding-left:5px;font-family:monospace">cmd</span> (command), among others. For example, the <span style="background-color:#FAFAFA;border-radius:3px;padding3px;border:1px solid #CCC;padding-right:5px;padding-left:5px;font-family:monospace">ConUdpServer</span> accepts two types of messages:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li><span style="background-color:#FAFAFA;border-radius:3px;padding3px;border:1px solid #CCC;padding-right:5px;padding-left:5px;font-family:monospace">FSY_UDP_Verify</span>: After verifying that the provided session key is valid, a <span style="background-color:#FAFAFA;border-radius:3px;padding3px;border:1px solid #CCC;padding-right:5px;padding-left:5px;font-family:monospace">ConTcpServer</span> is started;</li>



<li><span style="background-color:#FAFAFA;border-radius:3px;padding3px;border:1px solid #CCC;padding-right:5px;padding-left:5px;font-family:monospace">FSY_UDP_Request</span>: Creates a new session key.</li>
</ul>



<p>The different modules that can be set to listen on the network by the application use the same message format. Depending on the type of the message, these can be placed in a queue shared between all modules and processed by the <span style="background-color:#FAFAFA;border-radius:3px;padding3px;border:1px solid #CCC;padding-right:5px;padding-left:5px;font-family:monospace">CommandExecutor</span> module. <span style="background-color:#FAFAFA;border-radius:3px;padding3px;border:1px solid #CCC;padding-right:5px;padding-left:5px;font-family:monospace">CommandExecutor</span> is not about executing local commands but rather handling messages via the value of the <span style="background-color:#FAFAFA;border-radius:3px;padding3px;border:1px solid #CCC;padding-right:5px;padding-left:5px;font-family:monospace">cmd</span> field in the message header. This could involve forwarding a message, updating an encryption key, using Tor, etc.</p>



<p>It is important to note that the initial FsyNet binary was discovered in a folder associated with Ruckus network appliances. Consequently, we scanned more than 14k Ruckus appliances indexed by Censys and found that <strong>30 of them responded to our KCP scan</strong>, with 4 showing no <span style="background-color:#FAFAFA;border-radius:3px;padding3px;border:1px solid #CCC;padding-right:5px;padding-left:5px;font-family:monospace">rlogin</span> listening on them, even in Censys&#8217; history.</p>



<p>It is worth mentioning that these four appliances without rlogin on them have the SSL certificate <span style="background-color:#FAFAFA;border-radius:3px;padding3px;border:1px solid #CCC;padding-right:5px;padding-left:5px;font-family:monospace">C=US, ST=California, L=LA, O=LAPD, OU=Police department, CN=ROOT</span>. This certificate can be found on 353 other IP addresses, mixing <strong>Ruckus Wireless</strong> and <strong>D-LINK</strong> network appliances, as well as dozens of VPSs residing in autonomous systems notorious for hosting malicious activities (M247, CHOOPA, xTom, BLNWX, KAOPU etc.). As of this writing, although this appears to be related to yet another botnet, we have not been able to link the use of this certificate to the Quad7 operators.</p>



<p></p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-and-netd">… and Netd</h3>



<p>In another folder named <strong>ASUS</strong>, we discovered a shell script called <span style="background-color:#FAFAFA;border-radius:3px;padding3px;border:1px solid #CCC;padding-right:5px;padding-left:5px;font-family:monospace">exec.sh</span>, which is used against various vendors and network appliances such as <strong>ASUS</strong>, <strong>D-LINK DIR-610</strong>, and <strong>Netgear R7000</strong>. The script is designed to download files named netd and <span style="background-color:#FAFAFA;border-radius:3px;padding3px;border:1px solid #CCC;padding-right:5px;padding-left:5px;font-family:monospace">tun.ko</span>, execute netd, and set up firewall rules. Although we were unable to download the <span style="background-color:#FAFAFA;border-radius:3px;padding3px;border:1px solid #CCC;padding-right:5px;padding-left:5px;font-family:monospace">tun.ko</span> file, the netd binary was available for download.</p>



<p>It appears that the netd binary is intended to use the compromised network appliance as a relay node, this time with a fork of <a href="https://github.com/cjdelisle/cjdns/blob/master/client/cjdroute2.c" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener"><strong>cjdroute2</strong></a> utilizing the <strong><a href="https://github.com/cjdelisle/cjdns/blob/master/doc/Whitepaper.md" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">CJDNS protocol</a></strong>—a darknet protocol used to create independent networks over the internet—rather than the KCP protocol. When installed, a configuration file (<span style="background-color:#FAFAFA;border-radius:3px;padding3px;border:1px solid #CCC;padding-right:5px;padding-left:5px;font-family:monospace">netd.dat</span>) and a file containing system-related information (<span style="background-color:#FAFAFA;border-radius:3px;padding3px;border:1px solid #CCC;padding-right:5px;padding-left:5px;font-family:monospace">sys.dat</span>), both encrypted using <strong>Salsa20</strong>, are created and sent back to the attacker&#8217;s server. Once in possession of <strong>netd.dat</strong>, the operators will be able to create a secure connection over UDP between their ORB and their C2 server to tunnel communications.</p>



<p>Unfortunately, unlike the FsyNet binary, <strong>the listening UDP port is randomized for each ORB, making wide-scale scanning for compromised appliances impossible</strong>.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-conclusion">Conclusion</h2>



<p>Edge devices continue to play a significant role in the operational infrastructures of numerous threat actors, including the Quad7 botnet operators. These devices are often targeted due to their accessibility, vulnerabilities, and their use in providing exit nodes that facilitate anonymous and distributed attacks. With many compromised devices spread across various regions, malicious actors can conceal their operations and launch attacks with minimal risk of exposure. The Quad7 operators have particularly demonstrated how these compromised devices can be exploited for tasks such as relaying brute-force attacks.</p>



<p>However, as highlighted in our last investigation, the <strong>Quad7 operators have not been without mistakes</strong>. Their early infrastructure relied heavily on open SOCKS proxies and poorly designed code, such as bind shells with hardcoded passwords, making it easier for researchers to track their botnets. Nevertheless, <strong>they seem to be learning from their missteps and adapting their tactics</strong>. The development of new tools, such as HTTP reverse shells and the use of more secure communication protocols like KCP, suggests they are actively working to evade detection and complicate efforts to attribute their activities. These changes indicate a shift towards more stealthy methods of operation, likely in response to increased scrutiny from security researchers and organizations.</p>



<p>It is critical for defenders to stay vigilant, adapt their detection methods, and continue to track these actors closely as they evolve.</p>
